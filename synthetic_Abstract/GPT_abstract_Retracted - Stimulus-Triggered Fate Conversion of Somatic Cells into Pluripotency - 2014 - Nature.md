It is generally thought that acquisition of pluripotency by somatic cells requires direct nuclear manipulation, such as nuclear transfer or the forced expression of defined transcription factors. Here we show that committed somatic cells can be converted into pluripotent cells by transient exposure to strong external stimuli. Mouse CD45+ haematopoietic cells subjected to sublethal low-pH treatment generated Oct4-GFP+ clusters that lost lineage-marker expression, activated pluripotency-associated genes, and showed extensive demethylation at the Oct4 and Nanog loci. These stimulus-triggered acquisition of pluripotency (STAP) cells differentiated into derivatives of all three germ layers in vitro and formed teratomas in vivo. After blastocyst injection, STAP cells contributed to chimaeric embryos and mice, including germline transmission, and supported embryonic development in tetraploid complementation assays. Under ACTH-containing culture conditions, STAP cells gave rise to expandable STAP stem-cell lines with embryonic-stem-cell-like properties. These findings indicate that somatic cells retain a latent, stimulus-responsive capacity for radical fate conversion.
