Precise methods for rewriting mammalian genomes are essential for linking genetic variation to biological function. Here, we report the adaptation of the Streptococcus pyogenes type II CRISPR/Cas system as an RNA-guided platform for genome engineering in human and mouse cells. Heterologous expression of Cas9 with tracrRNA and pre-crRNA was sufficient to generate efficient double-stranded breaks at endogenous loci, whereas guide RNAs programmed cleavage specificity through simple base pairing to sequences adjacent to an NGG protospacer-adjacent motif. The system targeted multiple loci, supported homology-directed insertion, and could be converted to a nickase that maintained template-mediated repair while reducing detectable mutagenic repair. Encoding multiple spacers in a single CRISPR array enabled simultaneous modification of distinct genomic sites and deletion of an intervening region. These results establish CRISPR/Cas as a scalable, multiplexable genome engineering technology.
