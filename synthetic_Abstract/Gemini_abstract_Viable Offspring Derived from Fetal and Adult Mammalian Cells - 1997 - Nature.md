It has long been debated whether a fully differentiated adult somatic nucleus can be reprogrammed to support complete development to term. Previous successes in mammalian cloning have been restricted to the use of early embryonic cells as nuclear donors. Here, we demonstrate that live, normal offspring can be generated using nuclei derived from both fetal and adult mammalian tissues. We established cell populations from an ovine day-9 embryo, a day-26 fetus, and the mammary gland of a 6-year-old adult ewe. Crucially, prior to nuclear transfer, these donor cells were induced to exit the growth phase and arrest in the G0 phase of the cell cycle through serum starvation. When these quiescent nuclei were fused with enucleated oocytes, they successfully supported full development. We report the birth of eight live lambs, including one derived from the adult mammary gland cell, whose genetic identity was confirmed by microsatellite analysis. These results prove that the genetic information of a differentiated adult cell retains complete totipotency. This breakthrough offers unprecedented opportunities for animal breeding, transgenics, and the study of cellular reprogramming and aging.
