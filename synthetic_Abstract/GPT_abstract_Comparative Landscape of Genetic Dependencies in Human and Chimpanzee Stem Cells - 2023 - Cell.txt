Comparative studies of humans and chimpanzees have identified many genetic and regulatory differences, but it remains difficult to determine which changes alter cellular physiology. Here, we developed a comparative CRISPR-interference screening framework to map genetic dependencies in homologous human and chimpanzee pluripotent stem cells. Genome-wide screens and validation across multiple independently derived lines showed that, despite a largely conserved essential gene set, stem cells from the two species could be distinguished by their responses to genetic perturbation. We identified 75 genes with robust species-specific effects on proliferation, organized into coherent pathways and protein complexes, including UFMylation, the MOZ histone acetyltransferase complex, lysosomal V-ATPase accessory factors, and cell cycle regulators. Human PSCs were specifically robust to depletion of CDK2, CDK4, and Cyclin E1, a difference that persisted in neural progenitor cells and cerebral organoids. Comparisons with orangutan PSCs further indicated that altered G1/S control evolved along the human lineage. These results establish loss-of-function profiling as a functional approach for uncovering recently evolved molecular differences between closely related species.
