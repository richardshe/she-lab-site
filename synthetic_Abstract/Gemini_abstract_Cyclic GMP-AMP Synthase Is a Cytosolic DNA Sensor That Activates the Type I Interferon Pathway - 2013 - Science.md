Cytosolic DNA from invading pathogens triggers a powerful innate immune response, primarily through the induction of type I interferons. However, the identity of the primary cytosolic DNA sensor has been controversial. We recently demonstrated that cytosolic DNA triggers the synthesis of cyclic GMP-AMP (cGAMP), which binds and activates the adaptor protein STING to induce interferon-β (IFN-β). Here, using quantitative mass spectrometry and biochemical fractionation, we identify cyclic GMP-AMP synthase (cGAS) as the enzyme responsible for this activity. cGAS belongs to the nucleotidyltransferase family and directly binds to DNA, which robustly activates its catalytic synthesis of cGAMP from ATP and GTP. Knockdown of cGAS severely impairs IFN-β production and IRF3 activation in response to DNA transfection or DNA virus infection in both mouse and human cells. Thus, cGAS is a critical cytosolic DNA sensor.
