An outbreak of pneumonia of unknown cause emerged in Wuhan, Hubei province, China, in December 2019 and was epidemiologically linked to a local seafood market. Here we report the identification and characterization of the causative agent from seven patients with severe disease. Using pan-coronavirus PCR, metagenomic sequencing and targeted amplification, we detected a previously unknown betacoronavirus, tentatively named 2019-nCoV, in bronchoalveolar lavage fluid and oral swabs, and obtained five nearly identical full-length genomes. The virus shares 79.6% genome sequence identity with SARS-CoV and 96.2% identity with a bat coronavirus, RaTG13, indicating that it belongs to the species SARS-related coronavirus and suggesting a probable bat origin. Serological analyses showed virus-specific IgG or IgM responses in infected patients, and patient sera neutralized the isolated virus in vitro. We successfully isolated 2019-nCoV in Vero E6 and Huh7 cells, where infected cells displayed typical coronavirus morphology. Functional entry assays demonstrated that 2019-nCoV uses angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 from several species, including humans, but not mouse ACE2, as its receptor. These findings establish the genetic, serological and cellular-entry characteristics of 2019-nCoV and provide tools for diagnosis, surveillance and further investigation.
