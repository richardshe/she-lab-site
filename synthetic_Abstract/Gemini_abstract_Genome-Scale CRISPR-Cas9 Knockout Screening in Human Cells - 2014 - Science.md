Targeted loss-of-function screening is a powerful method for discovering the genetic basis of biological processes and disease. While RNA interference (RNAi) is widely used, it is limited by incomplete protein depletion and off-target effects. Here, we demonstrate that the RNA-guided CRISPR-Cas9 nuclease system can be adapted for highly efficient, genome-scale knockout screening in human cells. We engineered a single lentiviral vector to simultaneously deliver Cas9 and a single guide RNA (sgRNA) to target cells. Using this system, we constructed a genome-scale CRISPR knockout (GeCKO) library targeting 18,080 human genes. We performed both negative and positive selection screens. In negative selection, GeCKO robustly identified essential genes required for cell viability in human melanoma and stem cells. In positive selection, we screened for genes whose loss confers resistance to the BRAF inhibitor vemurafenib in melanoma. The GeCKO screen identified previously known candidates and uncovered novel genes (such as NF2, CUL3, TADA1, and TADA2B) not previously linked to resistance, demonstrating superior screening consistency and validation rates compared to RNAi. This Cas9-based technology provides a transformative approach for genome-wide functional interrogation.
