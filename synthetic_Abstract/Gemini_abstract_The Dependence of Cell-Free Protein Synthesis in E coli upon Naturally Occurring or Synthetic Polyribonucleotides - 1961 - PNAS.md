We have developed a highly active and stable cell-free system from Escherichia coli capable of incorporating amino acids into protein at a rapid, energy-dependent rate. A novel and critical characteristic of this system is its absolute requirement for template RNA to direct amino acid incorporation, even when soluble RNA and ribosomes are abundant. While the addition of naturally occurring ribosomal or viral RNA robustly stimulates protein synthesis, we demonstrate that a synthetic polynucleotide can also serve as a functional template. Specifically, the addition of polyuridylic acid directs the highly specific incorporation of L-phenylalanine, resulting in the synthesis of a polypeptide resembling poly-L-phenylalanine. This process exhibits all the hallmarks of de novo protein synthesis and is completely dependent on both ribosomes and soluble factors, while being inhibited by ribonuclease, puromycin, and chloramphenicol. Our findings suggest that polyuridylic acid contains the precise genetic code for phenylalanine, providing a powerful experimental framework for deciphering the universal genetic code.
