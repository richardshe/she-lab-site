Patient-specific human embryonic stem cell lines may provide immunologically compatible cells for transplantation and in vitro models for complex human diseases. We established 11 nuclear transfer-derived human embryonic stem cell (NT-hESC) lines from somatic cells donated by patients with spinal cord injury, juvenile diabetes, or congenital hypogammaglobulinemia, including donors of both sexes and ages ranging from 2 to 56 years. Most lines were generated by heterologous nuclear transfer using oocytes from biologically unrelated women and were cultured on human feeder cells. DNA fingerprinting and HLA analyses showed that each NT-hESC line matched its corresponding somatic cell donor, excluding parthenogenetic origin with high probability. The lines expressed characteristic hESC markers, maintained normal karyotypes in representative cases, and differentiated into derivatives of all three embryonic germ layers in embryoid bodies and, for tested lines, teratomas. NT-hESC derivation occurred from 35.4% of nuclear transfer blastocysts, requiring an average of 16.8 injected oocytes per line. These results suggest that patient-specific NT-hESCs can be established efficiently, although mitochondrial inheritance, epigenetic stability, xenoexposure, differentiation fidelity, and preclinical safety remain essential considerations before clinical application.
