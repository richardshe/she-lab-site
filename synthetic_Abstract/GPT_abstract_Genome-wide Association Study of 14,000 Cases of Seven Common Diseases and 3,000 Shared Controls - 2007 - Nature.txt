Genome-wide association (GWA) studies offer a potentially powerful means of identifying genetic variants that contribute to common human disease, but their practical utility, design requirements and susceptibility to bias have required empirical assessment. We report the main experiment of the Wellcome Trust Case Control Consortium, comprising genome-wide analyses of 2,000 cases for each of seven major familial diseases--bipolar disorder, coronary artery disease, Crohn's disease, hypertension, rheumatoid arthritis, type 1 diabetes and type 2 diabetes--together with 3,000 shared controls from the 1958 British Birth Cohort and UK Blood Services. Samples were genotyped with the Affymetrix GeneChip 500K Mapping Array Set and called with CHIAMO, a new bayesian genotype-calling algorithm. After stringent quality control, 16,179 individuals and 469,557 SNPs were retained for association analyses, supplemented by bayesian tests, multilocus imputation and expanded reference group comparisons. The two control collections showed little evidence of systematic allele-frequency differences, supporting the use of common controls. Although geographical structure within Britain was detectable, its overall effect on association statistics was small after exclusion of individuals with appreciable non-European ancestry. We identified numerous strong disease associations, including signals at 9p21.3 for coronary artery disease, ATG16L1 and additional loci including IRGM, NKX2-3 and PTPN2 for Crohn's disease, shared autoimmune susceptibility regions involving IL2RA, PTPN2 and 12q24, and established and novel loci for type 1 and type 2 diabetes. Hypertension produced no association below the primary significance threshold, although several suggestive signals were observed. Across diseases, most convincing effects were modest in size and explained only a small fraction of familial aggregation. These data demonstrate the effectiveness of large GWA studies for discovering common disease loci, emphasize the importance of rigorous genotype and sample quality control, and indicate that still larger studies and extensive replication will be required to define the remaining genetic architecture of common disease.
