RNA-mediated interference in Caenorhabditis elegans has provided a powerful means of reducing gene activity, but the nature of the active interfering molecule has remained unclear. We show here that double-stranded RNA is a potent and specific trigger of genetic interference. Purified sense or antisense strands corresponding to unc-22 had little activity, whereas annealed sense-antisense RNA, or gel-purified double-stranded RNA, produced strong loss-of-function phenotypes at much lower doses. Interference required sequence homology within the double-stranded region and was not enhanced by unrelated double-stranded RNA, indicating that the effect is specific rather than a generalized response to RNA duplexes. Double-stranded RNAs corresponding to unc-54, fem-1, hlh-1 and gfp likewise produced phenotypes or reporter losses expected from inhibition of the target genes. Interference was accompanied by loss of endogenous mex-3 RNA and could spread from the site of injection into germline and somatic tissues. These results identify double-stranded RNA as an efficient, sequence-specific agent of gene silencing in C. elegans.
