The human brain is unique in its size and complexity, displaying organizational features absent or only sparsely represented in rodents, including an outer subventricular zone populated by outer radial glia (oRG). These distinctions have made the recapitulation of human brain development and disease challenging in animal models. Here, we describe a three-dimensional culture system that allows pluripotent stem cells to generate cerebral organoids, brain-like tissues comprising discrete, interdependent regions reminiscent of cerebral cortex, choroid plexus, hippocampus, ventral forebrain, and retina. Cerebral cortical regions display a progenitor zone organization, with abundant oRGs separated from the ventricular zone by an inner fibre layer, similar to early human cortex. Pyramidal neurons mature, exhibit calcium activity, and segregate with modest spatial separation into deep and superficial cortical identities. Furthermore, we use patient-derived induced pluripotent stem cells and shRNA to model microcephaly, demonstrating that loss of CDK5RAP2 causes premature neural differentiation at the expense of progenitor expansion, providing insight into a disorder that has been difficult to recapitulate in mice.
