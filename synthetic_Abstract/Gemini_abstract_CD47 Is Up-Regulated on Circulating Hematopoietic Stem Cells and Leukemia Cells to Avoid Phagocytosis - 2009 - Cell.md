Hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) frequently migrate to ectopic niches during normal development and in response to physiological stress, necessitating navigation through macrophage-lined vascular networks. We discovered that HSCs transiently up-regulate CD47, an immunoglobulin-like protein that binds to SIRPα to inhibit phagocytosis, specifically during experimental mobilization and inflammatory stress. Ectopic expression or increased surface density of CD47 provides HSCs with robust protection against macrophage-mediated clearance. Intriguingly, we found that both mouse and human myeloid leukemias similarly up-regulate CD47 to evade innate immunosurveillance. Functional studies using the human leukemia cell line MOLM-13 demonstrated that enforced CD47 expression promotes in vivo tumor engraftment and growth, whereas blocking CD47 significantly enhances macrophage-mediated phagocytosis of these leukemic cells. Our findings illuminate a shared immune-evasion mechanism between mobilized HSCs and leukemic cells, highlighting CD47 as a potent determinant of stem cell fitness and a compelling therapeutic target in leukemia.
