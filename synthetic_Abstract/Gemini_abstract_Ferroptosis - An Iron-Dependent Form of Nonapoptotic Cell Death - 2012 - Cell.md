Cell death is essential for normal physiology, and while apoptosis is well understood, other regulated forms of nonapoptotic cell death remain poorly characterized. Here, we describe ferroptosis, a uniquely iron-dependent form of nonapoptotic cell death triggered by the small molecules erastin and RSL3, which were originally identified for their selective lethality to oncogenic RAS-mutant cancer cells. Ferroptosis is morphologically, biochemically, and genetically distinct from apoptosis, necrosis, and autophagy. It is characterized by the overwhelming, iron-dependent accumulation of lethal lipid reactive oxygen species (ROS) and requires a unique set of genes, including the iron metabolism regulator IREB2. We identified a potent small-molecule inhibitor, ferrostatin-1, which suppresses ferroptosis in cancer cells and prevents glutamate-induced excitotoxic cell death in brain slices. Erastin initiates ferroptosis by inhibiting the cystine/glutamate antiporter, system xc-, starving cells of antioxidant defenses. Our findings suggest that ferroptosis represents a broad vulnerability in both cancer and neurodegeneration that can be pharmacologically exploited.
