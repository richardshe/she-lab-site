Multivalent macromolecules are central to many biological processes, including signal transduction, RNA metabolism and the organization of non-membrane-bound cellular bodies. Yet, despite the well-known capacity of multivalent small molecules and polymers to undergo sharp sol-gel transitions, it has remained unclear whether intracellular multivalent proteins, which typically combine high valency, modest affinity and long flexible linkers, exhibit analogous behaviour. Here we show that engineered repeats of SRC homology 3 (SH3) domains and their proline-rich motif (PRM) ligands, as well as an unrelated RNA-binding system and the natural nephrin-NCK-N-WASP signalling module, undergo sharp liquid-liquid demixing transitions to produce micrometre-scale, protein-rich droplets in vitro and in cells. Light scattering, X-ray scattering, photobleaching and cryo-electron microscopy indicate that the droplet phase contains large, dynamic polymeric assemblies, suggesting that the phase transition is thermodynamically coupled to a molecular sol-gel transition. The phase boundary depends steeply on valency and affinity and can be tuned by tyrosine phosphorylation of nephrin, which in turn switches on Arp2/3-mediated actin assembly. These data suggest that multivalent interactions can generate sharp, regulatable transitions between physically and functionally distinct states, providing a general mechanism for nonlinear behaviour and the assembly of cellular bodies.
