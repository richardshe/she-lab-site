A major obstacle to defining the etiology of scrapie has been the unusual resistance of the infectious agent to procedures that inactivate conventional viruses. We developed a rapid hamster bioassay based on incubation time and used it to follow purification of the agent from infected brain. Partially purified preparations contained high titers of infectivity with little DNA or RNA and exhibited pronounced hydrophobicity. Multiple treatments that modify or denature proteins, including proteinase K, trypsin, diethyl pyrocarbonate, sodium dodecyl sulfate, chaotropic salts, phenol, and urea, diminished infectivity. In contrast, nucleases, zinc-catalyzed hydrolysis, psoralens, hydroxylamine, and high doses of ultraviolet irradiation failed to reveal a required nucleic acid. The molecular properties of the agent differ from those of viruses, viroids, and plasmids. These findings suggest that scrapie is caused by a small proteinaceous infectious particle, or prion, although a protected nucleic acid cannot yet be excluded.
