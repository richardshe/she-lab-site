Bacteria and archaea utilize CRISPR/Cas systems to defend against foreign genetic elements, including bacteriophages. Despite the prevalence of these adaptive immune systems, the mechanisms by which phages might evade CRISPR/Cas targeting have remained largely unknown. Here, we identify a widespread mechanism of phage-encoded inhibition of the CRISPR/Cas system. Through a screen of Pseudomonas aeruginosa lysogens, we discovered phages that robustly resist type I-F CRISPR/Cas immunity. We identified five distinct "anti-CRISPR" genes encoded within these phage genomes. The expression of any single anti-CRISPR gene is sufficient to completely inactivate the P. aeruginosa CRISPR/Cas system, allowing successful phage replication despite the presence of targeted protospacer sequences. We demonstrate that these anti-CRISPR proteins function downstream of crRNA biogenesis, likely by directly interfering with the crRNA-Cas ribonucleoprotein complex. The discovery of anti-CRISPR genes uncovers a fundamental evolutionary arms race between phages and bacterial adaptive immunity and suggests that such evasion mechanisms may drive the remarkable diversification of CRISPR/Cas systems in nature.
