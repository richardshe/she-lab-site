How somatic mutations accumulate in normal cells before cancer arises has remained largely unobservable, obscured by the polyclonal architecture of healthy tissue. To directly characterize this earliest stage of tumor evolution, we performed ultradeep sequencing of 74 cancer-associated genes across 234 small biopsies of physiologically normal, sun-exposed eyelid epidermis from four individuals, complemented by whole-genome sequencing of one clonal biopsy. We identified 3760 somatic mutations, with a spectrum dominated by ultraviolet-induced damage, and detected clones ranging from 0.01 to several square millimeters in size. Aged sun-exposed skin carried a striking mutation burden of two to six substitutions per megabase per cell, approaching that of many adult cancers. Using a context-aware dN/dS framework, we found pervasive positive selection acting on NOTCH1, NOTCH2, NOTCH3, FAT1, TP53, RBM10, and FGFR3, with biallelic NOTCH1 inactivation already common in normal cells. On average, every square centimeter of aged eyelid skin harbored around 140 clones carrying driver mutations. These results reveal that physiologically normal skin is a battleground of competing mutant clones, with the selective forces and driver landscape closely mirroring those of cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma.
