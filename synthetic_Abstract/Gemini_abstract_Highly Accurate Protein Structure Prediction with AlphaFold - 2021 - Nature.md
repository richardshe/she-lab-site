The prediction of a protein's three-dimensional structure from its amino acid sequence has been a grand challenge in computational biology for decades. While evolutionary and physical approaches have made steady progress, predictions have historically fallen short of experimental accuracy unless a close homologous structure was available. Here, we present AlphaFold, a novel machine learning approach that predicts protein structures with unprecedented accuracy. In the 14th Critical Assessment of protein Structure Prediction (CASP14), AlphaFold produced predictions that were vastly more accurate than competing methods, achieving a median backbone accuracy of 0.96 Å r.m.s.d.95, comparable to the width of a single atom. AlphaFold integrates evolutionary, physical, and geometric constraints into its deep learning architecture. At its core is the Evoformer, a novel neural network block that continuously exchanges information between multiple sequence alignment (MSA) and pairwise residue representations, reasoning directly about spatial and evolutionary relationships. This is followed by a structure module that iteratively refines global rigid body frames for each residue to construct precise 3D coordinates in an end-to-end manner. The network is trained with a combination of labeled Protein Data Bank structures and unlabelled sequence data via self-distillation. Our system can accurately predict domain structures, domain packing, and precise side-chain conformations, while also providing reliable per-residue confidence estimates. AlphaFold demonstrates that deep learning can routinely achieve near-experimental accuracy in structure prediction, overcoming a major bottleneck in structural bioinformatics and accelerating biological discovery.
