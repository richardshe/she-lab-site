Human brain development displays features not readily accessible in animal models, including expanded progenitor zones and outer radial glia. Here, we describe a three-dimensional culture method that allows human pluripotent stem cells to self-organize into cerebral organoids containing discrete, interdependent brain regions. These tissues form neuroepithelial ventricular zones, dorsal cortical domains, choroid plexus, retina, and ventral forebrain identities, and cortical regions display radial glia, intermediate progenitors, rudimentary cortical plate organization, and spontaneously active neurons. Importantly, human organoids recapitulate aspects of species-specific cortical architecture, including an outer subventricular zone-like region populated by outer radial glia, which is not evident in comparable mouse organoids. We further use patient-derived induced pluripotent stem cells carrying loss-of-function CDK5RAP2 mutations, together with rescue and knockdown experiments, to model primary microcephaly. Mutant organoids exhibit reduced neuroepithelial growth, altered spindle orientation, and premature neuronal differentiation at the expense of progenitors, suggesting that failed expansion of radial glia contributes to human brain hypoplasia.
