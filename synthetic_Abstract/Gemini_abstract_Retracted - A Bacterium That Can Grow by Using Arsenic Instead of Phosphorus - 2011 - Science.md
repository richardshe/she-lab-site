# Abstract

Life is fundamentally dependent on six major nutrient elements: carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, sulfur, and phosphorus. While trace elements can substitute for one another, no substitution for a major biological element has ever been reported. Here, we describe a bacterium, GFAJ-1 of the Halomonadaceae, isolated from Mono Lake, California, that can grow using arsenic in place of phosphorus. Cultured in artificial medium devoid of added phosphate but supplemented with arsenate, GFAJ-1 exhibits robust growth. Utilizing mass spectrometry and radiotracer studies, we demonstrate that intracellular arsenic is assimilated into critical biomolecules, including nucleic acids and proteins. Synchrotron x-ray data indicate that arsenic occupies the structural role normally assumed by phosphorus in the DNA backbone. This capacity to substitute arsenic for phosphorus redefines the elemental requirements for life.
