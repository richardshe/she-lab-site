Tumors frequently exploit immune checkpoint pathways, such as the programmed death 1 (PD-1) receptor pathway, to evade immune-mediated destruction. Blocking the interaction between PD-1 and its ligands is a promising immunotherapeutic strategy to enhance T-cell responses and restore antitumor immunity. In this phase 1 study, we evaluated the safety, pharmacokinetics, and clinical activity of BMS-936558, a fully human IgG4 blocking monoclonal antibody targeting PD-1, in patients with advanced, heavily pretreated solid tumors. Patients with melanoma, non-small-cell lung cancer (NSCLC), renal-cell cancer, castration-resistant prostate cancer, and colorectal cancer received intravenous infusions of anti-PD-1 antibody at doses of 0.1 to 10.0 mg per kilogram of body weight every 2 weeks. A total of 296 patients were enrolled. The antibody exhibited a favorable safety profile; grade 3 or 4 treatment-related adverse events occurred in 14% of patients. The most common drug-related adverse events were fatigue, rash, diarrhea, pruritus, and nausea. Immune-related adverse events, including pneumonitis, vitiligo, colitis, and hepatitis, were observed, with mild-to-moderate pneumonitis manageable via treatment interruption or glucocorticoids. Three deaths related to pneumonitis occurred (1%). Antitumor activity was observed across multiple tumor types and dose levels. Among 236 evaluable patients, objective responses were achieved in 18% of those with NSCLC (14 of 76 patients), 28% of those with melanoma (26 of 94 patients), and 27% of those with renal-cell cancer (9 of 33 patients). Notably, the responses were remarkably durable; 20 of 31 responding patients who were followed for 1 year or more maintained their response for 1 year or longer. Preliminary biomarker analysis suggested an association between the expression of the PD-1 ligand PD-L1 on tumor cells in pretreatment biopsy specimens and objective clinical response to PD-1 blockade. In conclusion, anti-PD-1 antibody therapy is safe, tolerable, and induces substantial and highly durable tumor regression in a diverse range of advanced cancers, including NSCLC, thereby broadening the potential applications of immune checkpoint blockade in oncology.
