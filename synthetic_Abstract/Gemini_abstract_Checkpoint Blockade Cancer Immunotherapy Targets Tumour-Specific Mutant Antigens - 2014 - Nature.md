Immune checkpoint blockade therapies, including anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4, induce durable responses in a subset of patients across diverse cancer types; however, the precise antigenic targets driving these responses and the underlying mechanisms of therapeutic efficacy remain incompletely understood. Using progressor 3-methylcholanthrene-induced (MCA) sarcomas that are sensitive to checkpoint blockade, we demonstrate that therapy-induced tumour rejection is dependent on CD8+ T cells and cross-presenting dendritic cells, culminating in immunologic memory. Utilizing genomics and a refined bioinformatics approach, we predicted the landscape of major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I-restricted tumour-specific mutant antigens (TSMA) in the d42m1-T3 sarcoma line. Two dominant neoepitopes, arising from mutations in the Lama4 and Alg8 genes, were validated as the primary targets of the CD8+ T-cell response in anti-PD-1-treated mice. T cells specific for these antigens accumulate within the tumour microenvironment and exhibit distinct transcriptomic and functional alterations tailored by the specific checkpoint blockade utilized. While both anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 enhanced anti-tumour immunity, anti-PD-1 predominantly drove metabolic and effector responses, whereas anti-CTLA-4 promoted T-cell proliferation and activation; their combination produced a potent synergistic effector profile. Furthermore, a therapeutic vaccine incorporating the synthetic long peptides corresponding to the identified Lama4 and Alg8 TSMA elicited robust tumour rejection equivalent to that achieved with checkpoint blockade. These findings define tumour-specific mutant antigens as crucial targets of checkpoint blockade immunotherapy and illuminate the distinct molecular pathways through which anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 invigorate neoantigen-specific T cells. The identification and therapeutic application of TSMA provide a compelling rationale for the development of personalized cancer vaccines to complement or enhance checkpoint blockade therapies in the clinic.
