Acute myeloid leukemia (AML) is organized as a cellular hierarchy maintained by leukemia stem cells (LSC), which must be eliminated to eradicate disease. We report that CD47, the ligand for signal regulatory protein alpha (SIRPalpha), is more highly expressed on human AML LSC than on their normal counterparts, hematopoietic stem cells and multipotent progenitors. In three independent cohorts of adult AML patients, increased CD47 expression at diagnosis was associated with worse event-free and overall survival, independent of known prognostic factors including FLT3-ITD status. We hypothesized that elevated CD47 expression contributes to pathogenesis by inhibiting phagocytosis of leukemia cells. Consistent with this, blocking monoclonal antibodies against CD47 preferentially enabled phagocytosis of AML LSC, but not normal CD34+ cells, by macrophages in vitro, and eliminated AML in vivo by enabling phagocytosis, without inducing apoptosis or unacceptable toxicity. These findings establish CD47 as an adverse prognostic factor and therapeutic antibody target in human AML.
