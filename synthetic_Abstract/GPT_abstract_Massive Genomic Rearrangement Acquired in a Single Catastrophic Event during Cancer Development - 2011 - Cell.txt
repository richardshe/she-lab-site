Cancer development is usually viewed as a gradual evolutionary process in which successive driver mutations are acquired over many years. Here, we describe a contrasting route in which tens to hundreds of genomic rearrangements are acquired in a single catastrophic event, which we term chromothripsis. In chronic lymphocytic leukemia, cancer cell lines, and primary bone tumors, paired-end sequencing and SNP arrays revealed localized clusters of rearrangements, rapid oscillation between two copy number states, alternating heterozygosity and loss of heterozygosity, and pronounced breakpoint clustering. Similar patterns occur in at least 2%-3% of cancers and are especially common in bone cancers. The restriction of copy number states, preservation of heterozygosity, and consistency of cytogenetic findings argue against progressive accumulation of rearrangements and support shattering and imperfect repair of one or more chromosomes. Chromothripsis can promote cancer development by generating double minutes, amplifying MYC, and disrupting or deleting tumor suppressor genes including CDKN2A, WRN, and FBXW7. Thus, cancer genomes can evolve by punctuated catastrophe as well as gradual mutation.
