The genetic basis of common human diseases remains largely elusive despite extensive research. Genome-wide association (GWA) studies offer a powerful new approach to identify disease-susceptibility variants, driven by advances in the HapMap project, high-density genotyping arrays, and the assembly of large clinical cohorts. Here, the Wellcome Trust Case Control Consortium (WTCCC) presents the results of a massive GWA study involving 14,000 cases across seven major diseases—bipolar disorder, coronary artery disease, Crohn's disease, hypertension, rheumatoid arthritis, type 1 diabetes, and type 2 diabetes (2,000 cases each)—and a shared reference group of 3,000 controls. By genotyping over 500,000 single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) and implementing rigorous quality control and novel calling algorithms, we robustly demonstrate the efficacy of the case-control design using a common control group. Our analysis reveals minimal confounding from population stratification within the British population, validating the use of shared controls for GWA studies. We identified 24 independent association signals with P-values below 5 × 10^-7. Among these, we replicated several known associations and discovered numerous novel loci, including regions on chromosomes 16p12 for bipolar disorder, 9p21.3 for coronary artery disease, and multiple new genes for Crohn's disease and type 1 diabetes. Notably, our results indicate that some susceptibility loci are shared across multiple autoimmune diseases. The identified variants typically confer modest disease risks, suggesting that the allelic architecture of common diseases involves many loci of small effect rather than a few of large effect. This underscores the necessity for large sample sizes in future studies. Our findings provide fundamental insights into the genetic architecture of complex traits, offer new avenues for investigating disease mechanisms, and firmly establish GWA studies as a robust methodology for dissecting the biological basis of common human diseases.
