In vitro-transcribed mRNA is an attractive nonviral vector because it directs rapid protein synthesis without risk of genomic integration, yet its clinical utility has been limited by poor stability, low translational efficiency, and potent activation of innate immunity. We examined whether incorporation of naturally occurring modified nucleosides could overcome these barriers. Reporter mRNAs in which uridine was replaced with pseudouridine were generated by in vitro transcription and compared with unmodified and other modified transcripts in cell-free systems, cultured mammalian cells, primary cells, and mice. Pseudouridine-containing mRNAs produced substantially more protein than unmodified mRNAs in rabbit reticulocyte lysates and in multiple mammalian cell types, including dendritic cells, splenocytes, fibroblasts, CHO cells, and neurons. The enhancement was independent of cap and poly(A) elements and persisted in RIG-I-deficient cells, indicating that it was not explained by reduced type I interferon signaling. Following systemic delivery to mice, pseudouridine-containing luciferase mRNA yielded higher and longer-lasting protein expression in spleen, while failing to induce serum IFN-alpha or splenic TNF-alpha mRNA. Thus, pseudouridine modification renders mRNA a biologically stable, highly translatable, and nonimmunogenic vector for safe protein expression in vitro and in vivo.
