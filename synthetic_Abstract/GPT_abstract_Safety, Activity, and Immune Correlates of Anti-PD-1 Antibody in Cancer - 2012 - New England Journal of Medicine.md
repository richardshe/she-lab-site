Background
Programmed death 1 (PD-1) is an inhibitory receptor expressed by activated T cells and is engaged by ligands that may be expressed by tumor cells and stromal cells. Blockade of this pathway may restore antitumor immunity. We evaluated the safety, antitumor activity, pharmacokinetics, and immune correlates of repeated doses of the anti-PD-1 antibody BMS-936558 in patients with advanced cancer.

Methods
In this phase 1 study, patients with advanced melanoma, non-small-cell lung cancer, renal-cell cancer, castration-resistant prostate cancer, or colorectal cancer received BMS-936558 at doses ranging from 0.1 to 10.0 mg per kilogram every 2 weeks. Treatment was continued for up to 2 years unless a complete response, unacceptable toxic effects, or progressive disease occurred. Tumor responses were assessed after each 8-week cycle. PD-1-receptor occupancy on circulating T cells and pretreatment tumor-cell expression of PD-L1 were evaluated in available specimens.

Results
A total of 296 patients were treated, and 236 could be evaluated for efficacy. A maximum tolerated dose was not reached. Grade 3 or 4 treatment-related adverse events occurred in 14% of patients, and 5% discontinued therapy because of treatment-related adverse events. Drug-related pneumonitis occurred in 3% of patients and was fatal in three patients. Objective responses were observed in patients with non-small-cell lung cancer, melanoma, and renal-cell cancer at all dose levels tested. Response rates were 6 to 32% in non-small-cell lung cancer, 19 to 41% in melanoma, and 24 to 31% in renal-cell cancer, according to dose level. Many responses were durable; among patients with at least 1 year of follow-up after response, 20 of 31 had responses lasting 1 year or more. No objective responses were observed in prostate or colorectal cancer. Anti-PD-1 antibody showed linear pharmacokinetics, and receptor occupancy on circulating T cells was sustained. Among 42 patients with evaluable tumors, objective responses occurred in 9 of 25 patients with PD-L1-positive tumors and in none of 17 patients with PD-L1-negative tumors.

Conclusions
Anti-PD-1 antibody produced durable objective responses in several advanced solid tumors, including non-small-cell lung cancer, with manageable but potentially serious immune-related toxic effects. Tumor PD-L1 expression warrants further study as a predictive biomarker.
