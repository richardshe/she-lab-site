The central nervous system has long been considered devoid of a classical lymphatic drainage system, despite the presence of immune surveillance and the capacity of cerebrospinal fluid-derived antigens to reach peripheral lymph nodes. Here we describe functional lymphatic vessels lining the dural sinuses of the mouse brain. These vessels express classical lymphatic endothelial cell markers, including Lyve-1, Prox1, podoplanin and VEGFR3, and display molecular and ultrastructural features characteristic of initial lymphatic vessels. Meningeal lymphatic vessels are organized alongside the superior sagittal and transverse sinuses, are distinct from the blood vasculature, and can be visualized as conduits containing cerebrospinal fluid tracers after intracerebroventricular injection. In addition to carrying soluble material from the cerebrospinal fluid, these vessels contain T cells, MHCII-expressing cells, CD11c+ cells and B cells, indicating their capacity to support immune cell trafficking. Tracer studies, deep cervical lymph node resection and lymphatic ligation further demonstrate a physical and functional connection between the meningeal lymphatic network and the deep cervical lymph nodes. These findings identify a classical route for fluid and immune cell egress from the central nervous system.
