Phosphorus is considered an essential element for all known life, whereas arsenic is generally toxic because arsenate can mimic phosphate but forms less stable biochemical compounds. Here we describe strain GFAJ-1, a member of the Halomonadaceae isolated from arsenic-rich Mono Lake sediments, that grows aerobically in a defined medium containing arsenate and no added phosphate. Growth under these conditions was accompanied by arsenate uptake, greatly reduced intracellular phosphorus, altered cellular morphology, and accumulation of arsenic in cellular fractions containing nucleic acids, proteins, lipids, and metabolites. NanoSIMS and synchrotron x-ray analyses further indicated arsenic associated with purified DNA and As(V)-oxygen-carbon coordination. These results suggest that GFAJ-1 can substitute arsenic for phosphorus in major biomolecules.
