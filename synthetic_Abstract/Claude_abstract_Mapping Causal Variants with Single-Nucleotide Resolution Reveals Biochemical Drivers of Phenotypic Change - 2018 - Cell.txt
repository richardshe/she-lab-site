Heritable traits arise from DNA polymorphisms, but the statistical resolution of mapping genotype to phenotype is fundamentally limited by genetic linkage between adjacent variants. Even in highly inbred crosses, quantitative trait loci span dozens of candidate genes, and the true causal variant is typically obscured by passenger mutations. Here we develop a theoretical framework for identifying causal variants with single nucleotide resolution and put it into practice by constructing a six-generation inbred cross between two ecologically distinct S. cerevisiae strains chosen for their uniformly low genetic divergence. Whole-genome sequencing of 1,125 F6 progeny generated a dense recombination map that liberated each polymorphism from its original haplotype, enabling us to map 370 causal variants across 26 traits. We find that synonymous, missense, and cis-regulatory mutations contribute comparably to phenotypic diversity, that multiple causal variants frequently coexist within a single locus, and that functionally coupled mutations cluster in linkage groups, challenging core assumptions of quantitative genetics.
