Gene targeting provides a means for introducing defined alterations into the mammalian genome. We have used homologous recombination to inactivate the endogenous Hprt gene in male mouse embryo-derived stem (ES) cells. Recombinant vectors were constructed in which a redesigned neo gene, optimized for expression in ES cells, was inserted into exon 8 of cloned Hprt sequences. Following electroporation and selection for resistance to G418 and 6-thioguanine, all 38 independently isolated cell lines examined contained targeted disruptions of the Hprt locus and lacked detectable HPRT activity. Both sequence replacement and sequence insertion vectors mediated targeting, with frequencies strongly dependent on the extent of homology between the introduced DNA and the chromosomal target. Under optimal conditions, approximately 1 in 1000 G418-resistant transformants resulted from gene targeting, without detectable random integration of additional vector copies. These results demonstrate that ES cells can be used for site-directed mutagenesis of endogenous genes and should permit the generation of mice carrying predetermined mutations.
