Mitochondrial calcium uptake is a critical process that regulates energy production, shapes cytosolic calcium transients, and controls cell death. Despite decades of study, the molecular identity of the mitochondrial calcium uniporter has remained a major unsolved mystery in cell biology. Recently, MICU1 was identified as an essential regulator of the uniporter, but the core pore-forming component remained unknown. Here, we deployed an integrative genomics approach—combining phylogenetic profiling across 500 genomes, genome-wide mRNA co-expression analysis, and organelle-specific protein co-expression—to identify the uncharacterized protein CCDC109A, which we now call mitochondrial calcium uniporter (MCU), as a crucial component of the uniporter complex. We demonstrate that MCU physically interacts with MICU1 and localizes to the mitochondrial inner membrane. Silencing MCU expression in cells and in vivo profoundly attenuates mitochondrial calcium uptake without altering other vital bioenergetic parameters or mitochondrial membrane potential. Biochemical and topological analyses reveal that MCU forms an oligomeric complex with two transmembrane domains, and site-directed mutagenesis indicates that conserved acidic residues within the linker region are essential for its calcium transport activity. Furthermore, a specific point mutation in MCU confers striking resistance to Ru360, the most potent known inhibitor of the uniporter. Together with MICU1, MCU constitutes an evolutionarily conserved protein complex that is essential for mitochondrial calcium uptake, thereby providing a molecular foundation for investigating the role of mitochondrial calcium in physiology and disease.
