During development, neural circuits are refined by activity-dependent synaptic pruning, yet the cellular and molecular mechanisms underlying this process remain poorly understood. Here we demonstrate that microglia, the resident immune cells of the CNS, engulf presynaptic retinal ganglion cell inputs undergoing remodeling in the postnatal dorsal lateral geniculate nucleus. Using high-resolution imaging, genetic labeling, pH-sensitive tracers, and ultrastructural analyses, we show that engulfment is developmentally regulated, enriched during peak retinogeniculate pruning, and preferentially directed toward less active inputs following manipulation of retinal activity. We further identify complement receptor 3, expressed by microglia, and its ligand C3, localized to synaptically enriched regions, as a key phagocytic signaling pathway regulating synaptic engulfment. Genetic disruption of CR3 or C3, or pharmacological perturbation of microglia, reduces engulfment and causes sustained defects in eye-specific segregation and excess retinogeniculate synapses. These findings identify microglia as active mediators of complement-dependent synaptic pruning in the healthy developing brain.
