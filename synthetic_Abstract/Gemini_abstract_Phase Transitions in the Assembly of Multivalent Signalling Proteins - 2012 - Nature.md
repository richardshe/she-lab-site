# Abstract

Interactions between multivalent molecules can generate sharp sol-gel phase transitions, producing macroscopic polymer networks. While extensively studied in classical polymer chemistry and extracellular biology, the occurrence and consequences of such transitions among intracellular multivalent proteins have been unclear. Here, we investigate the assembly of multivalent signaling proteins using engineered and natural systems. We show that combining proteins with multiple SH3 domains and multiple proline-rich motifs leads to a sharp liquid-liquid demixing phase separation. This phase transition is thermodynamically coupled to a molecular sol-gel transition, creating macroscopic, highly concentrated liquid droplets containing dynamic, non-covalent protein polymers. This phenomenon depends strongly on valency and affinity and is reproduced in living cells. Furthermore, we demonstrate that a natural three-component system—nephrin, NCK, and N-WASP—undergoes a similar phase transition, which can be dynamically regulated by nephrin phosphorylation. Crucially, this phase transition sharply increases the system's ability to stimulate Arp2/3-mediated actin assembly. Our findings suggest that macroscopic phase transitions coupled to sol-gel transitions may be a general mechanism for organizing intracellular space and generating switch-like, non-linear responses in biological signaling networks.
