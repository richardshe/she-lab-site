Chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) is driven by the BCR-ABL fusion protein, a constitutively active tyrosine kinase generated by the Philadelphia chromosome translocation. STI571 is a specific, rationally designed inhibitor of the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase. In this phase 1, dose-escalation trial, we evaluated the safety, tolerability, and antileukemic activity of oral STI571 in 83 patients with chronic-phase CML who had failed previous interferon alfa therapy. Patients received daily doses of STI571 ranging from 25 to 1000 mg. The drug was well tolerated, with mild to moderate adverse effects, predominantly nausea, myalgias, edema, and diarrhea; no maximum tolerated dose was identified. Pharmacokinetic analysis indicated a half-life of 13 to 16 hours, suitable for once-daily dosing. Remarkably, complete hematologic responses were achieved in 53 of 54 patients treated with daily doses of 300 mg or more, typically within four weeks of initiating therapy. Furthermore, 29 of these 54 patients (54 percent) exhibited cytogenetic responses, including 17 (31 percent) with major cytogenetic responses and 7 (13 percent) who achieved complete cytogenetic remissions. Biochemical analysis of patient leukocytes confirmed the in vivo inhibition of BCR-ABL kinase activity by STI571, as evidenced by reduced phosphorylation of the CRKL substrate. These results demonstrate that STI571 is highly active and well tolerated in patients with CML, establishing the clinical viability of targeting a specific molecular abnormality with a selective kinase inhibitor. This study provides strong justification for further clinical trials of STI571 in CML and validates the paradigm of molecularly targeted cancer therapy.
