RNA activates innate immune cells, yet the molecular basis by which self RNA is distinguished from microbial RNA has remained unclear. Here we show that nucleoside modification is a critical determinant of RNA recognition by Toll-like receptors. In vitro-transcribed RNA and bacterial RNA stimulated human dendritic cells and 293 cells expressing human TLR3, TLR7, or TLR8, whereas mammalian RNA species rich in modified nucleosides were weakly stimulatory or inactive. Incorporation of naturally occurring modified nucleosides, including N6-methyladenosine, 5-methylcytidine, 5-methyluridine, 2-thiouridine, pseudouridine, and 2'-O-methyluridine, reduced or abolished RNA-mediated cytokine induction and dendritic cell maturation. Suppression was proportional to the frequency of modified nucleosides, and even a single modification within short oligoribonucleotides diminished immune activation. These findings identify nucleoside modification as a feature by which the innate immune system discriminates among RNAs and suggest that the evolutionary accumulation of RNA modifications limits inappropriate recognition of host RNA while permitting responses to bacterial and mitochondrial RNA.
