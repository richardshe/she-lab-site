# Abstract

The phenomenon of RNA interference (RNAi) in Caenorhabditis elegans has been poorly understood, particularly regarding the observation that both sense and antisense RNA preparations can induce gene silencing. Here, we demonstrate that double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) is actually the potent trigger for this specific genetic interference. We found that injecting a mixture of sense and antisense RNA, or purified dsRNA, is at least two orders of magnitude more effective than injecting either single strand alone. The interference is highly specific to the targeted gene, as demonstrated by the precise phenocopying of null mutations for several genes, including unc-22, unc-54, fem-1, and hlh-1. Furthermore, we show that dsRNA injection leads to a drastic reduction in the corresponding endogenous mRNA levels. Remarkably, this dsRNA-mediated interference can cross cellular boundaries, spreading from the site of injection to affect somatic tissues and even the germline, resulting in phenotypic alterations in the subsequent generation. These findings indicate that dsRNA is a powerful and specific tool for functional genomics, revealing a previously unappreciated mechanism for gene silencing that may be widely conserved among eukaryotes.
