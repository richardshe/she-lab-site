Life is composed of the six major bioessential elements: carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, sulfur, and phosphorus. Although trace element substitutions are well documented, no substitution for any of the major elements has previously been reported. Here, we describe a bacterium, strain GFAJ-1 of the Halomonadaceae, isolated from Mono Lake, California, that can substitute arsenic for phosphorus to sustain its growth. Our data, derived from radiotracer experiments, inductively coupled plasma mass spectrometry, NanoSIMS, and synchrotron x-ray analyses, show that arsenate is incorporated into key biomolecules, including nucleic acids, proteins, and metabolites. The implications of arsenate substituting for phosphate at fundamental cellular levels are profound for evolution and astrobiology.
