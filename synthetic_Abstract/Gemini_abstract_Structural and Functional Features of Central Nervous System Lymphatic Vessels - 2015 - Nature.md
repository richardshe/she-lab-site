The central nervous system (CNS) has historically been considered an immune-privileged organ lacking a classical lymphatic drainage system. In this study, we fundamentally revise this dogma by describing the discovery and characterization of a functional lymphatic vascular network within the mammalian meninges. Using a novel whole-mount preparation of mouse brain meninges, we identified vessels expressing classic lymphatic endothelial markers, including Lyve-1, Prox1, podoplanin, and VEGFR3, running parallel to the dural venous sinuses. These vessels exhibit the structural features of initial lymphatic capillaries, lacking smooth muscle coverage and possessing a discontinuous basement membrane. Through in vivo imaging and tracer injection studies, we demonstrate that the meningeal lymphatic vessels are functionally capable of draining cerebrospinal fluid and transporting both fluid and immune cells directly to the deep cervical lymph nodes. Resection or ligation of these downstream lymph nodes leads to an accumulation of immune cells within the meninges. The identification of this major fluid and leukocyte clearance pathway provides a missing mechanistic link in neuroimmunology and suggests that the dysfunction of meningeal lymphatics may contribute to the pathology of diverse neurological diseases associated with altered immune responses, such as multiple sclerosis and Alzheimer's disease.
