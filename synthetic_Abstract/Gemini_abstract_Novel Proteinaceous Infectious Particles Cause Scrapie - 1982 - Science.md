# Abstract

The scrapie agent causes a degenerative neurological disorder in sheep and goats, yet its molecular structure has remained mysterious. To characterize this enigmatic pathogen, we developed an accelerated bioassay based on incubation time in Syrian hamsters, permitting substantial purification of the infectivity. The infectious agent exhibits marked hydrophobicity and an array of anomalous biochemical properties. Notably, it is resistant to numerous treatments that inactivate nucleic acids, including nucleases, ultraviolet irradiation, and psoralens. In contrast, the agent is readily inactivated by proteases and protein-denaturing agents such as sodium dodecyl sulfate, phenol, urea, and chaotropic salts. These results strongly suggest that the pathogen lacks a conventional nucleic acid genome and instead requires a protein for its infectivity. Because of its unprecedented properties, which distinguish it from viruses, plasmids, and viroids, we introduce the term "prion" to denote these novel proteinaceous infectious particles.
