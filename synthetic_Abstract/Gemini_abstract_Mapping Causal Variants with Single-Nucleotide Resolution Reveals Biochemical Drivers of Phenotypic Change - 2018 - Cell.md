Identifying the precise genetic variations that drive phenotypic differences is a central challenge in quantitative genetics. While genome-wide association studies routinely identify broad quantitative trait loci, genetic linkage typically prevents the resolution of individual causal mutations. Here, we present a theoretical framework and an optimized crossing strategy in Saccharomyces cerevisiae that dramatically increases meiotic recombination relative to the density of segregating polymorphisms. By genotyping and phenotyping a highly inbred cross, we mapped hundreds of causal variants underlying diverse quantitative traits with single-nucleotide resolution. We demonstrate that complex traits are driven by a diverse array of mutations, including numerous synonymous and cis-regulatory variants that exert significant phenotypic effects. Furthermore, we uncover pervasive functional coupling between closely linked mutations, revealing complex interactions within individual loci. Our study provides a comprehensive look at the richness of natural genetic variation and establishes a powerful paradigm for systematically mapping the biochemical drivers of phenotypic change at the resolution of single nucleotides.
