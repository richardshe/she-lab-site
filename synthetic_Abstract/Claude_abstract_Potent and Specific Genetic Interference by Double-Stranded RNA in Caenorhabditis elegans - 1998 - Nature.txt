Experimental introduction of RNA into cells can be used to inhibit the function of endogenous genes in Caenorhabditis elegans. Although the mechanism of this interference is not understood, two puzzling features have been observed: both sense and antisense RNA preparations are sufficient to cause interference, and these effects persist well into the next generation. We report here that double-stranded RNA is substantially more effective at producing genetic interference than either single strand. Purified single strands had only marginal activity, whereas double-stranded mixtures were at least two orders of magnitude more potent. Effects of injected double-stranded RNA were gene-specific, mimicking the phenotype of the corresponding loss-of-function mutation, and could cross cellular boundaries, with injection into the body cavity producing interference in progeny and in somatic tissues. Injection of double-stranded RNA also resulted in a marked decrease in the endogenous messenger RNA. These results indicate that double-stranded RNA could target a specific class of cellular events, and provide a powerful tool for the analysis of gene function in C. elegans and, potentially, in other organisms.
