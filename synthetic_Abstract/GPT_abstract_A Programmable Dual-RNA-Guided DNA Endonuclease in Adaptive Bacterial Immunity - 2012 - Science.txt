Bacteria and archaea use CRISPR/Cas systems to defend against foreign genetic elements by means of small RNAs that direct sequence-specific silencing. Here we show that in the Streptococcus pyogenes Type II CRISPR/Cas system, Cas9 is an RNA-guided DNA endonuclease activated by a duplex formed between tracrRNA and crRNA. This dual-RNA structure directs Cas9 to cleave double-stranded DNA at sites specified by complementarity between the crRNA spacer and a target protospacer, together with an adjacent NGG protospacer adjacent motif. The HNH and RuvC-like nuclease domains of Cas9 cleave the complementary and non-complementary DNA strands, respectively, generating site-specific double-stranded breaks. Target recognition requires a PAM-proximal seed sequence and PAM-dependent DNA binding. We further demonstrate that Cas9 can be programmed with a single chimeric guide RNA, providing a simple, versatile strategy for RNA-directed DNA cleavage and genome targeting.
