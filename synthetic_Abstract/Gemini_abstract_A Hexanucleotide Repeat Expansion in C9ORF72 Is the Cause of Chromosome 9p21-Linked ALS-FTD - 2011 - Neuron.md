Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) and frontotemporal dementia (FTD) form an overlapping neurodegenerative disease continuum, often characterized by TARDBP/TDP-43 pathology. Linkage analysis and genome-wide association studies recently identified a major locus for ALS and FTD on chromosome 9p21. Here, we used next-generation sequencing in two families showing linkage to this region to identify a large hexanucleotide (GGGGCC) repeat expansion within the noncoding region of the C9ORF72 gene. This massive repeat expansion was found to be the underlying cause of disease in these kindreds. Screening further patient cohorts revealed that this expansion accounts for nearly half of familial ALS cases in Finland and over a third of familial ALS cases in wider European populations. Additionally, the same expansion underlies a substantial proportion of familial FTD cases. Our findings identify a massive C9ORF72 repeat expansion as the most common genetic cause of ALS and FTD, highlighting the role of disrupted RNA metabolism in neurodegeneration.
