# Abstract

Cancer is generally thought to arise through a gradual, stepwise accumulation of mutations over time. However, using massively parallel paired-end sequencing, we identified multiple cancer samples that instead exhibit tens to hundreds of genomic rearrangements acquired in a single catastrophic cellular event. This phenomenon, which we term chromothripsis, involves the localized shattering and chaotic restitching of one or a few chromosomes. It occurs in at least 2% to 3% of all cancers, across diverse tumor types, and is particularly prevalent in bone cancers, affecting up to 25% of cases. The distinctive genomic architecture of chromothripsis, characterized by frequent oscillations between two copy number states with retention of heterozygosity, is incompatible with progressive rearrangement models. Chromothripsis can generate profound oncogenic consequences simultaneously, including the formation of double minute chromosomes harboring amplified oncogenes like MYC, and the concurrent disruption of multiple tumor suppressor genes, thereby driving rapid tumor evolution.
