The presence of microbial or self DNA in the cytoplasm of mammalian cells is a danger signal that triggers the production of type I interferons, but the identity of the cytosolic DNA sensor has remained elusive. We previously showed that delivery of DNA to mammalian cell extracts results in the generation of cyclic GMP-AMP (cGAMP), which binds to and activates the adaptor protein STING. Here, through a combination of quantitative mass spectrometry and conventional biochemical fractionation, we identify a cytosolic enzyme that we name cyclic GMP-AMP synthase (cGAS) as a DNA sensor that catalyzes the synthesis of cGAMP from ATP and GTP in the presence of DNA. Overexpression of cGAS activated the transcription factor IRF3 and induced interferon-beta in a STING-dependent manner. Knockdown of cGAS inhibited IRF3 activation and interferon-beta induction by DNA transfection or DNA virus infection. Thus, cGAS is a cytosolic DNA sensor that triggers type I interferon production through the second messenger cGAMP.
