The formation and storage of a specific memory is thought to involve defined populations of neurons that constitute a cellular memory engram. Previous studies have shown that selective ablation or inhibition of neurons activated during learning can impair subsequent memory expression, indicating that these cells are necessary for behavioural recall. However, a critical test of the engram hypothesis is whether direct activation of such cells is sufficient to induce the associated behavioural output. Here we used c-fos-tTA transgenic mice combined with a doxycycline-regulated AAV9-TRE-ChR2-EYFP system to label dentate gyrus neurons that were active during contextual fear conditioning and to reactivate them later with light. Activity-dependent labelling was sparse, restricted largely to excitatory dentate gyrus granule cells, and recapitulated endogenous c-Fos induction during training. Optical stimulation of the labelled population in a neutral context induced robust freezing after fear conditioning, whereas stimulation of similarly labelled cells from mice exposed to the context without shock, or of EYFP-expressing control cells, did not. Reducing background labelling or stimulating bilaterally increased the magnitude of light-evoked freezing. Moreover, dentate gyrus cell populations recruited by different contexts showed only chance overlap, and optical activation of cells labelled in a non-conditioned context failed to evoke fear memory recall. These results demonstrate that reactivation of a hippocampal neuronal population recruited during learning is sufficient to elicit the behavioural expression of a fear memory, and support the idea that sparse dentate gyrus ensembles form contextual components of memory engrams.
