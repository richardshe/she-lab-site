Chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) is caused by the BCR-ABL fusion protein, a constitutively activated tyrosine kinase, and the kinase activity of BCR-ABL is required for leukemogenesis. We conducted a phase 1, dose-escalation study of STI571, an oral inhibitor of BCR-ABL, in patients with chronic-phase CML in whom therapy with interferon alfa had failed or could not be tolerated.

Eighty-three Philadelphia chromosome-positive patients were assigned to receive 25 to 1000 mg of STI571 daily. Safety and tolerability were the primary end points, and hematologic and cytogenetic responses, pharmacokinetics, and inhibition of BCR-ABL kinase activity in vivo were assessed.

STI571 was rapidly absorbed, and at a daily dose of 400 mg plasma trough concentrations exceeded those required to inhibit BCR-ABL phosphorylation in vitro. A maximal tolerated dose was not identified. The most common adverse effects were nausea, myalgias, edema, and diarrhea, which were usually mild or moderate; grade 3 neutropenia and thrombocytopenia occurred in patients receiving 200 mg or more. There were no deaths. Hematologic responses occurred in all patients receiving 140 mg or more daily. Among 54 patients treated with 300 mg or more, 53 had complete hematologic responses, generally within four weeks, and 51 remained in response at a median follow-up of 265 days. Cytogenetic responses occurred in 29 of these 54 patients (54 percent), including 17 major responses and 7 complete cytogenetic remissions. Inhibition of BCR-ABL kinase activity, measured by decreased phosphorylation of CRKL, increased with dose and was substantial at clinically active doses.

STI571 was well tolerated and induced rapid, durable hematologic responses and frequent cytogenetic responses in interferon-refractory CML. These findings support inhibition of BCR-ABL as a rational and effective therapeutic strategy.
