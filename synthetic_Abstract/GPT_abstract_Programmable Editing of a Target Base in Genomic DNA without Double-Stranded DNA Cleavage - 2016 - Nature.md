Current genome editing methods typically introduce double-stranded DNA breaks (DSBs) at target loci, which are repaired by non-homologous end joining or, less efficiently, by homology-directed repair in the presence of a donor template. Although powerful, these processes often generate stochastic insertions or deletions and are poorly suited for the precise correction of point mutations, which underlie many genetic diseases. Here we describe programmable base editing, a strategy for the direct conversion of one DNA base pair into another in genomic DNA without requiring DSBs or donor DNA. We fused a cytidine deaminase to catalytically impaired Cas9 to create an RNA-programmed enzyme that converts cytosine to uracil within the single-stranded DNA bubble generated by Cas9 binding. Optimization of deaminase identity, fusion geometry, and linker length yielded a first-generation base editor that efficiently deaminated target cytosines in vitro. Fusion to uracil DNA glycosylase inhibitor improved editing in human cells, and nicking of the non-edited strand further increased product formation by directing cellular repair of the U:G intermediate. The resulting base editors mediate targeted C to T conversion in mammalian cells with efficiencies up to 37% at endogenous human genomic loci, while producing far fewer indels than Cas9-mediated homology-directed repair. Off-target base editing was confined to a subset of known Cas9 off-target sites and was not accompanied by detectable untargeted C to T mutagenesis near the examined loci. We further demonstrate efficient correction of disease-relevant APOE4 and TP53 mutations in mammalian cells. These findings establish base editing as a programmable approach for precise single-nucleotide genome modification.
