In vitro-synthesized mRNA holds great promise for gene therapy due to its rapid protein expression and lack of insertional mutagenesis risks. However, its clinical application has been severely limited by high immunogenicity and relative instability. Recent findings show that incorporating naturally occurring modified nucleosides into RNA suppresses immune activation. Here, we investigate the translational potential and in vivo immunogenicity of mRNAs containing modified nucleosides. We discovered that substituting uridine with pseudouridine (Ψ) in in vitro-transcribed mRNA not only abrogates immune activation but also significantly enhances its translational capacity and biological stability in mammalian cells. This superior translation is independent of cap and poly(A) tail length and does not rely on the evasion of RIG-I-mediated interferon responses, as demonstrated in wild-type and RIG-I(-/-) fibroblasts. Crucially, intravenous administration of Ψ-modified mRNA in mice resulted in robust, prolonged protein expression—primarily in the spleen—without inducing inflammatory cytokines like interferon-alpha and tumor necrosis factor-alpha, which are strongly triggered by unmodified mRNA. Our results demonstrate that Ψ-containing mRNA is a highly efficient, stable, and non-immunogenic vector, representing a powerful new platform for safe and effective in vivo gene replacement and therapeutic vaccination.
