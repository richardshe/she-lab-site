# Abstract

Current CRISPR/Cas9 genome editing technologies primarily rely on the creation of double-stranded DNA breaks (DSBs), which frequently lead to stochastic insertions or deletions (indels) via non-homologous end joining. For correcting point mutations underlying many genetic diseases, precise base conversion without DSB-induced indels is highly desirable. Here, we describe the development of "base editors," a novel class of engineered proteins capable of direct, programmable conversion of one target DNA base into another without requiring DSBs or donor templates. We constructed these editors by fusing a catalytically inactive Cas9 (dCas9) to a cytidine deaminase enzyme, which catalyzes the conversion of cytosine (C) to uracil (U) within a narrow window of the single-stranded DNA bubble formed by the Cas9-guide RNA complex. To overcome the cellular base-excision repair response to the resulting U:G mismatch, we tethered a uracil DNA glycosylase inhibitor (UGI) to the complex, substantially increasing base editing efficiency in human cells. Finally, to manipulate mismatch repair and favor the desired U:A resolution, we restored nickase activity to the Cas9 domain, directing the cell to replace the unedited G-containing strand. This third-generation base editor (BE3) mediates highly efficient (up to 37%) and specific C-to-T (or G-to-A) conversions at targeted genomic loci in human and mouse cells, with minimal (<1%) indel formation. We demonstrate the utility of this approach by efficiently correcting disease-relevant point mutations, including the APOE4 Alzheimer's risk variant and a dominant-negative p53 mutation. Programmable base editing expands the scope of precision genome engineering for research and therapeutic applications.
