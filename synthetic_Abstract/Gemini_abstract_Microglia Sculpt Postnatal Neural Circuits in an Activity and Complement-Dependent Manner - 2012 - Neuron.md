# Abstract

The precise cellular mechanisms underlying activity-dependent synaptic pruning during postnatal brain development remain poorly understood. Here, we demonstrate that microglia, the resident immune cells of the central nervous system, actively engulf presynaptic inputs during the peak of robust synaptic remodeling in the developing mouse retinogeniculate system. Using in vivo phagocytosis assays, we show that this microglial engulfment is developmentally regulated and dependent on neural activity, with microglia preferentially targeting less active presynaptic terminals. Furthermore, we identify the classical complement cascade as a critical molecular pathway mediating this process. Complement receptor 3 (CR3), expressed specifically by microglia, and its ligand, complement component C3, which localizes to developing synapses, are required for the phagocytosis of presynaptic inputs. Disruption of CR3/C3 signaling in microglia leads to sustained deficits in eye-specific segregation and an enduring overabundance of structurally intact synapses. These findings reveal a central role for microglia in normal brain wiring.
