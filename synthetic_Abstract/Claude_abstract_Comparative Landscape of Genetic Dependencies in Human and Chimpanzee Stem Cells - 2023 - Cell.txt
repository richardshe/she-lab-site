Comparative studies of humans and chimpanzees have long sought to define the molecular changes that underlie the evolution of unique human traits, yet existing transcriptomic and epigenomic surveys yield thousands of differences whose functional consequences remain obscure. To directly probe how gene function has diverged across recent hominin evolution, we performed genome-wide CRISPR-interference screens in human and chimpanzee pluripotent stem cells, followed by validation screens across six individuals of each species. Despite extensive conservation of essential genes, we identified 75 genes with robust species-specific effects on cellular proliferation, organized into coherent protein complexes and biochemical pathways including UFMylation, the MOZ histone acetyltransferase complex, lysosomal V-ATPase signaling, and regulators of the G1/S transition. Human pluripotent and neural progenitor cells were strikingly robust to depletion of CDK2, CDK4, and Cyclin E1, a difference rescued in chimpanzee cells by CDK1 overexpression. Comparisons with orangutan stem cells localized this rewiring to the human lineage, linking cell cycle changes to longstanding hypotheses of cortical expansion.
