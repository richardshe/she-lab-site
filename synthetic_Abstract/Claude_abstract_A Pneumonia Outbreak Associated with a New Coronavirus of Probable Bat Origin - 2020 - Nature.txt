Since the SARS outbreak nearly two decades ago, severe acute respiratory syndrome-related coronaviruses (SARSr-CoV) have been widely discovered in bats and have generally been thought to pose a substantial risk of a future disease outbreak. Here we report on a recent cluster of acute respiratory illness in Wuhan, Hubei province, central China, which was determined to be caused by a previously unidentified coronavirus. Samples from seven patients with severe pneumonia, six of whom were sellers or deliverymen from a local seafood market, were sent to the laboratory at the Wuhan Institute of Virology for the diagnosis of the causative pathogen. Using metagenomic next-generation sequencing of bronchoalveolar lavage fluid and targeted PCR, we obtained the full-length genome sequence of a novel coronavirus, which we tentatively call 2019-nCoV. The virus shared 79.6% sequence identity to SARS-CoV across the genome, but was 96.2% identical at the whole-genome level to a coronavirus (BatCoV RaTG13) previously detected in Rhinolophus affinis from Yunnan province, indicating that 2019-nCoV may have originated in bats. We also show that 2019-nCoV is able to use ACE2 proteins from humans, Chinese horseshoe bats, civets and pigs—but not mice—as an entry receptor to enter ACE2-expressing cells, suggesting that ACE2 is probably the cellular receptor through which 2019-nCoV enters cells. The disease and viral spread of this outbreak warrant further investigation.</content>
