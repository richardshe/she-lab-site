Acute myeloid leukemia (AML) is organized hierarchically, maintained by leukemia stem cells (LSC) that evade standard therapies. Eradicating these cells requires identifying specific molecular targets. Here we show that CD47, a transmembrane protein functioning as a 'don't eat me' signal through its interaction with macrophage receptor SIRPα, is highly expressed on human AML LSC relative to normal hematopoietic stem cells. Clinical analysis of adult AML patients reveals that elevated CD47 expression is a strong adverse prognostic factor associated with worse event-free and overall survival. Functionally, blocking the CD47-SIRPα interaction with specific monoclonal antibodies promotes the phagocytosis of AML LSC by macrophages in vitro. In vivo, anti-CD47 antibody treatment clears leukemia cells from peripheral blood and bone marrow, prevents engraftment, and specifically targets LSC in xenograft models. Thus, CD47 is a critical immune evasion mechanism and a promising therapeutic target for AML.
