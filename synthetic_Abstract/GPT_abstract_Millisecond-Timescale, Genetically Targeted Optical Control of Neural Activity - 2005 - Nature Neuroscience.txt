The ability to control activity in genetically specified neurons with single-spike temporal precision would enable causal dissection of neural circuit function. We found that Channelrhodopsin-2 (ChR2), a light-gated cation channel from Chlamydomonas reinhardtii, could be expressed safely in mammalian hippocampal neurons and used to drive rapid depolarizing currents without exogenous retinal. Brief pulses of blue light reliably elicited single spikes, Poisson-distributed spike trains, subthreshold depolarizations and excitatory or inhibitory synaptic events with millisecond-scale jitter over physiologically relevant frequencies. ChR2 expression and photostimulation did not alter basal electrical properties or neuronal survival. Thus ChR2 enables noninvasive, genetically targeted, temporally precise optical control of neural activity.
