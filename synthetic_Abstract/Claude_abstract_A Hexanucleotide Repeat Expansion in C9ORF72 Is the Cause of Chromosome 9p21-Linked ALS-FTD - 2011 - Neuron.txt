The chromosome 9p21 locus has been repeatedly implicated by genome-wide association and linkage studies as the major genetic contributor to amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) and frontotemporal dementia (FTD), yet the underlying mutation has remained elusive. Using next-generation deep resequencing of the 232 kilobase block of linkage disequilibrium in a Welsh ALS-FTD kindred and a Dutch ALS-FTD kindred, we identified a large GGGGCC hexanucleotide repeat expansion in a noncoding region of C9ORF72. The expansion segregated with disease in five multigenerational families and was confirmed by fluorescence in situ hybridization to be at least 1.5 kilobases in length. Repeat-primed PCR screening showed that the expansion accounts for 46.4% of familial and 21.0% of sporadic ALS in Finland, 38.1% of familial ALS of wider European ancestry, and 29.3% of Finnish FTD cases. Our findings indicate that this expansion is the most common genetic cause of ALS and FTD identified to date, and they implicate aberrant RNA metabolism as a central mechanism of neurodegeneration.
