While it is widely accepted that the sequence of amino acids in a protein is determined by the sequence of nucleotide bases in the genetic material, the exact nature of this genetic code has remained elusive. Through genetic analysis of proflavin-induced mutations in the B cistron of the rII region of bacteriophage T4, we provide evidence for the general properties of the genetic code. We demonstrate that the code is of the triplet type, meaning a group of three bases codes for one amino acid. We show that the code is non-overlapping and is read sequentially from a fixed starting point without special "commas" separating the triplets. Our results rely on the discovery that acridine mutations typically consist of the addition or deletion of single bases. Recombining mutations of opposite signs (+ and -) can restore gene function by correcting the reading frame, whereas combinations of the same sign do not. Crucially, combining three mutations of the same sign (+ + + or - - -) also restores the reading frame, providing compelling evidence for a triplet coding ratio. Finally, the ability of shifted reading frames to span significant lengths without hitting a "nonsense" triplet suggests that the code is highly degenerate.
