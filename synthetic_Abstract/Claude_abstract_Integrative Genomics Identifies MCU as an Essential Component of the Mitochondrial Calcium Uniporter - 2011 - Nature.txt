Mitochondrial calcium (Ca2+) uptake shapes cytosolic Ca2+ signals, stimulates ATP production and, when dysregulated, triggers cell death. For half a century the existence of a ruthenium-red-sensitive Ca2+ uniporter in the inner mitochondrial membrane has been established physiologically, yet its molecular identity has remained unknown. Here, building on our recent identification of MICU1 as a regulator of the uniporter, we apply three complementary genome-wide analyses, phylogenetic profiling across 500 organisms, mRNA co-expression across 81 mouse tissues, and protein co-expression across 14 tissues from our MitoCarta atlas, to nominate a previously uncharacterized protein, CCDC109A, which we name the mitochondrial calcium uniporter (MCU). MCU physically interacts with MICU1, harbours two transmembrane domains with both termini facing the matrix, and assembles into an oligomeric ~450 kDa complex in the inner membrane. Silencing MCU in HeLa and HEK-293 cells, and in mouse liver in vivo, profoundly attenuates mitochondrial Ca2+ uptake and Ca2+-stimulated NAD(P)H production, without disrupting cytosolic Ca2+ signalling, respiratory chain function or membrane potential. Mutation of conserved acidic residues within an intermembrane-space-facing 'DIME' motif abolishes Ca2+ transport, and a single point mutation, S259A, confers resistance to Ru360, the most potent inhibitor of the uniporter. Together, our genomic, biochemical and physiological data establish MCU as an essential, pore-associated component of the mitochondrial Ca2+ uniporter.
