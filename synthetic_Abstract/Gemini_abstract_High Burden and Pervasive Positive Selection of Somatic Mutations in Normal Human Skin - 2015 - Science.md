Cancer evolution is driven by the accumulation of somatic mutations, but the early stages of this process in normal tissues remain largely obscured. To investigate the burden, mutational signatures, and clonal architecture of somatic mutations in normal human tissue, we performed ultradeep targeted sequencing of 74 cancer genes in 234 small biopsies of physiologically normal, sun-exposed eyelid epidermis from four individuals. We discovered an unexpectedly high burden of somatic mutations, with an average of two to six mutations per megabase per cell, strongly bearing the signature of ultraviolet light exposure. Strikingly, we observed pervasive positive selection for driver mutations in several genes. NOTCH1, NOTCH2, NOTCH3, FAT1, TP53, and FGFR3 exhibited a significant excess of protein-altering mutations and formed distinct clonal expansions. We estimate that over 25% of normal skin cells carry driver mutations in NOTCH genes, with NOTCH1 alone mutated in 14-21% of cells. These findings reveal that physiologically normal sun-exposed skin is a patchwork of thousands of evolving clones carrying oncogenic driver mutations, illuminating a vast, pre-malignant landscape that fundamentally alters our understanding of the earliest stages of cancer development.
