Pluripotential cells capable of prolonged growth in vitro have previously only been obtained from teratocarcinomas as embryonal carcinoma (EC) cells. We report the successful establishment of pluripotential cell cultures directly from normal early mouse embryos. By utilizing blastocysts in delayed implantation, we derived cell lines that closely resemble EC cells in morphology, growth, and surface antigen expression. Crucially, unlike most EC lines, these embryo-derived (EK) cells maintain a completely normal karyotype. When injected into mice, they form complex teratocarcinomas, and in vitro, they differentiate into various tissues. These directly derived pluripotential cells offer a powerful new system for studying early mammalian development and differentiation, bypassing the abnormal chromosomal complement typical of teratocarcinoma-derived cells.
