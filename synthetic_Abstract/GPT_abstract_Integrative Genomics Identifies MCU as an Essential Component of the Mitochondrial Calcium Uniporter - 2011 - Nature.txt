Rapid Ca²⁺ uptake into mitochondria is a central process that shapes cytosolic Ca²⁺ signals and stimulates matrix metabolism, yet the molecular identity of the mitochondrial calcium uniporter has remained unknown. Here we combine phylogenetic profiling, genome-wide mRNA co-expression and mitochondrial protein co-expression to identify an unstudied inner membrane protein, formerly CCDC109A, which we name mitochondrial calcium uniporter (MCU). MCU closely tracks MICU1 across evolution and tissue expression, is detected as a mitochondrial protein in multiple mouse tissues, and physically interacts with MICU1. Silencing MCU in HeLa and HEK-293 cells markedly attenuates mitochondrial Ca²⁺ uptake without disrupting cytosolic Ca²⁺ mobilization, respiration, membrane potential or gross mitochondrial morphology, and diminishes Ca²⁺-dependent stimulation of NAD(P)H. Liver-directed siRNA knockdown of MCU in mice yields mitochondria with intact respiratory state transitions and ADP:O ratios, but a profound and near-complete loss of Ca²⁺ uptake. MCU localizes to the mitochondrial inner membrane, contains two transmembrane domains with both termini facing the matrix, and oligomerizes within an approximately 450 kDa complex. Conserved acidic residues in the intermembrane-space linker are required for activity, and a point mutation in this region confers resistance to Ru360. These genomic, physiological, biochemical and pharmacological data establish MCU as an essential, conserved component of the mitochondrial calcium uniporter.
