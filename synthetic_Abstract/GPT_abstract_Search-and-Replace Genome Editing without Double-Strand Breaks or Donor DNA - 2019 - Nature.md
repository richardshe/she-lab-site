The ability to install any desired genomic change in living cells remains a central goal of genome editing. Current methods that use programmable nucleases can efficiently disrupt genes, but precise correction of pathogenic alleles commonly requires double-strand DNA breaks (DSBs) and donor DNA templates, which can generate mixtures of indels, translocations, and inefficient homology-directed repair outcomes. Base editors avoid DSBs and donor templates, but are presently limited to transition mutations and cannot directly mediate most transversions, targeted insertions, or targeted deletions. Here we describe prime editing, a versatile search-and-replace genome editing strategy that uses a Cas9 nickase fused to an engineered reverse transcriptase and a prime editing guide RNA (pegRNA) that both specifies the target site and encodes the desired edit. The nicked target DNA primes reverse transcription of the pegRNA-encoded sequence, generating an edited DNA flap that can be incorporated by cellular repair. Optimized prime editors install all 12 possible base-to-base conversions, small insertions, small deletions, larger deletions, and combinations of these edits in human cells without requiring DSBs or exogenous donor DNA. Prime editing efficiencies commonly reached 20-50% in HEK293T cells, with substantially fewer indels than Cas9-initiated homology-directed repair, and with low activity at known Cas9 off-target sites. Prime editing also corrected or installed pathogenic alleles including HBB E6V, HEXA 1278+TATC, and CFTR DeltaF508-related edits. These results establish prime editing as a broadly applicable precision genome editing platform with the potential to address most known pathogenic human genetic variants.
