The therapeutic efficacy of checkpoint blockade immunotherapy with monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) against programmed cell death 1 (PD-1) and cytotoxic T-lymphocyte-associated antigen 4 (CTLA-4) has been firmly established for the treatment of several human cancers, yet the antigenic targets responsible for tumour rejection have remained poorly defined. Here we use genomics, bioinformatics and mass spectrometry to identify the tumour-specific mutant antigens (TSMA) that drive CD8+ T-cell responses underlying checkpoint blockade-mediated rejection of two distinct progressor 3-methylcholanthrene-induced (MCA) sarcomas in syngeneic mice. By integrating cDNA capture sequencing with three major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class I epitope-binding algorithms and applying filters based on predicted proteasomal processing and differential binding to wild-type sequences, we identify two dominant H-2Kb-restricted epitopes encoded by mutations in Lama4 and Alg8 as the principal targets of tumour-infiltrating CD8+ T cells in anti-PD-1-treated mice bearing d42m1-T3 tumours. The mutant Lama4 and Alg8 peptides are presented by H-2Kb in vivo as confirmed by tetramer staining and direct mass spectrometric detection in immunoaffinity-purified MHC class I eluates, and therapeutic vaccination with synthetic long peptides encoding these epitopes plus poly(I:C) induces tumour rejection with efficacy comparable to that of anti-PD-1 therapy itself. RNA sequencing of antigen-specific tumour-infiltrating lymphocytes reveals that anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 each engage distinct, predominantly non-overlapping transcriptional programs converging on enhanced effector function, with dual blockade producing synergistic activation of T-cell-mediated anti-tumour pathways. These findings demonstrate that TSMA are major targets of checkpoint blockade immunotherapy, provide a mechanistic framework for the distinct and complementary actions of anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4, and support the use of TSMA-based vaccines as a clinically tractable strategy for personalized cancer immunotherapy.
