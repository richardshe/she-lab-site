Human embryonic stem cells (hESCs) hold tremendous promise for regenerative medicine, yet the potential for immune rejection remains a major barrier to the clinical transplantation of these cells. A strategy to overcome this obstacle is the derivation of patient-specific hESCs through somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT). In this study, we report the reliable and highly efficient establishment of eleven patient-specific hESC lines from SCNT blastocysts. Nuclear transfer was performed using skin fibroblasts from patients with varying diseases, including spinal cord injury, juvenile diabetes, and congenital hypogammaglobulinemia, and donated enucleated oocytes. The SCNT-derived hESCs (NT-hESCs) were established irrespective of the somatic cell donor's age or sex and without the use of mouse feeder layers or animal-derived immunosurgery. These NT-hESC lines exhibited normal karyotypes, expressed classic pluripotency markers, and differentiated into cell types representing all three embryonic germ layers both in vitro and in vivo. DNA fingerprinting and human leukocyte antigen (HLA) typing confirmed that each NT-hESC line was genetically matched to the patient donor. This significant improvement in derivation efficiency provides a crucial step toward realizing the potential of therapeutic cloning and creating autologous cells for disease research and future clinical therapies.
