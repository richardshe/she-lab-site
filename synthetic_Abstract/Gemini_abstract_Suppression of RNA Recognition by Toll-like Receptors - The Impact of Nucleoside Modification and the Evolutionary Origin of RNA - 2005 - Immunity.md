The innate immune system utilizes Toll-like receptors (TLRs) to distinguish pathogenic nucleic acids from host molecules. While DNA recognition relies on unmethylated CpG motifs, the basis for discriminating host from pathogen RNA remains elusive. Here, we demonstrate that the immunostimulatory potential of RNA is governed by naturally occurring nucleoside modifications. Unmodified RNAs, such as bacterial RNA and in vitro transcripts, potently activate dendritic cells and trigger human TLR3, TLR7, and TLR8. In contrast, eukaryotic RNAs, which are highly abundant in post-transcriptional modifications, are significantly less stimulatory. Incorporating specific modified nucleosides—such as 5-methylcytidine, pseudouridine, 2-thiouridine, and N6-methyladenosine—into synthetic transcripts severely suppresses their capacity to activate TLR signaling and induce cytokine secretion. This immune evasion effect correlates with the frequency of modifications present within the RNA. These findings identify nucleoside modification as a fundamental mechanism allowing the innate immune system to avoid auto-reactivity against self-RNA, providing critical insights into autoimmune pathogenesis and the optimization of therapeutic RNA design.
