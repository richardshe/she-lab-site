It has often been surmised that the sequence of the four bases of the nucleic acid of the genetic material is in some way a code for the sequence of the twenty amino-acids found in proteins, but the general character of this code has not been established. We report here genetic experiments on the B cistron of the rII region of bacteriophage T4 which, taken together with the work of others on tobacco mosaic virus, suggest that the code is of a particular general type. Working with the acridine-induced mutant FC0 and a large family of suppressors, suppressors of suppressors, and so on, we find that combinations of one + with one - mutant frequently restore a wild or pseudo-wild phenotype, whereas combinations of like sign do not, and that triple mutants of like sign are often pseudo-wild. From these results we conclude that a group of three bases (or a small multiple of three) codes one amino-acid; that the code is not of the overlapping type; that the sequence of bases is read from a fixed starting point, with no special commas; and that the code is probably degenerate, more than one triplet coding for the same amino-acid.
