While programmable nucleases like CRISPR-Cas9 have revolutionized genome editing, precisely correcting the majority of known human pathogenic mutations remains challenging due to the reliance on double-strand DNA breaks (DSBs) or exogenous donor DNA, which often result in unwanted byproducts. Here, we present "prime editing," a highly versatile and precise genome editing technology that mediates targeted insertions, deletions, and all 12 possible base-to-base conversions without requiring DSBs or donor DNA templates. Prime editors utilize a catalytically impaired Cas9 fused to an engineered reverse transcriptase, programmed with a prime editing guide RNA (pegRNA) that both specifies the genomic target and encodes the desired edit. The pegRNA directs the reverse transcription of new genetic information directly into the nicked target DNA strand. Through the systematic optimization of the editor components and the pegRNA architecture, we established prime editor 3 (PE3), which routinely achieves editing efficiencies of 20-50% with low indel formation (1-10%) in human cells. We demonstrate the extraordinary versatility of PE3 by performing over 150 diverse edits, including the precise correction of the primary genetic causes of sickle cell disease and Tay-Sachs disease. Prime editing exhibits substantially lower off-target activity than standard Cas9 and offers complementary advantages over existing base editors. By liberating precision editing from the constraints of DSBs and homology-directed repair, prime editing vastly expands the scope of capabilities for research and therapeutic genome modification.
