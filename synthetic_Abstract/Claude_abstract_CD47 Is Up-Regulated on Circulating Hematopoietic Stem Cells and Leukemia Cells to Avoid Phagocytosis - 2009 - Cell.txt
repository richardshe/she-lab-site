Macrophages clear damaged, foreign, and aged cells from the circulation by recognizing altered surface molecules, while CD47 on healthy cells serves as a "don't eat me" signal through its interaction with macrophage SIRP alpha. Hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells (HSPCs) and leukemic stem cells migrate through macrophage-lined vasculature of the marrow, spleen, and liver, where they are potentially vulnerable to phagocytic clearance. Here we show that CD47 is transiently up-regulated on mouse HSPCs during mobilization and inflammation, and is constitutively elevated on circulating human HSCs and on nearly all mouse and human myeloid leukemias examined, including AML and blast-crisis CML. CD47-deficient and heterozygous HSCs are cleared by macrophages and exhibit reduced fitness during transplantation and inflammatory stress. Conversely, enforced CD47 expression on a non-engrafting human AML line confers tumorigenicity in immunodeficient mice in a dose-dependent, macrophage-sensitive manner. CD47 thus represents a compelling therapeutic target for myeloid malignancies.
