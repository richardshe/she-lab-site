Hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) enter the blood during stress and must pass through macrophage-rich tissues. We show here that mouse HSCs and progenitors transiently up-regulate CD47 during cyclophosphamide/G-CSF mobilization or LPS-induced inflammation, and that circulating human HSCs likewise express higher CD47 than sessile marrow HSCs. Although CD47 deficiency does not grossly impair mobilization, CD47-null HSCs fail to engraft wild-type recipients and are rapidly phagocytosed, while CD47 heterozygous HSCs have reduced fitness during inflammatory challenge in a macrophage-dependent manner. We further find that CD47 is constitutively increased in murine and human myeloid leukemias, particularly AML and blast crisis CML. In MOLM-13 leukemia cells, the level of CD47 determines tumor engraftment, selection in vivo, and resistance to macrophage phagocytosis through SIRP alpha. Thus, leukemias co-opt a physiological HSC protection pathway to evade innate immune clearance.
