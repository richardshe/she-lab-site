Since the emergence of severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS) and Middle East respiratory syndrome (MERS), the threat of future coronavirus outbreaks has been widely recognized. Here we investigate the causative agent of a severe pneumonia outbreak that began in Wuhan, China, in late 2019. From patients linked to a local seafood market, we isolated and sequenced a novel coronavirus, tentatively named 2019-nCoV. The full-length viral genome shares 79.6% sequence identity to SARS-CoV and 96.2% identity to a bat coronavirus (BatCoV RaTG13), suggesting a probable origin in bats. The novel virus features distinct insertions in the spike protein and utilizes the same cellular entry receptor as SARS-CoV, angiotensin-converting enzyme 2 (ACE2). We confirm viral RNA and protein seroconversion in infected patients, establishing a clear link to the acute respiratory disease. 2019-nCoV was successfully cultured from patient samples and shown to infect ACE2-expressing cells from humans and several animal species. These findings identify 2019-nCoV as the etiological agent of the Wuhan pneumonia outbreak and highlight the urgent need for broad-spectrum antiviral strategies against emerging bat-borne coronaviruses.
