Nuclear transplantation has shown that amphibian nuclei from differentiated adult cells can direct development to larval stages, but it has remained uncertain whether nuclei from differentiated mammalian cells can be reprogrammed to support development to term. We have previously obtained live lambs after transfer of nuclei from cultured embryonic cells induced into quiescence, suggesting that donor-cell-cycle state may influence the capacity of oocyte cytoplasm to reprogramme gene expression. Here we report the production of viable offspring after nuclear transfer from three cultured cell populations derived from a day-9 embryo, a day-26 fetus and adult mammary gland. Donor cells were made quiescent by serum starvation before fusion with enucleated metaphase-II oocytes. Reconstructed embryos developed to morulae or blastocysts, were transferred to recipient ewes, and gave rise to live lambs representing all three donor-cell sources. Microsatellite analysis confirmed that the lambs were derived from the respective nuclear donor populations. One lamb born from an adult mammary-gland cell is, to our knowledge, the first mammal to develop from a cell derived from adult tissue. These results show that the mammalian genome can remain developmentally totipotent after extensive differentiation.
