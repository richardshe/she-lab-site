The acquired immune deficiency syndrome (AIDS) is associated with severe depression of cellular immunity and alterations of T-lymphocyte subpopulations, suggesting that T cells may be a target for an infectious agent. We report the isolation of a retrovirus from a cervical lymph node of a homosexual patient with persistent multiple lymphadenopathies, a syndrome that may represent an early or mild form of AIDS. Reverse transcriptase activity was detected after culture of lymph node lymphocytes with phytohemagglutinin, T-cell growth factor, and antiserum to human alpha interferon, but not in similarly cultured peripheral blood lymphocytes. The agent was transmitted to normal adult and umbilical cord T lymphocytes and displayed properties of a type-C retrovirus, including a density of 1.16, uridine labeling, and budding particles in electron microscopy. Its reverse transcriptase resembled that of human T-cell leukemia viruses, but its major p25 core protein was immunologically distinct from HTLV-I p24 and p19. The relation of this T-lymphotropic retrovirus to AIDS remains to be determined.
