Several lines of evidence have implicated a locus on chromosome 9p21 as a major cause of amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), frontotemporal dementia (FTD), and the overlapping ALS-FTD phenotype. Here we used next-generation sequencing of affected members from chromosome 9p21-linked families, together with targeted analysis of Finnish and outbred European case-control series, to identify the pathogenic lesion. We found a large GGGGCC hexanucleotide repeat expansion within C9ORF72, located upstream of one transcript and within the first intron of another. Repeat-primed PCR confirmed segregation of the expansion with disease in multiple kindreds and demonstrated a strong association with ALS and FTD. The expansion was present in 46.4% of Finnish familial ALS cases, 21.0% of Finnish sporadic ALS cases, 38.1% of European-ancestry familial ALS probands, and 29.3% of Finnish FTD cases, but was absent or rare in population controls. These data establish C9ORF72 repeat expansion as the cause of chromosome 9p21-linked neurodegeneration and provide further evidence that disrupted RNA metabolism contributes to ALS and FTD pathogenesis.
