A novel retrovirus has been isolated from a cervical lymph node biopsy of a 33-year-old homosexual male presenting with multiple lymphadenopathies, a condition that may represent an early or milder form of the acquired immune deficiency syndrome (AIDS). The virus was propagated in cultures of T lymphocytes from a healthy adult donor and from umbilical cord blood of newborns, with virus production enhanced by inclusion of antiserum to human alpha interferon. Electron microscopy revealed C-type particles budding at the plasma membrane, and the buoyant density of the virus in sucrose was 1.16. The reverse transcriptase exhibited ionic requirements close to those of the human T-cell leukemia virus (HTLV), with an optimal Mg2+ concentration of 5 mM and a strong preference for poly(A)-oligo(dT). The major viral core protein, p25, was immunologically distinct from the p24 and p19 of HTLV-I, although the patient's serum reacted with surface antigens on HTLV-I-infected cells. This isolate appears to represent a new member of the HTLV family that may be involved in AIDS.
