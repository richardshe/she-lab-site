Current models of cancer evolution assume that driver mutations arise in rare normal cells and expand only after malignant transformation, but the extent of such processes in physiologically normal tissues is poorly defined. We analyzed 234 small biopsies of sun-exposed eyelid epidermis from four individuals by ultradeep sequencing of 74 cancer genes, with whole-genome sequencing of one highly mutated clone. Normal skin carried thousands of somatic mutations with ultraviolet-light signatures and a burden of two to six coding substitutions per megabase per cell. Despite normal histology, many mutations were under strong positive selection. NOTCH1, NOTCH2, NOTCH3, FAT1, TP53, RBM10, and FGFR3 showed evidence of driver activity, and biallelic NOTCH1 inactivation was already frequent. Approximately one quarter of cells carried NOTCH mutations, and hundreds of mutant clones populated each square centimeter of aged skin. Driver clones were usually small, suggesting constraints on expansion, whereas FGFR3-mutant benign-associated clones could be large. Thus normal human skin is a dense mosaic of competing, positively selected clones, revealing early cancer evolution before malignancy.
