There is now considerable evidence that the amino-acid sequence of a protein is determined by the order of bases in the nucleic acid of the genetic material. We describe genetic experiments on the B cistron of the rII region of bacteriophage T4 which bear on the general nature of this code. Acridine mutants, typified by FC0, usually lack function completely and revert chiefly by nearby suppressor mutations in the same gene. About eighty such mutants and their suppressors fall within a limited region and are non-leaky r mutants. When pairs are combined in the same piece of genetic material, mutants assigned the same sign fail to grow on Escherichia coli K12, whereas suitable pairs of opposite sign are wild or pseudo-wild, except where the displaced reading encounters an unacceptable triplet. These predictions were confirmed in the combinations tested. Further, six triple mutants of like sign restore function, as expected if the coding ratio is three, or a multiple of three. Experiments with deletion 1589 support the view that the sequence is read in groups from a fixed starting point. Taken together with evidence against overlapping codes, these results suggest that the code is non-overlapping, probably triplet, read without commas, and probably degenerate.
