Microglia, the resident CNS immune cells, have recently been implicated in synaptic remodeling in the developing brain, yet the precise function of microglia at synapses and the underlying molecular mechanisms remain unclear. Here, using the postnatal mouse retinogeniculate system, we demonstrate that microglia actively engulf presynaptic retinal ganglion cell inputs during a peak period of synaptic pruning. Engulfment was developmentally regulated and modulated by neural activity, such that microglia preferentially phagocytosed inputs from the less active eye when retinal activity was pharmacologically manipulated. We further identify complement receptor 3 (CR3/CD11b-CD18) and its ligand, complement component C3, as a key molecular pathway underlying microglia-mediated engulfment. Genetic disruption of CR3/C3 signaling, which in the postnatal dLGN is specific to microglia, reduced engulfment and resulted in sustained deficits in eye-specific segregation and excess retinogeniculate synapses into adulthood. Together, these data implicate microglia as active mediators of activity- and complement-dependent synaptic pruning in the developing CNS.
