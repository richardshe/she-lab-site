Background. Chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) is caused by the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase, the product of the Philadelphia chromosome. STI571 (formerly CGP 57148B) is a small molecule that was developed as a specific, competitive inhibitor of the ATP-binding site of the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase. On the basis of its antileukemic activity in preclinical models, we conducted a phase 1 trial of STI571 in patients with CML in whom treatment with interferon alfa had failed.

Methods. We administered STI571 orally, once daily, to 83 patients with CML in the chronic phase in whom treatment with interferon alfa had failed. Fourteen dose cohorts received doses ranging from 25 to 1000 mg per day. The primary end point was the establishment of the safety and tolerability of STI571; antileukemic activity was a secondary end point. Pharmacokinetic studies and in vivo assessment of inhibition of the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase were also performed.

Results. STI571 was well tolerated, and a maximal tolerated dose was not identified. The most common adverse effects were nausea, myalgias, edema, and diarrhea; most were mild. Complete hematologic responses occurred in 53 of 54 patients treated with daily doses of 300 mg or more, and these responses typically began within four weeks after the initiation of therapy. Of the 54 patients receiving 300 mg or more, 29 (54 percent) had cytogenetic responses, with major responses (35 percent or less of cells in metaphase positive for the Ph chromosome) in 17 patients (31 percent), including 7 patients (13 percent) with complete cytogenetic remissions. Inhibition of the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase in vivo, as measured by decreased phosphorylation of CRKL, was observed at doses of 140 mg or more.

Conclusions. STI571, an inhibitor of the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase, is well tolerated and has substantial antileukemic activity in patients with CML in whom treatment with interferon alfa has failed.
