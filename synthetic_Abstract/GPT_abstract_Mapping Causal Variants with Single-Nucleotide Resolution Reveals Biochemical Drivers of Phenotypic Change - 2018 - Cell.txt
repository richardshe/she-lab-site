Genetic linkage obscures the individual mutations that drive heritable traits, leaving most quantitative trait loci as long lists of statistically indistinguishable candidates. Here we develop an inbred yeast crossing strategy that uses dense meiotic recombination to map causal variants with single-nucleotide resolution. By reducing founder polymorphism while preserving phenotypic diversity, we generated 1,125 F6 haploid segregants containing more crossovers than segregating SNPs. Across 26 chemical perturbations, this design resolved hundreds of causal variants, including a synonymous mutation in UPC2 that confers pan-azole resistance and tightly linked SKY1 variants with opposing effects on 5-fluorouracil sensitivity. Most traits were genetically complex, driven by an average of 14 variants spanning missense, synonymous, and regulatory classes. Causal mutations frequently clustered in linkage groups and often revealed functional relationships missed by deletion screens. These data establish natural genetic variation as a genome-wide toolkit for dissecting the biochemical drivers of phenotypic change.
