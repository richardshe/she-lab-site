The acquired immune deficiency syndrome (AIDS) is a devastating disease characterized by a profound depression of cellular immunity, leading to severe opportunistic infections and Kaposi's sarcoma. Epidemiological evidence suggests horizontal transmission by sexual contact, blood transfusion, and intravenous drug use. The specific depletion of T lymphocytes, particularly the T-helper subset, suggests that these cells may be the primary target of an infectious agent. Here, we report the isolation of a novel T-lymphotropic retrovirus from a cultured lymph node biopsy of a homosexual patient presenting with multiple lymphadenopathies, a condition that may represent an early or milder form of AIDS. The virus exhibits reverse transcriptase activity similar to that of the human T-cell leukemia virus (HTLV) family and produces characteristic C-type budding particles. However, the viral core proteins are immunologically distinct from the known p19 and p24 proteins of HTLV-I. Specifically, the major viral protein, p25, is recognized by the patient's serum but not by antibodies against HTLV-I p24. This novel retrovirus is readily propagated in cultures of normal human T lymphocytes. We tentatively conclude that this isolate belongs to a broader family of human T-lymphotropic retroviruses that may be etiologically involved in AIDS and related syndromes.
