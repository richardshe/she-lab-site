Bacteria and their phages are locked in a continuing evolutionary contest, and CRISPR/Cas systems provide adaptive immunity by using small CRISPR RNAs to guide destruction of invading DNA. Although phage mutation can permit escape from this immunity, whether phages encode dedicated CRISPR/Cas antagonists has been unknown. Here we show that several Pseudomonas aeruginosa Mu-like phages carry genes that profoundly inhibit the type I-F CRISPR/Cas system. Prophages bearing these genes enabled normally CRISPR-sensitive phages and protospacer-containing plasmids to escape targeting. Expression of individual phage genes was sufficient to block immunity against multiple protospacers and in several P. aeruginosa strains, but did not inhibit an Escherichia coli type I-E system. Anti-CRISPR activity did not alter cas gene expression, crRNA biogenesis or crRNA stability, indicating inhibition after assembly of the crRNA-Cas machinery. Mutational and codon-variation analyses demonstrated that the activity requires protein production. Finally, deletion or transfer of an anti-CRISPR gene respectively abolished or conferred phage replication in CRISPR-competent cells. These findings reveal a genetic route by which mobile elements counter adaptive bacterial immunity.
