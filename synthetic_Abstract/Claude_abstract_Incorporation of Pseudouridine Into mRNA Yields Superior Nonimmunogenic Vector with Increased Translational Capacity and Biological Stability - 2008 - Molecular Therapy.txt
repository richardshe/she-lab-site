In vitro-transcribed mRNA offers a promising nonviral platform for gene replacement and vaccination, but its therapeutic application has been hindered by inefficient translation, poor stability, and strong activation of the innate immune system. We previously reported that incorporation of naturally occurring modified nucleosides into RNA abolishes recognition by Toll-like receptors and RIG-I. Here, we systematically tested mRNAs containing pseudouridine, 5-methylcytidine, N6-methyladenosine, 5-methyluridine, or 2-thiouridine for their translational potential and in vivo immunogenicity. Strikingly, pseudouridine-containing mRNA was translated more efficiently than its unmodified counterpart in rabbit reticulocyte lysates, in cultured mammalian cell lines, and in primary cells including dendritic cells, fibroblasts, splenocytes, and neurons, yielding up to ten-fold more protein. Using RIG-I-deficient mouse embryonic fibroblasts, we excluded a role for type I interferon in this translational enhancement. Following intravenous administration into mice, pseudouridine-modified mRNA accumulated in the spleen, produced markedly higher and more sustained reporter protein expression, and elicited no detectable proinflammatory cytokine response. These findings establish pseudouridine-containing mRNA as a uniquely effective, stable, and nonimmunogenic vector for in vivo protein expression.
