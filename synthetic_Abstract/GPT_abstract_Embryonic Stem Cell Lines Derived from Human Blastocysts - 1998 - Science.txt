Human embryonic stem (ES) cell lines were derived from donated in vitro fertilized blastocysts and maintained in an undifferentiated state after prolonged culture. Five lines, each from a separate embryo, showed characteristic primate ES cell morphology, normal XX or XY karyotypes, high telomerase activity, and expression of SSEA-3, SSEA-4, TRA-1-60, TRA-1-81, and alkaline phosphatase. The cells could be cryopreserved and expanded without evidence of replicative crisis. After injection into immunodeficient mice, all lines formed teratomas containing derivatives of endoderm, mesoderm, and ectoderm. These human ES cells provide a renewable model for human developmental biology and a potential source of differentiated cells for drug discovery and transplantation.
