Most genetic variants that contribute to human disease are challenging to install or correct efficiently in therapeutically relevant cell types. Existing methods relying on double-strand DNA breaks (DSBs) generate undesired byproducts including indels, translocations, and p53 activation, while homology-directed repair (HDR) requires exogenous donor DNA templates and is inefficient in most cell types of interest. Base editors avoid DSBs but cannot install transversion mutations or perform targeted insertions and deletions. Here we describe prime editing, a versatile and precise genome editing method that directly writes new genetic information into a specified DNA site using a catalytically impaired Cas9 fused to an engineered reverse transcriptase, programmed with a prime editing guide RNA (pegRNA) that both specifies the target site and encodes the desired edit. We performed more than 175 edits in human cells, including targeted insertions, deletions, and all 12 possible base-to-base conversions, as well as combinations thereof. Prime editing in human cells is more efficient and has fewer byproducts than HDR, exhibits complementary strengths and weaknesses compared with current base editors, and induces much lower off-target editing than Cas9 nuclease at known Cas9 off-target sites. We applied prime editing to install or correct pathogenic mutations causing sickle cell disease and Tay-Sachs disease in human cells, and to install a prion-resistant allele. Prime editing substantially expands the scope and capabilities of genome editing, and in principle could correct up to 89% of known pathogenic human genetic variants.
