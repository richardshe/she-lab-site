The CRISPR/Cas immune system is a widespread bacterial defence that uses small CRISPR RNAs (crRNAs) complexed with Cas proteins to cleave invading nucleic acids derived from phages and other mobile genetic elements. Although phage-encoded inhibitors have been described for most major bacterial anti-phage defences, no genes that counteract CRISPR/Cas activity have previously been identified. Here we investigate the type I-F CRISPR/Cas system of Pseudomonas aeruginosa and report the discovery of five distinct families of phage-encoded 'anti-CRISPR' genes that profoundly inhibit this system in vivo. Anti-CRISPR genes are clustered at a single position within the genomes of closely related Mu-like phages, and each is required for the host phage to evade CRISPR/Cas-mediated immunity. The encoded proteins act after crRNA biogenesis and have no effect on crRNA or Cas protein amounts, indicating that they target a step subsequent to crRNA–Cas complex formation. These findings reveal a new arms race that may shape the evolution of bacterial and phage genomes.
