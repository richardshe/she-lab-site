Individual tissues contain many cell types and states, but single-cell RNA-seq has been limited by the time and cost of preparing libraries one cell at a time. We describe Drop-seq, a method for analyzing mRNA expression in thousands of individual cells by co-encapsulating cells with distinctly barcoded microparticles in nanoliter droplets. After cell lysis, each bead captures the transcripts of its companion cell, forming single-cell transcriptomes attached to microparticles that can be reverse-transcribed, amplified, sequenced, and digitally counted in one reaction. Species-mixing experiments show high single-cell integrity, low doublet rates at appropriate cell concentrations, and transcript capture efficiencies comparable to other approaches. Applied to 44,808 mouse retinal cells, Drop-seq identified 39 transcriptionally distinct populations and revealed markers of known and newly resolved cell types. These results establish Drop-seq as a scalable tool for discovering cellular diversity.
