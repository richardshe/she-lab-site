Checkpoint blockade with antibodies to CTLA-4 and PD-1 can induce durable tumour regression, yet the tumour antigens targeted by the responding T cells remain poorly defined. Here we show that therapeutic blockade of PD-1 and/or CTLA-4 rejects two independent methylcholanthrene-induced sarcomas through CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, interferon-γ and Batf3-dependent antigen cross-presentation. Using complementary DNA capture sequencing, MHC class I binding prediction and epitope filtering, we analysed 2,796 expressed non-synonymous mutations in the d42m1-T3 sarcoma and identified two dominant H-2Kb-restricted tumour-specific mutant antigens, mLama4 and mAlg8. Both epitopes were recognized by freshly isolated CD8+ tumour-infiltrating lymphocytes from anti-PD-1-treated mice, stimulated tumour-specific cytotoxic T-cell lines, stabilized H-2Kb on RMA-S cells and were detected directly in H-2Kb peptide eluates from d42m1-T3 but not unrelated F244 tumours. T cells specific for mLama4 and mAlg8 accumulated in tumours before rejection and were present before therapy, but checkpoint blockade altered their number, phenotype and transcriptional state. Anti-PD-1 induced metabolic and type I interferon-associated programmes, anti-CTLA-4 enhanced cell-cycle, NFAT and JAK-STAT pathways, and combined treatment produced the strongest effector profile, including increased IFNγ, Granzyme A, Granzyme B and Fas ligand. Therapeutic vaccination with synthetic long peptides containing mLama4 and mAlg8 plus poly(I:C) rejected established d42m1-T3 tumours as effectively as anti-PD-1, while remaining tumour specific. These data demonstrate that tumour-specific mutant antigens are principal targets of checkpoint blockade and provide a rational basis for individualized cancer vaccines and immune monitoring.
