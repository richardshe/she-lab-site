We report here the identification of increased CD47 expression on human acute myeloid leukemia (AML) stem cells (LSC) compared to normal hematopoietic stem and multipotent progenitor cells. Analysis of three independent adult AML cohorts demonstrated that high CD47 expression was associated with FLT3-ITD mutations and independently predicted worse event-free and overall survival. In primary AML specimens, differential CD47 expression enabled prospective separation of leukemic cells from residual normal progenitors. We hypothesized that CD47 contributes to AML pathogenesis by inhibiting macrophage phagocytosis through signal regulatory protein alpha (SIRP alpha). Consistent with this hypothesis, blocking monoclonal antibodies directed against CD47, or antibody-mediated disruption of SIRP alpha, preferentially enabled phagocytosis of AML LSC but not normal CD34+ cells in vitro. Treatment of AML-engrafted immunodeficient mice with anti-CD47 antibody rapidly eliminated circulating and bone marrow leukemia by a phagocyte-dependent mechanism and prevented secondary engraftment. These results establish CD47 as an adverse prognostic factor and provide a rationale for anti-CD47 antibody therapy in AML.
