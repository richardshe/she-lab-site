Two new techniques have been developed for the rapid prenatal diagnosis of sickle cell anemia. The first is a method for amplifying specific beta-globin genomic sequences with oligonucleotide primers and the Klenow fragment of DNA polymerase I. Twenty cycles of the polymerase chain reaction (PCR) yield an approximately 220,000-fold amplification of a 110-base pair segment spanning the sixth codon of the beta-globin gene. The second is the analysis of the amplified DNA by solution hybridization with an end-labeled complementary oligomer followed by sequential digestion with Dde I and Hinf I, allowing the beta^A and beta^S alleles to be distinguished. Together, these procedures permit a diagnosis to be made from as little as 20 nanograms of genomic DNA in less than 10 hours, and they may be generally applicable to the detection of other genetic and infectious diseases.
