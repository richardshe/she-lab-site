Despite more than a decade of intensive research, the genetic basis of common human diseases remains largely unresolved. Linkage and candidate gene studies have yielded only a modest number of replicable findings, leaving most of the heritable contribution to common familial disorders unaccounted for. The convergence of three recent advances—the International HapMap resource, dense genotyping chips that interrogate hundreds of thousands of single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), and the assembly of large, well-characterized clinical sample collections—now makes adequately powered genome-wide association (GWA) studies feasible. Here we describe the main experiment of the Wellcome Trust Case Control Consortium (WTCCC): a GWA study of 2,000 cases for each of seven common diseases of major public health importance (bipolar disorder, coronary artery disease, Crohn's disease, hypertension, rheumatoid arthritis, type 1 diabetes and type 2 diabetes) together with 3,000 shared controls drawn from the 1958 British Birth Cohort and the UK Blood Services. All 17,000 samples were genotyped with the Affymetrix GeneChip 500K Mapping Array Set, and a new genotype-calling algorithm, CHIAMO, was developed to extract calls of high accuracy. After stringent quality control, 469,557 SNPs in 16,179 individuals were available for analysis. We identified 24 independent association signals at P < 5 × 10⁻⁷: 1 in bipolar disorder, 1 in coronary artery disease, 9 in Crohn's disease, none in hypertension, 3 in rheumatoid arthritis, 7 in type 1 diabetes and 3 in type 2 diabetes. On the basis of prior findings and replication studies thus far, almost all of these signals reflect genuine susceptibility effects. We observed loci that influence susceptibility to multiple autoimmune diseases and obtained suggestive evidence of a sex-differentiated effect in rheumatoid arthritis. Beyond these disease-specific findings, our study addresses important methodological issues affecting all GWA studies, including the choice of controls, the impact of population structure, sample-size requirements and improvements in genotype-calling and analytical methods. The variants identified are characterized by modest effect sizes, consistent with an allelic architecture in which few large effects, a handful of modest effects and many small effects together contribute to common disease risk, and they account for only a small fraction of overall familiality—pointing to the need for still larger studies, extensive fine-mapping and functional validation.
