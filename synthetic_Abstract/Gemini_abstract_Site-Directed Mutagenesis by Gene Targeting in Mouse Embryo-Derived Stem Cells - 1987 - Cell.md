Gene targeting via homologous recombination holds tremendous potential for the precise modification of the mammalian genome. In this study, we demonstrate the successful site-directed inactivation of the endogenous hypoxanthine phosphoribosyl transferase (Hprt) gene in mouse embryo-derived stem (ES) cells. By employing custom-designed recombinant vectors containing a neomycin resistance (neo) gene embedded within Hprt sequences, we selectively disrupted the target locus. We optimized our targeting strategy by modifying the neo gene for robust expression in ES cells and utilizing both sequence replacement and insertion vectors. When introduced via electroporation, these vectors facilitated homologous recombination at a remarkable frequency of 1 in 1000 transformants. This targeted disruption rendered the cells resistant to 6-thioguanine, allowing direct selection of Hprt-deficient clones. Southern blot analysis confirmed the precise integration of the neo gene into the intended site. Our findings establish ES cells as a robust platform for gene targeting, paving the way for generating custom-designed mutant mice to study development and disease.
