Embryonic stem (ES) cells possess the unique ability to grow indefinitely while maintaining the potential to differentiate into all three germ layers. Generating patient-specific pluripotent cells directly from somatic cells would circumvent both the ethical and immunological issues associated with ES cell therapies. Here, we demonstrate the induction of pluripotent stem cells from mouse embryonic and adult fibroblast cultures by the retroviral introduction of a defined set of just four transcription factors: Oct3/4, Sox2, c-Myc, and Klf4. We selected these factors based on their roles in maintaining ES cell pluripotency and rapid proliferation. The resulting induced pluripotent stem (iPS) cells exhibit morphology, growth properties, and marker expression closely resembling those of ES cells. Furthermore, subcutaneous transplantation of iPS cells into nude mice results in the formation of complex teratomas containing tissues from all three germ layers. These findings demonstrate that somatic cells can be reprogrammed to a pluripotent state by a small number of defined factors, opening new avenues for generating patient-specific stem cells.
