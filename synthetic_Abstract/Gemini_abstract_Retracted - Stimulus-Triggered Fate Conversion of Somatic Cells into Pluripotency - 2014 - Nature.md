Cellular differentiation is widely considered a unidirectional process, where the fate of somatic cells is fixed unless subjected to complex nuclear manipulations like nuclear transfer or the introduction of transcription factors. Here, we demonstrate that terminally differentiated somatic cells can be reprogrammed into a pluripotent state simply by applying transient but strong external stimuli. We show that brief exposure of mature CD45+ hematopoietic cells to a low-pH environment rapidly induces a dramatic fate conversion, independent of any genetic manipulation. These reprogrammed cells, which we termed stimulus-triggered acquisition of pluripotency (STAP) cells, undergo extensive epigenetic remodeling and express key pluripotency markers. STAP cells demonstrate widespread developmental potential, contributing extensively to chimeric embryos and differentiating into all three germ layers in vitro and in vivo, including germline transmission. Furthermore, STAP cells can be adapted in culture to establish expandable pluripotent stem cell lines. These findings reveal an innate, self-driven plasticity in somatic cells, capable of resetting their developmental state purely in response to environmental stress.
