To understand the evolutionary origins of unique human traits, it is critical to identify molecular pathways with divergent functions in conserved developmental cell types. We performed genome-wide CRISPR-interference (CRISPRi) screens in human and chimpanzee pluripotent stem cells (PSCs) to systematically map the comparative landscape of genetic dependencies. Despite high overall conservation between the species, we identified 75 genes with strongly divergent effects on cellular proliferation. These species-specific genetic dependencies are organized into coherent functional pathways. Notably, we discovered that human PSCs have evolved a unique robustness to the depletion of key cell cycle regulators, including Cyclin E1 and CDK2, which control the G1/S transition. This human-specific robustness to G1/S perturbations persists in neural progenitor cells and cerebral organoids. Our functional genomics approach provides a robust framework for identifying recently evolved gene networks, connecting molecular changes to evolutionary differences in human development.
