The prediction of protein structures from amino acid sequence has long been a central challenge in structural biology, limiting the use of genomic data to infer molecular mechanism. Here we describe AlphaFold, a neural network method that combines evolutionary information from multiple sequence alignments with physical and geometric constraints to predict three-dimensional protein structures directly from sequence. The model uses a new architecture, Evoformer, to exchange information between residue-pair and sequence-alignment representations, followed by an equivariant structure module that iteratively refines backbone frames and side-chain geometry. In the blind CASP14 assessment, AlphaFold achieved a median backbone accuracy of 0.96 Angstrom r.m.s.d.95, compared with 2.8 Angstrom for the next best method, and produced accurate all-atom models and calibrated residue-level confidence estimates. This accuracy transferred to a large set of recently released Protein Data Bank chains that were excluded from training, including many cases without close structural templates. Analysis of the network suggests that AlphaFold forms and continuously improves a structural hypothesis through depth and recycling, while self-distillation on unlabelled sequences further improves performance. Accuracy remains affected by shallow sequence alignments and by chains whose conformation is dominated by heterotypic contacts. Nevertheless, AlphaFold provides protein coordinates in GPU minutes to hours and offers a practical route to proteome-scale structural modelling and to broader computational approaches for biological structure prediction.
