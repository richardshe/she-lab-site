# Abstract

This paper builds upon previous topological studies of the rII region in bacteriophage T4 to investigate its genetic topography—specifically, the distribution and mutability of distinct genetic sites within this locus. Using a rapid, qualitative mapping technique based on overlapping deletions, we mapped thousands of independent spontaneous and induced rII mutants. Our analysis reveals a highly non-random distribution of mutations, characterized by distinct "hotspots" of high mutability alongside numerous sites with low or singular occurrences. Furthermore, different mutagens produce unique mutational spectra, indicating distinct chemical specificities at the nucleotide level. Despite identifying over 300 distinct mutable sites, statistical estimates suggest many more remain undiscovered, implying a high degree of complexity within the cistron. These findings demonstrate that the genetic fine structure is topographically differentiated, likely reflecting the underlying nucleotide sequence and its local interactions, and pave the way for understanding mutation at the molecular level.
