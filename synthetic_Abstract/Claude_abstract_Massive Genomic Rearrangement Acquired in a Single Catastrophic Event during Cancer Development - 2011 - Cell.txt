Cancer is conventionally thought to develop through the gradual accumulation of mutations over years to decades, with successive waves of clonal expansion driving progression toward malignancy. Here, we describe a strikingly different pattern of genome evolution. Using paired-end sequencing, SNP array profiling, and cytogenetic analysis of cancer samples, we identify tumors in which tens to hundreds of genomic rearrangements are confined to one or a few chromosomes, with copy number oscillating between just two states and heterozygosity retained in the higher state. Several lines of evidence, including Monte Carlo simulations of progressive rearrangement, indicate that the vast majority of these rearrangements were acquired in a single catastrophic event, which we term chromothripsis. This phenomenon occurs in 2%-3% of all cancers across many subtypes, and is particularly common in bone tumors. Such cataclysmic shattering and reassembly of the genome can disrupt multiple tumor suppressor genes and generate oncogene amplification, accelerating the path to cancer.
