A stable cell-free system from E. coli has been used to examine the role of added polyribonucleotides in amino acid incorporation into protein. Incorporation stimulated by ribosomal RNA required washed ribosomes, the 105,000 x g supernatant fraction, ATP, and an ATP-generating system, and was inhibited by RNAase, puromycin, and chloramphenicol, but not by DNAase. Soluble RNA did not replace the active RNA fraction, and fractionation experiments indicated that the activity was associated with high-molecular-weight RNA rather than soluble RNA or intact ribosomes. RNA from several sources, including yeast ribosomal RNA and tobacco mosaic virus RNA, was active. Polyuridylic acid caused a marked and specific incorporation of C14-phenylalanine into an acid-insoluble product, whereas other polynucleotides or uridylic acid derivatives were inactive. These results indicate that template RNA is required for cell-free protein synthesis and that polyuridylic acid functions as a synthetic messenger specifying phenylalanine incorporation.
