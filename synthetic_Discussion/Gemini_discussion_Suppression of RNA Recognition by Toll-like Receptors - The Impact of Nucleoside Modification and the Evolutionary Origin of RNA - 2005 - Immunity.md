The findings presented in this study provide a fundamental new perspective on how the mammalian innate immune system discriminates between self and foreign RNA. It is well established that the immune system utilizes Toll-like receptors (TLRs) to detect pathogen-associated molecular patterns, triggering inflammatory responses essential for host defense. While double-stranded RNA, single-stranded RNA, and DNA containing unmethylated CpG motifs have been identified as potent activators of TLR3, TLR7/8, and TLR9, respectively, the mechanisms preventing catastrophic autoimmune responses against the host's own abundant nucleic acids have remained incompletely understood. Here, we demonstrate that the presence of naturally occurring nucleoside modifications—such as 5-methylcytidine (m5C), N6-methyladenosine (m6A), 5-methyluridine (m5U), 2-thiouridine (s2U), and pseudouridine (Ψ)—acts as a critical immune-suppressive signal, effectively abrogating the immunostimulatory potential of RNA.

Our investigation was prompted by the observation that while in vitro-transcribed RNA and RNA derived from necrotic cells potently activate dendritic cells (DCs), bulk RNA isolated from mammalian sources does not. This discrepancy highlighted an intrinsic difference in the structural or chemical nature of the RNA itself. By systematically fractionating cellular RNA, we discovered that mitochondrial RNA (mtRNA) and bacterial RNA are highly stimulatory, whereas mammalian total, nuclear, and cytoplasmic RNAs are immunologically inert. This pattern perfectly mirrors the evolutionary distribution of post-transcriptional nucleoside modifications. Bacterial mRNA and mtRNA (which is of endosymbiotic bacterial origin) contain very few, if any, modified nucleosides, whereas eukaryotic ribosomal RNA (rRNA) and transfer RNA (tRNA) are heavily decorated with them. 

To definitively link nucleoside modification to immune suppression, we utilized in vitro-transcribed RNA in which standard nucleotides were systematically replaced with their modified counterparts. Our results clearly showed that incorporation of m5C, m6A, m5U, s2U, or Ψ dramatically reduced the ability of RNA to activate human DCs, resulting in significantly diminished secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as TNFα and IL-12, and reduced expression of maturation markers like CD80, CD83, and CD86. Crucially, we mapped this suppression directly to the level of TLR signaling. Using TLR-expressing cell lines, we established that unmodified RNA robustly activates human TLR3, TLR7, and TLR8. However, the introduction of modified nucleosides into the RNA sequence profoundly abrogated these responses. Specifically, m6A and s2U modifications suppressed TLR3 activation, while m5C, m6A, m5U, s2U, and Ψ universally blocked signaling through TLR7 and TLR8.

The suppressive effect of these modifications is remarkably potent and dominant. Our experiments incorporating titrated amounts of modified nucleosides, as well as our use of chemically synthesized short oligoribonucleotides, revealed that even a very low frequency of modification—sometimes a single modified residue within a short RNA strand—is sufficient to abolish TLR activation. This extreme sensitivity suggests that TLRs have evolved highly selective binding pockets that are sterically or electrostatically disrupted by the presence of these methylations and isomerizations, thereby preventing receptor dimerization or downstream signal transduction.

These data provide a compelling evolutionary rationale for the extensive post-transcriptional modification of mammalian RNA. Beyond their known roles in stabilizing RNA structure, facilitating translation, and regulating RNA processing, nucleoside modifications appear to serve as an indispensable molecular watermark of "self." By cloaking endogenous RNA with these modifications, the mammalian host ensures that its own abundant ribosomal and transfer RNAs do not inadvertently trigger continuous, systemic inflammation upon normal cellular turnover or localized tissue damage. Conversely, the innate immune system can maintain highly sensitive surveillance for unmodified, "foreign" RNA introduced by invading bacteria or certain viruses. 

Interestingly, our finding that mtRNA is a potent immune activator adds an important dimension to our understanding of sterile inflammation. Because mitochondria are evolutionary descendants of bacteria, they retain a largely unmodified, prokaryotic-like RNA genome. In the event of severe cellular necrosis or trauma, the release of mtRNA into the extracellular space or endosomal compartments could act as a potent endogenous danger signal (an alarmin), activating DCs via TLRs to initiate wound healing or inflammatory responses even in the absence of infection. This mechanism provides a molecular basis for the profound inflammatory responses often observed following severe tissue injury or trauma, uncoupling inflammation from the strict requirement of an active microbial infection.

The differential response of primary monocytoid (DC1) and plasmacytoid (DC2) dendritic cells to specific modifications further highlights the complexity of RNA recognition. While DC2s rely primarily on TLR7 and TLR9, DC1s express a different repertoire of pattern recognition receptors. The observation that m5C and m6A modifications suppress cytokine production in some DC populations but not others suggests the existence of additional, perhaps cytoplasmic, RNA sensors whose discriminatory mechanisms might differ from those of the endosomal TLRs. The interplay between these diverse receptor systems and the vast array of potential RNA modifications remains a rich area for future investigation.

Furthermore, these findings illuminate the evolutionary arms race between hosts and pathogens. It is highly probable that certain viruses have evolved to exploit this self-recognition system by actively incorporating modified nucleosides into their own genomic or messenger RNAs. By mimicking the host's modification profile, such pathogens could effectively evade TLR-mediated immune surveillance, establishing a foothold within the host before an effective antiviral response can be mounted. Elucidating the modification profiles of viral RNAs and their interaction with host immune receptors will be essential for fully understanding viral pathogenesis and immune evasion strategies.

From a therapeutic and biotechnological perspective, our findings have profound implications for the design of RNA-based medicines. The emerging field of mRNA therapeutics and vaccines relies on the delivery of exogenous RNA to express therapeutic proteins or antigens in vivo. A major hurdle in this endeavor has been the dose-limiting toxicity and transient translation caused by the strong innate immune response triggered by the delivered RNA. Our data suggest that by synthesizing therapeutic mRNAs using naturally occurring modified nucleosides, it is possible to "stealth" these molecules, evading TLR activation, preventing interferon-mediated translational shutdown, and drastically reducing inflammatory side effects. This strategy could greatly enhance the safety and efficacy of RNA-based gene therapies, propelling the development of a new class of highly specific, transient, and well-tolerated pharmaceutical interventions.

In conclusion, we have identified nucleoside modification as a fundamental mechanism by which the innate immune system distinguishes self from non-self RNA. Unmodified RNA, characteristic of bacteria and mitochondria, serves as a potent danger signal that activates TLR3, TLR7, and TLR8. In contrast, the heavily modified RNA characteristic of the mammalian cytoplasm is immunologically silent. This elegant system of molecular discrimination underscores the co-evolution of host RNA modifications and pattern recognition receptors, ensuring robust pathogen defense while maintaining tolerance to endogenous nucleic acids. The ability to manipulate this system through the rational incorporation of modified nucleosides offers unprecedented opportunities to control immune responses and develop safer, more effective RNA-based therapeutics.
