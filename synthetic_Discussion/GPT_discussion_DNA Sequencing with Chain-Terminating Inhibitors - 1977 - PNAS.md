The results described here indicate that chain-terminating analogues provide a useful alternative to the plus and minus procedures for determining DNA sequences. The main advantage is that the sequence is read from four reactions in which termination occurs at the corresponding base, rather than from complementary sets of plus and minus products. Thus most of the ambiguities inherent in the former method are avoided. In the examples shown, long stretches of the phiX174 sequence could be read directly from the gels and, where the sequence was already known, the agreement was close. In several regions the new results gave corrections to the provisional sequence, showing that the method is not merely confirmatory but is capable of detecting errors in independently derived data.

The simplicity of the procedure is also important. When a small restriction fragment is used as primer and no subsequent cleavage is required, the operations consist only of annealing, extension in the presence of the inhibitor, a chase with the limiting labeled nucleotide, and electrophoresis. Under these conditions the method is rapid and gives a clear pattern of bands over a considerable length of sequence. The useful reading distance is limited partly by the resolving power of the gel and partly by irregular migration of some fragments. These limitations are similar to those encountered in the other gel-reading methods, but the interpretation is in general less complicated because each band corresponds to termination at a particular residue.

The choice of inhibitor has a considerable effect on the quality of the result. The dideoxynucleoside triphosphates appear to be the most satisfactory reagents. They act as efficient chain terminators and, once incorporated, do not seem to be significantly removed or extended under the conditions normally used. The arabinonucleoside triphosphates can also be used, and araCTP gave useful results in reactions that were applied directly to gels. However, when a restriction enzyme digestion at 37 degrees was included, the C bands obtained with araCTP were less satisfactory. This may be due either to removal of the terminal arabinonucleotide by the exonuclease activity of the polymerase or to further extension of some terminated chains at the higher temperature. For this reason the dideoxy derivatives are preferable whenever subsequent incubation is necessary.

There remain some practical difficulties. If the primer must be removed by a restriction enzyme, a second site near the desired site can produce a superimposed pattern and make the gel unreadable. The single-site ribo-substitution procedure provides a way of overcoming this difficulty and is especially useful with fragments generated by enzymes such as Alu, where the restriction enzyme cannot be used conveniently for the final cleavage. This modification adds an extra purification step, but it extends the range of fragments that can be used as primers and preserves the advantages of the chain-termination reaction.

The method should be applicable to many DNA sequencing problems in which a suitable primer and template can be prepared. It is particularly well suited to the checking and extension of sequences obtained from restriction enzyme fragments, since different primers can be used to examine overlapping regions from either strand. The present work with phiX174 shows that sequences of approximately one hundred nucleotides can be obtained from a single priming site with sufficient accuracy to locate insertions and substitutions in a provisional sequence. With improved preparations of the dideoxynucleoside triphosphates and optimization of electrophoresis conditions, the length and reliability of the readings should be increased. The chain-termination principle therefore provides a rapid and accurate enzymic method for DNA sequencing and should complement, and in many cases replace, the plus and minus procedures.
