The birth of lambs after nuclear transfer from cells derived from embryo, fetus and adult tissue shows that the procedure is not restricted to the particular embryo-derived cell line used previously. Most importantly, the production of a viable lamb from mammary gland cells taken from a 6-year-old ewe demonstrates that at least some nuclei from an adult mammalian somatic cell can be reprogrammed by the cytoplasm of an enucleated oocyte to direct development to term. This result provides direct evidence that cellular differentiation in that donor cell had not been accompanied by irreversible genetic changes incompatible with the formation of a whole animal.

The present results extend earlier observations in amphibians and mammals by showing complete development from a cultured adult cell nucleus in a large domestic species. The identity of the lambs was supported by coat and breed characteristics and by microsatellite analysis, excluding the possibility that they arose from inadvertent mating or from the oocyte donor genotype. Because the mitochondrial genome is inherited from the recipient oocyte, these animals are not genetically identical at all loci to the nuclear donor, but their nuclear genotype is that of the donor cell population. The findings therefore support the view that the nuclear genome of differentiated cells can retain the information required for all tissues and organs, provided that appropriate reprogramming of gene expression is achieved.

The use of quiescent donor cells is likely to have been important. Serum deprivation induced cells to leave the growth cycle and enter G0 before fusion with the enucleated oocyte. We have suggested that this state may facilitate the coordination of donor nucleus and recipient cytoplasm, either by avoiding inappropriate cell-cycle interactions or by producing chromatin conditions more amenable to reprogramming. The success obtained with three independent cell populations is consistent with this interpretation, although it does not prove that G0 is uniquely required. Other differentiated cells, and cells arrested by other procedures, will have to be tested to define the range of nuclei capable of supporting development and the conditions that optimize their reprogramming.

Despite the birth of live offspring, development was inefficient. A high proportion of fetuses detected by ultrasound was subsequently lost, and four dead fetuses from embryo-derived cells were recovered late in gestation. Some fetal loss may have resulted from embryo manipulation, culture, transfer, or incomplete reprogramming of the donor nucleus. The abnormal liver development seen in two fetuses indicates that nuclear transfer can be associated with developmental defects, even when early development and implantation occur. By contrast, perinatal loss among lambs born alive was not greatly different from that observed in commercial sheep, and the surviving lambs appeared morphologically normal at birth. Continued observation will be required to establish growth, reproductive performance and long-term health.

These observations have several implications. Nuclear transfer from adult cells offers a route to reproduce valuable genotypes and to propagate animals carrying defined genetic modifications introduced into cultured cells. It also provides an experimental system for studying the mechanisms by which oocyte cytoplasm remodels chromatin and resets gene expression. The present study does not imply that all adult cells are equivalent donors, nor that cloned animals will always be normal. It does, however, show that the differentiated state of an adult mammalian cell is not necessarily a permanent barrier to development. The ability of the oocyte to re-establish embryonic potential in such a nucleus will now allow the limits and mechanisms of mammalian nuclear reprogramming to be examined directly.
