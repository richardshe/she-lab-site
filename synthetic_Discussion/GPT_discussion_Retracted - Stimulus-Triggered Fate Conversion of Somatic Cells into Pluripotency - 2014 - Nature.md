The present findings indicate that the differentiated state of mammalian somatic cells is not invariably fixed, but can be markedly altered by a transient environmental stimulus. In this study, short exposure to sublethal low pH converted lineage-committed cells into Oct4-expressing cells with molecular, epigenetic and developmental properties of pluripotent cells. This phenomenon, which we term stimulus-triggered acquisition of pluripotency, differs from previously described reprogramming procedures in that it requires neither nuclear transfer nor forced expression of defined transcription factors. Thus, a strong external cue can initiate a substantial change in cell fate, suggesting that the epigenetic landscape of somatic cells may retain a context-dependent reversibility greater than generally assumed.

Several observations argue that STAP cells arise through conversion of somatic cells rather than expansion of pre-existing pluripotent contaminants. Oct4-GFP+ cells were not detected in untreated CD45+ cultures, and the emergence of Oct4-GFP expression after low-pH treatment occurred without substantial proliferation. The gradual loss of CD45 immunoreactivity, the appearance of Oct4-GFP in live imaging, and the presence of Tcrb rearrangements in Oct4-GFP+ cells derived from purified lymphocyte populations further support an origin from committed haematopoietic cells. In addition, similar induction of Oct4-GFP+ cells from several non-haematopoietic tissues indicates that the response is not restricted to a single lineage. These results suggest that somatic cells exposed to particular stress conditions can enter a dynamic state in which lineage-associated features decline and pluripotency-associated programmes become activated.

The acquisition of pluripotency by STAP cells was accompanied by changes at multiple levels. STAP cells expressed key pluripotency markers, showed demethylation of Oct4 and Nanog regulatory regions, differentiated into derivatives of the three germ layers, and contributed to chimaeric embryos and the germline. Their ability to generate embryos in tetraploid complementation assays provides stringent evidence for broad developmental potency. At the same time, STAP cells were not identical to conventional ES cells. They had limited self-renewal under the initial culture conditions, showed low Esrrb expression, and retained H3K27me3-dense foci in a fraction of female cells. These features suggest that STAP cells represent a pluripotent state generated by stress-induced conversion, but one that is not fully equivalent to the established ES-cell state.

The derivation of expandable STAP stem-cell lines from STAP cells further indicates that the primary STAP state can be stabilized and propagated under suitable culture conditions. ACTH-containing medium enabled outgrowth of proliferative colonies that subsequently acquired ES-cell-like properties, including single-cell passaging, long-term expansion, pluripotency marker expression, low DNA methylation at pluripotency loci, chimaera contribution and germline transmission. The disappearance of H3K27me3 foci and induction of Esrrb in STAP stem cells suggest that additional culture-dependent transitions occur after the initial stimulus-triggered conversion.

These observations raise important questions about the mechanisms by which transient stress initiates reprogramming. Low-pH treatment may affect membrane properties, metabolic status, chromatin regulation or stress-response pathways, and these processes may cooperate with LIF-dependent signalling during the early culture period. Defining the molecular events that distinguish reversible stress adaptation from fate conversion will be essential. More broadly, STAP suggests that mammalian cells may possess latent plasticity that can be uncovered by environmental perturbation, providing a simple experimental system for studying how external cues remodel nuclear state and developmental potential.
