Our experiments show that the artificial reactivation of a sparse population of DG neurons that was active during fear conditioning is sufficient to induce the behavioural expression of a fear memory. Previous studies have shown that neuronal populations activated during learning can be subsequently identified, and that their ablation or inhibition can impair memory expression. These findings established an important requirement for such populations in memory recall. Here, by contrast, we directly tested sufficiency. Labelling c-Fos-expressing DG neurons during fear conditioning with ChR2-EYFP and later stimulating these neurons in a neutral context induced freezing only during light delivery. Thus, the activation of a defined population of hippocampal neurons can mimic, at least in part, the natural retrieval of a learned contextual fear memory.

Several observations support the conclusion that the light-induced freezing reflected recall of a specific memory rather than a nonspecific consequence of optical stimulation or ChR2 expression. Mice in which DG neurons were labelled during exposure to the training chamber without shock did not freeze in response to light, although the number and intensity of labelled cells were comparable to those in fear-conditioned mice. Similarly, fear-conditioned mice expressing EYFP alone showed no light-induced freezing. These controls indicate that the behavioural response requires both the appropriate learning episode and the ability to drive the activity of the neurons recruited during that episode. In addition, in vivo recordings confirmed that labelled DG neurons could be reliably activated by the same optical stimulation used in the behavioural experiments, and optical stimulation induced c-Fos expression in downstream CA3 neurons. These results suggest that reactivation of the labelled DG ensemble engages hippocampal circuitry in a manner related to natural contextual recall.

The context-specificity of the effect is particularly consistent with the proposed role of the DG in pattern separation. Distinct contexts activated largely non-overlapping populations of DG granule cells, and optical stimulation of neurons labelled in an open field failed to evoke freezing associated with fear conditioning in a different chamber. This was true whether the open-field labelling occurred before or after fear conditioning. Therefore, the mere activation of a sparse DG population is not sufficient to produce freezing; rather, the population must correspond to the representation associated with the aversive experience. These data support a model in which the DG contributes to the formation of relatively orthogonal contextual representations, and in which a subset of the neurons recruited into such a representation can serve as an entry point for recall of the associated memory.

The magnitude of light-induced freezing was lower than that observed during re-exposure to the conditioned context. This difference may reflect several limitations of the experimental manipulation. First, some labelled cells probably arose from background activity during the off-Dox period and may have diluted or interfered with the fear-conditioning ensemble. Consistent with this idea, shortening the off-Dox period reduced background labelling and increased light-induced freezing. Second, unilateral stimulation may have activated only a fraction of the relevant hippocampal representation. Bilateral stimulation produced stronger freezing, indicating that the size or completeness of the reactivated ensemble influences the behavioural output. Finally, natural contextual recall is likely to recruit a distributed network involving multiple hippocampal subregions, the amygdala and other structures. Artificial stimulation of DG cells may therefore initiate recall through a restricted component of this network rather than reproducing the full sensory and associative state produced by the conditioned context.

Together, these findings provide causal evidence that a sparse, learning-activated neuronal ensemble in the hippocampus is a functional component of a memory engram. The results do not imply that the entire memory is stored exclusively in the DG, but they show that reactivating the DG component of the ensemble is sufficient to trigger a specific learned response. This strategy of activity-dependent labelling combined with optogenetic reactivation should be useful for mapping the distributed cellular components of memory traces, determining how different brain regions cooperate during recall, and examining how engrams are modified during consolidation, extinction or the formation of new associations.
