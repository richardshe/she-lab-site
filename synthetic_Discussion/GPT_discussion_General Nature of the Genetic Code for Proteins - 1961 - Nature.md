The experiments described here give, we believe, a simple interpretation of a rather diverse set of genetic facts. They suggest that the genetic message is read in successive groups from a fixed point, and that a displacement of this reading by the addition or loss of bases destroys the subsequent interpretation of the message. The restoration of function by a second mutation of the opposite kind, and especially by three mutations of like kind, is difficult to explain except on some such basis.

It should be emphasized that the evidence is not a direct chemical reading of the code. We have not identified the bases altered in any one mutant, nor have we shown the amino-acid changes in the presumed product of the B cistron. The argument therefore depends on the regularity of the genetic behaviour and on the assumption, now a very plausible one, that this cistron determines a polypeptide chain. Within these limits the results strongly favour a coding ratio of three, or conceivably a multiple of three, and they make the triplet interpretation the simplest one.

The same experiments also make improbable a comma system in which special bases or triplets mark the divisions between coding units. The facts are more naturally explained if the divisions are imposed by the starting point itself. A mutation which changes the number of bases then changes the reading frame, while a second compensating mutation can restore it beyond the intervening region. The pseudo-wild character of many double and triple mutants is just what would be expected if a short stretch of amino-acid sequence had been altered while the remainder of the chain was again correctly read.

Finally, the extent over which frame-shifted sequences may be tolerated argues that the code is unlikely to contain only twenty meaningful triplets. Some degeneracy is therefore probable. The exact dictionary of the code remains to be found, but its general form now appears experimentally accessible.
