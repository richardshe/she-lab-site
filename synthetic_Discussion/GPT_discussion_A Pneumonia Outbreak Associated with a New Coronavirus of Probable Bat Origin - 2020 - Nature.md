Our study identifies a new coronavirus, 2019-nCoV, as the causative agent associated with the outbreak of viral pneumonia in Wuhan. The nearly identical genome sequences obtained from five patients at the early stage of the outbreak indicate a recent introduction of this virus into humans. Although 2019-nCoV is related to SARS-CoV and belongs to the species SARSr-CoV, the virus forms a distinct lineage together with the bat coronavirus RaTG13, to which it shows 96.2% whole-genome identity. These results provide strong evidence that bats are the probable natural reservoir of 2019-nCoV, although the possibility that one or more intermediate hosts participated in transmission to humans requires further investigation.

The detection of viral RNA in BALF and oral swabs from patients, together with virus isolation in cell culture, seroconversion and neutralization by patient sera, fulfils several key criteria for linking this virus to the disease. The successful isolation of infectious virus will facilitate the development of diagnostics, antiviral assays and vaccine studies. Our qPCR assay based on the receptor-binding domain of the S gene can distinguish 2019-nCoV from other known human coronaviruses and from closely related bat SARSr-CoVs, but additional targets, including RdRp and E genes, should be incorporated for routine detection.

Notably, 2019-nCoV uses ACE2 from humans and several animal species for cell entry, as does SARS-CoV. This finding may help to explain its capacity for human infection and suggests that surveillance of ACE2-compatible animal hosts is urgently needed. Further epidemiological, clinical and experimental studies are required to define transmission routes, pathogenic mechanisms and the risk of continued spread.
