The present study identifies nucleoside modification as a critical determinant of the capacity of RNA to activate innate immune cells. We demonstrate that RNA synthesized from the four canonical nucleotides is a potent stimulus for human TLR3, TLR7, and TLR8, whereas incorporation of selected naturally occurring modified nucleosides markedly reduces or abolishes this activity. This effect is not restricted to a single sequence, length, or transcript configuration, and it is observed in TLR-transformed cells as well as in human dendritic cells. In parallel, RNAs isolated from natural sources display stimulatory activities that correlate inversely with the expected abundance of modified nucleosides. Bacterial RNA and mitochondrial RNA, which contain relatively few modifications, are efficient inducers of TLR signaling and dendritic cell activation, whereas mammalian total RNA, mRNA, and especially tRNA, which contain higher levels of modified nucleosides, are weakly stimulatory or inactive. These findings suggest that the immune system can discriminate among RNAs not only by their location, strandedness, or sequence but also by the chemical composition of their nucleosides.

The ability of RNA to stimulate human TLR7 and TLR8 is particularly important because natural ligands for these receptors have been incompletely defined. Previous studies indicated that murine TLR7 and human TLR8 recognize synthetic ssRNA oligonucleotides, and that human TLR7 and TLR8 respond to the antiviral imidazoquinoline compound R-848. Here, long in vitro-transcribed RNAs with natural phosphodiester linkages also activated human TLR7 and TLR8. Thus, the capacity to sense RNA is not limited to chemically stabilized oligoribonucleotides or to mouse receptors. Together with the established recognition of dsRNA by TLR3, these results expand the repertoire of RNA-responsive human TLRs and indicate that RNA can engage several endosomal receptors. Although TLR3, TLR7, and TLR8 differ in their expression pattern and signaling properties, all three receptors are affected by nucleoside modification. The suppressive effect, however, is not identical for each receptor. Modified adenosine and 2-thiouridine most strongly inhibited TLR3 activation, whereas several modifications, including m5C, m6A, m5U, s2U, and pseudouridine, inhibited activation of TLR7 and TLR8. These differences imply that each receptor may interrogate distinct structural features of RNA.

The data also indicate that RNA modifications do not simply impair delivery of RNA into cells or destabilize the RNA after transfection. Modified and unmodified RNAs were similar in integrity, cellular uptake, and persistence as assessed by electrophoretic and Northern analyses. Therefore, the diminished immune stimulation most likely reflects altered recognition or altered downstream signaling after entry into endosomal compartments. Modified nucleosides may influence receptor activation by changing hydrogen-bonding patterns, base stacking, local secondary structure, or the capacity of RNA to form receptor-accessible motifs. The potency of the effect was striking: in long RNAs, relatively low percentages of modified nucleosides reduced stimulatory capacity, and in short oligoribonucleotides a single modification was sufficient to diminish or abolish activity. These observations suggest that TLR-mediated RNA recognition is highly sensitive to discrete chemical marks and does not require wholesale remodeling of the RNA molecule.

Our results provide a biochemical explanation for previous observations that RNA from bacteria, but not most RNA from mammalian cells, induces strong dendritic cell activation. Mammalian cells contain large quantities of RNA, and cell injury can release such RNA into the extracellular space. If all cellular RNA were equally immunostimulatory, tissue turnover and necrosis would be expected to cause persistent and widespread activation of innate immune pathways. The abundance of modified nucleosides in mammalian RNA may represent one mechanism by which host RNA is rendered less visible to TLRs. This concept parallels the discrimination of bacterial and mammalian DNA by TLR9, in which unmethylated CpG motifs in microbial DNA are stimulatory, whereas CpG methylation in mammalian DNA suppresses activity. RNA, however, contains a far broader array of chemical modifications than DNA. The present findings suggest that this diversity is not only important for RNA maturation and function but also contributes to immunological identity.

The evolutionary distribution of RNA modifications is consistent with this model. Bacterial rRNA and tRNA contain fewer modifications than their mammalian counterparts, and bacterial mRNA is generally unmodified. In contrast, mammalian rRNA, tRNA, and mRNA contain multiple modified nucleosides, including pseudouridine, methylated bases, and 2'-O-methylated residues. Mitochondria are derived from eubacterial ancestors and retain several bacterial features, including comparatively limited RNA modification. Consistent with this relationship, mitochondrial RNA was a potent activator of dendritic cells and stimulated TLR8-expressing cells. Thus, the immune system may have exploited an ancient biochemical difference between prokaryotic and eukaryotic RNA. RNA with sparse modification would be interpreted as microbial or as released from organelles associated with severe cellular injury, whereas heavily modified host RNA would be less immunogenic.

The stimulation of dendritic cells by mitochondrial RNA may also help explain innate immune activation during necrosis. We previously observed that RNA released from necrotic mammalian cells can activate dendritic cells. The present results suggest that mitochondrial RNA is a likely contributor to this activity. In living cells, mitochondrial RNA is compartmentalized and inaccessible to endosomal TLRs. During cell disruption, however, mitochondrial contents may be released and delivered to phagocytic or endocytic pathways, where RNA-sensing TLRs are located. This provides a mechanism by which innate immune cells can detect tissue damage through molecules that carry microbial-like molecular signatures. The distinction is therefore not simply between foreign and self, but between chemically modified host RNA in its normal cellular context and poorly modified RNA appearing in an immunologically accessible compartment.

The responses of primary dendritic cell subsets add complexity to this interpretation. In monocyte-derived dendritic cells and in TLR-transformed 293 cells, several modified nucleosides suppressed RNA activity. In primary DC1 and DC2 populations, however, RNAs containing m5C or m6A retained considerable stimulatory capacity, whereas RNAs containing modified uridines were nonstimulatory. Because DC2 cells lack detectable TLR3 and TLR8 activity, yet responded to certain modified RNAs, these findings suggest that additional RNA recognition pathways may operate in primary dendritic cells. Such pathways may differ in their sensitivity to individual modifications and may recognize RNA structures not adequately represented by the transformed cell lines. The complete suppression observed with double-modified RNA containing m6A and pseudouridine further suggests that modified uridines may exert a dominant inhibitory effect in these cells. Identification of the responsible receptors or cofactors will be important for understanding how different dendritic cell subsets evaluate RNA.

The present work has implications for the design of RNA-based therapeutics and vaccines. In vitro-transcribed RNA has been used experimentally to express antigens and to activate dendritic cells. Depending on the intended application, immunostimulation can be advantageous or detrimental. For vaccination, unmodified RNA may provide both antigenic information and innate immune activation, thereby functioning as an intrinsic adjuvant. For applications in which RNA is intended primarily as a vehicle for protein expression, excessive activation of TLRs may reduce translation, induce inflammatory toxicity, or limit repeated administration. Incorporation of modified nucleosides offers a strategy to tune or suppress these innate immune responses. The finding that even low levels of modification can alter stimulation indicates that relatively modest chemical changes may have significant biological consequences.

These observations also raise the possibility that pathogens may manipulate RNA modification to influence host recognition. Viruses and intracellular bacteria that generate RNA in host cells may differ in the extent to which their RNA is modified or shielded from endosomal receptors. Some viral RNAs contain modified bases or specialized cap structures, whereas other viral replication intermediates expose unmodified or double-stranded regions. The balance between these features may shape the magnitude and quality of innate immune activation. Conversely, host cells may use RNA modification as a general strategy to prevent recognition of endogenous transcripts by innate sensors. Defects in RNA modification enzymes, RNA turnover, or compartmentalization could therefore contribute to inappropriate immune activation and possibly to inflammatory or autoimmune disease.

Several limitations should be considered. The modified in vitro transcripts used here contain one modified nucleoside type at frequencies higher than those found for most individual modifications in native RNA. Although titration experiments showed that lower levels of incorporation can suppress activity, natural RNAs contain complex combinations of modifications distributed nonrandomly across defined structural domains. Current technology does not allow precise reconstruction or selective removal of all native modifications in long RNAs. In addition, lipofectin-mediated transfection provides efficient delivery to cells but may not fully mimic physiological routes of RNA uptake during infection or tissue damage. Nevertheless, the convergence of results from synthetic transcripts, chemically synthesized oligoribonucleotides, natural bacterial RNA, mammalian RNA, mitochondrial RNA, TLR-expressing cell lines, and primary dendritic cells strongly supports the conclusion that nucleoside modification regulates innate recognition of RNA.

In summary, these studies reveal that nucleoside modifications suppress the immunostimulatory capacity of RNA and thereby contribute to discrimination between RNAs of different biological origin. RNA lacking such modifications, as found in bacteria and mitochondria or generated by in vitro transcription, efficiently activates TLR3, TLR7, TLR8, and dendritic cells. In contrast, RNA enriched in modified nucleosides is poorly recognized. The innate immune system therefore appears to read chemical signatures embedded within RNA as markers of origin and context. This mechanism provides a unifying explanation for the immunogenicity of bacterial and mitochondrial RNA, the relative inactivity of most mammalian RNA, and the ability of defined modified nucleosides to regulate TLR-dependent immune activation.
