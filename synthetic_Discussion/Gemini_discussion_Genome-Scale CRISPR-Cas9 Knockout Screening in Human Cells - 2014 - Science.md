The development and application of a genome-scale CRISPR-Cas9 knockout (GeCKO) library represents a transformative advance in our ability to conduct high-throughput, loss-of-function genetic screens in human cells. Our results demonstrate that Cas9-mediated cleavage, guided by a complex pool of single guide RNAs (sgRNAs), efficiently induces targeted indels across the genome, facilitating the reliable identification of essential genes and genes conferring resistance to specific selective pressures, such as vemurafenib in melanoma cells. Compared to RNA interference (RNAi), which often suffers from incomplete knockdown and significant off-target effects, the GeCKO system provides complete gene ablation, yielding clearer and more robust phenotypic readouts. This technology greatly accelerates functional genomics, providing an accessible and highly scalable platform for unbiased discovery of genetic dependencies, drug targets, and cellular pathways across diverse biological contexts.
