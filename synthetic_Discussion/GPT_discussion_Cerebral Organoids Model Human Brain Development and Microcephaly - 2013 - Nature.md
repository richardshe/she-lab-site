Here we describe a method for generating complex 3D brain tissue from human pluripotent stem cells. These cerebral organoids develop large neuroepithelial regions and form discrete brain identities, including dorsal and ventral forebrain, choroid plexus, retina, and meninges-like tissues. Importantly, these regions are not randomly interspersed, but rather appear as organized domains that can influence one another, as illustrated by interneuron migration from ventral to dorsal forebrain regions. Thus, cerebral organoids provide a system in which early regional specification and local tissue interactions can occur in vitro.

The cerebral cortical regions of organoids recapitulate several fundamental features of mammalian corticogenesis, including radial glial organization, interkinetic nuclear migration, apical mitosis, generation of intermediate progenitors, and the production of neurons with rudimentary layer identity. Furthermore, organoids display features that are especially characteristic of the developing human cortex, namely an OSVZ-like region containing abundant oRG cells separated from the ventricular zone by a fiber layer. These findings suggest that intrinsic human developmental programs are sufficient to generate aspects of cortical organization that are poorly represented in rodent models.

Finally, we demonstrate the utility of cerebral organoids for modeling neurodevelopmental disease. Patient-derived CDK5RAP2 mutant organoids showed reduced neuroepithelial expansion, altered spindle orientation, and premature neuronal differentiation. Rescue and RNAi experiments support a specific role for CDK5RAP2 in maintaining early neural progenitor expansion. Although organoids lack vasculature and do not fully reproduce mature cortical architecture, this system offers a tractable human model for investigating brain development and disorders of brain size.
