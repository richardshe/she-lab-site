We have shown that a single genetically encoded microbial opsin can be used to control mammalian neuronal activity with the temporal precision required for manipulating individual action potentials and synaptic events. Lentiviral expression of ChR2 produced large, rapidly activating inward currents in hippocampal neurons, without the need for exogenous chemical cofactors during recording. These currents were sufficient to drive spikes with millisecond latency and low temporal jitter, and repeated brief light pulses could impose extended spike trains with a reliability appropriate for probing neural coding. Thus ChR2 provides a direct means of converting optical pulse trains into defined patterns of neuronal activity.

The speed of ChR2 distinguishes this approach from earlier genetically encoded methods for optical control. Previous strategies have been powerful for changing the excitability or biochemical state of cells over relatively slow timescales, but the elementary signals of neural computation occur on the millisecond scale. By using a light-gated ion channel rather than a multi-component signaling pathway, ChR2 allows optical stimulation to enter the same temporal regime as action potential initiation and synaptic release. The observation that steady illumination produced only an initially well-timed spike further emphasizes that the useful mode of operation is not sustained depolarization, but rapidly switched illumination. In this regime, the fast rising phase of the ChR2 conductance repeatedly recruits the intrinsic spike-generating machinery of the neuron.

Several features of this system should make it broadly useful for circuit analysis. First, ChR2 expression was compatible with normal resting membrane properties, excitability and cell survival, both before and after repeated light exposure. Second, a single optical protocol could elicit similar spike patterns across neurons with heterogeneous passive electrical properties, suggesting that the threshold nonlinearity of action potential generation can normalize modest differences in expression level and membrane physiology. Third, light pulse duration and intensity provide simple experimental parameters for tuning stimulation strength. These properties should allow investigators to activate genetically or anatomically defined populations without the physical invasiveness of electrodes and without requiring cell-by-cell adjustment in many applications.

The present results also indicate important constraints. ChR2-mediated spiking was most reliable at lower stimulation frequencies, and spike probability decreased at higher frequencies. This limitation probably reflects a combination of channel inactivation, membrane refractoriness and the finite kinetics of spike recovery. Similarly, subthreshold depolarizations, although reliable in amplitude, are expected to be more sensitive than spikes to expression level, membrane resistance and illumination geometry because they do not benefit from an all-or-none threshold. These considerations will be especially important when adapting the method to intact tissue, where light scattering, differential viral expression and dendritic cable properties may alter the effective photocurrent in individual cells.

Despite these limitations, the ability to evoke both excitatory and inhibitory postsynaptic responses by photostimulating ChR2-expressing presynaptic neurons suggests immediate applications in synaptic and network physiology. ChR2 should make it possible to ask whether precisely timed activity in a defined presynaptic population is sufficient to recruit downstream firing, to induce plasticity, or to alter ongoing circuit dynamics. Because optical stimulation can be patterned arbitrarily, the same preparation can be challenged with regular trains, sparse events or naturalistic spike sequences. This flexibility should help bridge the gap between descriptive measurements of neural activity and causal tests of the temporal patterns that carry information.

Future development of this approach will likely involve targeting ChR2 to specific neuronal classes, subcellular compartments and intact circuits. Cell-type-specific promoters, viral vectors and transgenic strategies could restrict expression to neurons defined by molecular identity, projection target or developmental origin. Improvements in light delivery and optical instrumentation should further extend the method to complex preparations and behaving animals. In combination with electrical or optical readout of neural activity, ChR2-based stimulation offers a genetically specified actuator for testing how identified neurons contribute to circuit function. The experiments presented here establish that light can be used not merely to observe neurons, but to drive their fundamental electrical events with millisecond precision.
