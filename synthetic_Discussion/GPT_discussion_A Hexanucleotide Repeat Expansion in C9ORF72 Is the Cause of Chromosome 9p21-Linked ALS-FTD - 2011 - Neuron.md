We have identified a large GGGGCC hexanucleotide repeat expansion within the first intron of C9ORF72 as the genetic lesion responsible for chromosome 9p21-linked ALS-FTD. This repeat expansion was discovered by deep resequencing of the minimal associated interval and was subsequently shown to segregate with disease in multiple families previously linked to this region. The expansion was absent from unaffected family members in the kindreds examined and was present at a strikingly high frequency in both familial and apparently sporadic ALS in the Finnish population. Furthermore, the same mutation was detected in a large proportion of familial ALS cases of European descent and in Finnish patients with FTD, thereby establishing C9ORF72 repeat expansion as the most common genetic cause of ALS and ALS-FTD identified to date.

The data presented here resolve a long-standing question in the genetics of ALS and FTD. Linkage studies had repeatedly implicated chromosome 9p21 in families with ALS, FTD, or a combination of both phenotypes, and genome-wide association studies had independently identified the same region as the strongest common signal for ALS. Previous haplotype analyses narrowed the disease interval and demonstrated the existence of a shared founder haplotype, but conventional sequencing of coding regions had failed to reveal a causative point mutation. The discovery of a noncoding repeat expansion explains why the lesion escaped standard approaches, while also unifying linkage, association, and family-based segregation data within a single genetic mechanism. The repeat expansion lies near the 5' end of C9ORF72, within an intronic region common to major transcripts, and its position is consistent with the possibility that it alters gene expression, RNA processing, or produces a toxic RNA species.

The population frequency of the expansion is remarkable. In Finland, the mutation accounts for nearly half of familial ALS and approximately one-fifth of apparently sporadic ALS. Together with the known enrichment of the D90A SOD1 mutation in this population, these findings indicate that the majority of familial ALS in Finland can now be attributed to simple monogenic causes. The presence of the C9ORF72 repeat expansion in sporadic cases is also important. Some of these patients may reflect unrecognized familial disease, small family size, premature death of relatives, or reduced penetrance. However, the high frequency observed suggests that genetic testing for this expansion will have immediate relevance beyond classical multiplex families. The identification of the expansion in more than one-third of outbred European familial ALS cases further demonstrates that this is not solely a Finnish founder phenomenon, although the shared haplotype argues that many European cases may derive from an ancestral mutation.

Our results also provide compelling genetic support for the concept that ALS and FTD are clinically distinct manifestations of a common neurodegenerative disease spectrum. The C9ORF72 repeat expansion was identified in families containing both ALS and FTD, in isolated ALS cases, and in Finnish patients presenting with FTD. Among the Finnish FTD expansion carriers, clinical presentations included both behavioral-variant FTD and progressive nonfluent aphasia, and a substantial fraction had a personal or family history of ALS. These observations are consistent with neuropathological studies showing TDP-43-positive inclusions in both disorders and suggest that the same molecular lesion can produce degeneration in motor, frontal, and temporal neural systems. The basis for this clinical variability remains unknown. It may reflect genetic modifiers, repeat length, somatic instability, environmental exposures, or stochastic differences in neuronal vulnerability.

The repeat-primed PCR assay used in this study provides a rapid and robust method for detecting expanded alleles, but it does not determine the true size of large expansions. The assay can classify individuals into expanded and nonexpanded groups, and the separation between affected expansion carriers and control alleles was clear in our cohorts. Nevertheless, FISH analysis indicated that the repeat expansion may comprise at least several hundred repeat units in affected individuals, far beyond the range measurable by repeat-primed PCR. Accurate sizing of the expansion will require Southern blotting or other methods capable of resolving large, GC-rich repeat regions. Such studies will be necessary to determine whether repeat length correlates with age at onset, phenotype, penetrance, disease duration, or tissue-specific pathology.

Although the genetic evidence that the C9ORF72 repeat expansion causes disease is strong, the pathogenic mechanism remains to be determined. Several models may be considered. First, the expansion could reduce expression of one or more C9ORF72 transcripts, resulting in haploinsufficiency. The location of the repeat near the promoter and within the first intron is compatible with altered transcription initiation, elongation, splicing, or chromatin state. Our preliminary expression analyses did not provide conclusive evidence for a consistent reduction in C9ORF72 RNA in frontal cortex, and immunocytochemical studies of patient fibroblasts suggested possible reduction and relocalization of C9ORF72 protein but were limited by small sample size and antibody specificity. Thus, whether loss of C9ORF72 function contributes to disease remains unresolved.

A second possibility is that the expanded repeat gives rise to a toxic RNA gain of function. Several repeat expansion disorders are caused by accumulation of abnormal repeat-containing RNA, which can form nuclear foci and sequester RNA-binding proteins. The high GC content and repetitive nature of the C9ORF72 expansion make this an attractive hypothesis. If such RNA foci are present in patient tissue, they may disrupt RNA metabolism in vulnerable neurons and glia. A third possibility is that the expansion could promote repeat-associated non-ATG translation, generating abnormal dipeptide repeat proteins. This mechanism has not yet been demonstrated for this locus, but the location and structure of the expansion justify investigation of unconventional translation products. These mechanisms are not mutually exclusive, and disease may arise through a combination of decreased C9ORF72 function and toxic effects mediated by expanded repeat RNA or repeat-derived products.

The identification of this repeat expansion has immediate implications for clinical genetics. The high frequency of the mutation in familial ALS, ALS-FTD, and FTD indicates that diagnostic testing for C9ORF72 expansions should be considered in patients with these phenotypes, particularly when there is a family history of either motor neuron disease or frontotemporal dementia. At the same time, counseling will require caution. Penetrance has not been fully defined, repeat length has not yet been accurately measured in large numbers of individuals, and the expansion was observed at low frequency in control samples. Longitudinal study of asymptomatic carriers and careful phenotyping of families will be needed to clarify age-dependent penetrance, clinical variability, and the predictive value of repeat size or haplotype background.

This discovery also creates a direct route toward disease modeling and therapeutic development. Cellular and animal models carrying the expanded repeat will be required to determine whether disease is mediated by loss of C9ORF72, toxic RNA, abnormal repeat-associated proteins, or a combination of mechanisms. Such models should also allow testing of antisense oligonucleotides, RNA-targeting strategies, and approaches designed to restore normal C9ORF72 expression. The large effect size and high prevalence of this mutation make it an especially important target for translational studies.

In summary, a GGGGCC repeat expansion in C9ORF72 accounts for a major proportion of chromosome 9p21-linked ALS, ALS-FTD, and FTD. This finding explains the strongest known genetic association signal for ALS, establishes a common molecular basis for clinically overlapping ALS and FTD syndromes, and substantially expands the fraction of disease attributable to identifiable genetic causes. Future work defining the size, stability, penetrance, and cellular consequences of the expansion will be essential for understanding disease pathogenesis and for developing targeted therapies for this devastating neurodegenerative disease spectrum.
