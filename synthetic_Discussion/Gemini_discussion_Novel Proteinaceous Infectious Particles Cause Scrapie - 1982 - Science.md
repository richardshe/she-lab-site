# Discussion

The discovery that the scrapie agent is resistant to procedures that alter nucleic acids but susceptible to those that denature proteins marks a profound departure from the accepted paradigms of infectious disease. Our results indicate that this agent depends upon a protein for its infectivity, yet lacks a nucleic acid genome that can be inactivated by standard methodologies such as nuclease digestion, ultraviolet irradiation, or exposure to alkali. Because these novel properties cleanly differentiate the scrapie agent from viruses, plasmids, and viroids, we introduce the term "prion" to denote a small, proteinaceous infectious particle that resists inactivation by most procedures known to modify nucleic acids.

The failure to identify a nucleic acid genome within the prion raises profound questions regarding its replication and transmission. If prions indeed lack nucleic acid, they must replicate through mechanisms fundamentally distinct from all known infectious agents. Several theoretical models might account for this replication. A host gene might encode the prion protein, and the infectious prion could simply induce its own expression or trigger a conformational change in an already existing host precursor. Alternatively, a small, yet undiscovered nucleic acid might be intimately bound and protected by the prion protein, though our exhaustive physical and chemical treatments argue against this unless the protection is extraordinarily robust. Another possibility is that the prion represents an abnormal, self-replicating structural form of a normal cellular protein.

The marked hydrophobicity of the prion protein, as evidenced by its requirement for detergents during purification and its behavior in gel electrophoresis and density gradients, suggests it interacts extensively with cellular membranes. This property likely accounts for the significant heterogeneity in the agent's size, which has historically confounded purification efforts. By recognizing and mitigating this hydrophobicity, we have achieved a substantial purification, allowing for more precise biochemical characterization. The smallest infectious form of the prion appears to have a molecular weight of 50,000 or less, further distinguishing it from conventional viruses.

These findings also carry significant implications for our understanding of human neurodegenerative diseases. The striking neuropathological and clinical similarities between experimental Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD) in goats and natural scrapie, along with the parallel epidemiological enigma of kuru, strongly suggest these diseases share a common etiology rooted in prion infection. The experimental transmission of scrapie via cannibalism in hamsters, albeit inefficient, directly mirrors the proposed spread of kuru among the Fore people of New Guinea. The unique properties of prions might also shed light on the pathogenesis of other degenerative disorders, such as Alzheimer's disease, where conventional viral etiologies have eluded detection. 

In conclusion, the identification of the prion as a fundamentally new class of infectious agent demands a reevaluation of our basic concepts of infectious disease. Future research must inevitably focus on defining the primary structure of the prion protein, elucidating its mechanism of replication, and investigating its potential role in a broader spectrum of degenerative neurological diseases. The resilience and uniqueness of this agent promise to uncover new principles of macromolecular biology and pathogenesis.
