In this phase 1 study, STI571, an oral inhibitor of the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase, was well tolerated and had substantial antileukemic activity in patients with chronic-phase CML in whom treatment with interferon alfa had failed or who were unable to tolerate interferon alfa. Although the primary objective of the study was to define safety and tolerability, a maximal tolerated dose was not reached, and the clinical responses observed at doses of 300 mg or more per day were striking. Nearly all patients treated at these doses had complete hematologic responses, usually within the first month of therapy, and more than half had cytogenetic responses during follow-up.

The rapidity and frequency of the hematologic responses are notable because the study population consisted of patients whose disease had not been adequately controlled by standard medical therapy. In most patients, leukocytosis and thrombocytosis resolved without the use of other cytoreductive agents. The persistence of complete hematologic responses in the majority of patients, with a median follow-up of approximately nine months among those receiving 300 mg or more per day, suggests that inhibition of BCR-ABL can produce sustained control of the proliferative component of chronic-phase CML. Two patients had progression or relapse, indicating that suppression of kinase activity may not be sufficient in all cases and that resistant subclones or more advanced biologic features may already have been present in some patients.

The cytogenetic responses provide further evidence that STI571 acts on the leukemic clone rather than merely reducing the peripheral white-cell count. Major cytogenetic responses occurred in 31 percent of patients treated with 300 mg or more per day, and complete cytogenetic remissions occurred in seven patients. In some of these patients, BCR-ABL was no longer detected by fluorescence in situ hybridization or by polymerase-chain-reaction analysis. These findings compare favorably with the expected results in patients in whom interferon alfa has failed, although direct comparisons cannot be made in the absence of a randomized control group. The time course of cytogenetic improvement, which occurred over several months, is consistent with progressive replacement of Ph-positive hematopoiesis by Ph-negative cells after selective inhibition of the leukemic clone.

The pharmacokinetic and biologic data support the choice of daily doses of at least 300 mg. Plasma concentrations achieved with 400 mg once daily exceeded concentrations required in vitro to inhibit BCR-ABL-dependent phosphorylation and to kill BCR-ABL-positive cells. In addition, analysis of CRKL phosphorylation in peripheral-blood cells showed dose-dependent inhibition of BCR-ABL kinase activity, with prominent inhibition at doses associated with clinical responses. Thus, the clinical activity of STI571 is linked to inhibition of the molecular abnormality that is central to the pathogenesis of CML.

Treatment was associated mainly with mild or moderate adverse effects, including nausea, myalgias, edema, and diarrhea. Hematologic toxic effects were observed, particularly at doses of 200 mg or more, but these effects were generally manageable and may reflect, in part, suppression of Ph-positive hematopoiesis in patients whose marrow was heavily replaced by leukemic cells. Hepatic enzyme abnormalities and rash required interruption or discontinuation of therapy in a small number of patients. The absence of severe nonhematologic toxicity at the highest doses tested is consistent with the relative specificity of STI571 for a limited group of tyrosine kinases and distinguishes this approach from conventional cytotoxic therapy.

Several limitations of this study should be emphasized. The trial was not randomized, the number of patients in each dose cohort was small, and the data represent an interim analysis of an ongoing study. Longer follow-up is required to determine the durability of hematologic and cytogenetic responses and whether treatment with STI571 delays progression to accelerated-phase disease or blast crisis. It will also be important to define mechanisms of resistance, to determine whether molecular remissions can be achieved more frequently or sustained, and to compare STI571 directly with established therapies.

Despite these limitations, the results show that a drug designed to inhibit a specific molecular lesion in cancer can produce substantial clinical benefit with limited toxicity. CML is uniquely suited to this approach because BCR-ABL is present in nearly all patients and is required for leukemogenesis. The activity of STI571 in this study provides clinical confirmation of the essential role of BCR-ABL kinase activity in CML and supports further evaluation of this agent as therapy for chronic-phase disease, including its use earlier in the course of illness and in combination or sequence with other treatments.
