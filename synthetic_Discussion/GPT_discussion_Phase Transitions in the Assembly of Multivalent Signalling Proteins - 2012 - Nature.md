Our findings show that weak interactions among multivalent macromolecules can drive sharp transitions from small soluble complexes to concentrated, dynamic liquid phases containing polymeric networks. This behaviour provides a physical mechanism by which molecular-scale binding events can be converted into micrometre-scale cellular organization. The dependence of the transition on valency, affinity and stoichiometry, together with the ability of monovalent ligands to suppress assembly, indicates that phase separation is a direct consequence of network formation rather than nonspecific aggregation.

The nephrin-NCK-N-WASP system illustrates how such transitions can be coupled to biological regulation. Phosphorylation of nephrin increases the effective valency of the complex and shifts the phase boundary into a concentration range relevant to cells. Thus, kinase activity can regulate not only the recruitment of binding partners but also the physical state of the assembled signalling machinery. The resulting condensed phase concentrates N-WASP and promotes Arp2/3-dependent actin assembly, suggesting that phase transitions can function as biochemical switches.

Because many signalling, RNA-binding and scaffold proteins contain repeated modular interaction elements connected by flexible linkers, similar transitions may be widespread. Regulated phase separation may therefore provide a general strategy for organizing cellular reactions in space and time while preserving rapid molecular exchange.
