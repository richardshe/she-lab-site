The isolation of a novel T-lymphotropic retrovirus from a patient exhibiting signs of pre-AIDS marks a critical advancement in understanding the etiology of Acquired Immune Deficiency Syndrome. This virus, distinguished from previously known human T-cell leukemia viruses (HTLV-I and II) by its unique immunological and morphological characteristics, exhibits a pronounced tropism for human T-lymphocytes. The detection of reverse transcriptase activity and the presence of retroviral particles in the patient's cultured cells provide compelling evidence of viral infection. Given the profound immunodeficiency characterizing AIDS, the identification of a retrovirus targeting the very immune cells depleted in the disease strongly suggests a causal relationship. Further epidemiological and molecular studies are urgently needed to confirm its role as the primary etiological agent of AIDS.
