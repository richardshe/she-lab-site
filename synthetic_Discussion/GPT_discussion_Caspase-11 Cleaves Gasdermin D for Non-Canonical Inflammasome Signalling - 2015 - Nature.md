Our genetic and biochemical data identify gasdermin D as the critical effector through which caspase-11 executes non-canonical inflammasome signalling. A recessive ENU mutation in Gsdmd, as well as targeted deletion of Gsdmd, abolished pyroptosis, caspase-1 processing and IL-1β secretion in response to cytoplasmic LPS and Gram-negative bacteria, while leaving TLR priming and canonical inflammasome responses largely intact. Thus Gsdmd functions downstream of LPS sensing by caspase-11 and upstream of both membrane rupture and NLRP3-dependent cytokine maturation.

Mechanistically, caspase-11 directly cleaves Gsdmd at a conserved aspartate residue to generate an amino-terminal fragment that is sufficient to induce cell death. The inability of the D276A cleavage mutant to restore LPS responsiveness demonstrates that proteolytic maturation of Gsdmd is not merely correlative, but is required for non-canonical inflammasome signalling. The I105N mutation further indicates that the amino-terminal region contains essential determinants of Gsdmd activity. These findings support a model in which full-length Gsdmd is an inactive precursor whose carboxy-terminal region restrains a latent pro-pyroptotic amino-terminal domain until caspase-mediated cleavage releases this activity.

Gsdmd-dependent activation of NLRP3 appears to be cell intrinsic rather than a consequence of paracrine stimulation by pyroptotic cell products. How Gsdmd p30 engages the NLRP3 pathway remains to be determined, but membrane damage or ion flux induced by the amino-terminal fragment may provide the relevant trigger. The conservation of GSDMD cleavage by human caspase-4 and caspase-5 further suggests that this mechanism is a central mammalian host-defence pathway against cytosolic LPS. Finally, resistance of Gsdmd-/- mice to lethal endotoxemia establishes Gsdmd as a key in vivo mediator of caspase-11-driven sepsis.
