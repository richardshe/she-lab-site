The results described here show that a defined human genomic sequence can be amplified sufficiently to permit direct restriction site analysis on very small amounts of DNA. By combining primer-mediated amplification of a 110-bp segment of the beta-globin gene with oligomer restriction analysis, the beta^A and beta^S alleles can be distinguished without the need for Southern transfer, cloned probes, or the analysis of linked polymorphic markers. The genotypes assigned by this procedure were in agreement with those obtained by conventional Mst II restriction fragment analysis, and the autoradiographic signals obtained after 20 cycles of amplification were strong enough to allow scoring of the samples after short exposures.

The principal advantage of this method is the increase in sensitivity produced by enzymatic amplification before allele analysis. Under the conditions used, 20 cycles of PCR resulted in an estimated 220,000-fold increase in the amount of beta-globin target sequence. This permits the analysis of a small fraction of an amplified sample and indicates that DNA quantities far below those ordinarily required for Southern blotting can be used. The ability to determine genotype from 20 ng of genomic DNA, with the likelihood that still smaller samples could be examined by using a larger proportion of the amplification reaction, is of particular importance for prenatal diagnosis. Chorionic villus sampling and amniocentesis may yield limited amounts of fetal material, and a procedure requiring less DNA reduces the constraints imposed by sample size.

The second important feature of the method is that the amplified DNA is examined by a restriction assay in solution. The oligomer restriction procedure depends on the formation of a probe-target duplex in which a single base substitution abolishes cleavage at the Dde I site. The use of the adjacent invariant Hinf I site provides an internal alternative cleavage product, so that both alleles give positive and distinguishable signals. Thus, an AA sample produces the 8-nt fragment, an SS sample produces the 3-nt fragment, and an AS sample produces both. This design avoids reliance on the absence of a band alone and makes the interpretation of heterozygous and homozygous samples straightforward.

Some low-level background products were observed. In AA samples, a faint 3-nt fragment was present in addition to the expected 8-nt product. This may reflect incomplete Dde I digestion or retention of the 8-nt fragment in the probe-target duplex long enough to allow subsequent cleavage by Hinf I. In SS samples, a faint 8-nt fragment was detected. Evidence from digestion of the template DNA with Mbo I before amplification indicates that this signal is due to amplification of homologous delta-globin sequences, which retain the Dde I site and can hybridize weakly to the RS06 probe. Although these background bands did not prevent correct genotype assignment, they show that amplification primers and diagnostic probes must be chosen with attention to closely related sequences in the genome. For clinical application, the assay conditions should be standardized to maintain a large difference between the allele-specific signal and any cross-hybridizing background.

The time required for the combined PCR and OR procedure is also a substantial improvement over existing methods. With 20 amplification cycles and a short autoradiographic exposure, a diagnosis can be made in less than 1 day, and in favorable cases in less than 10 hours. Conventional restriction fragment analysis requires more DNA and several additional steps, including electrophoresis of genomic fragments, transfer to a membrane, and hybridization. The present method therefore reduces both the amount of starting material and the time between specimen collection and interpretation.

This approach should not be limited to sickle cell anemia. Any inherited or acquired sequence change that creates or destroys a restriction site, or that can be placed within a restriction site by the design of an appropriate probe, should in principle be amenable to the same analysis after amplification. More generally, amplification of selected DNA segments followed by specific probe-based detection may be useful for the diagnosis of other genetic diseases and for the detection of infectious agents present in small amounts. The combination of specificity conferred by oligonucleotide primers, sensitivity conferred by repeated enzymatic copying, and allele discrimination conferred by the oligomer restriction assay provides a rapid and direct method for DNA diagnosis.
