Prime editing establishes a versatile approach for installing precise genetic changes without requiring double-strand DNA breaks or exogenous donor DNA templates. By coupling Cas9 nickase-mediated target recognition to pegRNA-programmed reverse transcription, prime editors directly write new sequence information into genomic DNA. This strategy supports all 12 base-to-base conversions, targeted insertions, targeted deletions, and combinations of these edits, substantially expanding the scope of precision genome editing beyond the capabilities of current base editors and DSB-initiated homology-directed repair.

Several features of prime editing are likely to contribute to its broad utility. First, the edit is encoded by the pegRNA, allowing the same editor protein to mediate many classes of sequence change. Second, the method does not depend on HDR and therefore can operate in cell types in which donor-template repair is inefficient, including post-mitotic primary neurons. Third, because prime editing requires target binding, primer binding, and productive flap resolution, the system can show very low off-target editing at known Cas9 off-target sites. These properties distinguish prime editing from nuclease-based editing, which frequently generates indels and other DSB-associated byproducts, and from base editing, which can be limited by editing windows, bystander nucleotides, and the chemical scope of available deaminases.

Prime editing nevertheless remains an early-stage technology with important limitations. Editing efficiencies vary substantially with genomic locus, cell type, edit position, PBS length, RT template length, and nicking strategy. PE3 can increase editing levels but can also elevate indels, whereas PE3b offers a useful strategy to reduce indel formation when the desired edit creates a selectively nickable allele. The mechanistic determinants of productive flap resolution, pegRNA stability, reverse transcription termination, and repair of the edited heteroduplex remain incompletely understood. Improved pegRNA design rules, editor architectures, delivery systems, and cellular repair modulation should further increase editing efficiency, product purity, and applicability across primary cells and tissues.

The ability of prime editing to correct or install disease-relevant transversions, insertions, and deletions in human cells illustrates its potential for studying and eventually treating genetic disease. Because most pathogenic variants are not simple transition mutations, a DSB-free technology that can precisely install the majority of sequence changes represents an important advance. Continued evaluation of genome-wide off-target effects, RNA or transcriptome perturbations, delivery constraints, and long-term cellular consequences will be essential before therapeutic use. More broadly, prime editing provides a programmable search-and-replace framework for genome manipulation and a foundation for future precision editing technologies.
