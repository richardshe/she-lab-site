Our results identify cGAS as a cytosolic DNA sensor that links the detection of foreign DNA to the activation of the type I interferon pathway. cGAS binds to DNA in the cytoplasm and catalyzes the synthesis of cGAMP from ATP and GTP. cGAMP then functions as a second messenger that activates STING, leading to IRF3 activation and induction of IFN-beta. Thus, the DNA-sensing pathway contains an enzyme that converts the presence of cytosolic DNA into a diffusible cyclic dinucleotide signal.

Several lines of evidence support the conclusion that cGAS is the enzyme responsible for cGAMP production in response to DNA. First, cGAS copurified with the cGAMP-synthesizing activity through three independent purification routes. Second, overexpression of cGAS, but not several other putative DNA sensors, led to robust cGAMP production and IFN-beta induction in a STING-dependent manner. Third, mutations of conserved residues in the nucleotidyltransferase domain abolished the ability of cGAS to activate IRF3. Finally, purified recombinant cGAS catalyzed the synthesis of cGAMP from ATP and GTP, and this reaction was stimulated by DNA but not RNA. These results demonstrate that cGAS is not merely an adaptor or regulatory factor, but a bona fide DNA-activated cyclase.

cGAS appears to have an essential role in the cellular response to cytosolic DNA. Knockdown of cGAS in mouse and human cells strongly inhibited IRF3 activation and IFN-beta induction by transfected DNA and by DNA viruses, including HSV-1, vaccinia virus, or both. In contrast, depletion of cGAS did not impair responses to Sendai virus, which activates the RNA-sensing pathway. Moreover, cGAS depletion markedly reduced the production of cGAMP induced by DNA transfection or DNA virus infection. These findings place cGAS upstream of STING and indicate that cGAS is a major source of cGAMP in the DNA-triggered interferon pathway.

The identification of cGAS helps explain how structurally diverse DNAs can stimulate a common innate immune response. cGAS was activated by several DNA species and bound to DNA through its N-terminal region, whereas the conserved NTase and Mab21 domains were required for catalytic activity. This organization suggests that DNA binding and cyclic dinucleotide synthesis are coupled within one protein. The relative lack of sequence conservation in portions of the N terminus may allow variation in DNA recognition or regulation among species, whereas the conserved catalytic domains maintain the core function of cGAMP synthesis.

The discovery of cGAS also raises several questions. It remains to be determined how DNA binding activates the catalytic domain of cGAS, how cGAS distinguishes cytosolic DNA from self DNA under physiological conditions, and how cGAS activity is regulated to prevent inappropriate interferon production. Because abnormal responses to self DNA are associated with inflammatory and autoimmune diseases, the cGAS-cGAMP-STING pathway may have broad relevance to host defense and immune pathology.
