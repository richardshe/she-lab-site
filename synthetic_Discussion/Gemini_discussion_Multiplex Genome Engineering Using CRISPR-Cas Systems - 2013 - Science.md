In this study, we have successfully repurposed the type II CRISPR/Cas system from Streptococcus pyogenes to serve as a versatile and robust platform for precise genome engineering in mammalian cells. By defining a minimal three-component system comprising the Cas9 nuclease, tracrRNA, and pre-crRNA, we demonstrated efficient and site-specific cleavage of endogenous loci in both human and mouse genomes. Unlike existing technologies such as zinc finger nucleases and TALENs, which require complex protein engineering for each new target, our RNA-guided approach is easily programmable simply by altering the short spacer sequence within the crRNA array.

Crucially, the conversion of the Cas9 nuclease into a DNA nickase (Cas9n) enables high-fidelity homology-directed repair while minimizing the occurrence of mutagenic nonhomologous end joining events. This enhanced precision is vital for minimizing off-target effects in therapeutic and reverse genetics applications. Furthermore, we showed that the natural architecture of the CRISPR array allows for multiplexed targeting, facilitating simultaneous modifications at multiple genomic loci or targeted deletions of larger regions. Taken together, this transformative CRISPR/Cas technology offers a highly scalable, affordable, and accessible toolkit that will broadly accelerate biological research and therapeutic development.
