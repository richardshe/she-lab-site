Our findings identify tumour-specific mutant antigens as dominant targets of T cells mobilized by checkpoint blockade immunotherapy. By combining exome/cDNA sequencing, epitope prediction, tetramer-based detection, functional T-cell assays and direct mass spectrometric validation of MHC-associated peptides, we found that two H-2Kb-restricted mutant epitopes, derived from Lama4 and Alg8, account for major CD8+ T-cell responses associated with rejection of d42m1-T3 sarcomas. These antigens were not shared by an unrelated sarcoma, were not detected as corresponding wild-type peptides, and stimulated T cells in a mutation-specific manner. Thus, checkpoint blockade can unmask effective immunity directed against private somatic mutations that distinguish the tumour from normal tissues.

The observation that mLama4- and mAlg8-specific T cells were present in progressively growing tumours before therapy indicates that failure of tumour control was not due simply to absence of relevant tumour-reactive T cells. Rather, these cells persisted in a dysfunctional state that could be altered by blockade of PD-1 and/or CTLA-4. Anti-PD-1, anti-CTLA-4 and the combination each produced tumour rejection, but they did so through partially distinct transcriptional and functional programmes in antigen-specific TIL. Anti-PD-1 was associated prominently with metabolic and type I interferon-related changes, whereas anti-CTLA-4 enhanced proliferative, NFAT/JAK-STAT and effector pathways. Combined treatment produced the strongest induction of cytolytic and inflammatory molecules. These results suggest that different checkpoint receptors restrain tumour-specific T cells through non-identical mechanisms, and that therapeutic combinations may act by converging on a shared outcome while engaging distinct cellular pathways.

A further implication of this work is that mutant epitopes identified as targets of checkpoint blockade can be converted into effective therapeutic vaccines. Synthetic long peptides containing mLama4 and mAlg8, administered with poly(I:C), induced rejection of established tumours with efficacy comparable to anti-PD-1 treatment and protected mice in a tumour-specific manner. The superiority of long peptides over minimal epitopes is consistent with the requirement for antigen processing and efficient presentation by professional antigen-presenting cells. These data provide experimental support for personalized vaccination strategies based on expressed somatic mutations.

Several issues remain unresolved. The sarcomas studied here contain many mutations and are responsive to checkpoint blockade; tumours with lower mutational burdens or impaired antigen presentation may behave differently. In addition, only a small number of predicted high-affinity epitopes proved immunogenic, underscoring the need for improved methods to predict naturally processed, therapeutically relevant neoantigens. Nevertheless, these results establish a direct mechanistic link between checkpoint blockade, mutant-antigen-specific T cells and tumour rejection, and provide a framework for using cancer genomics to guide individualized immunotherapy.
