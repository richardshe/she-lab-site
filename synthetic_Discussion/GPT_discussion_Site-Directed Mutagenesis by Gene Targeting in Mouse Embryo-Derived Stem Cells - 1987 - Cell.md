We have shown that an endogenous mammalian gene can be altered in a predetermined manner by homologous recombination between chromosomal sequences and DNA introduced into cultured cells. The experiments described here differ from earlier demonstrations of gene correction or gene disruption in that the target was not an experimentally introduced marker gene, but the native mouse Hprt gene. Thus, the results establish that cloned genomic sequences can be used to direct a defined mutation into the corresponding chromosomal locus. In the present case, insertion of the neo gene into exon 8 of Hprt abolished HPRT activity and conferred resistance to 6-TG. The diagnostic restriction patterns and the absence of detectable HPRT activity in independently isolated clones show that the selectable phenotype resulted from modification of the Hprt locus rather than from random integration of the vector accompanied by an unrelated Hprt mutation.

A central feature of these experiments was the use of ES cells as the recipient cell type. These cells can be propagated, selected, and cloned in vitro, yet retain the developmental potential to contribute to chimeric animals and to the germ line. The ability to carry out gene targeting in such cells therefore provides a route by which mutations designed in vitro may be introduced into the mouse. Since male ES cells carry a single X-linked Hprt gene, the loss of HPRT function provided a convenient selection for targeted events. However, the importance of the system is not limited to Hprt. The neo gene serves both as a mutagen and as a dominant selectable marker, and the observed frequency of targeting indicates that comparable strategies should be applicable to loci for which no direct loss-of-function selection exists. In such cases, selection for G418 resistance followed by molecular screening should allow the recovery of cells carrying the desired chromosomal alteration.

The design of the neo cassette was an important component of the strategy. To use neo as a general tag for chromosomal modification, it must be expressed after integration into many different genomic environments. The pMC1Neo construction, containing the HSV-tk promoter, polyoma enhancer sequences, and an improved translational initiation signal, was markedly more efficient than pRSVNeo or pSV2Neo in ES cells. This difference was especially striking because the same vectors gave comparable results in fibroblasts. Thus, the regulatory sequences that function well in differentiated cells are not necessarily adequate in ES cells. The increased efficiency and colony size obtained with pMC1Neo should be particularly useful when the targeted gene is expressed weakly, as is Hprt, or is transcriptionally inactive.

The frequency of gene targeting depended strongly on the length of homology between the introduced DNA and the chromosomal locus. Among the sequence replacement vectors, increasing the homologous segment from 4.0 kb to 9.1 kb increased the fraction of G418-resistant cells that were also 6-TG-resistant from approximately 1 in 40,000 to approximately 1 in 950. A similar effect was observed with the sequence insertion vectors. These results are consistent with the expectation that longer regions of homology provide more efficient substrates for chromosomal recombination. They also suggest that the practical efficiency of targeting at other loci may be improved by using vectors containing extensive isogenic or closely related genomic sequences flanking the mutation to be introduced.

The two classes of vectors produced different kinds of targeted alleles. Sequence replacement vectors yielded replacement of the endogenous Hprt sequence by the neo-containing exogenous sequence. In contrast, sequence insertion vectors most often inserted the entire vector into the Hprt locus, thereby generating a duplication in which one copy contained the neo insertion and the other retained wild-type Hprt sequences. Nevertheless, insertion vectors could also give rise to replacement products. These findings indicate that linear DNA molecules with homologous ends can participate in more than one recombination pathway in mammalian cells. For some applications, replacement will be the desired outcome because it produces a simple mutant allele. In other cases, insertion events may be useful because they create duplicated chromosomal segments that could subsequently undergo further recombination or be exploited to introduce more complex changes.

Several features of the protocol contributed to the recovery and interpretation of targeted clones. The vectors were linearized and stripped of bacterial plasmid sequences before transfection, thereby reducing nonhomologous DNA and producing molecules favorable for recombination. Electroporation conditions were chosen to give efficient survival and predominantly single-copy integration. In addition, the selection scheme was arranged to allow expression of neo and decay of preexisting HPRT activity before exposure to 6-TG. Because ES cells grow as tight colonies and can cross-feed, dispersion during selection was necessary for reliable scoring. The appearance of bursts of G418-resistant, 6-TG-resistant colonies on individual plates indicates that most targeting events occurred at or near the time of electroporation.

The present work provides a general approach for site-directed mutagenesis in the mouse. The method should permit the introduction of null alleles, subtle coding changes, or regulatory mutations into cloned genes and then into ES cells. Once such cells are shown to retain their developmental potential, they can be used to generate chimeric animals and, through germ-line transmission, mice carrying the engineered mutation. Because the initial chimera or its progeny can be maintained as a heterozygote, even mutations that are recessive lethal should be recoverable and studied. Thus, gene targeting in ES cells offers a means to analyze gene function in development, physiology, and disease with a precision not obtainable by chemical mutagenesis or random insertional mutagenesis.
