# Discussion

The cancer stem cell hypothesis posits that to achieve long-term remission and ultimate cure in malignancies like acute myeloid leukemia (AML), therapies must effectively eradicate the rare, self-renewing subpopulation of leukemia stem cells (LSC) that drive and maintain the disease. Our study provides compelling evidence that the cell-surface molecule CD47 is not only a critical functional marker of human AML LSC but also a highly significant prognostic factor and a potent therapeutic target. We have demonstrated that CD47 is preferentially upregulated on AML LSC compared to their normal hematopoietic stem cell (HSC) and multipotent progenitor (MPP) counterparts. This differential expression strongly suggests a pathogenic role for CD47 in leukemogenesis, enabling LSC to evade innate immune surveillance by signaling through the inhibitory macrophage receptor SIRPα. By circumventing macrophage-mediated phagocytosis, AML LSC secure a crucial survival advantage within the bone marrow niche, allowing them to propagate the leukemic clone and ultimately overwhelm normal hematopoiesis.

The clinical relevance of CD47 upregulation is profoundly illustrated by our extensive prognostic analysis. Across multiple, independent cohorts of adult AML patients, encompassing diverse cytogenetic and molecular subtypes, increased CD47 mRNA expression consistently predicted worse event-free and overall survival. The prognostic power of CD47 was robust even when stratifying patients within the clinically challenging subset of normal karyotype AML. Notably, while CD47 expression correlated with the presence of the FLT3-ITD mutation—a well-established marker of poor prognosis—multivariate analysis confirmed that elevated CD47 remains an independent predictor of adverse clinical outcomes. This independence indicates that CD47-mediated immune evasion is a fundamental, perhaps convergent, mechanism of leukemic progression that operates across various mutational backgrounds. The identification of CD47 as a prognostic biomarker offers immediate clinical utility, providing physicians with an additional tool to stratify risk and tailor aggressive consolidative therapies, such as allogeneic stem cell transplantation, for patients most likely to experience disease relapse.

Beyond its prognostic value, our findings firmly establish CD47 as an actionable therapeutic target on human AML LSC. We hypothesized that disrupting the CD47-SIRPα interaction using blocking monoclonal antibodies would strip the leukemia cells of their "don't eat me" signal, thereby unleashing the phagocytic capacity of macrophages. Our in vitro experiments unequivocally supported this hypothesis; treatment with blocking anti-CD47 antibodies (B6H12.2 and BRIC126) enabled robust phagocytosis of AML LSC by both human and murine macrophages, whereas non-blocking antibodies and isotype controls had no effect. Crucially, this antibody-mediated phagocytosis was selective for leukemic cells, as normal CD34+ bone marrow cells were spared. Furthermore, our mechanistic investigations rigorously ruled out direct antibody-induced apoptosis as the primary mode of action. Unlike previously reported observations with immobilized anti-CD47 antibodies on other malignant cell types, soluble B6H12.2 did not induce Annexin V positivity or apoptosis in primary human AML LSC prior to phagocytic engulfment. This confirms that the therapeutic efficacy of our anti-CD47 blockade relies fundamentally on the restoration of macrophage-mediated innate immune clearance.

The translation of these in vitro findings to in vivo preclinical models yielded dramatic results. Using our robust xenotransplantation model of human AML LSC in NOG mice, we demonstrated that anti-CD47 antibody therapy not only clears circulating leukemic blasts but also effectively targets the bone marrow-resident leukemia stem cells. The pre-coating strategy showed that antibody engagement alone is sufficient to prevent leukemic engraftment. More importantly, the therapeutic treatment strategy, wherein antibody was administered to mice with established human AML, resulted in rapid and near-complete elimination of the leukemic burden. The profound reduction in bone marrow engraftment and the histological evidence of leukemic clearance alongside phagocytosing macrophages vividly illustrate the in vivo potency of this approach. The critical role of macrophages in executing this therapeutic effect was further validated by our macrophage depletion experiments using liposomal clodronate, which completely abrogated the antileukemic activity of the anti-CD47 antibody.

Perhaps the most significant finding regarding the therapeutic potential of anti-CD47 therapy is its ability to directly target and eliminate the LSC compartment. Eradicating the bulk blast population is often achievable with standard induction chemotherapy, but relapse occurs because the quiescent LSC are spared. Our secondary transplantation assays provided definitive functional proof that anti-CD47 antibody treatment exhausts the AML LSC pool. Bone marrow from treated mice completely failed to engraft secondary recipients, confirming that the self-renewing capacity of the leukemia had been extinguished. This LSC-depleting capability is the hallmark of a curative therapeutic strategy and positions anti-CD47 blockade as a transformative approach in AML treatment, potentially overcoming the high relapse rates associated with current cytotoxic regimens.

A major theoretical concern with targeting a widely expressed molecule like CD47 is the potential for unacceptable off-target toxicity, particularly the destruction of normal hematopoietic cells and erythrocytes. However, our evaluation of a blocking anti-mouse CD47 antibody in a syngeneic, serially transplantable mouse model of AML provides highly encouraging safety data. Despite achieving complete antibody coating of bone marrow cells, daily administration of the anti-CD47 antibody for two weeks did not perturb overall bone marrow cellularity, HSC frequency, or cause anemia in wild-type mice. The only observed hematological toxicity was an isolated, manageable neutropenia, with no biochemical evidence of hepatic or renal damage. This favorable therapeutic window is likely due to the fact that while CD47 blockade removes a negative signal, phagocytosis generally requires the concomitant presence of positive "eat me" signals—such as calreticulin or altered glycosylation—which are often upregulated on stressed or malignant cells but absent on healthy, resting cells. Thus, the preferential phagocytosis of AML LSC by anti-CD47 therapy appears to be driven by an imbalance of these signals unique to the malignant state.

Future investigations should aim to elucidate the precise molecular mechanisms that drive CD47 upregulation in leukemic stem cells. It remains to be determined whether this overexpression is a direct consequence of specific oncogenic drivers, such as FLT3-ITD, or if it represents a generalized cellular stress response co-opted during malignant transformation. Understanding the transcriptional and epigenetic regulation of the CD47 gene in the context of normal hematopoiesis versus leukemogenesis could reveal additional nodes for therapeutic intervention. Furthermore, exploring the synergy between anti-CD47 antibodies and existing standard-of-care chemotherapies or emerging targeted agents is a critical next step. Combining innate immune checkpoint blockade with therapies that induce cellular stress and upregulate pro-phagocytic "eat me" signals could dramatically enhance LSC eradication and provide durable cures for patients suffering from this intractable disease.

In conclusion, our study identifies the CD47-SIRPα axis as a critical immune checkpoint in human AML and establishes CD47 as a powerful, independent prognostic indicator. By demonstrating that blocking anti-CD47 monoclonal antibodies can specifically enable macrophage-mediated phagocytosis and eradication of AML LSC in vivo without severe toxicity, we provide a strong preclinical rationale for the development of CD47-targeted therapies. Given that LSC share many features across different types of leukemia and potentially solid tumors, CD47 upregulation may represent a universal mechanism of immune evasion. If these findings successfully translate to the clinic, anti-CD47 antibodies could emerge as a foundational component of novel, stem cell-directed immunotherapies, offering a radically new approach to curing AML and potentially other devastating malignancies.
