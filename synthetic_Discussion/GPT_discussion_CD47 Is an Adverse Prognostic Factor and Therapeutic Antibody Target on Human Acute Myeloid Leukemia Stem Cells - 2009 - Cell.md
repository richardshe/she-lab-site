The results presented here identify CD47 as both a clinically relevant marker and a therapeutic target on human AML LSC. We found that CD47 was more highly expressed on AML LSC than on normal HSC and MPP, and that increased expression of CD47 was associated with inferior clinical outcome in three independent cohorts of adult AML patients. Moreover, disruption of the CD47-SIRP alpha interaction with blocking monoclonal antibodies enabled phagocytosis of AML LSC in vitro and eliminated leukemia in xenotransplanted mice through a macrophage-dependent mechanism. Together, these data support a model in which AML LSC exploit increased CD47 expression as a mechanism of immune evasion, and suggest that therapeutic blockade of this pathway may permit the innate immune system to recognize and eliminate the cells responsible for leukemia propagation.

Several lines of evidence indicate that CD47 expression has biological significance in AML rather than simply representing a surface marker associated with disease. First, CD47 expression was elevated on the LSC-enriched fraction of multiple AML specimens compared to the corresponding normal progenitor populations. Second, within a single specimen containing both leukemic and residual normal progenitors in the Lin -CD34+CD38 -compartment, the CD47 hi cells contained the FLT3 -ITD mutation and were part of the leukemic clone, whereas the CD47 lo cells lacked the mutation and reconstituted normal hematopoiesis. This observation suggests that increased CD47 expression can distinguish leukemic from normal progenitors in some cases, and is consistent with the hypothesis that CD47 upregulation is acquired during leukemic transformation or progression. Finally, the functional consequence of CD47 expression was demonstrated by the ability of blocking anti-CD47 antibodies to permit phagocytosis of AML LSC, while non-blocking anti-CD47 antibody and control antibodies did not.

The association of increased CD47 expression with adverse outcome further supports the importance of this pathway in human AML. High CD47 expression predicted worse overall survival in a cohort containing diverse cytogenetic and molecular subtypes, and this finding was validated in two independent cohorts of patients with normal karyotype AML. In the validation cohort, increased CD47 expression was associated with both inferior event-free survival and overall survival. Although high CD47 expression correlated with FLT3 -ITD, a known adverse prognostic marker in AML, the prognostic effect of CD47 was also observed among patients lacking FLT3 -ITD and remained significant in multivariable analysis. These findings indicate that CD47 expression provides prognostic information beyond established clinical and molecular features. Since CD47 expression is linked here to a defined mechanism of resistance to phagocytosis, the adverse clinical association may reflect a contribution of immune evasion to disease persistence, relapse, and poor survival.

The cancer stem cell model predicts that durable therapeutic responses require elimination of the self-renewing population capable of maintaining and reinitiating the disease. AML has provided the clearest experimental evidence for such a population, as human AML LSC can be prospectively enriched and assayed by transplantation into immunodeficient mice. Most conventional therapies are evaluated by their ability to reduce bulk blast burden, but relapse may result from persistence of LSC that survive treatment. Our data demonstrate that anti-CD47 antibody treatment targets the LSC compartment functionally, as measured by loss of secondary transplantability. This is a stringent assay of leukemia-propagating activity and suggests that CD47 blockade may not merely debulk leukemia, but may eliminate the cells required for disease maintenance. The reduction in CD34+ leukemia cells in treated mice is consistent with this conclusion, although the secondary transplantation assay provides the most direct evidence.

The mechanism of anti-CD47 antibody activity appears to be distinct from direct induction of apoptosis. Prior reports have shown that antibodies to CD47 can induce apoptosis in some malignant hematopoietic cells, particularly under conditions of immobilization or cross-linking. In contrast, the blocking anti-CD47 antibody used here did not induce Annexin V positivity in primary AML LSC under the conditions tested, including when plate-bound. Phagocytosis was detectable rapidly after antibody treatment, at time points preceding detectable apoptosis. In vivo, GFP-labeled human AML cells were found within mouse phagocytes shortly after anti-CD47 antibody administration, and depletion of phagocytes with liposomal clodronate impaired the ability of anti-CD47 antibody to eliminate leukemia. These findings indicate that the dominant mechanism of leukemia clearance in this system is the enabling of phagocytosis through disruption of an inhibitory signal delivered by CD47 to SIRP alpha.

The ability of anti-CD47 antibody to spare normal hematopoietic cells in these assays is an important feature of this therapeutic strategy. CD47 is expressed at low levels on most normal tissues, including hematopoietic cells, raising the possibility that systemic blockade could cause toxicity. However, blocking anti-CD47 antibodies enabled phagocytosis of AML LSC but not normal human bone marrow CD34+ cells in vitro. In addition, treatment of wild type mice with a blocking anti-mouse CD47 antibody resulted in coating of bone marrow cells without reduction in marrow cellularity, KLS cells, or HSC, and without evidence of anemia or hepatic or renal damage. Isolated neutropenia was observed, indicating that hematopoietic toxicity will require careful evaluation. Nevertheless, the differential susceptibility of AML cells and normal progenitors to phagocytosis suggests that blockade of CD47 is not sufficient by itself to trigger engulfment of all CD47-expressing cells.

One possible explanation for this selectivity is that phagocytosis depends on the balance between inhibitory signals, such as CD47, and prophagocytic signals that are more abundant on leukemia cells than on normal progenitors. In this model, increased CD47 expression allows AML LSC to counteract prophagocytic stimuli generated during transformation, thereby preserving survival in the presence of innate immune effector cells. Blocking CD47 removes this inhibitory signal and exposes leukemia cells to macrophage-mediated clearance. Normal hematopoietic cells, which may express fewer prophagocytic signals, would be less susceptible despite antibody coating. Identification of the relevant prophagocytic signals on AML LSC will be important for understanding the selectivity of CD47 blockade and may suggest rational combinations with agents that increase leukemia cell recognition.

The studies described here also have implications for the development of clinical biomarkers. Since CD47 expression can be measured by gene expression profiling or flow cytometry, it may be possible to incorporate CD47 into risk stratification algorithms, particularly for patients with normal karyotype AML. CD47 expression may also identify patients most likely to benefit from therapies targeting the CD47-SIRP alpha axis. However, the relationship between CD47 expression level and therapeutic response remains to be defined. Although high CD47 expression is associated with poor outcome and AML LSC express increased CD47 compared to normal progenitors, even cases with lower expression may remain dependent on CD47-mediated inhibition of phagocytosis. Future studies should determine whether CD47 expression, SIRP alpha genotype, macrophage abundance, or additional prophagocytic markers predict response to CD47 blockade.

Several limitations should be considered. The clinical analyses were retrospective and relied on previously reported patient cohorts, and therefore prospective validation will be necessary to establish CD47 as a prognostic biomarker in current therapeutic settings. The functional studies used xenotransplantation into immunodeficient mice, a system that permits assay of human AML LSC but does not fully recapitulate the human immune microenvironment. Moreover, the therapeutic antibodies used in these experiments were not optimized for clinical application, and antibody dose, schedule, pharmacokinetics, and potential toxicity will require further investigation. Despite these limitations, the convergence of expression, prognostic, in vitro functional, and in vivo therapeutic data provides strong support for CD47 as a biologically relevant target in AML.

In summary, increased CD47 expression on human AML LSC represents a mechanism by which leukemia cells evade phagocytosis and contributes to adverse clinical outcome. Blocking monoclonal antibodies directed against CD47 disrupt this protective signal, enable macrophage-mediated phagocytosis of AML LSC, reduce leukemic engraftment, and eliminate leukemia-propagating activity in vivo. These findings provide evidence that the innate immune checkpoint mediated by CD47 and SIRP alpha can be therapeutically targeted in AML. More broadly, they suggest that successful cancer stem cell-directed therapy may require not only inhibition of intrinsic survival and self-renewal pathways, but also reversal of immune evasion mechanisms that protect these cells from host clearance.
