# Discussion

Our study identifies a novel coronavirus, 2019-nCoV, as the etiological agent responsible for the acute respiratory syndrome outbreak in Wuhan, China. The remarkable genomic similarity between 2019-nCoV and the bat coronavirus RaTG13 (96.2% identity) provides compelling evidence that bats are the probable natural reservoir for this emerging pathogen. Despite the sequence divergence in the receptor-binding domain of the spike protein compared to SARS-CoV, our results unequivocally demonstrate that 2019-nCoV utilizes the same cellular entry receptor, human ACE2. This finding has immediate implications for understanding viral pathogenesis and highlights the potential for cross-species transmission of SARS-related coronaviruses from wildlife to humans. The rapid human-to-human transmission and the detection of the virus in patient bronchoalveolar lavage fluid underscore the critical need for robust surveillance, rapid diagnostic tests, and the development of targeted therapeutics and vaccines. While the exact intermediate host remains to be definitively identified, the emergence of 2019-nCoV serves as a stark reminder of the ongoing threat posed by zoonotic coronaviruses. Future studies must focus on elucidating the structural basis of ACE2 interaction, monitoring viral evolution, and investigating potential antiviral compounds to mitigate the impact of this unprecedented global health crisis.
