Mechanically-activated cation currents have been described in many vertebrate cell types, yet the molecular identity of the channels underlying these currents has remained elusive. Our results identify Piezo1 and Piezo2 as large multipass transmembrane proteins required for distinct MA channel activities. Loss of Piezo1 markedly reduced endogenous MA currents in N2A cells, and expression of either Piezo1 or Piezo2 was sufficient to confer robust mechanically-evoked cation currents in multiple cell lines. These observations argue that Piezos are essential components of MA channels, although additional associated proteins may modulate their localization, gating, or adaptation in native cells.

Piezo1- and Piezo2-dependent currents share several properties with previously described MA currents, including non-selective cation permeability, block by gadolinium and ruthenium red, and activation by membrane indentation or stretch. However, the two proteins generate currents with different kinetics. Piezo2 currents inactivate rapidly, whereas Piezo1 currents display slower adaptation. Thus, the diversity of MA current kinetics observed in native cells may reflect, at least in part, expression of different Piezo family members. The presence of Piezo homologs in invertebrates, plants, and protozoa further suggests that this family represents an ancient and broadly used mechanism for coupling mechanical force to ionic signaling.

The expression pattern of Piezos is consistent with roles in multiple mechanotransduction pathways. Piezo1 is present in tissues that experience stretch or pressure, including bladder, colon, lung, kidney, and skin. Piezo2 is enriched in dorsal root ganglia neurons, and its knockdown selectively reduced rapidly-adapting MA currents in these neurons. This selective effect supports a role for Piezo2 in somatosensory mechanotransduction, including touch and possibly noxious mechanical sensation. Future studies should determine whether Piezos form the pore of MA channels, how force gates these proteins, and how their activity contributes to mechanosensory physiology in vivo.
