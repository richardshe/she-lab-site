Our findings reveal an unprecedented phenomenon in mammalian cellular biology: the stimulus-triggered acquisition of pluripotency (STAP). The observation that robust environmental stressors, particularly transient low-pH exposure, can rapidly reprogram committed somatic cells to a pluripotent state without the introduction of exogenous transcription factors or nuclear transfer fundamentally challenges the established paradigm of epigenetic canalization. This context-dependent fate conversion demonstrates a remarkable degree of cellular plasticity previously thought to exist only in plants or lower vertebrates. 

The demonstration that CD45+ hematopoietic lineages undergo dynamic epigenetic reorganization, including extensive demethylation at the Oct4 and Nanog promoter regions, confirms that STAP cell generation is a bona fide reprogramming event rather than the selective survival of preexisting multipotent progenitors. This conclusion is further corroborated by T-cell receptor gene rearrangement analyses and live-cell imaging, which trace the origin of STAP clusters directly back to lineage-committed lymphocytes. Notably, while these STAP cells exhibit hallmark features of pluripotency, including the capacity for multi-lineage differentiation in vitro and robust teratoma formation in vivo, they retain unique characteristics distinguishing them from conventional embryonic stem (ES) cells. Their limited self-renewal capacity under standard LIF-containing conditions and the presence of inactivated X-chromosomes in female cells suggest that STAP cells may represent an distinct transitional pluripotent state.

Yet, perhaps the most striking demonstration of STAP cell potency is their ability to contribute to all somatic lineages and the germline following blastocyst injection, satisfying the most stringent criteria for pluripotency. The generation of completely STAP-derived embryos through tetraploid complementation unequivocally proves their developmental equivalence to blastomere cells. Furthermore, our success in deriving robustly expandable STAP stem cell lines when cultured in ACTH-containing medium provides a critical bridge between this novel biological phenomenon and potential biomedical applications. These STAP stem cells acquire ES-cell-like properties, including continuous self-renewal and loss of X-chromosome inactivation, highlighting an additional layer of plasticity within the reprogrammed state itself.

These discoveries open new avenues for both basic research and regenerative medicine. The ability to generate patient-specific pluripotent cells through simple chemical or physical perturbations circumvents the technical complexities and ethical concerns associated with somatic cell nuclear transfer or viral transduction. Future investigations must focus on elucidating the precise molecular mechanisms orchestrating this rapid epigenetic remodeling and determining whether similar stress-induced plasticity operates during tissue regeneration or pathogenesis in vivo. Ultimately, understanding how external cues dismantle the barriers of terminal differentiation will profoundly advance our comprehension of cellular identity and epigenetic regulation.
