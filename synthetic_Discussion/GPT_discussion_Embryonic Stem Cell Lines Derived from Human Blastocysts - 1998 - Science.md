The cell lines described here satisfy the criteria proposed for primate embryonic stem cells. They were derived from the inner cell mass of preimplantation human embryos, proliferated for prolonged periods without loss of an undifferentiated morphology, retained normal karyotypes, expressed high levels of telomerase activity, and maintained the capacity to form differentiated derivatives of endoderm, mesoderm, and ectoderm. Although contribution to the germ line cannot be used as a practical or ethical test for human ES cells, the combination of origin, stable growth, marker expression, and multilineage differentiation provides strong evidence that these cells represent the human counterpart of primate ES cells.

The establishment of these lines also emphasizes important species differences in early development. Human ES cells, like nonhuman primate ES cells and human EC cells, expressed SSEA-3, SSEA-4, TRA-1-60, TRA-1-81, and alkaline phosphatase, but did not express SSEA-1 in the undifferentiated state. This marker pattern differs from that of mouse ES and EC cells, in which SSEA-1 is expressed and SSEA-3 and SSEA-4 are not. These differences suggest that conclusions drawn from mouse ES cells may not always be directly applicable to human development. Human ES cells should therefore provide a complementary experimental system for defining the molecular events that regulate early human cell fate, lineage commitment, and differentiation.

The prolonged proliferation of these cells in culture, together with high telomerase activity, suggests that they may provide a renewable source of normal human cells with broad developmental potential. This property distinguishes them from most differentiated human somatic cells, which have a finite replicative life-span in vitro. The ability to cryopreserve and recover the cell lines further increases their utility as reproducible experimental material. Because the lines were not derived by single-cell cloning, however, some variation among undifferentiated cells cannot be excluded, and future studies will be required to determine the extent to which individual cells within a line share identical developmental potential.

The differentiation observed in teratomas and in culture indicates that human ES cells can give rise to diverse embryonic cell types and, under some conditions, trophoblast. Defined culture conditions that direct differentiation toward specific lineages will be required before these cells can be used effectively for drug discovery, toxicology, or transplantation. In addition, issues of immunologic compatibility, genetic stability during prolonged culture, and the elimination of undifferentiated cells from differentiated preparations must be addressed. Nevertheless, the derivation of human ES cell lines provides a new experimental foundation for the study of human development and for the generation of specialized human cell types in vitro.
