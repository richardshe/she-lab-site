We demonstrate here that CD47 expression is dynamically regulated on hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells in response to mobilizing and inflammatory stimuli, and that this regulation has a direct functional consequence for stem cell survival in vivo. The increase in CD47 was transient, occurring at the time when HSPCs expand and enter the circulation, and returned toward baseline as mobilization resolved. These data indicate that CD47 is not simply a constitutive marker of hematopoietic cells, but rather a regulated protective signal that is induced when stem and progenitor cells are most likely to encounter macrophages in the marrow, spleen, liver, and circulation.

The requirement for CD47 in this setting appears to be protection from phagocytosis rather than an essential role in migration. CD47 deficient stem and progenitor cells were able to appear in peripheral tissues after mobilization, and IAP-/- mice did not display a major defect in the overall movement of KLS cells or GMPs. However, CD47 deficient HSCs failed to engraft wild-type recipients, were efficiently phagocytosed by macrophages in vitro, and were unable to seed wild-type marrow in parabiosis experiments. Moreover, HSCs expressing only one allele of CD47 had reduced fitness after inflammatory challenge, and this disadvantage was corrected by macrophage depletion. Thus, the level of CD47 expression determines the probability that an HSPC will be retained or eliminated when it enters macrophage-rich environments.

These findings suggest a physiological model in which inflammation creates two linked pressures on the hematopoietic system. First, inflammatory signals stimulate HSPC proliferation and migration, thereby increasing the number of cells that must traffic through vascular beds populated by active macrophages. Second, the same inflammatory state increases macrophage phagocytic activity. Up-regulation of CD47 may therefore serve as a temporally restricted adaptation that allows normal HSPCs to migrate during stress while avoiding inappropriate clearance. This model also raises the possibility that other migratory hematopoietic populations, including cells that circulate through lymphatic tissues, may similarly regulate CD47 when they encounter sinusoidal macrophages.

Leukemic cells appear to appropriate this normal protective mechanism. CD47 was increased in multiple murine myeloid leukemias and in human AML and blast crisis CML, but not uniformly in earlier or less aggressive myeloproliferative disorders. In human disease, the increase in CD47 accompanying progression from chronic phase CML to blast crisis suggests that fixed high expression of CD47 may be a late event associated with malignant evolution. Because leukemic stem and progenitor cells share the migratory behavior of normal HSCs and must occupy the same macrophage-rich hematopoietic organs, constitutive expression of a signal that normally protects migrating HSCs would provide a substantial selective advantage.

Our experimental studies with MOLM-13 cells directly support this interpretation. MOLM-13 cells with low endogenous CD47 failed to grow efficiently in immunodeficient mice, whereas ectopic expression of murine CD47 allowed these cells to engraft multiple hematopoietic tissues and cause disease. Direct intrafemoral injection did not rescue control cells, indicating that the failure of low CD47 cells was not explained simply by an inability to reach a marrow site. Instead, tumorigenic potential correlated with CD47 expression level. High-expressing cells were more pathogenic, low-expressing populations underwent selection for higher CD47 in vivo, and suppression of CD47 expression after transplant markedly reduced disseminated growth. These results demonstrate that CD47 is not merely a marker of leukemic cells but can be a determinant of their survival and expansion.

Macrophages are the principal mediators of this selective pressure. Depletion of macrophages prevented enrichment for CD47 hi MOLM-13 variants and allowed low CD47 cells to engraft at levels comparable to CD47-expressing cells. In vitro and short-term in vivo phagocytosis assays further showed that the likelihood of engulfment was inversely related to CD47 expression. Finally, blockade of SIRP alpha eliminated the protective effect of CD47, indicating that the CD47-SIRP alpha interaction is the relevant pathway through which leukemic cells inhibit phagocytosis. Together, these results identify macrophage-mediated phagocytosis as a significant form of tumor immunosurveillance, even in hosts lacking T, B, and NK cell function.

Why leukemic cells require such protection remains an important question. Tumor cells may display pro-phagocytic signals that distinguish them from normal tissue, analogous to the altered surface features of aged erythrocytes or damaged cells. Stress-associated ligands such as calreticulin can promote phagocytosis and may be counterbalanced by CD47. We propose that leukemic transformation creates or exposes such prophagocytic signals, and that increased CD47 expression permits malignant cells to evade this innate checkpoint. The regulatory mechanism responsible for CD47 up-regulation is also unknown. The precise induction and resolution of CD47 expression during normal mobilization implies tight transcriptional or post-transcriptional control, whereas leukemic cells maintain constitutively high expression. Defining whether Nrf1 or other regulatory pathways control CD47 in both inflammation and leukemia will be important for understanding how this physiological program becomes fixed in cancer.

These observations have therapeutic implications. Because CD47 expression can determine the fitness of both normal HSCs and leukemic cells, therapies that interrupt CD47-SIRP alpha signaling may render leukemic cells susceptible to macrophage-mediated clearance. Such an approach could have additional benefit if phagocytosed tumor material is processed and presented by macrophages or dendritic cells, thereby engaging adaptive immunity. The association of increased CD47 transcript levels with poorer survival in human AML further suggests that CD47 may serve both as a prognostic marker and as a functional target. Our findings therefore support the development of strategies that block the CD47-mediated "don't eat me" signal in leukemia and potentially in other cancers that disseminate to macrophage-rich tissues.
