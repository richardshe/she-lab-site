The results described here show that a genetic structure which is topologically simple may nevertheless possess a highly differentiated topography. The rII region behaves, for the purposes of these tests, as a linear array of sites. Deletions provide overlapping landmarks by which the array can be divided into small segments, and point mutations can then be assigned to these segments by qualitative recombination tests. Within the limits imposed by reversion noise and by the finite size of the segments, no evidence has been obtained for a branch. Thus, the same material which previously served to test the topology now provides a framework in which local differences in mutability can be examined.

The agreement between the order obtained by deletion mapping and that obtained by recombination frequencies is important in this connection. The deletion method does not measure distance in the ordinary genetic sense; it asks only whether two lesions overlap. Nevertheless, the order which it gives is the same as that given by quantitative crossing. This agreement justifies the use of the deletion map as a scaffold for the topographic analysis. It also emphasizes that, for many purposes, a qualitative recombination test can be more powerful than a quantitative one, provided that suitable overlapping deficiencies are available. A large number of mutants can then be placed on the map without making all possible crosses.

The most striking fact about the distribution of spontaneous mutations is its non-randomness. If all mutable sites in the rII region had the same probability of alteration, the repeated recovery of some sites hundreds of times and the failure to recover many others would be very improbable. The data instead require the existence of sites of very different spontaneous mutability. Some sites are conspicuously hot, some are represented only once or twice, and many must still be unobserved. The estimate of undiscovered sites is necessarily conservative, since it assumes that the least frequently observed sites form a uniform low-mutability class. If there are sites still colder than these, as there is no reason to exclude, then the true number of possible sites is larger and the present map is less saturated than the numerical estimate suggests.

It follows that a genetic map of this kind cannot be regarded simply as a list of equally probable positions at which mutation may occur. The apparent frequency with which a site is found depends on at least two things: the existence of a possible alteration which produces the rII phenotype and the probability that such an alteration will occur under the conditions of origin. The present experiments do not distinguish fully between these two factors. A site absent from the collection may be unable to give a detectable rII mutant by a small change, or it may be capable of doing so but only with very low probability. Similarly, a hot site may be hot because the corresponding chemical change is especially favored, because the altered product is readily detected as rII, or because both circumstances apply. The topographic map is therefore a map of observed mutability in the genetic test system, not yet a direct map of chemical structure.

The use of mutagens sharpens this conclusion. Each mutagen produces a spectrum which differs from the spontaneous one and from the spectra of the other mutagens. Certain sites respond strongly to one treatment and weakly or not at all to another. Other sites are common to several spectra. This behavior is difficult to reconcile with a view in which mutation is a uniform accidental break in an otherwise featureless line. It is more naturally interpreted as evidence that different positions in the genetic structure present different chemical opportunities for alteration. The same genetic site may be accessible to more than one chemical process, whereas another site may be characteristic of a single process. The map thus becomes not merely a positional map, but a record of chemical specificity expressed through genetic behavior.

At the same time, the comparison of spectra must be made cautiously. None of the induced collections is saturated. Each contains some spontaneous background. Differences between two spectra at a site represented by only a few mutants may therefore arise by chance. Conversely, the absence of a site from one collection cannot be taken as proof that the corresponding mutagen is unable to produce that alteration. The strongest conclusions are those based on repeated occurrences, especially the distinct hotspots. These show that some local features of the rII region are recognized, in effect, by particular mutagens with high efficiency.

The addition of induced mutants has increased the number of known sites, but has not exhausted the region. This is not paradoxical. A mutagen may uncover sites whose spontaneous mutability is so low that they would scarcely appear in a spontaneous collection of this size, while still leaving many other possible sites unseen. Thus, increasing the variety of mutational agents changes the visible topography as well as increasing the total count of sites. The estimate that at least 120 further sites remain to be found after combining all the material should therefore be understood as a lower limit for the class of sites detectable by the present methods and present mutagens. The complete fine structure is almost certainly richer than the catalog now available.

Several limitations of the analysis should also be kept in mind. A reverting mutant is ordinarily treated as a point mutant, and this is a useful rule for the rII region. But exceptions occur. A small deletion or other extended alteration may revert and may overlap several true point mutations. In such cases, mutations grouped as repeats of a single site may later be divided. For this reason the identification of different sites is a firm conclusion when recombination is observed, whereas the identification of identical sites remains provisional when recombination is not observed. This asymmetry is inherent in the method and is especially important in the most crowded segments.

Another limitation arises from the threshold of detection. A negative spot test means that recombination, if present, is below the level observable in the assay. It does not prove that no recombination of any kind can occur. Thus, the term "site" as used here has an operational meaning. Two mutations at the same site are those which have not been separated by the present test; two mutations at different sites are those which have been separated. As methods are refined, some sites may be subdivided. The topographic features described here are therefore features at the resolution of the present recombination system.

Even with these qualifications, the main result is clear. The rII region contains a large number of distinguishable mutable sites arranged in a linear order, and these sites differ greatly in their tendency to mutate spontaneously and in their responsiveness to particular mutagens. This conclusion brings the genetic analysis close to chemical specificity. The genetic map no longer represents only the order of functional elements; it begins to reveal differences among the elements themselves. Such differences may reflect the sequence of chemical units in the genetic material, the local environment in which they reside, or the consequences of particular changes for the function of the rII cistrons.

The present work therefore extends fine-structure mapping from topology to topography. Topology asks how the parts are connected; topography asks how the parts differ. The answer obtained for the rII region is that the structure is linear to a high degree of resolution, but far from uniform. It has landmarks of mutability, some general and some agent-specific. Continued mapping with larger collections and additional mutagens should reveal still more sites and may eventually permit the genetic positions to be correlated with particular chemical changes. In that case, the pattern of hotspots and cold spots described here will provide a bridge between the formal genetic map and the molecular constitution of the genetic material.
