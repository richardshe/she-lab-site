# Discussion

The mechanism by which the innate immune system detects cytosolic DNA to mount an antiviral response has been a central question in immunology. Our study resolves this longstanding enigma by identifying cyclic GMP-AMP synthase (cGAS) as the definitive cytosolic DNA sensor that activates the type I interferon pathway. Through rigorous biochemical fractionation and quantitative mass spectrometry, we isolated cGAS and demonstrated that it catalyzes the synthesis of the novel second messenger cyclic GMP-AMP (cGAMP) from ATP and GTP, strictly in a DNA-dependent manner. This cGAMP subsequently binds to and activates the adaptor protein STING, culminating in the phosphorylation of IRF3 and the robust induction of interferon-β.

Several lines of evidence unequivocally establish cGAS as the primary sensor for cytosolic DNA. First, overexpression of cGAS potently induces interferon-β in a STING-dependent manner, whereas mutations in its catalytic nucleotidyltransferase domain completely abolish this activity. Second, depletion of cGAS via multiple independent shRNAs severely compromises IRF3 activation and interferon-β production in response to both transfected DNA and DNA virus infection, such as herpes simplex virus 1 (HSV-1), in both mouse and human cell lines. Importantly, cGAS depletion does not affect the interferon response to RNA viruses, demonstrating its strict specificity for the DNA sensing pathway. Third, we show that cGAS directly binds DNA through its N-terminal region and colocalizes with cytosolic DNA in intact cells. Finally, purified recombinant cGAS is fully sufficient to synthesize cGAMP in vitro when stimulated by double-stranded DNA, confirming its direct catalytic role.

The discovery of cGAS provides a unifying mechanism for cytosolic DNA sensing. Unlike previously proposed DNA sensors—such as DAI, IFI16, or various DNA helicases, whose relative contributions have remained controversial and cell-type specific—cGAS appears to be universally required across multiple cell types for STING-dependent interferon induction by DNA. The generation of cGAMP represents a highly elegant biological solution: it amplifies the initial DNA binding event through the enzymatic production of a diffusible second messenger, ensuring a rapid and robust systemic antiviral response. 

This breakthrough has profound implications beyond viral immunity. Cytosolic DNA is also a hallmark of various autoimmune diseases, such as Aicardi-Goutières syndrome and systemic lupus erythematosus, and plays a role in the immunogenicity of tumors and vaccine adjuvants. The identification of cGAS as the enzymatic generator of cGAMP opens up entirely new avenues for therapeutic intervention. Small-molecule inhibitors of cGAS could provide powerful treatments for severe autoimmune disorders driven by aberrant DNA sensing, while the cGAMP molecule itself, or its synthetic analogs, hold tremendous promise as potent adjuvants for vaccines and cancer immunotherapies.
