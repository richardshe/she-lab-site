The data presented here require a reassessment of the nature of the scrapie agent. A protein within the infectious particle is required for biological activity, whereas no comparable evidence has been obtained for an essential nucleic acid. Proteolytic digestion, carbethoxylation, detergents, chaotropic ions, phenol, and urea all diminish infectivity under conditions in which nucleic acids ordinarily retain, or can be recovered with, biological activity. Conversely, nucleases, zinc-catalyzed hydrolysis, hydroxylamine, psoralens, and ultraviolet irradiation at doses sufficient to modify nucleic acids have failed to inactivate the agent. These observations do not prove the absence of all nucleic acid from the infectious particle, but they place severe limits on any nucleic acid that could serve as its genome.

The resistance of the scrapie agent to many procedures that destroy conventional viruses, together with its sensitivity to protein modification, distinguishes it from known animal viruses, plant viroids, and plasmids. The term prion is proposed to emphasize this distinction. By this term we do not imply that all structural features of the agent are now understood. Rather, prion denotes a proteinaceous infectious particle in which a protein is an essential component and in which a conventional nucleic acid genome has not been demonstrated. This designation should be useful until the molecular structure of the agent has been established by direct chemical analysis.

The apparent heterogeneity of the scrapie agent may be explained, at least in part, by its hydrophobicity. Binding to phenyl-Sepharose, loss of titer after removal of detergent, and broad distributions in density and size gradients are all consistent with an infectious particle containing hydrophobic domains that promote aggregation or association with membrane constituents. Such behavior could account for previous failures to assign a single sedimentation coefficient, density, or molecular weight to the agent. The recovery of smaller forms in sulfobetaine 3-14 suggests that the infectious unit may be much smaller than many preparations indicate. If a dissociated particle of 50,000 daltons or less retains infectivity, then the chemical nature of the agent is more unusual than estimates based on aggregated material have suggested.

Several qualifications are necessary. The purification of the scrapie agent is incomplete, and infectivity must still be measured by animal bioassay. Since a highly sensitive in vitro assay is not available, small losses of activity and contaminating host molecules remain difficult to evaluate. Alkali inactivation also deserves further study, for it could reflect modification of either protein or an unidentified nonprotein component. Nevertheless, the concordance of many independent experiments argues against the possibility that the observed effects are artifacts of one reagent, one preparation, or one assay.

The existence of an infectious particle with these properties has important implications for slow degenerative diseases of the central nervous system. Scrapie, kuru, and Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease share long incubation periods and neuropathological features, yet their agents have resisted classification by conventional microbiological criteria. A proteinaceous infectious particle could explain their unusual stability, apparent lack of antigenicity, and failure to yield readily to methods devised for viruses. It also raises new questions concerning replication. The prion may carry information through the conformation, covalent modification, or ordered association of a protein, or it may act upon a host molecule to generate additional infectious material. Elucidating these mechanisms will require more highly purified preparations, identification of the essential protein, and determination of whether host or agent sequences contribute to infectivity. Such studies should clarify not only scrapie but also the pathogenesis of related human dementias.
