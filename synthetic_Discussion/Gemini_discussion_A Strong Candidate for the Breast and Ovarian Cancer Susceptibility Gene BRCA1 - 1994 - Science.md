# Discussion

The identification of *BRCA1* as a strong candidate for the 17q-linked breast and ovarian cancer susceptibility gene marks a pivotal milestone in cancer genetics. Our positional cloning efforts culminated in the isolation of a large transcription unit, spanning approximately 100 kb of genomic DNA and encoding a 1863-amino acid protein. The presence of potentially predisposing mutations in five of eight families clearly segregating 17q-linked cancer susceptibility provides compelling evidence that this gene is indeed *BRCA1*. The nature of these mutations—which include a frameshift deletion, a nonsense mutation, a single base-pair insertion, a missense mutation at a highly conserved residue, and a likely regulatory mutation—strongly supports the hypothesis that *BRCA1* functions as a tumor suppressor gene, where loss of wild-type function predisposes individuals to oncogenesis.

The predicted *BRCA1* protein contains an amino-terminal C3HC4 zinc finger domain, a motif often found in proteins that interact with nucleic acids and regulate transcription. This suggests that *BRCA1* might govern the expression of other genes involved in cell cycle progression, DNA repair, or apoptosis. Aside from this zinc finger, the lack of significant homology to previously characterized proteins indicates that *BRCA1* may define a novel pathway in the regulation of mammary and ovarian epithelial cell growth. The broad tissue expression pattern of *BRCA1*, despite its specific association with breast and ovarian cancer predisposition, suggests that its tumor-suppressive function might either be redundant in other tissues or uniquely critical to the hormonal or developmental context of the breast and ovary. The complex alternative splicing observed at the 5' end of the transcript further hints at an intricate regulatory mechanism that warrants future investigation.

The diverse array of mutations identified in our study also offers preliminary insights into genotype-phenotype correlations. For instance, the nonsense mutation found in Kindred 2082, which truncates a substantial portion of the carboxy-terminus, is associated with a strikingly high incidence of ovarian cancer and an older average age of breast cancer onset. Conversely, mutations occurring earlier in the sequence, such as the zinc finger-disrupting 11-bp deletion in Kindred 1901 and the potential regulatory mutation in Kindred 2035, appear to correlate with a very early age of breast cancer onset. While these observations are based on a limited number of families, they raise the intriguing possibility that different *BRCA1* alleles confer distinct risks for breast versus ovarian cancer, and influence the penetrance and age of onset of the disease.

The clinical implications of cloning *BRCA1* are profound. With the sequence in hand, it is now possible to develop molecular diagnostics for the identification of individuals at high risk for breast and ovarian cancer. Such screening could facilitate intensive surveillance and early intervention strategies, potentially reducing the high mortality associated with these malignancies. Moreover, uncovering the physiological role of the *BRCA1* protein will inevitably enhance our fundamental understanding of breast and ovarian cancer biology. Future functional studies must prioritize determining the specific cellular pathways regulated by *BRCA1*, investigating the mechanism by which the mutant alleles promote tumorigenesis, and ultimately exploring targeted therapeutic avenues capable of restoring or bypassing *BRCA1* function in susceptible patients.
