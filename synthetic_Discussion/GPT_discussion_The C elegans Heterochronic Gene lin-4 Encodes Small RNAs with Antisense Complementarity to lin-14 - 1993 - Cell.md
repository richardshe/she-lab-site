## The lin-4 Gene Product

The molecular analysis of the C. elegans heterochronic gene lin-4 indicates that this locus defines an unusual class of developmental regulatory gene. lin-4 was localized by transformation rescue to a 693 bp genomic fragment, and this fragment is sufficient to restore essentially wild-type development to animals carrying the lin-4(e972) mutation. Since the e972 lesion deletes most or all of the rescuing sequences, and since lin-14 null mutations suppress the lin-4 mutant phenotype, lin-4(e972) is likely to eliminate the activity required for the normal negative regulation of lin-14. Thus the principal, and perhaps sole, genetically defined role of lin-4 in the heterochronic pathway is to reduce lin-14 activity at the proper time in early larval development.

Several features of the lin-4 locus argue against a conventional protein-coding function. The rescuing region is contained within an intron of another transcription unit, yet sequences corresponding to this intron alone can rescue lin-4 mutant animals. Moreover, this rescuing activity is not abolished by mutations that disrupt the most plausible open reading frames. The comparison of lin-4 sequences from four Caenorhabditis species is especially informative, because all four genomic clones rescue lin-4(e972) in C. elegans. The conserved sequences show strong nucleotide conservation without the pattern of codon degeneracy, conserved reading frame, and splice-site organization expected of a protein-coding gene. Although these observations cannot formally exclude every possible unorthodox mode of translation, they make it unlikely that the essential lin-4 product is a polypeptide.

The identification of two small lin-4 RNAs provides a more plausible explanation for lin-4 function. The 61 nt lin-4L RNA and the approximately 22 nt lin-4S RNA are absent from lin-4(e972) animals and are present in wild-type and rescued animals. The point mutation ma161, which causes loss of lin-4 function, changes a conserved nucleotide near the 5' end of both small RNAs. This result links the transcribed small RNA sequences directly to lin-4 activity. In addition, the sequences encoding these RNAs are highly conserved among the Caenorhabditis species examined. These data suggest that one or both of the small RNAs, rather than an encoded protein, constitute the functional lin-4 gene product.

## Relationship of lin-4L and lin-4S

The relationship between lin-4L and lin-4S is not yet clear. The two RNAs appear to have the same 5' end, and lin-4S corresponds to the 5' portion of lin-4L. lin-4S could be produced by processing of lin-4L, whereas lin-4L could arise by read-through of a termination site used to generate lin-4S. Alternatively, both RNAs could derive from a longer precursor that has not been detected in our experiments. The agreement between the sizes estimated by Northern blotting, RNase protection, and S1 nuclease mapping suggests that these RNAs are not extensively modified by splicing or polyadenylation, but these methods would not necessarily reveal small terminal modifications or altered nucleotides.

It is possible that only one of the two RNAs is active in lin-14 regulation and that the other is a byproduct of its synthesis. lin-4S is much more abundant than lin-4L in total RNA, which may favor lin-4S as the active species, particularly if the regulatory interaction with lin-14 is stoichiometric. In addition, predicted folding of lin-4L would place some lin-14-complementary residues in an intramolecular stem, which could reduce their availability for pairing with lin-14 mRNA. However, the precise ends and possible structure of lin-4S remain uncertain, and lin-4L may have functions not apparent from abundance alone. Direct tests of each RNA species will be required to determine whether lin-4S, lin-4L, or both are necessary and sufficient for lin-4 activity.

The conserved sequences upstream of the lin-4 RNAs may also be important for expression or processing. Some of this conserved DNA lies outside the transcribed small RNAs but within the rescuing region, and inversion of upstream sequences can still yield partial rescue. These sequences may contain promoter or processing signals, or they may encode additional low-abundance products below the detection limits of the assays used here. Nevertheless, the only consistent lin-4 products detected were lin-4L and lin-4S, and the ma161 lesion in their shared sequence provides strong evidence that these small RNAs are central to lin-4 function.

## Antisense Complementarity to lin-14

The most striking feature of the lin-4 RNAs is their complementarity to repeated elements in the lin-14 3' untranslated region. lin-14 is regulated posttranscriptionally: lin-14 mRNA levels remain relatively constant during development, while LIN-14 protein decreases sharply during the L1 stage. Previous genetic and fusion-gene studies showed that sequences in the lin-14 3' UTR are required in cis for this decrease and that lin-4 is required in trans. The present results identify a molecular basis for this relationship. Two short blocks of lin-4 sequence can pair with a sequence repeated seven times in the lin-14 3' UTR, and these repeated elements are located in the region affected by lin-14 gain-of-function mutations.

Several observations support the idea that lin-4 negatively regulates lin-14 through a direct RNA-RNA interaction. First, the complementary sequences in the lin-14 3' UTR are conserved in Caenorhabditis, consistent with their importance for regulation. Second, the ma161 mutation alters a nucleotide within one of the lin-14-complementary regions of lin-4. Third, the repeated complementary elements are within the 3' UTR sequences known to mediate the lin-4-dependent decrease in LIN-14 protein. These independent genetic, phylogenetic, and molecular correlations are most simply explained by a model in which lin-4 RNA base pairs with lin-14 mRNA.

The proposed pairing is short, with no single continuous duplex longer than about 10 nt. Nevertheless, short RNA duplexes can be biologically specific when they occur in an appropriate structural and molecular context. Interactions between small nuclear RNAs and pre-mRNAs provide precedents for regulatory RNA-RNA contacts involving limited complementarity. The seven repeated elements in lin-14 may increase the probability or stability of the interaction with lin-4 RNA, or may allow more than one lin-4 molecule to bind a single lin-14 transcript. The repeated arrangement could also permit partial pairing at multiple sites, thereby reducing the requirement for an extensive uninterrupted duplex.

Definitive proof of the antisense model will require compensatory mutations. A mutation in lin-14 that disrupts pairing should prevent normal downregulation of LIN-14 protein, and a corresponding mutation in lin-4 should restore regulation if base pairing is the essential feature. It will also be important to determine whether lin-4 forms a stable complex with lin-14 mRNA in vivo or acts transiently, perhaps with the assistance of cellular proteins. Such experiments should distinguish whether lin-4 behaves as a simple antisense RNA, a guide for modification of lin-14 mRNA, or part of a larger ribonucleoprotein regulatory complex.

## Temporal Regulation of lin-14

The genetic behavior of lin-4 and lin-14 suggests a developmental switch in which high lin-14 activity specifies early L1 programs, and reduced lin-14 activity permits later stage-specific programs. The data presented here suggest that the switch is executed posttranscriptionally by small lin-4 RNAs acting through the lin-14 3' UTR. In this model, lin-4 activity is absent or ineffective during late embryogenesis and the earliest part of the L1 stage, allowing LIN-14 protein to accumulate. Later in the L1, lin-4 activity increases or becomes competent to act, causing LIN-14 protein levels to fall without a corresponding reduction in lin-14 mRNA.

The activation of lin-4 could occur at several levels. lin-4 RNA abundance may increase at a particular time in larval development, or the RNAs may be present but inactive until a processing, localization, or association step occurs. The observation that LIN-14 protein does not decrease until after feeding suggests that lin-4 activation may be coupled to the initiation of postembryonic development. To regulate lin-14 in the diverse cells affected by the heterochronic pathway, lin-4 must act in many or all cells that require the L1-to-L2 transition in developmental fate. Whether this widespread effect reflects broad lin-4 expression or movement of a regulatory signal remains unknown.

It is also not known whether lin-4 acts only once to convert lin-14 mRNA into a persistently inactive state, or whether continued lin-4 activity is required to maintain repression of LIN-14 synthesis. If lin-4 acts transiently, it might induce a stable change in lin-14 mRNA or in a protein complex associated with the lin-14 3' UTR. If continued activity is required, lin-4 RNA levels or activity should remain sufficient during later larval stages to prevent renewed LIN-14 synthesis. Measurements of lin-4L and lin-4S abundance during staged development, together with experimental manipulation of lin-4 expression, should clarify how the temporal decline in LIN-14 protein is generated.

## A 3' UTR-Mediated Antisense Mechanism

Natural antisense RNAs are known to regulate target RNAs by affecting stability or translation, but the lin-4-lin-14 interaction may represent a distinct example of translational control through the 3' UTR. lin-4 probably does not regulate lin-14 primarily by altering mRNA abundance, because steady-state lin-14 mRNA levels are not appreciably changed during the period in which LIN-14 protein declines, nor are they strongly altered in lin-4 mutants. Thus lin-4 is more likely to affect the translatability, localization, or structure of lin-14 mRNA.

One possibility is that lin-4 RNA binds the lin-14 3' UTR in the cytoplasm and directly interferes with translation. Although many translational control mechanisms act at the 5' end of an mRNA, regulatory information in 3' UTRs is increasingly recognized as important for development. Pairing of lin-4 RNA with the 3' end of lin-14 mRNA could interfere with interactions between the 3' and 5' ends of the transcript, alter poly(A)-dependent translational stimulation, or recruit proteins that inhibit ribosome function. Alternatively, lin-4 might direct lin-14 mRNA to a subcellular compartment where it is poorly translated, or promote an mRNA modification that leaves abundance unchanged but reduces translational efficiency.

The conserved lin-4 sequences not predicted to pair with lin-14 are also noteworthy. In the proposed lin-4:lin-14 hybrid, these residues would be looped out. Their conservation suggests that they may bind proteins or contribute to a higher-order RNA structure required for lin-4 function. Such proteins might be essential for viability or might act redundantly, which could explain why additional heterochronic genes required specifically for lin-14 3' UTR regulation have not been identified genetically. Determining the cellular location of lin-4 RNAs and identifying associated proteins will be necessary to define the mechanism by which 3' UTR pairing inhibits LIN-14 protein synthesis.

## Small RNAs as Developmental Regulators

lin-4 may be a member of a broader class of genes whose products are small regulatory RNAs rather than proteins. Other untranslated RNAs, including Xist and H19 RNAs, have been implicated in developmental processes, but they are much larger than lin-4L or lin-4S. Small RNAs also have important functions in RNA processing and editing, yet the putative active lin-4S RNA is unusually short even by comparison with many of these molecules. Its small size may allow highly specific recognition of a target mRNA through short complementary elements, while limiting the number of other interactions it can make.

The difficulty of identifying lin-4 alleles may reflect the small target size of this type of gene. Only one additional lin-4 allele, ma161, was recovered after a large noncomplementation screen, and that mutation changes a single conserved nucleotide in the small RNA sequence. Similar small RNA genes, particularly those located within introns of other genes, may be missed in conventional searches for protein-coding sequences or may be difficult to recover genetically unless mutations affect a very small critical region. The existence of lin-4 suggests that some developmental regulatory information may be encoded in short noncoding transcripts that are not readily recognized by standard criteria.

The regulation of lin-14 by lin-4 provides a mechanism for converting a temporal developmental signal into the stage-specific repression of a key regulatory protein. By targeting a defined 3' UTR through sequence complementarity, a small RNA can impose highly specific translational control without changing target mRNA abundance. This strategy may be especially useful in developmental pathways, where the timing of protein expression must be sharply controlled in many cells. If other small RNAs act similarly, then antisense interactions with 3' UTRs may represent a general means by which developmental genes are regulated posttranscriptionally.
