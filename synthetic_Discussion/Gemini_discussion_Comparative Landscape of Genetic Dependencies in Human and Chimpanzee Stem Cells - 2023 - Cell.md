# Discussion

Our comparative loss-of-function approach using genome-wide CRISPR interference reveals a previously unappreciated landscape of evolutionary divergence in essential gene networks between humans and chimpanzees. By systematically perturbing the genomes of closely related primate pluripotent stem cells (PSCs), we discovered 75 genes that exhibit species-specific effects on cellular proliferation. This finding challenges the assumption that fundamental developmental pathways and the core essential-ome are rigidly conserved among closely related species. Instead, our data demonstrate that genetic dependencies can shift significantly within the relatively short evolutionary timespan separating humans and chimpanzees. Importantly, these differences do not manifest as random noise but are organized into coherent functional modules and protein complexes, indicating systematic evolutionary modifications of specific cellular pathways.

A striking example of this divergence is the human-specific dependency on the lysosomal V-ATPase accessory proteins, ATP6AP1 and ATP6AP2. While core V-ATPase function remains strictly essential across species for maintaining lysosomal pH, human cells have evolved an increased reliance on ATP6AP1 for mediating mTORC1 signaling. This suggests that the integration of nutrient sensing and growth factor signaling at the lysosomal surface has been rewired during human evolution, potentially to support the unique metabolic demands of human development.

Even more profoundly, our genetic screens uncovered a critical human-specific robustness to the depletion of central G1/S cell cycle regulators, including CDK2, CDK4, and Cyclin E1. Chimpanzee PSCs and neural progenitor cells (NPCs) strictly require these factors for timely G1/S transition, accumulating in the G1 phase upon their depletion. In stark contrast, human PSCs and NPCs seamlessly compensate for the loss of Cyclin E1-CDK2 activity, maintaining normal cell cycle progression. Our pharmacological and genetic rescue experiments suggest that this robustness is mediated, at least in part, by higher endogenous levels of CDK1 in human cells, which allows them to bypass the loss of CDK2 or upstream regulators like FAM122A and CHK1. This evolutionary rewiring results in human cells exhibiting a more relaxed S phase and G2/M checkpoint compared to chimpanzee cells.

The persistence of this human-specific robustness to cell cycle perturbations in NPCs and 3D cerebral organoids has profound implications for understanding human brain evolution. The 'G1-phase length hypothesis' posits that the duration of the G1 phase acts as a critical determinant of cell fate in the developing neocortex: a shorter G1 phase promotes continued proliferative divisions of neural progenitors, whereas a lengthening G1 phase triggers neurogenic differentiation. By acquiring an enhanced ability to bypass cell cycle checkpoints and maintain rapid G1/S transitions even in the face of genetic or metabolic stress, human neural progenitors may possess a heightened capacity to sustain prolonged proliferative expansion. This molecular resilience could be a foundational mechanism contributing to the massive expansion of the cerebral cortex—a defining hallmark of the human species.

To trace the evolutionary origins of these distinct genetic dependencies, our three-way comparison including orangutan PSCs was instrumental. By applying the principle of maximum parsimony, we deduced that the dependence on CDK2, CDK4, and Cyclin E1 represents the ancestral great ape state, still present in orangutans and chimpanzees. The robustness to depletion of these cell cycle factors is therefore a derived trait that evolved uniquely along the hominin lineage after the divergence from the chimpanzee ancestor. Similarly, the specific requirement for ATP6AP1 is also a human-derived trait. Conversely, the specific responses to genes like KAT6A in chimpanzee PSCs indicate that significant molecular innovations also occurred independently on the chimpanzee lineage. These findings emphasize that both lineages experienced substantial cellular rewiring, preventing us from viewing the chimpanzee cellular state simply as a proxy for the human ancestor.

While comparative genomics has traditionally focused on identifying changes in DNA sequence or steady-state gene expression, our functional genomics approach uniquely isolates the phenotypic consequences of these evolutionary changes. Many of the species-specific genetic dependencies we identified do not exhibit obvious signatures of positive selection in their coding sequences or striking differences in basal gene expression. This underscores the complexity of evolutionary biology: critical species differences can arise from subtle alterations in protein-protein interactions, shifts in complex stoichiometry, or rewiring of post-translational signaling networks that elude standard descriptive genomic analyses.

In conclusion, our study establishes an experimentally tractable platform for identifying latent molecular differences that have shaped human evolution. By shifting the comparative paradigm from sequence and expression to functional dependency, we have uncovered a new dimension of species divergence. The discovery that human cells have uniquely evolved robustness in cell cycle regulation provides a compelling molecular mechanism that may underpin the evolutionary expansion of the human brain. Moving forward, applying this comparative functional genomics framework to other specialized cell types, such as mature neurons, cardiomyocytes, or immune cells, will undoubtedly yield further insights into the molecular adaptations that make us uniquely human. This approach not only illuminates our evolutionary past but also highlights the potential limitations of using non-human primate models for understanding highly derived human cellular phenotypes and diseases.
