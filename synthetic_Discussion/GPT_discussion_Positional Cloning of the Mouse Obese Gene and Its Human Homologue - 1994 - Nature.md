The identification of the ob gene provides direct molecular evidence for a physiological pathway regulating body weight. The obese phenotype in the mouse has long suggested that food intake and energy expenditure are not determined solely by behaviour, but are subject to genetic and metabolic control. The data reported here indicate that ob encodes an adipose-tissue RNA whose product is likely to be secreted, and that mutations affecting either the coding sequence or expression of this gene result in profound obesity. These findings are consistent with the hypothesis that adipose tissue produces a circulating signal that acts to regulate the size of the body fat depot.

Several observations support the conclusion that the adipose RNA identified by exon trapping is the ob transcript. First, the gene maps within the genetic interval defined by recombination in more than 1,600 meioses segregating the ob mutation. Secondly, its expression is highly enriched in white adipose tissue, the tissue in which such a signal might be expected to originate if it reflected the size or metabolic state of fat stores. Thirdly, the transcript is absent in adipose tissue from SM/Ckc-Dac ob2J/ob2J mice, a co-isogenic strain in which the mutation is closely associated with a genomic alteration upstream of the mRNA start site. Finally, in the original C57BL/6J ob/ob strain, the coding region contains a nonsense mutation that would truncate the predicted protein before the second conserved cysteine. The occurrence of independent lesions in the same gene in two phenotypically similar mutant strains provides strong genetic evidence that this gene is ob.

The nature of the predicted protein is of particular interest. The open reading frame encodes a small, largely hydrophilic polypeptide with an amino-terminal signal sequence. In vitro translation in the presence of microsomal membranes produced a processed, protease-protected form, indicating that the signal sequence can mediate translocation into the microsomal lumen. The absence of other membrane-spanning regions, together with the hydrophilic sequence and the conservation of cysteine residues in the mature protein, is compatible with the idea that ob is secreted. Although direct demonstration of secretion from adipocytes and identification of the circulating protein remain to be established, the structure of the predicted product fits well with previous physiological experiments in which parabiosis suggested that ob/ob mice lack a humoral factor.

The marked increase of ob RNA in C57BL/6J ob/ob adipose tissue suggests that expression of the gene is subject to regulation by metabolic state or by its own product. Because the mutant transcript encodes a truncated and presumably inactive protein, increased RNA abundance may reflect a feedback response to the absence of functional ob activity. Alternatively, the elevated expression could be secondary to the increased mass, altered cellular composition, or changed endocrine state of adipose tissue in the obese animal. Distinguishing among these possibilities will require analysis of ob RNA and protein during development of obesity, after nutritional manipulation, and in other genetic models. Nonetheless, the difference between the coding mutation in C57BL/6J ob/ob mice and the apparent absence of RNA in ob2J/ob2J mice indicates that loss of ob function can occur by more than one molecular mechanism.

The apparent adipose specificity of expression argues that adipose tissue is not merely a passive site of energy storage, but can participate actively in the regulation of whole-body metabolism. A simple model is that the ob product is produced in proportion to adipose mass and acts at a distant site to reduce food intake, increase energy expenditure, or both. In the absence of this signal, the organism would behave as though fat stores were depleted, leading to hyperphagia, reduced energy expenditure, and progressive obesity. The endocrine and neural targets of the ob product are not identified by the present experiments. The central nervous system is an attractive candidate because feeding behaviour and autonomic output are centrally controlled, but peripheral sites involved in nutrient utilization may also respond to the factor. The isolation of the gene now makes it possible to test these alternatives directly.

The high degree of conservation between the mouse and human proteins indicates that the ob gene has an important vertebrate function. The predicted mature proteins are 84% identical, with particularly strong conservation near the amino terminus and around the cysteine residues. This conservation is especially striking because the untranslated regions show much less similarity, suggesting selective pressure on the protein sequence itself. The presence of related sequences in vertebrate genomic DNA further implies that the pathway in which ob participates is ancient. The isolation of the human homologue raises the possibility that variation in this gene, or in genes encoding its receptor or downstream effectors, may contribute to human obesity. Because most human obesity is probably polygenic and environmentally influenced, defects identical to those in the mouse need not be common to be informative.

The positional cloning strategy used here illustrates the utility of combining high-resolution genetic mapping, physical contig construction and exon trapping for the isolation of mammalian disease genes. The ob locus was initially defined only by phenotype, and no prior knowledge of the biochemical defect was available. By reducing the candidate interval and screening genomic clones for expressed sequences, we were able to identify a transcript whose expression pattern and mutant lesions revealed its identity. The success of this approach should encourage the isolation of other mouse obesity genes and of genes underlying complex physiological traits in which candidate-gene approaches have been limited.

There are several limitations to the present study. We have not yet shown that the ob protein circulates in vivo, that recombinant protein can correct the phenotype of ob/ob mice, or that the human homologue has an equivalent biological activity. The site of action of the protein, its receptor, and the pathways through which it alters feeding and metabolism remain unknown. It is also possible that the ob product influences several processes, including pancreatic islet function, insulin sensitivity, thermogenesis and reproductive physiology, all of which are disturbed in ob/ob mice. These questions can now be addressed using the cloned cDNAs, antibodies to the predicted protein, transgenic expression and administration of recombinant protein.

In summary, the cloning of ob identifies a conserved adipose-derived molecule whose loss produces severe obesity and diabetes in the mouse. These results provide molecular support for the concept that the adipose depot communicates with other tissues to maintain energy balance. The ob gene product may therefore represent a component of a feedback loop that monitors fat stores and coordinates nutrient intake and expenditure. Further characterization of this pathway should clarify the biological basis of obesity and may provide new approaches to the diagnosis and treatment of metabolic disease.
