The experiments presented in this study establish a robust, cell-free system from Escherichia coli that is capable of incorporating amino acids into protein at a rapid rate. The critical finding is that this incorporation is entirely dependent upon the addition of specific, high-molecular-weight RNA template fractions. This system provides the first direct biochemical evidence that RNA acts as the intermediate template, or "messenger," directing the sequence-specific synthesis of proteins. 

Our results demonstrate that the amino acid incorporation observed in this cell-free system possesses all the requisite characteristics of de novo protein synthesis. The process requires the simultaneous presence of both ribosomes and a 105,000 x g supernatant fraction, relies on a continuous supply of ATP, and is stimulated by a complete complement of L-amino acids. Furthermore, the reaction is potently inhibited by known antagonists of protein synthesis, including chloramphenicol and puromycin, as well as by ribonuclease, confirming that intact RNA is essential for the synthetic process. The observation that DNAase does not inhibit the RNA-stimulated incorporation—whereas it abolishes incorporation in cruder extracts—proves that the added RNA functions downstream of DNA.

We have shown that ribosomal RNA preparations from both E. coli and yeast, as well as viral RNA from tobacco mosaic virus, can robustly stimulate amino acid incorporation. The stoichiometric, rather than catalytic, relationship between the amount of added RNA and the amount of protein synthesized strongly supports the concept that these RNA molecules are serving as direct physical templates for polypeptide assembly. Most significantly, however, we discovered that a completely synthetic polynucleotide, polyuridylic acid, can direct this system with absolute specificity. The addition of polyuridylic acid to the cell-free extract resulted in the exclusive and massive incorporation of L-phenylalanine, yielding a product that mimics poly-L-phenylalanine. This extraordinary finding demonstrates that the specific nucleotide sequence of the RNA template dictates the specific amino acid sequence of the resulting protein. The complete inactivity of mixed poly(A,U) polymers or double-stranded structures further underscores the requirement for a specific, single-stranded nucleotide sequence to convey this information.

The implication that polyuridylic acid functions as a synthetic messenger RNA, directing the synthesis of a polypeptide containing only a single amino acid, represents a critical breakthrough in our understanding of the genetic code. It provides direct experimental proof that a sequence composed entirely of uridylic acid residues contains the specific coding dictionary for the amino acid phenylalanine. This discovery not only confirms the messenger RNA hypothesis but also provides a universal biochemical assay for deciphering the entire genetic code. By utilizing synthetic polynucleotides of varying, defined compositions within this stable, cell-free system, it should now be possible to systematically determine the specific nucleotide sequences—the codons—that correspond to each of the twenty naturally occurring amino acids.

In conclusion, we have developed a stable, highly active cell-free system whose protein-synthetic activity is strictly governed by the addition of template RNA. The successful programming of this system with a synthetic polynucleotide to produce a defined polypeptide unequivocally establishes the flow of genetic information from RNA to protein. This methodology opens the door to the complete elucidation of the genetic code and provides a powerful new tool for investigating the fundamental mechanisms of translation and its regulation.
