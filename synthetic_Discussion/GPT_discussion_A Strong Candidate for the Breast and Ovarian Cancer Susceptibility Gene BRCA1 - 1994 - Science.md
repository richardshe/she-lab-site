The genetic and molecular evidence presented here strongly suggests that the transcription unit described in this report is BRCA1. The gene lies within the genetically defined interval on chromosome 17q21, is expressed in tissues relevant to the inherited cancer phenotype, and contains rare sequence alterations in individuals from kindreds segregating breast and ovarian cancer susceptibility. The most compelling evidence is the detection of five different candidate predisposing mutations in eight kindreds chosen because of linkage or haplotype sharing at 17q21. Three of these mutations are frameshift or nonsense changes that would be expected to truncate the BRCA1 protein, one is an inferred regulatory mutation that appears to extinguish expression of one allele, and one is a missense substitution that may alter protein function. These findings are consistent with the behavior expected for a tumor suppressor gene involved in inherited cancer predisposition.

The diversity of the observed mutations indicates that BRCA1 susceptibility alleles are unlikely to be represented by a single common founder mutation. Rather, deleterious alleles may occur throughout the coding region or in sequences that control transcription, splicing, or RNA stability. This conclusion has important implications for genetic diagnosis. Screening strategies limited to a small number of nucleotide changes will identify some carriers but will not be sufficient for comprehensive testing. The large size of the gene, the presence of multiple coding exons, and the complexity of alternative splicing near the 5' end will complicate mutation detection. Nevertheless, the identification of BRCA1 provides the essential molecular substrate for carrier testing in high-risk families and for studies of risk in populations not represented in the present sample.

Several observations argue that loss or reduction of BRCA1 function is likely to be the common mechanism by which these alleles confer susceptibility. The 11-bp deletion in exon 2 would remove the zinc finger motif near the NH2-terminus, whereas the nonsense and insertion mutations would delete portions of the COOH-terminal region. The apparent regulatory mutation in kindred 2035 would be expected to reduce the amount of functional BRCA1 transcript produced from the inherited allele. In each case, the effect is compatible with a recessive mode of action in tumor cells, in which the inherited mutant allele is followed by somatic loss or inactivation of the remaining wild-type copy. Direct examination of tumors from mutation carriers will be necessary to test this prediction and to determine whether loss of heterozygosity, second intragenic mutation, or another mechanism eliminates normal BRCA1 function.

The relation between BRCA1 genotype and clinical phenotype remains incompletely defined. Kindreds carrying different mutations vary in age of breast cancer diagnosis, frequency of ovarian cancer, and occurrence of bilateral disease. Some of these differences may reflect ascertainment, family size, or chance clustering of tumors, but they may also indicate that distinct BRCA1 alleles have different tissue-specific effects. The existence of unaffected elderly carriers further indicates that penetrance is not absolute. Modifier genes, reproductive factors, environmental exposures, and stochastic events may all influence whether a carrier develops breast cancer, ovarian cancer, both diseases, or neither.

The predicted BRCA1 protein contains an NH2-terminal zinc finger domain but otherwise has no clear similarity to known proteins. This feature suggests a role in nucleic acid binding, transcriptional regulation, or protein interactions, but the biochemical function of BRCA1 remains unknown. Conservation of BRCA1-related sequences in several mammalian species should facilitate functional studies in experimental systems. Isolation of BRCA1 therefore represents not only a step toward clinical identification of inherited susceptibility, but also an entry point into the molecular pathways that restrain tumor development in breast and ovarian epithelium.
