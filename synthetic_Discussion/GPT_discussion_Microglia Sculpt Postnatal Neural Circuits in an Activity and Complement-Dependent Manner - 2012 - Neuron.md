Together, our findings identify microglia as active participants in activity-dependent remodeling of developing CNS synapses. During peak retinogeniculate pruning, microglia engulf RGC-derived presynaptic elements, and this engulfment is temporally restricted to the period in which excess inputs are normally eliminated. Importantly, microglia preferentially engulf inputs from the less active eye following manipulations of retinal activity, suggesting that they contribute to the competitive refinement of synaptic connections rather than passively clearing degenerating debris.

These data further define CR3/C3 signaling as a molecular pathway linking complement-dependent synapse elimination to microglial phagocytosis. Disruption of either C3 or the microglia-expressed receptor CR3 decreases engulfment and produces persistent defects in eye-specific segregation and synapse density, indicating that transient developmental failures in microglial function can have lasting effects on circuit architecture. Thus, complement may mark subsets of weak or inappropriate synapses for removal by microglia. More broadly, these results suggest that immune-related mechanisms can sculpt healthy neural circuits and may contribute to pathological synapse loss when aberrantly activated.
