These findings suggest that patient-specific NT-hESCs can be established from somatic nuclei of donors differing in age, sex, and disease status, and that such lines can retain pluripotency, normal karyotypes, and genetic identity with the nuclear donor. The improved derivation efficiency, together with the use of human feeder cells and direct blastocyst outgrowth, represents an important step toward research and possible therapeutic applications. Nevertheless, substantial barriers remain before clinical use can be considered. Xenoexposure during cell isolation, uncertain mitochondrial inheritance, epigenetic stability, differentiation fidelity, and long-term genomic integrity must be rigorously evaluated. Equally important, immune tolerance, safety, and functional efficacy of differentiated derivatives require testing in appropriate preclinical models before patient-specific transplantation can be responsibly advanced.
