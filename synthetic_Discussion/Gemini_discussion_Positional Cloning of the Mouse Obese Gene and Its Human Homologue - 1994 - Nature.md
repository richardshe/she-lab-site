The successful positional cloning of the mouse *obese* (*ob*) gene and its human homologue represents a significant milestone in our understanding of the molecular basis of energy homeostasis. For decades, the physiological regulation of body weight has been suggested by classical experiments, yet the specific components of the underlying signaling pathways remained elusive. The isolation of *ob* provides the first definitive evidence of a molecular link between adipose tissue and the central nervous system, effectively moving the study of obesity from the realm of descriptive physiology into the modern era of molecular genetics.

The technical success of this positional cloning effort—utilizing a massive genetic cross of 1,606 meioses and a 2.2-megabase physical map of YAC and P1 clones—demonstrates the power of this "forward genetics" approach to identify genes based solely on their mutant phenotype. Because the primary biochemical defect in *ob* mice was unknown, conventional biochemical approaches to isolate the gene product were unsuccessful. By pinpointing the *ob* locus on chromosome 6 and utilizing exon trapping to isolate candidate genes within the 650-kilobase critical region, we have identified a novel, adipose-tissue-specific transcript that is fundamentally disrupted in two independent mutant mouse strains.

The most striking feature of the *ob* gene is its highly restricted expression pattern. Both Northern blot analysis and high-sensitivity RT-PCR demonstrate that *ob* mRNA is expressed exclusively in white adipose tissue. This tissue specificity strongly suggests that the *ob* gene product functions as a sensor for the size of the body's fat depots. The discovery that *ob* encodes a 167-amino-acid protein with a functional N-terminal signal sequence further supports the hypothesis that it is a secreted hormone. Our in vitro translation experiments with microsomal membranes provide clear evidence of translocation and signal-sequence cleavage, resulting in a processed protein of approximately 16 kilodaltons. These findings are perfectly consistent with earlier parabiosis (cross-circulation) experiments, which suggested that *ob/ob* mice are deficient in a circulating factor that normally regulates food intake and body weight.

The characterization of the *ob* mutations in different mouse strains provides deep insight into the gene's function and its place within a homeostatic feedback loop. In the original C57BL/6J *ob/ob* strain, we identified a nonsense mutation at arginine 105. This premature stop codon presumably results in a truncated, non-functional protein. Notably, we observed a twenty-fold increase in *ob* mRNA levels in the adipose tissue of these mice. This massive upregulation suggests the existence of a compensatory feedback mechanism: in the absence of a functional *ob* signal, the adipose tissue attempts to normalize energy balance by overproducing the transcript. In contrast, the *ob2J* strain fails to synthesize any *ob* RNA, likely due to a promoter mutation or large structural rearrangement. The identical phenotype of these two strains—profound obesity, hyperphagia, and type II diabetes—confirms that the absence of the *ob* protein is the primary cause of the morbid obesity syndrome.

The high degree of evolutionary conservation of the *ob* gene across vertebrates is another remarkable finding. The 84% amino-acid identity between the mouse and human proteins implies that the physiological system regulating energy balance has been subject to intense selective pressure throughout vertebrate evolution. The ability to store energy efficiently as fat and to regulate those stores according to environmental availability was undoubtedly a critical survival advantage during periods of famine. The conservation of the human *ob* gene suggests that the same "adipostat" mechanism discovered in mice is also at work in our own species.

The identification of *ob* immediately raises several critical questions regarding human obesity. While the *ob/ob* mouse is a model of monogenic, recessive obesity, human morbid obesity is typically more complex and polygenic. Nevertheless, the *ob* gene provides a molecular entry point into this complexity. It is now possible to determine whether certain cases of human obesity are caused by mutations in the *ob* gene itself or by defects in the regulation of its expression. Furthermore, the secretion of the *ob* protein suggests that its primary site of action is likely the brain, specifically the hypothalamus, which is known to coordinate appetite and metabolic rate. This implies the existence of a specific *ob* receptor, which may correspond to another of the known mouse obesity mutations, such as *diabetes* (*db*).

Historically, the search for the *ob* gene has been guided by the work of researchers like Douglas Coleman, whose parabiosis experiments in the 1970s accurately predicted the nature of the *ob* and *db* defects. Our molecular cloning of *ob* confirms his insightful hypothesis: *ob* encodes the signal, and *db* likely encodes the receiver. The isolation of the *ob* gene product now allows for the production of recombinant protein, which will be essential for testing its therapeutic potential. If the administration of the *ob* protein can normalize the weight and metabolic abnormalities of *ob/ob* mice, it will represent a new paradigm for treating obesity—protein replacement therapy for a hormonal deficiency.

In conclusion, the positional cloning of the *ob* gene has transformed our understanding of body weight regulation. By identifying a secreted factor that is produced specifically by fat cells and whose absence leads to profound obesity, we have defined the first molecular component of the system that balances energy intake and expenditure. This discovery not only provides a long-awaited explanation for the *ob* phenotype in mice but also establishes a new framework for investigating the pathogenesis and treatment of human obesity. The high conservation of the gene and its elegant feedback regulation suggest that we have uncovered a fundamental and ancient biological system for the management of energy reserves.
