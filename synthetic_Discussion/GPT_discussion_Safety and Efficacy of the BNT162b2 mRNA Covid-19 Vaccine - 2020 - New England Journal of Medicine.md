The data reported here show that a two-dose regimen of BNT162b2, administered 21 days apart, was highly effective in preventing Covid-19 in persons 16 years of age or older. Among participants without evidence of previous SARS-CoV-2 infection, 8 cases of Covid-19 with onset at least 7 days after the second dose occurred among vaccine recipients, as compared with 162 cases among placebo recipients, corresponding to a vaccine efficacy of 95.0%. Efficacy was similarly high in the analysis that included participants with or without evidence of prior infection. The probability that vaccine efficacy exceeded the prespecified success criterion was high, and the observed lower bound of the interval estimate was well above the efficacy threshold that had been established for regulatory consideration.

The findings are notable in the context of a rapidly expanding pandemic and a trial conducted during periods of substantial community transmission. Protection was evident after completion of the vaccination regimen and was also suggested during the interval between the first and second doses. The cumulative incidence curves began to separate approximately 12 days after the first dose, a time at which vaccine-induced immune responses would be expected to emerge. Although the trial was not designed to define the efficacy of a single-dose regimen, these observations are consistent with the biologic plausibility of early protection after the first dose and strong protection after the second dose.

Vaccine efficacy was generally consistent across subgroups defined by age, sex, race, ethnic group, body-mass index, and the presence of coexisting conditions. These subgroup analyses were descriptive and were based on smaller numbers of cases than the primary analysis, but the point estimates were similar to the overall estimate. This consistency is important because the burden of Covid-19 has been unevenly distributed across demographic groups and is greater among older adults and persons with certain underlying medical conditions. The enrollment of a large and diverse population, including substantial numbers of older participants, obese participants, and participants with coexisting conditions, supports the applicability of the results to populations at increased risk for Covid-19 and its complications.

The number of cases of severe Covid-19 was small, but the distribution favored BNT162b2. Among 10 cases of severe Covid-19 with onset after the first dose, 9 occurred among placebo recipients and 1 occurred among vaccine recipients. These data do not provide a precise estimate of efficacy against severe disease, but they are encouraging and are concordant with the high efficacy observed against symptomatic Covid-19. Continued follow-up will be important to better define protection against severe outcomes, hospitalization, and death, particularly as additional cases accrue.

The safety profile observed in this trial was characterized primarily by transient local and systemic reactogenicity. Pain at the injection site was the most common local reaction and was usually mild or moderate, with severe pain reported by less than 1% of participants. Systemic events, especially fatigue and headache, were more frequent among vaccine recipients than among placebo recipients and were more common after the second dose than after the first dose. Fever and chills were also observed more often after the second dose. These events generally occurred during the first 1 to 2 days after vaccination and resolved shortly thereafter. Younger vaccine recipients reported systemic events more frequently than older recipients, a pattern that is consistent with reactogenicity findings from other vaccines and with the phase 1 experience with BNT162b2.

Unsolicited adverse events, serious adverse events, and deaths did not suggest a safety concern over the period reported here. The imbalance in reported adverse events between the vaccine and placebo groups was largely attributable to reactogenicity events, which were recorded as adverse events more frequently among vaccine recipients. Lymphadenopathy occurred more often among BNT162b2 recipients than among placebo recipients and is plausibly related to vaccination. Serious adverse events were uncommon and occurred at similar frequencies in the two groups. No deaths were considered by the investigators to be related to vaccine or placebo, and no Covid-19-associated deaths were observed during the reporting period. The prespecified monitoring criteria for vaccine-associated enhanced disease were not met.

The results also support the clinical strategy embodied by the BNT162b2 vaccine platform. BNT162b2 is a nucleoside-modified RNA vaccine encoding the prefusion stabilized, membrane-anchored full-length SARS-CoV-2 spike protein. Previous phase 1 data showed robust neutralizing antibody responses and antigen-specific cellular immune responses after two 30-microgram doses. The clinical efficacy shown in the present trial provides evidence that this immunogenicity translates into protection against symptomatic disease. The trial does not establish a correlate of protection, and immunogenicity analyses from the phase 2/3 population are ongoing. Such analyses may help inform future vaccine development, the interpretation of immune responses in special populations, and possible strategies for booster vaccination.

Several limitations should be considered. The median safety follow-up after the second dose was approximately 2 months for the main safety population, and longer-term safety data remain necessary. Although uncommon adverse events can be detected only in larger populations and with longer observation, the size of this trial provides substantial short-term safety information. The trial was not powered to evaluate very rare adverse events, nor was it powered to precisely assess efficacy in all subgroups or against severe Covid-19. Children younger than 16 years of age, pregnant persons, and immunocompromised persons were not represented or were represented only to a limited extent in the analyses reported here. Additional studies and postauthorization surveillance will be needed to assess safety and efficacy in these populations.

The trial evaluated prevention of symptomatic Covid-19 confirmed by nucleic acid amplification testing. It did not directly assess prevention of asymptomatic infection, viral shedding, or transmission. If vaccination reduces asymptomatic infection or infectiousness, the population-level benefit may be greater than that measured by the primary end point; if it does not, nonpharmaceutical interventions may remain particularly important during vaccine deployment. The durability of protection is also unknown. Continued blinded follow-up and ongoing surveillance will be essential to determine the duration of efficacy, the need for additional doses, and the effect of viral evolution on vaccine performance.

Operational considerations are also relevant. The vaccine regimen evaluated here requires two doses given 21 days apart and storage and distribution procedures appropriate for an mRNA vaccine. These requirements may pose challenges for rapid deployment, particularly in settings with limited cold-chain capacity. Nevertheless, the high efficacy observed in this large multinational trial indicates that BNT162b2 has the potential to make a substantial contribution to controlling the Covid-19 pandemic, provided that vaccination can be delivered broadly and equitably.

In conclusion, BNT162b2 administered as two 30-microgram doses 21 days apart was safe over the period assessed and provided 95% protection against Covid-19 among persons 16 years of age or older. The vaccine was associated mainly with transient local and systemic reactions, and serious adverse events were uncommon. Ongoing follow-up will define longer-term safety, durability of protection, and efficacy against severe disease and in populations not fully represented in this analysis.
