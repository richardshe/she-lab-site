Base editing represents a transformative shift in genome engineering, offering a method for precise point mutation correction without the deleterious consequences associated with double-strand DNA breaks (DSBs). By repurposing CRISPR/Cas9 as a programmable DNA-binding scaffold rather than a nuclease, we have decoupled base conversion from the error-prone non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) pathway. This approach effectively addresses the primary limitation of homology-directed repair (HDR), which is often inefficient and induces high indel frequencies in many cell types.

Our development of second- and third-generation base editors (BE2 and BE3) underscores the necessity of manipulating endogenous cellular responses to achieve high editing yields. By incorporating uracil glycosylase inhibitor (UGI) and utilizing a Cas9 nickase to direct mismatch repair, we successfully subverted base-excision repair pathways that would otherwise revert edited sites. The resulting system achieves permanent C→T conversion with efficiencies up to 75% and remarkably low indel formation. Given that the majority of known pathogenic human variants are point mutations, the ability to perform precise, single-nucleotide changes without donor templates significantly expands the therapeutic potential of genome editing, providing a powerful new tool for basic research and clinical applications.
