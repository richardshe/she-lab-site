The discovery of functional lymphatic vessels lining the dural sinuses fundamentally challenges the long-held dogma that the central nervous system lacks a classical lymphatic drainage system. Our characterization of these vessels demonstrates that they possess the anatomical, molecular, and functional hallmarks of initial lymphatic vasculature, including the expression of Lyve-1, Prox1, podoplanin, and VEGFR3, and the ability to drain both cerebrospinal fluid and immune cells directly into the deep cervical lymph nodes. The unique, concealed location of these vessels within the meninges likely explains how they eluded detection until now. 

This unexpected anatomical connection provides a clear physical pathway for the continuous immune surveillance of the brain and the clearance of CNS-derived antigens. These findings compel a comprehensive reassessment of basic assumptions in neuroimmunology. Furthermore, the identification of a direct CNS-to-peripheral immune system conduit sheds critical new light on the mechanisms underlying neuroinflammation. Dysfunction in this meningeal lymphatic drainage could significantly contribute to the aetiology and progression of neurodegenerative diseases characterized by impaired protein clearance and deleterious immune cell infiltration, opening entirely new therapeutic avenues.
