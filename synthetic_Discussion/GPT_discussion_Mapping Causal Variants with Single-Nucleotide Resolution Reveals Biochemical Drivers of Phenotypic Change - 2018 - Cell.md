The central challenge in quantitative genetics has been to move from loci to mechanisms. Linkage analysis and GWAS have identified thousands of genomic regions associated with human disease, agriculturally important traits, and evolutionary divergence. Yet these associations usually remain descriptive because causal variants are inherited together with nearby passengers. Here we show that this limitation is not intrinsic to genetics. By combining a restricted number of segregating polymorphisms with many generations of inbreeding and dense genotyping, we were able to map quantitative trait nucleotides across the yeast genome with single-nucleotide resolution. This strategy transforms meiotic recombination from a coarse tool for assigning traits to chromosomal intervals into a systematic source of natural allele replacements.

Our results reveal that the genetic architecture of complex traits is richer than can be appreciated from conventional QTL studies. Even in a compact genome and a highly controlled cross, most traits were not explained by one or two loci of large effect. Instead, phenotypic variation commonly arose from many variants of modest effect, often distributed across coherent functional networks. The azole-resistance traits illustrate this principle. A standard analysis would have highlighted a strong QTL at UPC2 and a small number of additional loci, but fine mapping uncovered a broader set of causal polymorphisms that converged on ergosterol biosynthesis, membrane composition, and pleiotropic drug response. Thus, single-nucleotide mapping preserves the statistical power of inbred crosses while adding the mechanistic resolution that is usually obtained only through directed follow-up experiments.

The ability to resolve adjacent variants also uncovered a surprising amount of local genetic complexity. A common assumption in QTL dissection is that one causal mutation explains one locus, with the remaining linked variants functioning as neutral passengers. This simplifying model was frequently incorrect. At SKY1 and IXR1, multiple mutations within a single gene influenced the same trait, sometimes in opposing directions. In such cases, the net effect of a parental haplotype can obscure the contributions of the individual alleles that compose it. The SKY1 example is especially instructive because both parental haplotypes were nearly neutral in 5-FU, whereas recombinant haplotypes exposed the functional consequences of each mutation. These observations argue that naturally evolving genomes should often be interpreted as mosaics of partially compensatory, reinforcing, or buffered variants rather than collections of independent drivers.

We also found that functionally coupled variants were frequently linked across short genomic distances, including in different genes. This observation has implications for how selection acts on eukaryotic genomes. Because recombination continually breaks apart favorable combinations of alleles, physical linkage can preserve beneficial multi-variant architectures across generations. Conversely, linkage may also allow deleterious alleles to persist when they are carried with variants that improve fitness in the same environment. Although yeast lacks operons, our data show that phenotypic units of inheritance can nevertheless extend beyond individual genes. This provides a genetic explanation for why haplotype blocks can retain biological meaning even when the underlying genome is not organized into obvious functional clusters.

The molecular identities of causal variants were also more diverse than expected. Missense variants were enriched, particularly among alleles of large effect, but they accounted for less than half of the causal variants we mapped. Regulatory and synonymous mutations made substantial contributions to phenotypic diversity. The discovery of a synonymous UPC2 variant controlling pan-azole resistance illustrates how natural variation can expose mechanisms that are poorly captured by current computational predictors. Synonymous mutations are often treated as neutral by default, yet they can alter codon optimality, translation, protein abundance, or other features of gene expression. Similarly, promoter mutations such as the PMP3 allele can create conditional changes in expression that become visible only in the relevant environmental context. These findings emphasize that causal inference based solely on variant class or conservation will miss important drivers of quantitative traits.

Our study also demonstrates the value of natural genetic variation as a functional genomics resource. Classical deletion and overexpression collections have been extraordinarily powerful in yeast, but they sample a restricted space of perturbations. Natural alleles are different. They include subtle changes in coding sequence, expression, protein stability, regulation, and genetic background dependence. They also have survived evolutionary filtering, making them enriched for alleles that can alter phenotype without necessarily abolishing gene function. The derived mutations in our cross were frequently beneficial in at least one condition, contrasting with the expectation from random mutagenesis that most new variants are deleterious. Thus, natural variation provides a complementary toolkit for discovering gene function and for learning which molecular changes are compatible with cellular fitness.

Several limitations define the boundaries of this approach. Yeast is unusually favorable for single-nucleotide mapping because it has a small genome, high recombination rate, inexpensive cultivation, and tractable population sizes. Larger genomes, lower recombination rates, and denser polymorphism will all increase the number of individuals required to distinguish neighboring variants. In addition, variants of very small effect, traits with strong environmental noise, rare higher-order epistasis, and phenotypes requiring developmental or tissue-specific contexts will remain challenging. Our design also intentionally restricted parental divergence, which improved mapping precision but may exclude alleles that segregate between more distant lineages. These tradeoffs do not diminish the value of the approach, but they indicate that single-nucleotide mapping will be most powerful when experimental design is matched to genome size, recombination density, and trait heritability.

Despite these constraints, the principles developed here are general. Reducing the density of segregating variation, increasing the number of independent recombination events, and preserving large genotyped cohorts should improve causal mapping in any organism where controlled crosses are possible. In yeast, these principles allowed us to move directly from phenotype to nucleotide, and from nucleotide to biochemical mechanism, across many traits. More broadly, our data suggest that much of the missing mechanistic content in quantitative genetics is not inaccessible; it is hidden within linkage. Once linked variants can be separated with sufficient resolution, complex traits become collections of interpretable molecular perturbations. Single-nucleotide mapping therefore offers a practical path toward a more complete genotype-to-phenotype map, one in which the causal variants underlying phenotypic change can be discovered systematically rather than inferred one locus at a time.
