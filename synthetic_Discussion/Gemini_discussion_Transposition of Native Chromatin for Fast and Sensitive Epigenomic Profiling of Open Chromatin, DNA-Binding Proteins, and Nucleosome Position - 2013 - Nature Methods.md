The ATAC-seq method described here represents a significant methodological advance for the field of epigenomics. By leveraging the hyperactive Tn5 transposase to simultaneously fragment and tag accessible DNA, ATAC-seq collapses the multi-day, complex protocols of DNase-seq and FAIRE-seq into a rapid, two-step process. Crucially, we demonstrate that this streamlined approach does not sacrifice sensitivity or specificity; ATAC-seq generates chromatin accessibility maps highly concordant with established methods but requires several orders of magnitude less input material.

Beyond simple accessibility profiling, the precise insertion patterns of Tn5 allow for the simultaneous extraction of multidimensional epigenetic data from a single assay. The characteristic insert size distribution uniquely reveals local nucleosome positioning, phasing, and compaction states. Furthermore, deeper sequencing yields nucleotide-resolution footprints of DNA-binding proteins, enabling the inference of transcription factor occupancy genome-wide without the need for target-specific antibodies.

The dramatic reduction in input requirements—down to 500 cells—opens new avenues for interrogating rare cell populations, such as stem cells, transient developmental intermediates, and specific clinical biopsies, which were previously inaccessible to epigenomic profiling. As a proof of principle, we generated a personal, allele-specific epigenomic map from a standard blood draw within a clinically relevant timeframe, accurately identifying the transcription factor network driving IL-2 expression in a human proband. This capability to construct patient-specific regulatory networks rapidly and from primary tissues underscores the potential of ATAC-seq to transition epigenomic analysis from basic research to precision medicine and clinical diagnostics.
