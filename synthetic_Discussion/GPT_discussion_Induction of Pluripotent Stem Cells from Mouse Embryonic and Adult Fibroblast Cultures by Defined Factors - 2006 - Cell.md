In this study, we have shown that pluripotent stem cells can be generated directly from mouse fibroblast cultures by the introduction of a small number of defined factors. Among 24 candidate genes selected on the basis of their roles in ES cells and early embryos, the combination of Oct3/4, Sox2, c-Myc, and Klf4 was sufficient to induce colonies that activated the Fbx15 locus, acquired ES cell-like morphology and proliferation, expressed many ES cell marker genes, and differentiated into derivatives of all three germ layers. These findings demonstrate that the differentiated state of fibroblasts can be reversed without nuclear transfer or cell fusion and indicate that reprogramming can be achieved by defined molecular components.

The present results support the idea that transcriptional networks responsible for maintaining ES cell identity can also participate in the induction of pluripotency. Oct3/4 and Sox2 are core regulators of pluripotent cells, and their requirement in our assay is consistent with their central roles in early embryos and ES cells. Klf4 and c-Myc are not generally viewed as lineage determinants, but both have been implicated in ES cell self-renewal and proliferation. Their inclusion may create a cellular context in which Oct3/4 and Sox2 can activate endogenous pluripotency-associated genes. c-Myc may contribute not only by promoting cell growth but also by altering chromatin structure or cellular metabolism, thereby permitting transcriptional remodeling. Klf4 may cooperate with Oct3/4 and Sox2 in the activation of ES cell-specific genes and may also oppose differentiation-associated programs. Thus, the induction of iPS cells is likely to reflect combined effects on transcription, chromatin, proliferation, and cell survival rather than the action of a single pathway.

Unexpectedly, Nanog was not required for the initial induction of iPS cells in our system. Nanog is essential for maintaining the undifferentiated state of ES cells under certain conditions and is expressed in the inner cell mass and ES cells. However, forced Nanog expression was dispensable in the factor combinations that produced iPS cells, and endogenous Nanog was activated after reprogramming. These observations suggest that Nanog may act downstream of the initial reprogramming events induced by Oct3/4, Sox2, c-Myc, and Klf4, or that its function can be partially substituted during the induction phase. It remains possible that Nanog expression contributes to the stabilization or quality of the pluripotent state after the Fbx15 locus has been activated.

Although iPS cells resemble ES cells in many respects, they are not identical to ES cells. iPS-MEF4 and iPS-MEF10 clones expressed many ES cell marker genes, showed alkaline phosphatase activity, had high telomerase activity, formed embryoid bodies, generated teratomas containing derivatives of all three germ layers, and contributed to embryonic development after blastocyst injection. Nevertheless, endogenous Oct3/4 and Sox2 expression levels were lower than in ES cells, and several ES cell-enriched genes, including Dnmt3a, Dnmt3b, Dnmt3l, Utf1, Tcl1, and the LIF receptor gene, were expressed more strongly in ES cells than in iPS cells. In addition, the Oct3/4 and Nanog promoters were only partially demethylated, and iPS cells required feeder cells for maintenance. These differences suggest that reprogramming by the four factors induces a pluripotent state that is closely related to, but distinct from, that of ES cells.

The comparison between iPS-MEF4 cells and iPS-MEF3 cells emphasizes that activation of some ES cell markers is not sufficient to establish pluripotency. The combination of Oct3/4, Klf4, and c-Myc was able to activate the Fbx15 locus and some ES cell-associated genes, but the resulting colonies had different morphology, failed to activate several important markers, and produced tumors composed of undifferentiated cells rather than tissues of three germ layers. Thus, the formation of G418-resistant colonies in the Fbx15 assay is a useful indicator of reprogramming but does not by itself prove pluripotency. Complete assessment requires gene-expression analysis, epigenetic characterization, and functional differentiation assays. These observations also indicate that Sox2 has an important role in converting partially reprogrammed cells into pluripotent cells.

The use of Fbx15 as a selectable marker was important for the identification of iPS cells, because Fbx15 is specifically expressed in ES cells and early embryos but is dispensable for pluripotency and development. Selection through this locus probably allowed the recovery of cells that had undergone substantial but not necessarily complete reprogramming. The fact that iPS cells are not identical to ES cells may reflect the properties of this selection system. Selection for activation of other loci, such as Oct3/4 or Nanog, may yield cells more similar to ES cells. Alternatively, additional factors, different culture conditions, longer selection periods, or removal of exogenous transgenes may be required to obtain cells that more completely match ES cells.

Several observations argue against the possibility that the iPS cells described here arose from contamination by preexisting ES cells. The starting populations were embryonic or adult fibroblast cultures, each iPS clone had a unique retroviral integration pattern, adult tail-tip fibroblasts gave rise to iPS cells with the expected genetic background, and GFP-labeled iPS cells contributed to embryonic tissues after blastocyst injection. In addition, some iPS clones had normal karyotypes, and subcloning experiments showed that individual iPS cells could give rise to colonies that retained pluripotent differentiation capacity. These data indicate that iPS cells were generated from somatic cells by the introduced factors.

The generation of iPS cells from adult tail-tip fibroblasts is particularly important, because it shows that reprogramming is not limited to embryonic fibroblasts. Adult somatic cells have undergone more prolonged differentiation and environmental exposure, yet they could still be converted into pluripotent cells by the same four factors. This raises the possibility that patient-specific pluripotent cells might be generated from accessible somatic tissues. Such cells could provide useful systems for disease modeling, drug screening, and, in principle, cell replacement therapy without the ethical issues associated with human embryos or the immune rejection problems associated with unrelated donor cells.

Several issues must be resolved before this approach can be considered for therapeutic application. The use of retroviral vectors leads to permanent integration of transgenes and creates the possibility of insertional mutagenesis. In addition, c-Myc is a potent oncogene, and Klf4 has also been implicated in tumor biology. Teratoma formation demonstrates pluripotency but also highlights the tumorigenic potential of undifferentiated cells. Therefore, safer methods for inducing pluripotency will be required, such as transient expression systems, excisable vectors, or replacement of oncogenic factors with less harmful molecules or culture conditions. It will also be important to determine whether iPS cells can generate germline chimeras and whether iPS cells derived under improved conditions are fully equivalent to ES cells at molecular and functional levels.

The present study provides a defined experimental system for dissecting the mechanisms of nuclear reprogramming. Because reprogramming can be initiated by introducing known factors into somatic cells, it should be possible to analyze the sequence of molecular events that leads from a fibroblast state to a pluripotent state. The system may also be useful for identifying additional genes and small molecules that enhance reprogramming efficiency or improve the quality of iPS cells. Our findings show that pluripotency can be induced by a surprisingly small set of factors and suggest that the barriers separating differentiated and pluripotent states can be overcome by defined transcriptional regulators.
