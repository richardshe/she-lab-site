The results presented here provide compelling evidence for the general nature of the genetic code, specifically supporting the hypothesis that it is a triplet code, read continuously from a fixed starting point. By analyzing the effects of acridine-induced mutations in the rII region of bacteriophage T4, we have demonstrated that these mutations likely represent the addition or deletion of a single base pair, which causes a shift in the reading frame of the genetic message. The ability to restore wild-type function by combining three mutations of the same sign (either three additions or three deletions) is a crucial finding; it strongly indicates that the fundamental coding unit, the codon, must consist of three nucleotides, or a multiple thereof.

Furthermore, our data indicate that the code is non-overlapping. If the code were overlapping, a single base change would be expected to alter multiple adjacent amino acids, which is inconsistent with biochemical evidence from other studies. The concept of a degenerate code is also supported by our findings, as it appears that multiple different triplets can code for the same amino acid, reducing the lethality of certain point mutations. The suppression of reading frame shifts by compensatory mutations further emphasizes the continuous nature of translation. These findings fundamentally shape our understanding of how genetic information is translated into protein structure, effectively ruling out overlapping codes and providing a clear framework for the future elucidation of the specific nucleotide assignments for each amino acid.
