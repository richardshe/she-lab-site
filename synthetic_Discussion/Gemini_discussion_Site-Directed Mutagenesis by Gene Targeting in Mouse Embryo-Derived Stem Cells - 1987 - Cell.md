The experiments presented in this study provide definitive evidence that endogenous genes in mammalian cells can be specifically inactivated through homologous recombination with newly introduced DNA vectors. By utilizing mouse embryo-derived stem (ES) cells and the hypoxanthine phosphoribosyl transferase (Hprt) gene as a model system, we have established a robust methodology for site-directed mutagenesis that fundamentally circumvents the limitations of random insertional or chemical mutagenesis. The ability to dictate the precise nature of the genetic alteration at a predetermined locus is a transformative advance, offering unprecedented control over the mammalian genome.

A critical component of this success was the rigorous optimization of the neo gene construct. Because gene targeting events are inherently rare, the efficient expression of the selectable marker from within the target locus is paramount. By engineering pMC1Neo to incorporate a highly active mutant polyoma enhancer, an efficient HSV-tk promoter, and an optimized mammalian translation initiation sequence, we significantly augmented the frequency of recovering G418-resistant transformants. This enhancement effectively increases the pool of cells in which the targeting event can be detected, a factor that will be indispensable when extending this technology to genes that are transcriptionally silent in ES cells. 

Our comparative analysis of sequence replacement and sequence insertion vectors revealed that both approaches successfully mediate homologous recombination at the endogenous Hprt locus, albeit with mechanistic distinctions. Replacement vectors generated the anticipated allelic substitution, resulting in a clean disruption of the target gene. In contrast, sequence insertion vectors, designed to integrate the entire plasmid into the chromosomal locus, predominantly yielded the expected duplication of homologous sequences. Interestingly, a subset of insertion vectors also produced replacement-type events. This dual outcome indicates that mammalian recombination machinery can resolve these intermediates through multiple pathways, suggesting that the topology of the input DNA significantly influences the final genomic architecture. Regardless of the vector class, the frequency of accurate targeting was strongly correlated with the extent of sequence homology between the exogenous construct and the endogenous gene. The largest vectors evaluated achieved targeting frequencies approaching one in every thousand G418-resistant cells, a rate highly amenable to experimental isolation even in the absence of a secondary selection like 6-thioguanine.

The choice of ES cells as the recipient line is strategically central to the broader biological implications of this work. Because ES cells retain their developmental pluripotency after extensive in vitro manipulation, including electroporation and clonal expansion, they serve as a unique bridge between cultured cells and the whole organism. A targeted mutation introduced into an ES cell can theoretically be incorporated into the germline of a chimeric mouse, allowing for the subsequent breeding of animals carrying the designed genetic defect. This capacity essentially enables the reverse-genetic study of mammalian biology: moving from a cloned DNA sequence to a specific mutation in a living animal. 

This protocol represents a generalized strategy that should be broadly applicable for targeting virtually any cloned gene within the mouse genome. The finding that targeting occurs at a workable frequency without requiring a gene-specific selection strategy beyond the acquisition of the neo marker implies that non-selectable genes are now accessible to targeted disruption. This opens the door to systematically dissecting complex developmental pathways by sequentially disabling key regulatory genes and observing the resulting phenotypic consequences in vivo. Furthermore, the ability to engineer precise mutations will facilitate the creation of highly accurate mouse models of human genetic diseases. For instance, the creation of Hprt-deficient mice through this methodology directly models Lesch-Nyhan syndrome, providing an invaluable in vivo system for testing potential gene therapies or pharmacological interventions.

In summary, we have demonstrated that gene targeting in mammalian ES cells is a feasible and practical method for generating site-specific mutations. The efficiency of the targeting reaction is governed by the length of homology and the optimal expression of the selectable marker. This technical achievement marks a pivotal transition in mammalian genetics, transitioning the field from a reliance on spontaneous or randomly induced mutations toward an era of deliberate, rationally designed genomic engineering. If this technology proves as generalizable as our initial results suggest, it will profoundly accelerate our understanding of gene function in mammalian development and disease.
