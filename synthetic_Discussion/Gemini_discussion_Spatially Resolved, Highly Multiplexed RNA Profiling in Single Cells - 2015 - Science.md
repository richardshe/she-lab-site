The development of multiplexed error-robust fluorescence in situ hybridization (MERFISH) marks a significant advancement in our ability to perform spatially resolved, system-wide transcriptomic profiling within single cells. By implementing highly multiplexed combinatorial labeling alongside novel error-correcting and error-detecting encoding schemes, we have fundamentally overcome the exponential scaling of misidentification errors that has traditionally plagued sequential smFISH methodologies. Our demonstration of simultaneously mapping 140 RNA species with high accuracy using the modified Hamming distance 4 (MHD4) code, and scaling this to 1001 RNA species utilizing the MHD2 code, underscores the immense potential of this approach for dissecting the complex molecular architecture of single cells in their native spatial context.

A defining strength of MERFISH is its quantitative precision, which we validated by demonstrating excellent agreement with both conventional smFISH and bulk RNA sequencing measurements over a dynamic range of three orders of magnitude. This sensitivity, capable of detecting transcripts present at less than one copy per cell on average, is critical for capturing the complete transcriptional landscape. The error-robust nature of our encoding schemes—particularly the MHD4 code, which enables the detection and correction of single-bit errors—was essential for maintaining a high calling rate while suppressing false positives to negligible levels. Although the 14-bit MHD2 code lacked error-correction capabilities, resulting in a predictable reduction in the overall calling rate, it nonetheless provided reliable relative abundance measurements for a massive thousand-gene network, proving that trade-offs between multiplexing capacity and absolute detection efficiency can be managed effectively based on experimental needs.

The massive single-cell data sets generated by MERFISH offer unparalleled opportunities to unravel gene regulatory networks through the analysis of stochastic cell-to-cell variations in gene expression. Our co-variation analysis of both the 140-gene and 1001-gene panels successfully identified numerous clusters of co-regulated genes, grouping them into functionally distinct modules such as those driving extracellular matrix production, cell cycle progression, and cellular motility. Notably, these analyses allowed us to putatively assign biological functions to dozens of previously unannotated genes based entirely on their tight co-expression with well-characterized gene clusters. This guilt-by-association approach demonstrates how high-throughput single-cell profiling can serve as a powerful hypothesis-generation engine for functional genomics, identifying novel participants in complex regulatory pathways and pinpointing potential transcriptional regulators driving these modular programs.

Furthermore, because MERFISH is an imaging-based technique, it inherently preserves the precise subcellular localization of every detected transcript, adding a crucial spatial dimension absent from traditional single-cell RNA sequencing methods. Our spatial correlation analyses revealed distinct classes of RNA localization, identifying groups of transcripts tightly restricted to the perinuclear region—likely reflecting co-translational translocation at the rough endoplasmic reticulum for secreted and membrane-bound proteins—and other groups specifically enriched at the cell periphery, corresponding predominantly to transcripts encoding cytoskeletal and motility-associated proteins. This robust capacity to link the spatial distribution of mRNAs directly to the function of their encoded proteins opens a new window into understanding how local translation and active RNA transport contribute to the establishment of cellular polarity, structure, and dynamic function.

In conclusion, MERFISH transforms smFISH from a targeted assay into a true systems-biology tool. The combination of error-robust encoding, combinatorial labeling, and high-resolution imaging provides an unprecedented view of the single-cell transcriptome, seamlessly integrating quantitative expression profiling with high-resolution spatial mapping. As we continue to refine optical sectioning and computational algorithms to handle increasingly dense molecular crowding, MERFISH will undoubtedly become instrumental for mapping distinct cell types in complex tissues in situ, resolving local regulatory networks, and illuminating the spatial logic of gene expression in health and disease.
