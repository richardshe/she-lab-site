# Discussion

Our study elucidates the biochemical mechanism of DNA targeting by the Type II CRISPR-Cas system, revealing that the Cas9 endonuclease relies on a dual-RNA guide consisting of mature crRNA and a trans-activating tracrRNA. We demonstrate that target recognition requires specific base-pairing between the crRNA and the complementary DNA strand, as well as the presence of a short protospacer adjacent motif (PAM). Upon target binding, Cas9 utilizes its HNH and RuvC-like nuclease domains to generate blunt-ended double-strand breaks. Crucially, we show that the dual-tracrRNA:crRNA complex can be engineered into a single chimeric guide RNA capable of directing sequence-specific Cas9 cleavage in vitro. This fundamental discovery highlights the remarkable elegance of bacterial adaptive immunity and unveils a powerful, highly programmable platform for RNA-directed genome editing. By simply altering the guide RNA sequence, Cas9 can be targeted to virtually any DNA sequence of interest, paving the way for versatile and precise genetic engineering applications across diverse organisms.
