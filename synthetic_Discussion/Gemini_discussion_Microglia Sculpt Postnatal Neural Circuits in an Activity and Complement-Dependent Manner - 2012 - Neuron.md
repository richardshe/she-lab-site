Our findings establish microglia as active participants in the activity-dependent refinement of CNS circuits. By demonstrating that microglia physically engulf presynaptic RGC terminals in the dLGN during a peak developmental period, we identify a crucial cellular mechanism for synaptic pruning. This process is strikingly selective; through pharmacological manipulation of retinal activity, we show that microglia preferentially eliminate "weaker" inputs, suggesting a direct role in the competition that shapes neural connectivity. Mechanistically, we identify the CR3/C3 signaling axis as a primary driver of this engulfment. The sustained deficits in eye-specific segregation and the significantly increased synapse density observed in CR3- and C3-deficient mice underscore the physiological importance of this phagocytic pathway. These results transition our understanding of microglia from simple immune responders to essential architects of the healthy developing brain, with significant implications for neurodevelopmental disorders characterized by aberrant synaptic pruning.
