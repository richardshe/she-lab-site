The discovery of lymphatic vessels within the dura mater challenges the long-standing view that the central nervous system is devoid of classical lymphatic drainage. These vessels possess the molecular, anatomical and ultrastructural hallmarks of initial lymphatics, yet occupy a distinctive perisinusal niche that may explain why they escaped detection in previous studies. Their ability to take up cerebrospinal fluid-derived tracers and to contain immune cells indicates that they represent a functional route for both soluble and cellular constituents to leave the meningeal compartment.

Our findings further suggest that drainage from the cerebrospinal fluid to the deep cervical lymph nodes is not mediated exclusively through extracranial pathways such as the nasal mucosa, but also through bona fide meningeal lymphatic vessels. This route provides a structural basis for the immune surveillance of the central nervous system and may influence the composition of meningeal immune populations. The presence of a potentially similar structure in human dura raises the possibility that dysfunction of this pathway contributes to neurological diseases in which immunity, protein clearance or cerebrospinal fluid homeostasis are altered.
