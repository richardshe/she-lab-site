# Discussion

The results of this phase 1 clinical trial provide compelling evidence that STI571, a specific inhibitor of the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase, is an exceptionally effective and well-tolerated therapy for patients with chronic-phase chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) who have failed prior treatment with interferon alfa. The demonstration that a targeted small-molecule kinase inhibitor can induce such profound hematologic and cytogenetic responses validates the central hypothesis of targeted cancer therapy: that drugs designed specifically against the molecular abnormality driving a malignancy can yield dramatic clinical benefits with minimal collateral toxicity.

The clinical activity of STI571 in this heavily pretreated patient population is unprecedented. Complete hematologic responses were achieved in 98 percent (53 of 54) of patients treated with daily doses of 300 mg or more. These responses were not only universal at therapeutic doses but also rapid, typically occurring within the first four weeks of therapy. Even more striking is the rate of cytogenetic responses. In this phase 1 study, 54 percent of patients receiving 300 mg or more achieved a cytogenetic response, with 31 percent achieving a major cytogenetic response and 13 percent achieving complete cytogenetic remission. Historically, achieving any major cytogenetic response in patients resistant to interferon alfa has been exceedingly rare with conventional chemotherapy. The depth of these responses is further highlighted by the fact that some patients who achieved complete cytogenetic remission also became negative for BCR-ABL by FISH and PCR.

Equally remarkable to its efficacy is the safety profile of STI571. In stark contrast to standard cytotoxic chemotherapy and interferon alfa, which are associated with severe and often dose-limiting systemic toxicities, STI571 was extraordinarily well tolerated. The most common adverse events—nausea, myalgias, edema, and diarrhea—were predominantly mild to moderate (grade 1 or 2) and rarely necessitated discontinuation of the drug. Importantly, a maximal tolerated dose was not identified within the tested range of 25 to 1000 mg per day. The dose-limiting toxicities typically seen with broad-spectrum antineoplastic agents, such as severe mucositis, alopecia, or severe myelosuppression, were notably absent. While mild to moderate anemia and cytopenias were observed, they were generally manageable and often transient, possibly reflecting the clearance of the leukemic clone prior to the recovery of normal Philadelphia chromosome-negative hematopoiesis.

The pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic data confirm the mechanism of action of STI571. The drug achieved therapeutic plasma concentrations at once-daily doses of 300 mg and above, which correlated precisely with the onset of profound clinical responses. Furthermore, our assays of BCR-ABL kinase activity, utilizing the phosphorylation status of the surrogate marker CRKL, demonstrated that the doses required to achieve hematologic responses correspond directly to the doses required to inhibit BCR-ABL kinase activity in vivo. This tight correlation provides definitive proof that the antileukemic effect of STI571 is a direct result of its intended molecular target inhibition.

The success of STI571 represents a paradigm shift in oncology. For decades, cancer drug development has largely relied on the empirical screening of compounds for general cytotoxic activity, leading to therapies with narrow therapeutic indices. The development of STI571, from the initial understanding of the molecular pathogenesis of CML—the Philadelphia chromosome and the resultant constitutively active BCR-ABL kinase—to the rational design and clinical testing of a specific inhibitor, serves as the ultimate proof-of-concept for molecularly targeted therapy. 

While the short-term results are highly encouraging, several questions remain to be answered in subsequent trials. The durability of these responses and the potential for the emergence of clinical resistance through secondary mutations in the BCR-ABL kinase domain require long-term follow-up. Additionally, the efficacy of STI571 in the more advanced, genetically complex accelerated and blast phases of CML needs to be determined. Future studies will also need to evaluate the role of STI571 as a frontline therapy for newly diagnosed CML and explore its potential in combination with other agents, including interferon or conventional chemotherapy.

In summary, STI571 is a highly active and remarkably safe oral agent for the treatment of chronic-phase CML. By specifically targeting the fundamental molecular lesion responsible for the disease, STI571 not only provides a highly effective therapeutic option for patients with interferon-resistant CML but also ushers in a new era of rationally designed, targeted cancer therapeutics.
