The identification of Piezo1 and Piezo2 solves a long-standing mystery in sensory biology by providing the molecular basis for mechanically-activated (MA) cation currents in vertebrates. While mechanotransduction is essential for fundamental processes such as touch, hearing, and blood pressure regulation, the identity of the primary mammalian transducers has remained elusive for decades. Our results demonstrate that Piezos are both necessary for endogenous MA currents in Neuro2A cells and sufficient to confer mechanosensitivity to several heterologous cell lines that otherwise lack these responses.

Piezo proteins are structurally unique, characterized by an exceptionally large number of predicted transmembrane domains—between 24 and 36. This architectural complexity distinguishes them from other known ion channel families and likely reflects the specialized requirements for sensing tension within the lipid bilayer. The broad conservation of Piezos across diverse eukaryotic kingdoms, including plants and ciliates, suggests that this protein family represents an ancient and evolutionarily successful solution to the fundamental challenge of mechanotransduction.

Functionally, we show that Piezo1 and Piezo2 mediate currents with distinct kinetic signatures. Piezo2 exhibits significantly faster inactivation than Piezo1, a property that is likely tuned to specific physiological needs. In dorsal root ganglia, the specific reduction of rapidly-adapting currents following Piezo2 knockdown strongly implicates it as a primary transducer for touch or noxious mechanical stimuli. In contrast, the wider distribution of Piezo1 in tissues such as the lung, bladder, and kidney suggests it may be involved in sensing fluid flow or organ distension.

Collectively, these findings establish Piezos as essential components of vertebrate MA cation channels. Future work must definitively demonstrate that Piezos form the ion-conducting pore themselves and elucidate how their massive structure couples mechanical force to channel gating. Understanding the mechanosensory role of Piezos will undoubtedly provide profound insights into mammalian physiology and the pathogenesis of various mechanosensory disorders.
