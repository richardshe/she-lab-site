The mutations described here identify a set of zygotic genes which are required for the establishment of the segmented pattern of the Drosophila larva. Their phenotypes show that segmentation is not a single event, nor can it be understood simply as the assignment of segmental identities to a pre-existing series of equivalent units. Rather, the pattern seems to be generated through several successive levels of spatial subdivision. The three classes of mutants define defects in regions of different size: continuous portions of the embryo, repeated units of two segments, and fractions of single segments. This order of effects suggests that the normal embryo is first organized along its antero-posterior axis, then subdivided in a double-segmental periodicity, and finally resolved into the individual segmental pattern.

The gap mutants are the simplest to interpret in terms of axial position. In Krüppel, hunchback and knirps a continuous stretch of the body pattern is missing, and the affected region is characteristic for each locus. These mutations therefore appear to disturb the allocation of unique positions along the length of the embryo. The terminal regions are not affected in the same way as the middle regions, and in Krüppel a posterior terminal pattern is followed by a mirror-image arrangement. Such phenotypes are reminiscent of those produced by maternal-effect mutations, but they are caused here by the zygotic genome. It is therefore likely that the maternal information present in the egg is not sufficient by itself to specify the entire segmental array, but must be interpreted by zygotic genes which define broad regions of the embryo.

The pair-rule mutants reveal a second and quite different level of organization. In these embryos the pattern is not deleted in one continuous block, but in a repeated fashion at intervals of two segments. The deleted regions do not correspond to whole segments, and their margins do not coincide with segment boundaries. Moreover, different loci remove different parts of the same double-segmental repeat. These observations make it unlikely that the primary defect is the absence of every other segment as an already determined unit. Instead, the data suggest that, before the final segmental boundaries are established, the embryo contains a periodic pattern with a wavelength corresponding to two future segments. The normal segmental series may be derived by subdivision or refinement of this earlier periodicity.

The combinations with homeotic mutations support this interpretation. In paired and even-skipped embryos, the altered pattern remains pairwise even when the identity of particular segments is changed by Ultrabithorax or Polycomb. Conversely, the homeotic transformations do not correct or otherwise modify the basic deletion pattern caused by the pair-rule mutation. The processes affected by pair-rule genes must therefore be distinct from those which determine segmental identity. They concern the number and position of segmental elements rather than the character assigned to them. The composite structures in paired and knirps larvae further show that segmental identity can be expressed in parts of an abnormal pattern, and that it is not dependent on the formation of normal segmental borders.

The segment polarity mutants define a third level of pattern formation, within the segment itself. In these embryos the normal number of segments, or at least the normal number of repeated segmental elements, is retained, but a defined part of each element is deleted and another part is duplicated with reversed polarity. The phenotype is therefore not a general failure of differentiation, nor a transformation of one segment into another. It is a local alteration of the arrangement within each segmental repeat. This suggests that after the embryo has acquired its segmental periodicity, further genetic functions are required to specify the anterior-posterior polarity and the relative positions of structures within each segment.

The frequent association of deletion and mirror-image duplication is especially informative. It indicates that the remaining part of the pattern can be organized symmetrically when the missing region is not specified. In the segment polarity mutants this occurs within each segment, whereas in Krüppel a similar principle may operate over a much larger part of the embryo. Such mirror-image arrangements suggest that neighbouring cells may acquire their pattern not only from absolute position, but also from local organizing influences which can be duplicated or reversed when a normal boundary or signalling region is absent. The mutants do not yet distinguish whether the affected genes act in the production of such local signals, in the response of cells to them, or in the stabilization of the resulting pattern.

All the genes described here act zygotically, yet their effects are already visible before or shortly after gastrulation. This timing is important because the larval cuticle is a late product of development, whereas the mutations alter the pattern at a much earlier stage. The reduced number of ventral bulges in paired and even-skipped embryos, the short germ bands of Krüppel, runt and knirps, and the temperature-sensitive period of paired all indicate that these loci act during the blastoderm or early gastrula stages. Thus the genetic programme for segmentation is initiated before the visible formation of larval structures, at a time when the blastoderm still consists of a simple epithelium.

The number of loci recovered in our screens suggests that the major zygotic components of segmentation may be relatively few. This conclusion must be cautious, since some genes may have escaped detection because of weak phenotypes, maternal contributions, or functions required earlier for viability. Nevertheless, the mutations obtained fall into coherent phenotypic classes, and many alleles were recovered repeatedly. It seems therefore probable that these loci represent a substantial fraction of the genes whose zygotic function is specifically required to establish the larval segmental pattern.

The phenotypes provide a genetic framework for analysing how a metameric pattern is generated. Maternal determinants may define the global coordinates of the egg. Gap genes then appear to establish broad regions along these coordinates. Pair-rule genes impose a repeated pattern with a two-segment periodicity, and segment polarity genes organize the final pattern within each segment. Homeotic genes, acting in a different process, assign identities to the segments or to parts of them. This hierarchy is inferred from mutant phenotypes rather than from direct observation of gene products, and its steps may overlap in time. Even so, the distinct classes of mutations show that the segmented body plan is assembled by separable genetic processes. The study of these genes should make it possible to determine how positional information is converted into a precise series of segmental units.
