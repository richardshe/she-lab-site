The results presented here identify ferroptosis as a regulated form of cell death that is distinct from apoptosis, necrosis, and autophagy. Ferroptosis is initiated by a specific set of lethal stimuli, requires intracellular iron, and proceeds through the accumulation of oxidative damage, including lipid ROS. This process lacks the defining morphological and biochemical features of apoptosis and necrosis, is not suppressed by inhibitors of caspases, RIPK1, lysosomal function, or autophagy, and is governed by a genetic network that differs from that required for apoptotic death. Thus, ferroptosis expands the known repertoire of regulated cell death mechanisms and suggests that mammalian cells contain additional death programs that may be engaged under particular metabolic and pathological conditions.

Several observations suggest that ferroptosis is not simply nonspecific oxidative toxicity. First, erastin and RSL3 cluster together by modulatory profiling and are separated from many other oxidative and nonoxidative lethal agents. Second, erastin-induced death requires a discrete collection of genes, including IREB2, RPL8, ATP5G3, CS, TTC35, and ACSF2, whose silencing protects against erastin but not against diverse unrelated lethal stresses. Third, ferroptosis can be suppressed even several hours after erastin treatment by antioxidants, iron chelators, or MEK inhibition, indicating that cell death depends on the continued operation of a regulated oxidative process rather than an immediate catastrophic injury. These data support the idea that ferroptosis is a specific iron-dependent death pathway in which metabolic state, iron handling, and lipid oxidation converge.

Our data also define system xc- as a key upstream node in erastin-induced ferroptosis. Erastin, like glutamate and sulfasalazine, inhibits Na+-independent cystine uptake, and this blockade can be bypassed by beta-mercaptoethanol or attenuated by increased expression of SLC7A11. Because cystine import is required for cysteine availability and glutathione synthesis, inhibition of system xc- likely creates a deficiency in the antioxidant capacity of the cell. In this setting, iron-dependent reactions and ROS-generating enzymes can drive lipid oxidation beyond a survivable threshold. The ability of RSL3 to induce ferroptosis without inhibiting cystine uptake suggests that ferroptosis can be initiated at multiple points, either by disabling antioxidant defenses upstream or by perturbing downstream components that control lethal lipid oxidation.

The involvement of iron metabolism is a central feature of this pathway. Iron chelation suppresses both ROS accumulation and death, exogenous iron potentiates erastin lethality, and IREB2-dependent control of iron uptake, storage, and metabolism modulates sensitivity to ferroptosis. These findings suggest that a bioavailable iron pool is required to amplify or execute the oxidative reactions that kill cells. The precise chemical species responsible for this toxicity remain to be established, but the dependence on lipid ROS and the activity of Fer-1 are consistent with a model in which iron-dependent lipid peroxidation is a critical lethal event. The requirement for ACSF2, CS, and glutamine metabolism further suggests that the production or remodeling of specific lipid substrates may be important for sensitizing membranes to ferroptotic damage.

The identification of ferrostatin-1 provides both a probe for ferroptosis and a starting point for therapeutic exploration. Fer-1 is structurally and functionally distinct from several canonical antioxidants and suppresses ferroptosis with high potency. Structure-activity relationships indicate that both the aromatic amine groups and the lipophilic N-cyclohexyl moiety are important for activity, supporting the hypothesis that Fer-1 acts as a membrane-localized radical scavenger. The ability of Fer-1 to block glutamate-induced toxicity in organotypic hippocampal slice cultures suggests that ferroptosis-like mechanisms are not restricted to transformed cells and may operate in neural tissue exposed to excitotoxic or oxidative stress. This observation links a small-molecule-defined cancer cell death process to long-studied forms of iron-dependent neurotoxicity.

Ferroptosis may therefore have opposing therapeutic implications in cancer and degenerative disease. In tumors, especially those with oncogenic RAS or altered redox metabolism, induction of ferroptosis may provide a means to exploit metabolic vulnerabilities that are not addressed by apoptosis-inducing agents. The RAS-selective activity of erastin, the sensitivity of some cancer cells to system xc- inhibition, and the contribution of NOX and PPP-dependent ROS production all suggest that ferroptosis may be particularly relevant in cells with high oxidative burden or enhanced dependence on cystine import. Conversely, in pathological states such as stroke, epilepsy, trauma, or neurodegeneration, inhibition of ferroptosis may protect vulnerable cells from iron-dependent oxidative death.

Important questions remain. The molecular target of RSL3, the complete set of lipid species involved in ferroptotic execution, and the relative contribution of NOX enzymes, mitochondrial metabolism, and other ROS sources in different cell types require further definition. Likewise, it will be necessary to determine whether ferroptosis occurs in vivo under physiological or disease-relevant conditions and whether its modulation can be achieved with useful selectivity. Nonetheless, the present findings establish ferroptosis as a distinct, druggable, iron-dependent form of nonapoptotic cell death and provide a framework for understanding how perturbations in amino acid transport, antioxidant defense, iron metabolism, and lipid oxidation can be integrated into a regulated lethal process.
