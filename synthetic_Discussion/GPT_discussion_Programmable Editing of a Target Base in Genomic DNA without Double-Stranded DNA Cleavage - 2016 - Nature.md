The results described here establish base editing as a general strategy for the programmable conversion of one genomic base pair into another without requiring double-stranded DNA cleavage or a donor DNA template. By coupling the targeting capabilities of CRISPR/Cas9 with the chemistry of cytidine deamination, and by engineering the subsequent cellular repair response, base editors convert C:G base pairs to T:A base pairs with efficiencies that substantially exceed those observed with Cas9-mediated homology-directed repair, while greatly reducing indel formation.

The activity window, sequence preferences, and processivity of these editors impose important constraints on their use, especially when additional cytidines are present near the desired target base. Nevertheless, the correction of disease-relevant APOE and TP53 alleles demonstrates that these constraints are compatible with precise and productive changes in mammalian cells. The observed off-target editing at a subset of known Cas9 off-target sites further indicates that guide RNA specificity remains a central determinant of base editor precision.

Future improvements in editor architecture, deaminase choice, delivery, and guide RNA design should expand the range of editable sequences and further improve specificity. Base editing therefore offers a complementary approach to genome editing, particularly for applications in which efficient correction of point mutations is required.
