The derivation of human embryonic stem (ES) cell lines from blastocysts represents a major methodological and conceptual advance in developmental biology. Consistent with previous work in nonhuman primates, our results demonstrate that these cells can be maintained in an undifferentiated state for prolonged periods while retaining their pluripotency. The ability of these cell lines to differentiate into derivatives of all three embryonic germ layers—endoderm, mesoderm, and ectoderm—underscores their remarkable developmental potential. Unlike somatic cells, which undergo replicative senescence, these human ES cells express high levels of telomerase activity, suggesting they possess an extended, if not indefinite, replicative life-span in culture.

This extended replicative capacity, coupled with their pluripotency, positions human ES cell lines as a powerful in vitro model for understanding fundamental aspects of human embryogenesis and early development. Currently, much of our understanding of early mammalian development is derived from murine models. While invaluable, significant developmental differences exist between mice and humans, making human-specific models essential for accurate biological insights.

Beyond basic developmental biology, these cell lines hold profound implications for drug discovery and toxicology. Human ES cells could provide a continuous source of normal human cells and tissues for high-throughput screening of pharmacological compounds, potentially reducing the reliance on animal testing and offering more accurate predictions of human responses to drugs.

Perhaps most significantly, human ES cells offer unprecedented potential for transplantation medicine. The ability to direct the differentiation of these cells into specific cell types, such as neural cells for neurodegenerative diseases, cardiomyocytes for heart failure, or pancreatic beta cells for diabetes, could revolutionize the treatment of numerous devastating conditions. However, realizing this clinical potential will require overcoming significant hurdles, including the challenge of directing differentiation uniformly and addressing the potential for immune rejection of transplanted tissues. Future research must focus on defining the signaling pathways that govern ES cell differentiation and exploring strategies to mitigate immune responses, such as genetic modification or the establishment of diverse ES cell banks. In conclusion, the establishment of human ES cell lines provides a transformative tool that will undoubtedly catalyze major advances in both basic research and clinical applications.
