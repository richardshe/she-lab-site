The data presented here demonstrate that the biological properties of in vitro-transcribed mRNA can be substantially improved by the incorporation of a naturally occurring modified nucleoside. Among the modifications tested, pseudouridine was unique in conferring two desirable characteristics to mRNA: it markedly increased translational capacity and, at the same time, diminished innate immune activation. These findings indicate that the limitations that have restricted the therapeutic use of synthetic mRNA are not intrinsic to RNA as a class of vectors, but can be altered by changing the chemical composition of the transcript.

The enhanced translation observed with Ψ-containing mRNA was unexpected, because pseudouridine is not known to be a constituent of natural mRNA. Nevertheless, replacement of uridine with Ψ did not interfere with protein synthesis in mammalian translation systems. On the contrary, Ψ-containing luciferase, β-galactosidase, and GFP mRNAs produced more encoded protein than the corresponding unmodified transcripts in reticulocyte lysates, cultured mammalian cell lines, and several primary cell types. This enhancement was most pronounced in intact cells, where Ψ-containing mRNA reproducibly yielded higher numbers of protein-expressing cells and greater levels of reporter activity. Importantly, the effect was observed with transcripts containing different 5'- and 3'-terminal structures and was additive with cap, translational enhancer sequences, and poly(A) tails. Thus, Ψ modification appears to improve mRNA function through a mechanism distinct from those established structural elements that facilitate translation.

The time-course experiments suggest that increased biological stability contributes to the superior performance of Ψ-containing mRNA. Although luciferase activity from both modified and unmodified transcripts was detectable shortly after RNA delivery, the difference between the two RNAs increased with time. In cultured cells, Ψ-containing transcripts continued to support higher levels of protein expression at later time points, and, in mice, luciferase activity remained detectable at 24 hours only after administration of Ψ-modified mRNA. Northern blot analyses confirmed that administered RNA could be recovered from the spleen in intact and partially degraded forms, supporting the conclusion that chemical modification can alter the persistence of synthetic mRNA in vivo. Whether this increased stability reflects resistance to ribonucleases, altered intracellular trafficking, reduced engagement of antiviral pathways, or improved association with translation complexes remains to be determined.

The increased translational capacity of Ψ-containing mRNA was not simply a consequence of avoiding RIG-I-mediated interferon induction. Although unmodified RNAs containing 5'-triphosphates are capable of activating RIG-I, Ψ-containing mRNA was translated more efficiently in both wild-type and RIG-I-deficient cells. Moreover, capped unmodified transcripts that induced little IFN-β in wild-type cells still translated less efficiently than Ψ-containing counterparts. These results suggest that Ψ directly improves the functional behavior of the RNA in mammalian cells, rather than acting only by suppressing one antiviral signaling pathway. Because modified nucleosides can influence RNA secondary structure, ribosome scanning, codon-anticodon interactions, and RNA-binding protein recognition, any of these properties may contribute to the higher translational output observed here.

Equally important, Ψ-containing mRNA lacked the strong immunostimulatory activity of unmodified RNA following systemic administration. At doses that supported robust splenic protein expression, unmodified mRNA induced high serum IFN-α and increased splenic TNF-α mRNA, whereas Ψ-modified mRNA did not. This result extends previous in vitro observations that nucleoside modifications suppress RNA recognition by TLRs and RIG-I and shows that the same principle applies after in vivo delivery. For gene replacement, such nonimmunogenicity is essential, because repeated or high-dose administration of an inflammatory RNA vector would be expected to limit expression and produce unacceptable toxicity. For vaccination, the ability to tune immunogenicity independently from antigen expression may also be valuable, allowing Ψ-containing mRNA to be combined with selected adjuvants when immune activation is desired.

The present study also reveals that not all modified nucleosides are suitable for therapeutic mRNA design. Some modifications, such as m5C and m5U, were compatible with translation, whereas s2U and especially m6A, when substituted throughout the transcript, severely reduced or abolished protein production. These observations underscore that the position, abundance, and chemical nature of nucleoside modification are critical determinants of mRNA function. In natural RNAs, modifications are placed selectively, but in the present approach every occurrence of a given nucleotide was replaced during transcription. Future studies may identify combinations or partial substitutions that further optimize translation, stability, and immunological properties.

Our in vivo experiments showed preferential reporter expression in the spleen after intravenous delivery of lipofectin-complexed mRNA. This distribution likely reflects the delivery vehicle and route of administration rather than an absolute tissue restriction of Ψ-containing mRNA. Development of improved formulations and targeting strategies should expand the range of tissues that can be transfected efficiently. Nevertheless, the present results establish proof of principle that chemically modified synthetic mRNA can mediate protein expression in vivo while avoiding potent systemic cytokine induction.

Taken together, these findings support Ψ-containing mRNA as a superior vector for transient gene expression. It combines rapid protein production, lack of genomic integration, improved biological stability, and markedly reduced immunogenicity. These attributes make modified mRNA especially attractive for applications in which short-term expression of a therapeutic protein is sufficient or desirable, including protein replacement, regenerative medicine, and immunotherapy. More broadly, the results suggest that nucleoside modification represents a general strategy for converting synthetic RNA from an inherently immunostimulatory molecule into a controllable and efficient platform for gene delivery.
