The discovery of multiple, unrelated phage proteins that inhibit the P. aeruginosa type I-F CRISPR/Cas system demonstrates that CRISPR immunity, like other bacterial anti-phage defences, is subject to direct antagonism by mobile genetic elements. These anti-CRISPR genes are sufficient to permit targeted phages to replicate in bacteria bearing an intact CRISPR/Cas system, and are required for productive infection by an anti-CRISPR phage that carries functional protospacers. Thus, in addition to mutation of protospacers or protospacer adjacent motifs, phages can use dedicated inhibitory proteins to evade sequence-specific bacterial immunity.

The anti-CRISPR proteins described here do not reduce cas gene transcription, CRISPR locus expression or processed crRNA abundance, indicating that they act after assembly or maintenance of the crRNA-Cas effector complex. This suggests that these proteins may interfere with target recognition, DNA binding, recruitment of Cas3, or target cleavage. Their lack of activity against a type I-E system further indicates that anti-CRISPR proteins can be highly specific for particular CRISPR/Cas subtypes, a property that should facilitate their use as probes of CRISPR/Cas mechanism.

The presence of related genes in prophages and in other predicted mobile elements suggests that anti-CRISPR activity may also promote horizontal gene transfer between bacterial strains. Such genes could allow incoming DNA to overcome resident CRISPR spacers that would otherwise block transfer. The sequence diversity and sparse database representation of these proteins indicate that many additional anti-CRISPR genes remain to be identified. Their discovery expands the known molecular strategies used in phage-bacterial co-evolution and reveals CRISPR/Cas immunity as a regulatory target for phage-encoded proteins.
