# Discussion

Our study fundamentally redefines the mechanism of non-canonical inflammasome signaling by identifying gasdermin D (Gsdmd) as the critical executor of caspase-11-dependent pyroptosis. Using an unbiased forward genetic screen and CRISPR/Cas9-mediated gene targeting, we established that Gsdmd is absolutely required for both pyroptotic cell death and interleukin-1β secretion in response to cytoplasmic lipopolysaccharide (LPS) and Gram-negative bacterial infections. We demonstrated that upon activation by intracellular LPS, caspase-11 directly cleaves pro-Gsdmd to release an active, 30-kDa N-terminal fragment. This single proteolytic event unleashes the pro-pyroptotic potential of Gsdmd, completely independent of the classical NLRP3-caspase-1 signaling axis.

Strikingly, our data reveal that Gsdmd cleavage serves as the vital molecular bridge linking the non-canonical caspase-11 pathway to the canonical NLRP3 inflammasome. The generation of the active Gsdmd N-terminal fragment triggers a cell-intrinsic signal that concurrently executes pyroptosis and activates NLRP3, leading to caspase-1-dependent processing of pro-IL-1β. Furthermore, we showed that human caspase-4 and caspase-5 share this conserved mechanism, highlighting the evolutionary importance of the gasdermin family in mammalian innate immunity. The profound protection against LPS-induced lethal septic shock observed in Gsdmd-deficient mice underscores the physiological relevance of this pathway in vivo. By identifying Gsdmd as the primary physiological substrate of caspase-11, our findings solve a major outstanding puzzle in innate immunity. Future investigations into the exact biochemical mechanism by which the Gsdmd N-terminal domain induces membrane pore formation and cytolysis will be crucial for developing novel therapeutic interventions against severe endotoxemia and autoinflammatory diseases.
