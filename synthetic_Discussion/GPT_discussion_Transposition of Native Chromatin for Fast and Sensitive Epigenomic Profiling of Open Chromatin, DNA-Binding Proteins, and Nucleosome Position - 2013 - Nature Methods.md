Here we have shown that direct transposition of native chromatin provides a rapid and sensitive route to integrative epigenomic profiling. ATAC-seq combines the experimental simplicity of tagmentation with the biological selectivity imposed by chromatin structure, generating sequencing-ready libraries from orders of magnitude fewer cells than existing chromatin-accessibility assays. From a single experiment, the resulting fragments report accessible regulatory DNA, nucleosome-associated and nucleosome-free regions, local spacing between nucleosomes and DNA-binding factors, and protected footprints consistent with factor occupancy.

The information content of ATAC-seq derives in part from the paired-end fragment-size distribution. Short insertions identify accessible sites and candidate factor-bound elements, whereas longer, phased fragments disclose nucleosome organization within regulatory chromatin. These measurements allowed us to distinguish chromatin states, recover canonical promoter architecture, and identify classes of factors with distinct relationships to adjacent nucleosomes. Such patterns suggest that the spatial coordination between transcription factors, chromatin remodelers and nucleosomes can be surveyed genome wide without separate assays for each component.

Several limitations remain. Transposase sequence bias, local chromatin compaction and cellular heterogeneity may influence insertion probability, and footprinting-based inference of factor binding should be interpreted together with motif quality, conservation and, when available, ChIP-seq validation. Nucleosome calls from fragment length are also most informative within accessible regions and may not replace genome-wide MNase-based maps of all nucleosomes.

Nevertheless, the speed and low input requirements of ATAC-seq make it well suited for samples that have been difficult to profile, including purified primary cell populations and serial clinical specimens. The ability to obtain a personalized regulatory map on a timescale compatible with medical decision-making suggests that open chromatin and factor-occupancy profiling may become practical components of functional genomics and, ultimately, precision medicine.
