The experiments described above indicate that a heat-stable RNA fraction is required for extensive amino acid incorporation in the cell-free system from E. coli. This requirement is not satisfied by soluble RNA, although soluble RNA is also needed for maximal activity. The active RNA fraction, therefore, appears to have a function different from that of amino acid acceptor RNA. Several observations suggest that this RNA acts as a template for protein synthesis. First, incorporation is proportional to the amount of added RNA at low concentrations, as would be expected if RNA molecules specify the formation of a limited amount of product. Secondly, the activity is destroyed by RNAase or alkali but not by trypsin, DNAase, or boiling. Thirdly, the reaction requires ribosomes, the 105,000 x g supernatant fraction, ATP and an ATP-generating system, and is inhibited by chloramphenicol and puromycin. These properties are those expected of protein synthesis rather than of nonspecific adsorption or exchange reactions.

The term ribosomal RNA has been used in this paper to designate the source of the RNA preparations. It should not be inferred from this terminology that the active material is necessarily identical with the structural RNA of ribosomes. The activity did not sediment with ribosomes, and preliminary fractionation showed that amino acid incorporation activity did not correspond simply with optical density at 260 mμ. It is possible that the active component is a minor RNA species extracted along with ribosomal RNA, or that a portion of the ribosomal RNA can acquire template activity after extraction. The greater activity of yeast ribosomal RNA and tobacco mosaic virus RNA also indicates that the E. coli system can respond to RNA molecules from other sources. These results are consistent with the view that the specificity of the product is determined principally by the added RNA rather than by the bacterial extract itself.

The experiments with polyuridylic acid provide the most direct evidence for this interpretation. Addition of this synthetic polynucleotide caused a marked incorporation of phenylalanine into a product which, after purification and hydrolysis, yielded radioactive phenylalanine. The reaction required ribosomes and the soluble enzyme fraction, was energy-dependent, and was inhibited by RNAase, puromycin, and chloramphenicol. Thus the incorporation stimulated by polyuridylic acid occurs through the same general mechanism as the incorporation stimulated by naturally occurring RNA preparations. The failure of UMP, UDP, UTP, polyadenylic acid, and mixed adenylic-uridylic acid polymers to replace polyuridylic acid shows that neither uracil nucleotides nor polyanionic character alone are sufficient for activity. The absence of activity in mixtures of polyuridylic and polyadenylic acids suggests further that the active polymer must be available in a single-stranded form.

Since omission of the other nineteen amino acids did not prevent phenylalanine incorporation, and since polyuridylic acid failed to stimulate the incorporation of the other amino acids examined, the simplest conclusion is that polyuridylic acid contains information which directs the incorporation of phenylalanine. The product has not yet been characterized completely, but its solubility behavior and the recovery of radioactive phenylalanine after acid hydrolysis are compatible with the formation of a polyphenylalanine-like material. These findings suggest that a sequence composed only of uridylic acid residues may specify a sequence composed only of phenylalanine residues.

The present data do not establish the length of the coding unit or the detailed mechanism by which the RNA template is read. They do, however, show that a defined synthetic polyribonucleotide can determine the amino acid incorporated in a cell-free protein-synthesizing system. This provides an experimental approach to the problem of the genetic code. By preparing polymers of known or partially known nucleotide composition and examining the amino acids incorporated in response to them, it should be possible to obtain direct information concerning the relationship between nucleotide sequence and amino acid sequence. The system described here therefore may be useful not only for the study of protein synthesis, but also for analysis of the chemical basis of genetic specificity.
