# Discussion

Our development of the cerebral organoid culture system marks a significant technological leap in our ability to model human brain development and disease in vitro. By optimizing 3D growth conditions without the restrictive use of exogenous patterning factors, we have empowered human pluripotent stem cells to rely on intrinsic self-organizing cues, resulting in the generation of complex, heterogeneous, and highly interdependent brain regions. Crucially, these organoids successfully recapitulate the intricate progenitor zone organization that is uniquely characteristic of the human developing cerebral cortex, notably the abundant presence of outer radial glia (oRG) and an inner fiber layer (IFL)—features critically absent in standard rodent models.

The utility of this system is powerfully demonstrated by our modeling of primary microcephaly using patient-derived induced pluripotent stem cells containing mutations in *CDK5RAP2*. Unlike mouse models which have largely failed to mirror the severe human phenotype, our patient-specific cerebral organoids exhibited a clear hypoplastic phenotype. Mechanistically, we discovered that this reduced tissue size is driven by premature neuronal differentiation at the expense of progenitor cell maintenance, likely due to defective horizontal spindle orientation during critical early proliferative divisions. This provides a compelling cellular mechanism for the pathogenesis of microcephaly. Ultimately, human cerebral organoids offer an unprecedented, physiologically relevant platform to investigate uniquely human aspects of neurodevelopment and to dissect the complex etiology of diverse neurological and psychiatric disorders.
