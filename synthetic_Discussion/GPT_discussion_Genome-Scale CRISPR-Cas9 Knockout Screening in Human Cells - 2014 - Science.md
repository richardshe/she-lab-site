Here we demonstrate that a pooled lentiviral CRISPR-Cas9 library can be used for genome-scale loss-of-function screening in human cells. The high efficiency of mutagenesis achieved by lentiCRISPR enabled robust depletion of sgRNAs targeting essential genes and strong enrichment of sgRNAs conferring drug resistance. In contrast to RNAi-based approaches, Cas9-mediated disruption can generate null alleles, which may explain the improved consistency observed among independent guides targeting the same gene and the high rate of validation for top candidates. Application of GeCKO screening to vemurafenib-treated melanoma cells recovered known resistance factors, including NF1 and MED12, and identified additional genes, such as NF2, CUL3, TADA1, and TADA2B, that suggest new mechanisms of therapeutic escape. Although off-target cleavage remains an important consideration, improved guide design and paired nicking strategies may further increase specificity. Genome-scale Cas9 knockout screening should therefore provide a general and powerful platform for systematic functional genomics in mammalian cells.
