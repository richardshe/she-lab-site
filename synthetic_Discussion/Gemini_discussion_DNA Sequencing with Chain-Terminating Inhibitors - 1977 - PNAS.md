# Discussion

The method described in this paper represents a fundamental advance in our ability to determine the primary structure of DNA. By employing 2',3'-dideoxynucleoside triphosphates (and arabinonucleoside analogues) as specific chain-terminating inhibitors of DNA polymerase I, we have developed a sequencing technique that is inherently more rapid, simpler, and more accurate than the previously established 'plus and minus' method. The underlying principle—that the incorporation of a nucleotide lacking a 3'-hydroxyl group irrevocably halts primer extension—allows for the generation of a complete nested set of radiolabeled oligonucleotide fragments terminating exclusively at specific bases. When resolved by high-resolution polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis, these fragments yield a clear banding pattern from which the nucleotide sequence can be directly and unequivocally read.

Our application of this technique to the genome of bacteriophage phiX174 has not only validated its efficacy but also allowed us to correct several errors and ambiguities present in the provisional sequence derived from older methods. For instance, the use of dideoxynucleotide terminators clearly resolved complex runs of nucleotides and identified missed insertions, such as the C-G-C trinucleotide at positions 4380–4381 and a missing cytosine at position 3547. Unlike the 'plus and minus' method, which often requires running both a plus and a minus system in parallel and sometimes yields ambiguous results requiring secondary confirmation, the chain-terminator method provides a definitive sequence read from a single set of four parallel reactions. Furthermore, by adjusting the ratio of terminating to normal triphosphates, the read length can be optimized, allowing stretches of 100 to over 120 nucleotides to be determined from a single priming site in a single experiment.

When using restriction enzyme fragments as primers, complications can arise if multiple adjacent restriction sites cause the primer to be cleaved unevenly after extension, resulting in overlapping sequence ladders. We have successfully circumvented this limitation by introducing the single-site ribo-substitution procedure. By substituting a single ribonucleotide at the priming site and subsequently cleaving it with ribonuclease or alkali, we ensure a uniform 5' end for all extended fragments prior to electrophoresis. This modification significantly enhances the versatility of the method, enabling the use of a wider variety of restriction fragments, including those generated by enzymes like Alu I, as primers.

While the chemical degradation method recently developed by Maxam and Gilbert also offers a rapid approach to DNA sequencing and is particularly advantageous for double-stranded DNA, the enzymatic chain-termination method described here requires less laborious sample preparation, as it bypasses the need for separating the strands of each restriction fragment. Instead, it relies simply on annealing a primer to a single-stranded template. 

In conclusion, the dideoxy chain-termination method provides a highly efficient and accurate tool for DNA sequencing. We anticipate that this technique will greatly accelerate the sequencing of viral genomes, plasmids, and ultimately, more complex cellular DNA. As the availability and quality of the dideoxynucleotide analogues improve, and as gel electrophoresis techniques are further refined to resolve longer polynucleotide chains, we believe this approach will become the standard methodology for elucidating the genetic information encoded within DNA.
