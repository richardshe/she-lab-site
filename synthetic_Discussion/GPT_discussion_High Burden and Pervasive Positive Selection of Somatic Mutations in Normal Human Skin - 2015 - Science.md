These data show that physiologically normal human skin is not a uniform population of genetically normal cells, but a dense mosaic of mutant clones shaped by environmental mutagenesis and positive selection. The burden of somatic mutations in aged, sun-exposed epidermis is comparable with that of many cancers, and the mutational spectra closely recapitulate those seen in cutaneous malignancies. Thus, the earliest genetic events in skin cancer are not rare accidents occurring in isolated precursor cells, but common consequences of life-long UV exposure across an apparently normal tissue.

The extent of positive selection is particularly striking. Mutations in NOTCH1, NOTCH2, FAT1, TP53, and other genes were present at high density and in substantial fractions of cells, despite the absence of clinical or histological abnormality. The close overlap between genes under selection in normal skin and those recurrently mutated in cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma indicates that many canonical cancer drivers can confer a selective advantage long before malignant transformation. Conversely, the absence of strong selection for CDKN2A point mutations suggests that some driver events may become advantageous only after earlier stages of clonal evolution have occurred. Cancer development therefore appears to depend not simply on whether a driver mutation is present, but on its order, cellular context, and interaction with the surrounding tissue.

The clone-size distribution provides an important constraint on this model. Although selected mutations were numerous, most mutant clones remained small, and the difference in size between driver and neutral clones was modest. This implies that expansion of mutant keratinocyte clones is limited by mechanisms operating within intact epidermis, potentially including spatial confinement, competition among neighboring clones, and density-dependent regulation. Such constraints may represent a major barrier preventing the frequent driver mutations observed here from progressing to overt cancer.

Our findings also distinguish clonal expansion from malignant potential. Activating FGFR3 mutations produced some of the largest clones, yet are associated with benign seborrheic keratoses rather than invasive cancer. Normal skin therefore contains many clones that are genetically advanced but biologically restrained. Understanding how these restraints fail, and how inherited background, environmental exposure, and local tissue architecture influence the selective landscape, will be central to explaining why only a small minority of mutant clones progress to cancer.
