The results show that cells with the growth characteristics and differentiative capacity of embryonal carcinoma cells can be obtained directly from normal mouse embryos without an intervening tumour stage. The delayed blastocyst culture system seems to provide a useful means of increasing the number of appropriate embryonic cells while preventing further organized development. Subsequent transfer to feeder layers then permits their continued proliferation in an undifferentiated state.

The normal karyotype of the cultures, including the recovery of both XX and XY lines, is important evidence that these cells are not contaminants from established EC cultures. Their ability to form teratocarcinomas after inoculation into syngeneic mice, and to give rise to embryoid bodies and differentiated tissues in vitro, indicates that they retain pluripotentiality after prolonged passage. Thus the malignant growth of EC cells is not a prerequisite for the establishment of pluripotential cell lines, although the culture requirements and morphology of EK cells are closely similar to those of feeder-dependent EC cells.

These observations suggest that the embryo contains cells capable of indefinite multiplication in vitro if isolated at, or brought to, an appropriate developmental state. Such cultures should provide a direct experimental system for analysing early mammalian differentiation, the relationship between normal embryonic cells and EC cells, and the conditions controlling the maintenance or loss of pluripotentiality.
