In this phase 1 study, repeated administration of the anti-PD-1 antibody BMS-936558 produced objective antitumor responses in patients with several advanced solid tumors, including non-small-cell lung cancer, melanoma, and renal-cell cancer. Responses were observed in heavily pretreated patients, across dose levels, and in diverse sites of metastatic disease. A substantial proportion of responses were durable, with many continuing for 1 year or longer. These findings provide clinical evidence that blockade of PD-1 can restore antitumor immunity in patients with established cancer and that the PD-1 pathway is an active mechanism of immune resistance in some human tumors.

The activity observed in non-small-cell lung cancer is particularly notable. Lung cancer has not traditionally been considered a tumor type in which immunotherapy has a major role, and previous immune-based approaches have had limited success. In the current study, objective responses were seen in both squamous and nonsquamous non-small-cell lung cancer, including responses that were sustained. This observation broadens the range of tumors in which immune-checkpoint blockade may be relevant and suggests that tumor types characterized by a high burden of somatic mutation may generate antigens capable of supporting an effective immune response once inhibitory signaling is relieved. Responses in melanoma and renal-cell cancer are also consistent with the known sensitivity of these diseases to immune modulation, but the magnitude and durability of benefit seen with PD-1 blockade suggest that this pathway may be distinct from, and potentially complementary to, other immunotherapeutic strategies.

The pattern of response in some patients differed from that typically observed with cytotoxic agents. Several patients had persistent reductions in target lesions despite the appearance of new lesions and were therefore not counted as having objective responses according to conventional criteria. Such findings are compatible with previously described immune-related response patterns and underscore the difficulty of applying standard radiographic criteria to agents that depend on modulation of the host immune response. Although objective response remains an important and conservative end point, future studies of PD-1 blockade should consider response criteria and trial designs that capture delayed or atypical responses without compromising patient safety.

Treatment-related adverse events were generally manageable, and a maximum tolerated dose was not reached within the dose range studied. The most common treatment-related events were constitutional, dermatologic, or gastrointestinal in nature. Events with potential immune-related causes, including colitis, hepatitis, thyroiditis, hypophysitis, vitiligo, and pneumonitis, were observed, a finding consistent with the mechanism of action of PD-1 blockade. Most hepatic, gastrointestinal, and endocrine events were reversible or manageable with treatment interruption, glucocorticoids, or hormone replacement. However, pneumonitis occurred in a small number of patients and was associated with three treatment-related deaths. This toxicity indicates that although the overall adverse-event profile does not appear to preclude further development, careful monitoring for pulmonary symptoms and early intervention are essential in subsequent studies.

The pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic findings support the feasibility of sustained PD-1 blockade with dosing every 2 weeks. Serum exposure increased in a dose-proportional manner, and receptor occupancy on circulating T cells was observed across dose levels. The absence of a clear relationship between dose and response in this study should be interpreted cautiously, given the small size of the individual cohorts and the nonrandomized nature of much of the dose expansion. Nonetheless, the observation of activity at multiple doses suggests that biologically meaningful PD-1 inhibition may be achieved without defining a conventional maximum tolerated dose, a feature that distinguishes immune-checkpoint inhibitors from many cytotoxic agents.

The exploratory analysis of PD-L1 expression provides preliminary evidence that intratumoral expression of the PD-1 ligand may identify patients more likely to respond to anti-PD-1 therapy. Objective responses were observed among patients with PD-L1-positive tumors, whereas no objective responses were observed among patients whose tumors were negative for PD-L1 in the specimens tested. These results are consistent with the hypothesis that tumor-cell expression of PD-L1 reflects an adaptive immune-resistance mechanism that can be reversed by PD-1 blockade. However, this analysis was performed in a limited, nonrandom subset of patients, with archival or newly obtained tissue and without a prespecified statistical hypothesis. PD-L1 expression may be heterogeneous within tumors, may vary over time, and may be influenced by prior therapy or local immune activity. Therefore, these findings should be regarded as hypothesis-generating and require prospective validation with standardized assays.

This study has several limitations. It was an early-phase trial, with heterogeneous tumor types, prior treatments, and dose cohorts. The efficacy population was smaller than the total treated population, and follow-up was variable. The study was not designed to compare dose levels, to estimate survival benefit, or to establish the predictive value of PD-L1 expression. In addition, patients with active autoimmune disease or conditions requiring immunosuppression were excluded, and the safety of PD-1 blockade in such populations remains uncertain.

In conclusion, BMS-936558 showed durable antitumor activity in patients with advanced non-small-cell lung cancer, melanoma, and renal-cell cancer, with a toxicity profile that warrants continued clinical development but requires vigilance for immune-mediated adverse events, particularly pneumonitis. The preliminary association between tumor PD-L1 expression and response supports further investigation of biomarkers that may guide patient selection. These results establish PD-1 blockade as a promising therapeutic approach and provide the basis for larger studies to define its efficacy, safety, optimal dosing, and role across tumor types.
