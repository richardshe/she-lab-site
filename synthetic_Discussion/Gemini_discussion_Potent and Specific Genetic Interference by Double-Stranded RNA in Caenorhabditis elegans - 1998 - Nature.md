Our study identifies double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) as the primary and most potent trigger for specific genetic interference in *C. elegans*. This discovery provides a definitive explanation for the long-standing observation that both sense and antisense RNA preparations can mediate gene silencing; we conclude that the trace amounts of dsRNA contaminating those earlier preparations were the actual interfering agents. The finding that purified single strands possess at most a marginal effect, while a deliberately annealed dsRNA hybrid is at least two orders of magnitude more effective, highlights a fundamental biological distinction in how the cellular machinery recognizes and responds to double-stranded RNA structures.

The characteristics of this interference process strongly suggest that it does not operate through a simple stoichiometric antisense mechanism. The extreme potency of the effect—whereby a few molecules of injected dsRNA are sufficient to silence thousands of copies of endogenous mRNA—argues for the existence of an underlying catalytic or amplification component. Furthermore, the observation that dsRNA causes the near-total elimination of target endogenous mRNA, as demonstrated by our *mex-3* in situ hybridization experiments, identifies the post-transcriptional stability of mRNA as the primary target of the interference pathway. This is further supported by the lack of interference observed when using dsRNA corresponding to non-coding intron or promoter sequences.

One of the most remarkable features of this phenomenon is its systemic nature. The ability of the interference signal to cross cellular boundaries, moving from the site of injection in the body cavity to affect somatic tissues in the progeny, implies an active mechanism for the transport and propagation of the dsRNA signal. Such a robust response, which can persist well into the next generation, suggests that *C. elegans* possesses a specialized and highly efficient biological system for managing dsRNA-mediated information.

While the natural physiological role of this pathway—perhaps in viral immunity or the regulation of mobile genetic elements—remains to be elucidated, its utility as an experimental tool is profound. The ability to precisely and potently knock down specific gene functions will undoubtedly accelerate the study of gene function and genetic networks. Future investigations must focus on identifying the molecular components responsible for detecting, amplifying, and executing this targeted mRNA degradation.
