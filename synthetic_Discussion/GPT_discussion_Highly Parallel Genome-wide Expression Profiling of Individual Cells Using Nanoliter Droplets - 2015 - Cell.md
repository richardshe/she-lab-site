Drop-seq provides a simple strategy for analyzing the transcriptional programs of large numbers of individual cells. By using droplets to isolate cells and barcoded microparticles to retain a molecular memory of cell-of-origin, Drop-seq converts thousands of single-cell transcriptomes into a pooled sequencing library while preserving the information needed to reconstruct each cell's expression profile. This combination of nanoliter-scale compartmentalization, split-and-pool barcode synthesis, and digital counting with UMIs makes single-cell RNA-seq substantially more scalable and accessible than approaches in which each cell must be handled and amplified separately.

The method was accurate enough to distinguish individual human and mouse cells in mixed-species experiments and sensitive enough to recover thousands of genes per cell. These experiments also clarified the principal technical limitations of the approach. Cell-cell doublets increase with cell concentration, and ambient RNA from damaged cells contributes to single-cell impurity. Both effects can be controlled experimentally and measured directly, allowing the user to balance throughput, purity, and cost for a given biological question. The use of UMIs and conservative collapsing of related UMI sequences further supports quantitative interpretation of digital expression measurements.

The analysis of asynchronously dividing HEK and 3T3 cells illustrates that Drop-seq can recover not only stable cell identities but also dynamic cell states. Without synchronizing cells chemically, expression profiles organized cells along the cell cycle and identified many conserved cell-cycle-regulated genes. This suggests that large-scale single-cell data can reveal biological processes that are obscured in bulk measurements, and can do so without perturbing the system to enrich for particular states.

The retinal analysis demonstrates the value of scale for complex tissues. From 44,808 mouse retinal cells, Drop-seq identified 39 transcriptionally distinct populations corresponding to known neuronal and non-neuronal classes, while also resolving finer distinctions among closely related amacrine populations. The recovery of all clusters across independent experimental sessions indicates that these structures were not artifacts of a single preparation or batch. Moreover, the increasing resolution obtained with increasing cell numbers shows why very large datasets are important: rare cell populations and subtle subdivisions become visible only when enough cells have been sampled.

The markers nominated by Drop-seq provide a practical route from transcriptomic classification to anatomical and functional study. Immunohistochemical validation of markers for specific amacrine populations confirmed the ability of the method to identify biologically meaningful cell types, including heterogeneity among nGnG amacrines that had not been evident from prior marker sets. Supervised analysis of retinal ganglion cells further suggests that additional diversity remains within the clusters identified here, and that deeper sampling or targeted enrichment should reveal still finer taxonomic structure.

Drop-seq should therefore be useful wherever cellular diversity or cellular state is central to biological function. Its strengths are greatest when many cells must be profiled across tissues, developmental stages, perturbations, or disease states. The approach does not eliminate the need for anatomical, physiological, or genetic validation, and transcriptomes alone may not fully define cell types. However, by making large-scale single-cell expression profiling routine, Drop-seq provides a general framework for building molecular atlases of complex tissues and for discovering the cell types and states that underlie their biology.
