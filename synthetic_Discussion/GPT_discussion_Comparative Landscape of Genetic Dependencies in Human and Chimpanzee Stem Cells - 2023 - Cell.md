In this study, we established a comparative CRISPRi screening framework to identify genetic dependencies that diverged during recent human and chimpanzee evolution. By performing genome-wide and validation screens across multiple independently derived PSC lines, we found that most growth-associated gene functions are conserved between species, but that a discrete set of perturbations produces reproducible species-specific effects. These differences were sufficiently coherent to distinguish human from chimpanzee stem cells by their sgRNA depletion profiles alone. Thus, although human and chimpanzee PSCs share core features of pluripotency, they differ in the molecular circuits that buffer or enforce proliferation under genetic perturbation.

Our results provide evidence that species-specific genetic dependencies are not simply a consequence of differential expression or coding divergence in the perturbed genes themselves. A small number of candidates, including ASPM and KATNA1, carry evolutionary signatures that are consistent with direct changes in gene function, and other genes displayed quantitative expression differences between species. However, the broader set of validated dependencies was depleted for both coding divergence and species-biased expression. This observation suggests that many functional differences between closely related species may emerge from changes in pathway state, network compensation, or upstream regulatory architecture rather than from obvious changes in the target gene. In this respect, comparative perturbation screening complements gene expression and comparative genomics approaches by identifying genes whose functional importance has changed even when their sequence or expression appears largely conserved.

The species-specific dependencies identified here were organized into biological modules, including UFMylation, the MOZ acetyltransferase complex, lysosomal signaling, and cell cycle control. This pathway-level coherence provides an internal validation of the screening approach and indicates that evolutionary changes have altered requirements for entire molecular processes. The human-specific requirement for ATP6AP1 and ATP6AP2 illustrates this principle. Although core V-ATPase function was essential in both species and lysosomal acidification was not detectably impaired by ATP6AP1 depletion, human cells exhibited a stronger loss of mTORC1 signaling after perturbation of ATP6AP1. These findings suggest that human PSCs acquired increased reliance on specific accessory components of the lysosomal signaling machinery, potentially reflecting broader changes in metabolic or growth factor responsiveness during early development.

The most prominent species difference that we identified involved regulators of G1/S progression. Human PSCs were unexpectedly robust to depletion of CDK2, CCNE1, and CDK4, whereas chimpanzee PSCs required these genes for efficient proliferation and cell cycle progression. Mechanistically, our data support a model in which human cells can compensate for reduced Cyclin E-CDK2 activity through increased CDK1 activity. CDK1 overexpression was sufficient to rescue the effects of CDK2 or CCNE1 depletion in chimpanzee PSCs, suggesting that a shift in the balance of cyclin-dependent kinase activity can alter the dependency landscape of pluripotent cells. The larger effects of RBL1 repression in chimpanzee cells and the species-specific response to FAM122A depletion further support the conclusion that human and chimpanzee PSCs differ in how they regulate entry into S phase and respond to checkpoint perturbation.

These cell cycle differences may be relevant to developmental features that distinguish humans from other apes. The length of G1 has long been proposed to influence neural progenitor behavior, with shorter or more permissive G1 progression favoring proliferative divisions and expansion of progenitor populations. Our findings show that human-specific robustness to CDK2 and CCNE1 depletion is not restricted to PSCs but persists in NPCs and cerebral organoids. In chimpanzee NPCs and organoids, depletion of these regulators increased G1 accumulation and reduced organoid growth, whereas human cells maintained proliferation under the same perturbations. These results do not demonstrate that the genes identified here caused human cortical expansion, but they provide experimental support for the idea that human neural progenitors possess derived mechanisms that stabilize proliferative cell cycle progression.

The inclusion of orangutan PSCs allowed us to infer the evolutionary direction of selected differences. Human robustness to depletion of CDK2, CDK4, and CCNE1 was not shared by chimpanzee or orangutan cells, arguing that altered G1/S dependency emerged on the human lineage. Similarly, sensitivity to ATP6AP1 depletion appeared to be human-derived. By contrast, other phenotypes, including the proliferative effect of KAT6A repression and altered sensitivity to UFL1 depletion, were more consistent with changes on the chimpanzee lineage. These observations emphasize that comparative studies between humans and chimpanzees should not presume that all differences are human-derived. Instead, phylogenetic sampling is required to distinguish ancestral, human-specific, and chimpanzee-specific states.

Several limitations remain. PSCs and stem-cell-derived neural models provide controlled and scalable systems for functional comparisons, but they do not recapitulate all aspects of embryonic development in vivo. Growth-based CRISPRi screens are also most sensitive to perturbations that alter proliferation or survival and may miss species differences related to differentiation, morphology, electrophysiology, or cell-cell interactions. In addition, CRISPRi reduces gene expression rather than producing complete loss of function, and the consequences of partial repression may differ from those of genetic mutation in organisms. Future work applying comparative perturbation screens across additional cell types, developmental stages, and great ape species should extend the principles established here and help connect molecular dependencies to organismal traits.

Together, our findings demonstrate that genetic dependencies have diverged over the short evolutionary interval separating humans and chimpanzees. These changes are reproducible across individuals, organized into functional modules, and can reveal latent cellular differences not apparent from gene expression or sequence analysis alone. Comparative functional genomics therefore provides a powerful route for identifying the molecular processes that changed during hominin evolution and for prioritizing candidate pathways that may have contributed to the emergence of human-specific developmental phenotypes.
