The ability to direct Cas9 to endogenous mammalian loci with short guide RNAs establishes type II CRISPR/Cas systems as a versatile platform for genome engineering. In contrast to protein-based targeting reagents, the specificity of this system is encoded largely by simple Watson-Crick base pairing, enabling rapid retargeting and parallel construction of many nucleases. Our results show that a minimal set of Cas9 and guide RNA components is sufficient to induce efficient modification in human and mouse cells, and that guide sequences can be assembled in a CRISPR array to permit simultaneous perturbation of multiple genomic sites.

The sensitivity of Cas9 activity to mismatches near the PAM indicates that guide design will be important for minimizing unintended cleavage. Additional improvements in guide architecture, delivery, and computational target selection should further increase activity and specificity. The conversion of Cas9 into a nickase also provides a strategy to promote homology-directed repair while limiting mutagenic NHEJ, expanding the range of precise genome modifications that can be introduced.

Together, these findings demonstrate that RNA-guided nucleases can be programmed for efficient, multiplexed genome editing in mammalian cells. This technology should facilitate systematic interrogation of gene function, construction of cellular models, and engineering of complex genetic variation.
