The results presented here establish MERFISH as a highly multiplexed, single-molecule imaging method for quantitative RNA profiling in individual cells. By combining sequential hybridization with error-robust binary encoding, MERFISH substantially extends the multiplexing capacity of smFISH while preserving the key advantages of imaging-based measurements: direct molecular counting, single-cell resolution, and spatial localization of individual RNA molecules. In the 140-gene measurements, the use of a 16-bit MHD4 code allowed single-bit errors to be corrected and double-bit errors to be detected, producing high calling rates and low misidentification rates. The agreement between independently encoded measurements, conventional smFISH, and bulk RNA sequencing indicates that this approach provides accurate and reproducible measurements over a broad range of RNA abundances. The extension to 1001 genes further demonstrates that transcriptome-scale imaging is feasible, even when error correction is sacrificed for increased coding capacity.

A central feature of this approach is that the scaling of the measurement is controlled by the encoding scheme rather than by the number of fluorescent colors. Because only a small number of readout probes are required and because each readout hybridization can be completed rapidly, MERFISH circumvents several practical barriers that have limited previous multiplexed smFISH measurements. The two-step labeling strategy, in which complex encoding probe libraries are synthesized from array-derived oligonucleotide pools and read out with a small set of fluorescent probes, should be adaptable to many different gene panels and cell types. Moreover, the use of misidentification control words provides an internal and direct measure of decoding accuracy, allowing conservative selection of genes for downstream analysis. These features should become increasingly important as the number of imaged RNA species is increased further and as MERFISH is applied to samples with higher autofluorescence, greater molecular crowding, or more complex morphology.

The multiplexing capacity of MERFISH enables analyses that are difficult to perform with lower-throughput imaging methods. In particular, measurements of hundreds to thousands of RNA species in the same single cells allow the natural cell-to-cell fluctuations in gene expression to be used as a source of regulatory information. The co-variation analysis reported here identified groups of genes with shared expression patterns and enriched functional annotations. These groups included genes involved in extracellular matrix organization, vesicle transport, cell motility, cell-cycle progression, and many other processes. The association of unannotated or poorly annotated genes with functionally coherent groups further illustrates the potential of single-cell co-variation analysis to generate hypotheses about gene function. Although such predictions require subsequent experimental validation, the strong enrichment of related GO terms among the identified groups suggests that the correlations reflect biologically meaningful regulatory structure rather than measurement noise alone.

Because MERFISH records the positions of individual RNA molecules, it also provides a direct view of the spatial organization of the transcriptome. The observed clustering of RNAs by spatial distribution revealed groups enriched either near the perinuclear region or near the cell periphery. The perinuclear enrichment of RNAs encoding secreted, extracellular, and membrane-associated proteins is consistent with translation on the endoplasmic reticulum and suggests that spatial RNA profiling can reveal common features of protein biogenesis. Conversely, the enrichment of RNAs encoding actin-binding, cytoskeletal, and motility-related proteins near the cell periphery suggests coordinated localization of transcripts involved in cellular architecture and movement. These results indicate that RNA localization patterns can be systematically classified at large scale and correlated with the cellular functions of the encoded proteins.

Several limitations remain. The current measurements were performed in cultured fibroblast cells and targeted selected gene panels rather than the complete transcriptome. Detection efficiency depends on hybridization efficiency, imaging quality, RNA abundance, and the density of molecules in the sample. The 1001-gene MHD2 measurements illustrate the tradeoff between multiplexing capacity and error correction, with reduced calling rates and increased misidentification relative to the MHD4 measurements. Future improvements in probe design, imaging speed, spot detection, sample registration, and coding strategies should further improve sensitivity, throughput, and accuracy. Increasing the number of bits, using additional colors, or designing codes that better match experimentally measured error profiles could permit still larger gene panels while maintaining robust decoding.

More broadly, MERFISH provides a framework for spatially resolved transcriptomics in single cells and, potentially, in intact tissues. By uniting molecular counting with spatial mapping and large-scale correlation analysis, this approach can connect gene expression, gene regulation, and subcellular organization in the same cells. Such measurements should be particularly useful for identifying cell types in situ, mapping regulatory states across heterogeneous populations, and determining how RNA localization contributes to cellular function. The ability to image hundreds to thousands of RNA species with single-molecule resolution therefore opens a path toward a more complete, spatially organized view of the transcriptome.
