The development of prime editing fundamentally expands the capabilities of precision genome editing, addressing many of the most persistent limitations associated with both Cas9-initiated homology-directed repair (HDR) and base editing. By engineering a system that writes new genetic information directly from a pegRNA into a specified genomic locus without the requirement for double-strand breaks (DSBs) or exogenous donor DNA templates, we have established a highly versatile 'search-and-replace' methodology. The ability of prime editors to efficiently install all twelve possible base-to-base conversions, targeted insertions, and precise deletions—as well as complex combinations of these changes—opens the door to correcting a vast majority of known pathogenic human alleles that were previously recalcitrant to existing technologies.

A central advantage of prime editing lies in its mechanism of action, which uncouples precise genome modification from the inherently unpredictable consequences of DSB repair. By relying on a nickase and an engineered reverse transcriptase, prime editing circumvents the frequent indel byproducts, translocations, and p53 activation commonly associated with Cas9 nuclease activity. Our extensive comparisons in diverse human cell lines demonstrated that prime editing consistently yields substantially higher editing-to-indel ratios than Cas9-mediated HDR. Furthermore, the requirement for three distinct DNA hybridization events—target-spacer complementary, target-primer binding site complementary, and target-reverse transcript product complementary—confers a stringent layer of specificity. Consequently, prime editing exhibits dramatically lower off-target editing activity at known Cas9 off-target loci compared to conventional Cas9 nuclease.

While base editors remain highly efficient for installing specific transition mutations within a defined targeting window, prime editing offers complementary strengths. It is uniquely capable of generating transversions and multi-base changes, and operates without the constraints of a rigid PAM positioning requirement or the complication of bystander edits in sequence contexts rich in the target nucleotide. Although editing efficiencies vary depending on the target site, the specific edit, and the cell type, the system's flexibility allows for extensive pegRNA optimization. 

Future optimization efforts will likely focus on enhancing the efficiency of prime editing in primary cells and in vivo models, as well as refining pegRNA design algorithms. Nevertheless, the robustness, precision, and broad applicability of prime editing represent a major step toward the comprehensive manipulation of mammalian genomes.
