These experiments identify double-stranded RNA as the active trigger for RNA-mediated genetic interference in C. elegans. The striking difference between purified single strands and the corresponding sense-antisense duplex argues that previous activities attributed to sense or antisense RNA may have resulted, at least in part, from small amounts of double-stranded material present in nominally single-stranded preparations. The requirement for homology between the duplex and the affected gene, together with the failure of unrelated dsRNA to potentiate interference, distinguishes this process from a general response to foreign or double-stranded RNA.

The potency of dsRNA-mediated interference is particularly notable. In several experiments, the number of injected RNA molecules was far below the number of endogenous target transcripts that would be present in the affected cells. Moreover, interference could persist after extensive cell division and was transmitted to progeny derived from injected animals. These observations make a simple stoichiometric model, in which injected RNA directly binds and inactivates each target mRNA molecule, unlikely. Instead, the data suggest that dsRNA initiates a process with a catalytic, regenerative, or otherwise amplified component. Such a process could act by promoting degradation of homologous transcripts, by preventing their accumulation, or by affecting the gene or its transcript through an intermediate molecule generated from the injected duplex.

Our observations favour an effect on RNA, although they do not yet define the precise target. Duplexes corresponding to introns or promoter sequences failed to produce detectable interference, whereas dsRNA corresponding to exonic sequence caused a marked reduction in endogenous mex-3 mRNA. These results are most simply explained by post-transcriptional elimination of homologous RNA. Nonetheless, the possibility of effects at the level of the gene, or of nascent transcripts, remains open.

The ability of dsRNA to move from the site of injection to distant tissues and to affect both somatic cells and germline-derived progeny indicates that C. elegans can distribute an interfering signal between cells. The mosaic patterns seen with gfp interference further suggest that delivery, persistence, or amplification of this signal varies with developmental history and tissue type. Thus, dsRNA-mediated interference provides both a powerful experimental method for reducing gene function and a biological phenomenon with unexpected specificity, potency and systemic character.
