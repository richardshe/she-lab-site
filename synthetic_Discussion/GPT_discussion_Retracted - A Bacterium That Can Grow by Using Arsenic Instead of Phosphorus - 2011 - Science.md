The results presented here show that strain GFAJ-1, isolated from an environment naturally enriched in arsenic, can maintain growth under conditions in which added phosphate is absent and arsenate is abundant. Although trace phosphate impurities were present in the medium, the repeated dilution, lack of growth in the -As/-P condition, and low cellular P content together indicate that this residual pool was insufficient to account for the observed biomass. The concomitant accumulation of As, incorporation of radiolabeled arsenate into multiple biochemical fractions, and detection of As in purified genomic DNA provide convergent evidence that arsenate entered cellular material rather than remaining solely as an extracellular or loosely associated contaminant.

These findings imply that GFAJ-1 possesses physiological adaptations that permit survival in a chemical regime usually considered incompatible with conventional phosphate biochemistry. The enlarged cell volume and vacuole-like structures observed during +As/-P growth may reflect mechanisms for managing arsenate burden, altered osmotic state, or compartmentalization of labile arsenylated compounds. The x-ray absorption data further indicate that intracellular arsenic was predominantly As(V) coordinated to oxygen, with longer-range features consistent with bonding environments expected for arsenate substituted into biological molecules. Thus, GFAJ-1 appears not merely resistant to arsenate toxicity but able to use arsenate in reactions dependent on phosphate.

The capacity to replace one major bioelement with a chemical analog has implications for microbial ecology, biogeochemistry, and the limits of life. Environments with high As:P ratios may select for organisms that exploit arsenate-rich niches through biochemical flexibility. More broadly, these observations suggest that the elemental requirements for life may be less fixed than previously assumed.
