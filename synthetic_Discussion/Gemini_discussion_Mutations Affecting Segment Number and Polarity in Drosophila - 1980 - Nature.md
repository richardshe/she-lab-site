The systematic identification of fifteen genetic loci that specifically control the number and polarity of Drosophila segments provides a comprehensive framework for understanding the zygotic contribution to embryonic pattern formation. Our results suggest that the subdivision of the antero-posterior axis into a metameric array is not a single-step process but involves a hierarchy of increasingly refined spatial organizational levels. By characterizing the phenotypes of gap, pair-rule, and segment polarity mutants, we can begin to reconstruct the logical sequence of events that transforms the relatively simple information of the maternal gradient into the complex, repetitive structure of the larval epidermis.

The gap genes—*Krüppel*, *hunchback*, and *knirps*—appear to represent the first level of zygotic response to the maternal coordinate system. Their function is required for the establishment of large, continuous body regions. The deletion of up to eight adjacent segments in these mutants suggests that these genes are active in broad domains of the blastoderm. Interestingly, the gap phenotypes do not show a repeated pattern along the axis, implying that they respond to unique, non-repeating values provided by the maternal morphogen gradient. The *Krüppel* phenotype is especially significant in this regard; the mirror-image symmetry observed in these embryos, which lacks a large central portion of the body, reminiscent of the maternal *bicaudal* effect, suggests that *Krüppel* is essential for interpreting the middle range of the maternal gradient. In its absence, the developmental program appears to "collapse" or revert to terminal identities that expand to fill the void. This supports a model where the gap genes subdivide the embryo into a few large, unique domains that serve as the foundation for all subsequent metameric subdivisions.

The discovery of the pair-rule mutants—*even-skipped*, *odd-skipped*, *paired*, *runt*, *barrel*, and *engrailed*—was perhaps the most unexpected finding of our search. These mutants demonstrate that the embryo is organized into repeating units with a periodicity of two segments before individual segments are defined. The fact that homologous parts of the pattern are deleted in every other segment indicates that the developmental machinery "counts" in units of two. The distinct, partially overlapping deletion patterns of loci like *even-skipped* (which affects even-numbered segments) and *odd-skipped* (which affects odd-numbered segments) suggest that the entire segmental array is first covered by a series of double-segment periodicities. This level of organization intervened between the broad domains of the gap genes and the individual segments. The composite nature of the segments in *paired* embryos, as revealed by our combinations with homeotic mutants like *Ultrabithorax*, provides compelling evidence that the establishment of segmental boundaries and the specification of segmental identity are independent processes. The pair-rule genes appear to be responsible for the periodic "rhythm" of the embryo, defining the positions of future segment boundaries based on their location within a double-segment repeat.

The third level of organization is represented by the segment polarity genes. Unlike the gap or pair-rule genes, these six loci—*wingless*, *gooseberry*, *hedgehog*, *patch*, *fused*, and *cubitus interruptus*—affect every segment. The common feature of this class is the deletion of a specific fraction of the pattern in each segment and its replacement by a mirror-image duplication of the remaining part. This suggests that these genes are required to specify the internal organization and polarity of individual segmental units. The mirror-image duplications are particularly telling; they imply that each segment possesses an internal coordinate system or gradient that dictates the arrangement of structures (e.g., denticle bands vs. naked cuticle) and their orientation. When a segment polarity gene is mutated, this internal system is disrupted, and the cells appear to adopt a "default" or duplicated state. The fact that the deletions in *patch* involve structures from two adjacent segments further highlights the importance of these genes in defining the boundaries between segments.

Our results also clarify the relationship between genes affecting segment number and the homeotic genes of the *bithorax* complex. Homeotic mutations transform the identity of segments without altering their number, size, or polarity. Our combinations of *paired* or *even-skipped* with *Ultrabithorax* and *Polycomb* demonstrate that the periodic grouping of segments in pairs occurs regardless of the segments' identity. This confirms that the segmentation genes we have identified operate "upstream" of, or in parallel to, the identity genes. The segmentation genes establish the physical units (the metameres), while the homeotic genes subsequently assign a specific "flavor" or identity to each of those units.

The timing of these genetic activities is consistent with their role as primary architects of the body plan. Our analysis of temperature-sensitive alleles and early morphological defects indicates that these genes are active during the blastoderm stage, a period when the embryo is still a syncytium or a single layer of cells. This is the stage when cell lineage restrictions are established, and our data suggest it is also when the final number and arrangement of segments are definitively determined. The fact that *Krüppel*, *runt*, and *knirps* embryos show germ-band defects as early as fifteen minutes after the onset of gastrulation underscores the immediacy of their impact on developmental topology.

In summary, the transition from an egg to a segmented larva is achieved through a hierarchical refinement of spatial information. The maternal gradient provides the global coordinates, which the zygotic gap genes translate into broad domains. These domains are then subdivided by the pair-rule genes into a periodic, double-segment pattern. Finally, the segment polarity genes define the internal structure and boundaries of each individual segment within that periodic array. This "divide and conquer" strategy allows the embryo to manage the enormous complexity of metameric organization using a relatively small set of genetic interactions. The identification of these fifteen loci likely represents the majority of the genes involved in this fundamental process, providing a molecular map for the spatial organization of the Drosophila embryo.
