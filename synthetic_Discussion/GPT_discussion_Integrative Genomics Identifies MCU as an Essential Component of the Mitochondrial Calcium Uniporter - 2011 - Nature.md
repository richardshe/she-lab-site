The molecular identity of the mitochondrial calcium uniporter has remained unknown despite decades of physiological and pharmacological investigation. Our results identify MCU as an essential component of this pathway. Three independent genomic strategies, based on evolutionary distribution, transcript abundance and mitochondrial protein expression, converged on MCU as a protein functionally linked to MICU1. This prediction was strengthened by physical interaction with MICU1 and by the profound loss of mitochondrial Ca²⁺ uptake after MCU silencing in intact cells, permeabilized cells and mouse liver mitochondria.

Several observations argue that MCU acts directly in mitochondrial Ca²⁺ transport rather than indirectly through respiratory or metabolic impairment. Loss of MCU did not compromise mitochondrial membrane potential, respiration, respiratory state transitions, ADP:O ratios or gross mitochondrial morphology. Nevertheless, MCU depletion nearly abolished rapid Ca²⁺ uptake and attenuated Ca²⁺-dependent stimulation of NAD(P)H production, establishing a functional link between MCU-mediated Ca²⁺ entry and mitochondrial metabolism. The in vivo silencing experiments further show that this pathway can be selectively disrupted in an intact animal without overt impairment of mitochondrial bioenergetics.

MCU is an integral inner membrane protein that oligomerizes and migrates within a large complex, consistent with a structural role in the uniporter. The requirement for conserved acidic residues in the intermembrane-space-facing linker, together with the Ru360 resistance conferred by S259A, implicates this region in uniporter activity and inhibitor sensitivity. These findings do not by themselves prove that MCU forms the ion-conducting pore, and additional components may shape gating, selectivity or regulation. However, they establish MCU as an indispensable molecular constituent of the uniporter complex and provide a foundation for mechanistic studies of mitochondrial Ca²⁺ uptake in physiology and disease.
