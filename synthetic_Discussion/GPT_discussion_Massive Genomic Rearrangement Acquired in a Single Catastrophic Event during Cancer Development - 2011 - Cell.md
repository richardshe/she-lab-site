The data presented here identify chromothripsis as a distinctive mechanism by which cancer genomes can be remodeled. In contrast to the conventional view that somatic mutations and rearrangements are acquired progressively over many years, chromothripsis implies that tens to hundreds of genomic rearrangements can arise in a single catastrophic episode. The resulting structures are not simply extreme examples of the disordered karyotypes long recognized in cancer. Rather, they carry a set of genomic signatures that point to a specific mode of origin: localization to one or a few chromosomal regions, rapid oscillation between a limited number of copy number states, preservation of heterozygosity in retained fragments, and highly clustered breakpoints joined in multiple orientations.

These features make it difficult to explain chromothripsis as the cumulative endpoint of sequential, independent rearrangements. Under a progressive model, multiple deletion, duplication, and inversion events would be expected to generate increasingly complex copy number profiles, including many distinct copy number states and widespread loss of heterozygosity. Instead, the observed profiles are often dominated by alternating regions of loss and retention, as would be expected if a chromosome were fragmented and only a subset of pieces were incorporated into a derivative chromosome. The fact that these configurations are stable across cells and persist at relapse in the CLL case further suggests that the genomic crisis occurred before clonal expansion, rather than representing ongoing instability in many subclones.

Chromothripsis therefore provides an example of punctuated evolution in cancer. A single event can abruptly generate a genomic architecture that would otherwise require a long succession of rearrangements. This does not exclude the importance of gradual mutation accumulation in most cancers, nor does it imply that all genomic complexity has a catastrophic origin. Indeed, some derivative chromosomes may subsequently duplicate, some rearrangements may occur after the main event, and focal deletions or amplifications may be acquired independently. Nevertheless, the majority of rearrangements in the cases described here are best explained by a one-off event, placing chromothripsis alongside other mechanisms through which cancer evolution can accelerate over a short period of biological time.

The mechanism that causes such catastrophic chromosome breakage remains uncertain. One possibility is that a chromosome or chromosomal region becomes physically isolated, damaged, or missegregated, leading to extensive double-stranded DNA breakage followed by imperfect repair. Telomere dysfunction, aborted breakage-fusion-bridge cycles, mitotic errors, or exposure to DNA-damaging processes could each, in principle, generate the necessary burst of DNA damage. The limited sequence overlap at breakpoint junctions is consistent with repair through nonhomologous end-joining or microhomology-mediated pathways rather than homologous recombination. Whatever the initiating lesion, survival of a cell after such damage must be rare, and the emergence of a cancer clone implies that the rearranged genome either conferred selective advantage or occurred in a cellular context already permissive for survival despite extensive DNA damage.

The selective consequences of chromothripsis can be considerable. In the examples studied here, the same catastrophic event can generate copy number loss of tumor suppressor genes, disruption of coding sequence, alteration of gene expression, and substrates for amplification. The SCLC-21H cell line illustrates how chromothripsis may give rise to double minute chromosomes carrying MYC, which can then be selected and amplified. In chordoma, the simultaneous involvement of CDKN2A, WRN, and FBXW7 shows that more than one cancer-relevant lesion can emerge from a single genomic crisis. Thus, chromothripsis can compress several steps of tumor evolution into one event, providing a route by which a clone may acquire multiple advantageous alterations without requiring them to arise independently.

The frequency of chromothripsis also has implications for cancer classification and genome interpretation. We estimate that at least 2%-3% of cancers show evidence of this phenomenon, with a much higher prevalence in bone tumors in the cohort analyzed here. These numbers are likely conservative, because detection depends on the resolution of copy number data, the availability of rearrangement information, and the ability to recognize the pattern after subsequent evolution of the cancer genome. As large-scale cancer genome sequencing expands, chromothripsis may prove to be a recurrent feature of particular histological subtypes or genetic backgrounds.

More broadly, chromothripsis changes how we interpret complex cancer genomes. It shows that extreme genomic complexity need not always represent a long history of stepwise instability. Instead, a cancer genome may contain the scar of a single catastrophic event, followed by clonal selection and further evolution. Recognizing this distinction is important for reconstructing tumor history, identifying driver lesions embedded within complex rearrangements, and understanding how malignant clones can arise from sudden, profound disruption of chromosome structure.
