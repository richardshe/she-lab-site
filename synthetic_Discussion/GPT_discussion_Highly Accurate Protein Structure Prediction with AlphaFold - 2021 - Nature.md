We have shown that a neural network can predict protein structures with an accuracy that is competitive with experimental structures in a large fraction of cases, including many proteins for which no close structural template is available. This level of accuracy changes the practical role of computational structure prediction. Rather than serving only as a source of approximate folds or hypotheses for subsequent modelling, predicted structures can now often provide detailed atomic models that are immediately useful for interpreting function, designing experiments and analysing large sequence datasets.

The success of AlphaFold suggests that the information required to specify protein structure is frequently present in the combination of amino acid sequence, evolutionary variation and the general constraints of protein geometry. A central feature of the method is that these sources of information are not treated as separate stages in a hand-designed pipeline, but are integrated within a single differentiable model that can repeatedly refine its structural hypothesis. The emergence of coherent intermediate structures within the network indicates that accurate prediction is not obtained simply by copying templates or by predicting independent distances, but by iterative reasoning over a constrained three-dimensional object.

Several limitations remain. Accuracy decreases when the available MSA is very shallow, indicating that evolutionary information is still important for many targets. Moreover, the current system predicts single chains and therefore has difficulty when the native conformation of a chain is largely determined by heterotypic interactions within a complex. Flexible regions, alternative conformations, ligand-dependent states and proteins whose structures depend on environmental context also remain outside the direct scope of the present model. The confidence measures are therefore an essential part of the output, as they provide a way to distinguish regions that can be interpreted with high confidence from regions that require caution or further experimental validation.

The results presented here do not remove the need for experimental structural biology. Experimental methods remain indispensable for determining complexes, conformational ensembles, ligand-bound states and cellular context, and for providing the data on which predictive methods depend. Instead, accurate prediction should complement experiments by focusing attention on the most informative measurements and by enabling structural interpretation in systems for which experimental determination is difficult.

More broadly, AlphaFold demonstrates that incorporating biological and physical structure into machine learning architectures can solve problems that have resisted purely heuristic or simulation-based approaches. Extending these ideas to protein complexes, dynamics, design and interactions with other molecules may substantially expand the reach of structural biology and make structural information available for a much larger fraction of known protein sequences.
