# Discussion

Our study fundamentally elucidates the mechanisms underlying the efficacy of checkpoint blockade immunotherapy by demonstrating that tumour-specific mutant antigens (TSMAs), or neoantigens, serve as the major targets for the reactivated CD8+ T cell response. Using a sophisticated genomics and bioinformatics pipeline, we successfully identified two dominant mutant epitopes (mLama4 and mAlg8) in progressively growing murine sarcomas that are specifically recognized by tumour-infiltrating T cells following anti-PD-1 or anti-CTLA-4 therapy. This confirms that the immune system's ability to eradicate established tumours upon checkpoint blockade is largely dependent on the recognition of these unique, mutation-derived peptides, which arise from the inherent genomic instability of the cancer cells.

Importantly, our findings reveal that T cells specific for these neoantigens are already present within the tumour microenvironment prior to therapy, but they exist in a dysfunctional or exhausted state, characterized by elevated expression of inhibitory receptors like LAG-3 and TIM-3. Treatment with checkpoint inhibitors effectively rescues these cells, triggering distinct, treatment-specific transcriptional and metabolic reprogramming. While both anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 lead to tumour rejection, they achieve this through non-overlapping mechanisms: anti-PD-1 primarily alters metabolic pathways and IL-2 signaling, whereas anti-CTLA-4 enhances proliferative and effector T cell pathways. The combination of both therapies results in a synergistic enhancement of effector functions, highlighting the complementary nature of these two clinical approaches.

Furthermore, we demonstrate that therapeutic vaccination using synthetic long peptides incorporating these identified mutant epitopes induces tumour rejection rates comparable to those achieved by checkpoint blockade itself. This indicates that targeting these specific neoantigens is sufficient to drive robust anti-tumour immunity. These results hold profound implications for the future of clinical oncology. By combining whole-exome sequencing with advanced epitope prediction algorithms, it is highly feasible to identify patient-specific neoantigens. Our work provides a strong preclinical foundation for the development of personalized cancer vaccines, which could be used either as standalone therapies or, more likely, in synergistic combination with checkpoint inhibitors to maximize antitumour efficacy and minimize toxicity in patients with advanced malignancies.
