The structure of the agonist-bound beta2AR-Gs complex provides a direct view of the conformational changes that couple ligand binding in a GPCR to nucleotide exchange in a heterotrimeric G protein. The outward displacement of TM6, extension of TM5, and rearrangement of ICL2 generate a receptor surface that engages primarily the Ras-like domain of Galphas, especially the amino- and carboxy-terminal helices that are linked to the nucleotide-binding pocket. These observations explain key features of the ternary complex, including enhanced agonist affinity and destabilization of GDP binding.

The large displacement of the Galphas alpha-helical domain probably reflects a dynamic nucleotide-free state, rather than a single obligatory conformation during signalling. Although T4 lysozyme and Nb35 were essential for crystallogenesis, the principal receptor-G protein interface is consistent with biochemical, mutational and biophysical data. This structure therefore establishes a framework for understanding GPCR activation, G protein specificity and the initial molecular events leading to GTP binding.
