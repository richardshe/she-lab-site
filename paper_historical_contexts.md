It fundamentally reshaped modern oncology, medical genetics, and preventative medicine. The discovery enabled predictive genetic testing, empowering high-risk families with preventive strategies—such as prophylactic surgeries or enhanced screening—and laid the mechanistic foundation for targeted treatments like PARP inhibitors in homologous recombination-deficient tumors.

### Caspase-11 Cleaves Gasdermin D for Non-Canonical Inflammasome Signalling - 2015 - Nature

Establishing the Gasdermin protein family as the central pore-forming agents of pyroptosis opened entirely novel therapeutic avenues for targeting sepsis, autoimmune disorders, and severe inflammatory conditions.

Alongside its companion paper, this cornerstone discovery cemented the foundational paradigm of macrophage immune checkpoints. By demonstrating that CD47 blockade permits the innate immune system to clear circulating cancer cells, it pioneered a major new branch of cancer immunotherapy aimed at stimulating phagocytosis, deeply influencing modern oncology.

### Cerebral Organoids Model Human Brain Development and Microcephaly - 2013 - Nature

This work was a massive leap forward for neuroscience and developmental biology. ### Checkpoint Blockade Cancer Immunotherapy Targets Tumour-Specific Mutant Antigens - 2014 - Nature

Published in 2014 by Matthew Gubin, Robert Schreiber, and colleagues—bolstered by significant advances in neoantigen discovery—this critical paper forged the definitive mechanistic link between immune checkpoint blockade (such as anti-PD-1/CTLA-4 therapies) and tumor-specific mutant antigens (neoantigens). This specific study was a milestone because it definitively explained *why* checkpoint blockade succeeds and why tumors bearing high mutational burdens are far more responsive. This was one of the major publications during Richard She's postdoc years in Jonathan Weissman's lab. ### Crystal Structure of the Beta2 Adrenergic Receptor-Gs Protein Complex - 2011 - Nature

In 2011, Søren Rasmussen, Brian Kobilka, and colleagues achieved a monumental milestone in structural biology by solving the high-resolution crystal structure of the β2-adrenergic receptor actively engaged with its stimulatory G protein (Gs). This study successfully captured the elusive, highly dynamic moment of transmembrane signaling, revealing the exact structural rearrangements caused by an extracellular hormone or drug that trigger the intracellular G protein. This work was a capstone achievement that directly yielded the 2012 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for Brian Kobilka and Robert Lefkowitz. The Nobel committee lauded their extensive studies of G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs), noting that dissecting this vast family of receptors—which mediate nearly half of all prescription drugs—is vital for understanding cellular environmental sensing and designing superior, targeted therapeutics.

### Cyclic GMP-AMP Synthase Is a Cytosolic DNA Sensor That Activates the Type I Interferon Pathway - 2013 - Science

In 2013, Lijun Sun, Zhijian "James" Chen, and colleagues published this groundbreaking paper identifying cyclic GMP-AMP synthase (cGAS) as the elusive cytosolic DNA sensor. They illustrated that cGAS binds to misplaced DNA—originating from viruses, bacteria, or internal cellular damage—and catalyzes the synthesis of the unique second messenger cGAMP, which subsequently activates the STING pathway to launch robust immune responses. This discovery is widely regarded as one of the most consequential findings in modern innate immunity. It resolved the major biological enigma of how the immune system perceives pathogenic or self-DNA, fundamentally overhauling the understanding of autoimmune diseases (like lupus), antiviral immunity, and anti-tumor responses, establishing the cGAS-STING axis as a premier target for modern cancer immunotherapies.

### DNA Sequencing with Chain-Terminating Inhibitors - 1977 - PNAS

In 1977, Frederick Sanger, Steve Nicklen, and Alan Coulson introduced the "Sanger sequencing" method, an elegant technique utilizing dideoxynucleotide chain-terminating inhibitors to accurately determine the exact nucleotide sequence of DNA. This highly reliable and scalable protocol revolutionized molecular biology, granting scientists the ability to read the underlying genetic code of any organism. This epoch-making paper earned Frederick Sanger his second Nobel Prize in Chemistry in 1980 (shared with Paul Berg and Walter Gilbert). The Nobel committee honored his contribution to determining base sequences in nucleic acids, a monumental breakthrough that catalyzed the genomics revolution, enabled the Human Genome Project, and forever altered the landscape of genetics, medicine, and evolutionary biology.

### Efficacy and Safety of a Specific Inhibitor of the BCR-ABL Tyrosine Kinase in Chronic Myeloid Leukemia - 2001 - New England Journal of Medicine

Published in 2001 by Brian Druker, Moshe Talpaz, Charles Sawyers, and colleagues, this landmark clinical trial reported the stunning success of imatinib (Gleevec) in combating chronic myeloid leukemia (CML). Imatinib was rationally engineered to specifically inhibit the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase, the mutant fusion protein directly responsible for CML. The drug achieved unprecedented remission rates while generating minimal side effects compared to broad-spectrum chemotherapy. It radically altered modern oncology. It provided definitive proof of concept for targeted cancer therapy—disabling cancer by directly neutralizing its specific driving molecular defect—and turned CML from a fatal diagnosis into a manageable chronic condition, ushering in the modern era of kinase inhibitors and precision medicine.

### Embryonic Stem Cell Lines Derived from Human Blastocysts - 1998 - Science

In 1998, James Thomson and colleagues achieved a historic breakthrough by deriving the first stable human embryonic stem cell (hESC) lines from human blastocysts. They proved that these cells could be sustained in an undifferentiated state indefinitely while harboring the pluripotent capacity to develop into derivatives of all three embryonic germ layers. This discovery redefined biology and medicine. It birthed the entire field of regenerative medicine, arming researchers with an unprecedented tool to investigate human developmental biology, model intricate genetic diseases *in vitro*, and chart pathways for cell-replacement therapies targeting conditions like Parkinson's disease, spinal cord injuries, and diabetes.

### Enzymatic Amplification of Beta-Globin Genomic Sequences and Restriction Site Analysis for Diagnosis of Sickle Cell Anemia - 1985 - Science

Published in 1985 by Randall Saiki, Stephen Scharf, Fred Faloona, Kary Mullis, and colleagues, this paper stands as the first peer-reviewed publication demonstrating the Polymerase Chain Reaction (PCR). Kary Mullis was awarded the 1993 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for his invention of the PCR method. The Nobel committee underscored how PCR made it remarkably simple to amplify minute quantities of DNA, accelerating basic molecular biology, medical diagnostics, forensics, and evolutionary studies by empowering scientists to synthesize millions of copies of a target DNA segment in mere hours.

### Establishment in Culture of Pluripotential Cells from Mouse Embryos - 1981 - Nature

In 1981, Martin Evans and Matthew Kaufman successfully isolated and cultured pluripotential stem cells from the inner cell mass of mouse blastocysts, with Gail Martin independently publishing parallel findings. These embryonic stem (ES) cells could be sustained in culture and, pivotally, could reintegrate into living embryos to generate chimeric mice. This work established the bedrock for mammalian genetic engineering. Martin Evans was awarded the 2007 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine (shared with Mario Capecchi and Oliver Smithies). The Nobel committee acknowledged their discoveries of principles for introducing specific gene modifications in mice via embryonic stem cells, an innovation that led to the creation of "knockout mice"—now an indispensable pillar for decoding gene function and modeling human disease.

### Ferroptosis - An Iron-Dependent Form of Nonapoptotic Cell Death - 2012 - Cell

In 2012, Scott Dixon, Brent Stockwell, and colleagues defined a novel, non-apoptotic form of regulated cell death which they designated "ferroptosis." Fueled by iron-dependent lipid peroxidation and mechanistically distinct from apoptosis, necrosis, and autophagy, ferroptosis is triggered by the inhibition of the cystine/glutamate antiporter (system xc−) or glutathione peroxidase 4 (GPX4). This discovery has been deeply influential. It carved out a brand-new paradigm in cell death research, exposing profound links between cellular metabolism, oxidative stress, and pathology. Ferroptosis is now intensely investigated as a fundamental driver in neurodegenerative diseases and ischemia-reperfusion injury, as well as a potent vulnerability to exploit in therapy-resistant cancers.

### General Nature of the Genetic Code for Proteins - 1961 - Nature

In 1961, Francis Crick, Leslie Barnett, Sydney Brenner, and Richard Watts-Tobin published this foundational paper confirming the structural mechanics of the genetic code. Executing brilliant genetic experiments with bacteriophage T4 and proflavine-induced frameshift mutations, they rigorously deduced that the genetic code is read in non-overlapping triplets (codons) from a fixed starting point, and that it is degenerate, meaning multiple triplets can encode the same amino acid. This 1961 paper was a logical tour de force that definitively mapped the structural framework dictating how genetic information is physically translated into proteins.

### Genome-Scale CRISPR-Cas9 Knockout Screening in Human Cells - 2014 - Science

In 2014, Ofir Shalem, Neville Sanjana, Feng Zhang, and colleagues (published back-to-back with a parallel study by Wang et al. from the Sabatini lab) proved that the CRISPR-Cas9 system could be effectively scaled to execute unbiased, genome-wide knockout screens in human cells. By deploying a massive library of single-guide RNAs (sgRNAs) via lentiviruses, they rapidly isolated genes essential for cell viability and drug resistance. This paper was a game-changer because it elevated CRISPR from a targeted editing tool into an industrial-scale discovery engine. It usurped RNA interference (RNAi) as the premier gold standard for functional genomics, empowering the precise, high-throughput discovery of novel drug targets and genetic dependencies underlying human disease.

### Genome-wide Association Study of 14,000 Cases of Seven Common Diseases and 3,000 Shared Controls - 2007 - Nature

Published in 2007 by the Wellcome Trust Case Control Consortium, this massive, collaborative initiative launched the modern era of Genome-Wide Association Studies (GWAS). By aggressively analyzing the DNA of 17,000 individuals across seven major common diseases—including bipolar disorder, coronary heart disease, type 1 and 2 diabetes, and rheumatoid arthritis—using dense microarray genotyping, the researchers surfaced numerous robust, replicable genetic risk variants. It completely overhauled the field of complex trait genetics. It provided hard proof that common diseases are intensely polygenic (driven by many variants of small effect) and instituted the rigorous statistical thresholds and collaborative frameworks now required to map the genetic architecture of human disease.

### High Burden and Pervasive Positive Selection of Somatic Mutations in Normal Human Skin - 2015 - Science

In 2015, Iñigo Martincorena, Peter Campbell, and colleagues published a startling discovery regarding the genetic reality of normal human tissue. This work upended traditional models of cancer initiation and aging. ### Highly Accurate Protein Structure Prediction with AlphaFold - 2021 - Nature

Published in 2021 by John Jumper, Demis Hassabis, and the DeepMind team, this paper detailed AlphaFold2, an artificial intelligence system that conquered the 50-year-old grand challenge of protein folding. AlphaFold showcased the unprecedented ability to predict the 3D structures of proteins directly from their amino acid sequences with atomic-level accuracy, directly rivaling labor-intensive experimental techniques like X-ray crystallography and cryo-EM. This work stands as one of the most monumental breakthroughs in the history of computational biology. It instantly revolutionized structural biology, granting scientists immediate access to high-quality structures for nearly all known proteins, thereby exponentially accelerating drug discovery, enzyme design, and the broader understanding of complex biological systems.

### Highly Parallel Genome-wide Expression Profiling of Individual Cells Using Nanoliter Droplets - 2015 - Cell

In 2015, Evan Macosko, Steven McCarroll, and colleagues debuted Drop-seq, a phenomenal technology for executing single-cell RNA sequencing at an unparalleled scale. It was a profound leap for modern transcriptomics. Drop-seq (alongside the parallel inDrop method) successfully democratized large-scale single-cell genomics, forcing biology to evolve from analyzing messy bulk tissue averages to mapping intricate cellular ecosystems at single-cell resolution, laying the direct technological groundwork for massive global initiatives like the Human Cell Atlas.

### Incorporation of Pseudouridine Into mRNA Yields Superior Nonimmunogenic Vector with Increased Translational Capacity and Biological Stability - 2008 - Molecular Therapy

In 2008, Katalin Karikó, Drew Weissman, and colleagues expanded upon their seminal 2005 work to demonstrate that substituting standard uridine with the modified nucleoside pseudouridine not only evades an inflammatory immune response but also drastically amplifies the translation and stability of engineered mRNA *in vivo*. It was a critical breakthrough for cell biology. Because mitochondrial calcium dynamics dictate bioenergetics, cell death, and critical signaling, identifying MCU finally handed scientists the genetic lever needed to manipulate and decode mitochondrial calcium behavior in health, aging, and neurodegenerative diseases after 50 years of mere electrophysiological observation.

### Mapping Causal Variants with Single-Nucleotide Resolution Reveals Biochemical Drivers of Phenotypic Change - 2018 - Cell

This was one of the major publications during Richard She's grad school years in Daniel Jarosz's lab. ### Mutations Affecting Segment Number and Polarity in Drosophila - 1980 - Nature

### Novel Proteinaceous Infectious Particles Cause Scrapie - 1982 - Science

### On the Topography of the Genetic Fine Structure - 1961 - PNAS

In 1961, Seymour Benzer published his masterful work detailing the ultra-fine mapping of the *rII* region of bacteriophage T4. ### Piezo1 and Piezo2 Are Essential Components of Distinct Mechanically Activated Cation Channels - 2010 - Science

This seismic discovery resulted in Fire and Mello securing the 2006 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. The Nobel committee celebrated their discovery of RNA interference—gene silencing executed by double-stranded RNA. This paper overhauled modern biology by unearthing a fundamental, highly conserved cellular mechanism for gene regulation and viral defense, while simultaneously equipping researchers with a simple, wildly effective tool to knock down gene expression to interrogate gene function on a genome-wide scale.

### Programmable Editing of a Target Base in Genomic DNA without Double-Stranded DNA Cleavage - 2016 - Nature

In 2016, Alexis Komor, David Liu, and colleagues debuted a revolutionary leap in genome engineering known as "base editing." By engineering a fusion between a catalytically impaired Cas9 (incapable of creating double-strand breaks) and a cytidine deaminase enzyme, they built a molecular machine capable of directly and seamlessly converting a C-G base pair into a T-A base pair at a highly specific genomic locus. This paper entirely reshaped the CRISPR field. It brilliantly sidestepped a major limitation of standard CRISPR-Cas9, which relies heavily on messy, error-prone cellular repair mechanisms following a double-strand break. Base editing granted the ability to make precise, clean point mutation corrections without triggering widespread genomic damage, forging realistic, inherently safer therapeutic pathways for correcting the precise point mutations responsible for the vast majority of human genetic diseases.

### Retracted - A Bacterium That Can Grow by Using Arsenic Instead of Phosphorus - 2011 - Science

In 2011, Felisa Wolfe-Simon and colleagues published a fiercely publicized paper claiming that a specific bacterium (GFAJ-1) found in Mono Lake could literally substitute arsenic for phosphorus within its essential biomolecules, including its DNA. If accurate, this assertion would have rewritten the foundational rules of biochemistry and astrobiology. However, the paper is historically crucial for its dramatic refutation and eventual retraction. Independent scientists rapidly exposed critical methodological flaws, confirming that the DNA still unequivocally relied on phosphorus and that the bacteria were merely surviving high arsenic levels while desperately scavenging trace phosphorus. While pivotal as a cautionary tale rather than a genuine discovery, this paper stands as a landmark case study in the modern era of post-publication peer review, aggressively highlighting the uncompromising necessity of rigorous replication and the unmatched power of open scientific scrutiny.

### Retracted - Patient-Specific Embryonic Stem Cells Derived from Human SCNT Blastocysts - 2005 - Science

In 2005, Woo Suk Hwang and his research team published a blockbuster paper asserting they had successfully synthesized patient-specific human embryonic stem cell lines via somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT), seemingly achieving a massive milestone toward personalized regenerative medicine. However, this paper carries a dark historical weight, as it was entirely fabricated. Intense subsequent investigations proved that the stem cell lines were completely faked and the underlying data deliberately manipulated. The paper was formally retracted in 2006. This massive scandal was a harsh reckoning for the scientific community, reigning as one of the most visible cases of scientific fraud in modern history. It triggered widespread, aggressive reforms in scientific publishing, mandated much stricter guidelines for image manipulation checks, and deeply influenced the global regulation and public perception of stem cell research.

### Retracted - Stimulus-Triggered Fate Conversion of Somatic Cells into Pluripotency - 2014 - Nature

In 2014, Haruko Obokata, Yoshiki Sasai, and colleagues published the astonishing claim that simply subjecting mature somatic cells to brief physical stress or low pH could rapidly reprogram them into pluripotent stem cells, a phenomenon they labeled "STAP" (stimulus-triggered acquisition of pluripotency). This breakthrough would have completely rendered Yamanaka factors obsolete. However, the paper rapidly imploded as independent global laboratories completely failed to replicate the findings, and deeper investigations unearthed severe data manipulation and blatant image fabrication. The paper was retracted in a matter of months. Historically, it serves as another brutal reminder of the blinding pressures inherent in high-stakes science. The STAP cell scandal permanently shifted the landscape of regenerative biology, forcing premier journals to implement vastly more stringent data availability and peer review policies to safeguard scientific transparency and reproducibility.

### Safety and Efficacy of the BNT162b2 mRNA Covid-19 Vaccine - 2020 - New England Journal of Medicine

Published in late 2020 by Fernando Polack, Stephen Thomas, Uğur Şahin, Kathrin Jansen, and colleagues, this historic paper detailed the Phase 3 clinical trial results of the Pfizer-BioNTech COVID-19 vaccine (BNT162b2). The data showcased a stunning 95% efficacy rate in preventing COVID-19 alongside a highly reassuring safety profile, an achievement realized in less than a year from the moment the virus was first sequenced. This clinical paper represents an absolute triumph of modern medicine and scientific logistics. It unequivocally validated the entire mRNA vaccine platform, marking the very first time an mRNA therapy was authorized for widespread human use. It decisively altered the grim trajectory of the COVID-19 pandemic and violently accelerated a new frontier for rapid-response vaccines and personalized mRNA therapeutics.

### Safety, Activity, and Immune Correlates of Anti-PD-1 Antibody in Cancer - 2012 - New England Journal of Medicine

In 2012, Suzanne Topalian, F. Stephen Hodi, and colleagues published the landmark Phase 1 clinical trial results testing the anti-PD-1 antibody nivolumab across a spectrum of solid tumors. The study revealed highly durable, robust tumor responses in patients suffering from advanced melanoma, non-small-cell lung cancer, and renal-cell cancer—malignancies historically notorious for resisting standard therapies. This clinical paper was a turning point because it provided undeniable proof that PD-1 blockade was both safe and exceptionally potent in humans. It cemented immune checkpoint inhibition as a broad, formidable new pillar of oncology alongside surgery, radiation, and chemotherapy, igniting a medical revolution in cancer care that continues to save countless lives.

### Search-and-Replace Genome Editing without Double-Strand Breaks or Donor DNA - 2019 - Nature

In 2019, Andrew Anzalone, David Liu, and colleagues published this monumental paper introducing "prime editing," an incredibly versatile evolution of the CRISPR toolkit. Prime editors physically fuse a catalytically impaired Cas9 to a reverse transcriptase and deploy an engineered prime editing guide RNA (pegRNA) to write fresh genetic information directly into a target DNA site, elegantly bypassing the need for double-strand breaks or separate donor DNA templates. This paper massively expanded the genome editing landscape. While base editing was restricted to specific single-letter swaps, prime editing unlocked genuine "search-and-replace" capabilities, permitting highly targeted insertions, deletions, and all 12 possible point mutations. This breakthrough vastly broadened the horizon of genetic engineering, theoretically putting the correction of up to 89% of known pathogenic human genetic variants within reach.

### Site-Directed Mutagenesis by Gene Targeting in Mouse Embryo-Derived Stem Cells - 1987 - Cell

In 1987, Thomas Doetschman, Oliver Smithies, and colleagues proved that homologous recombination could be harnessed to target and flawlessly modify specific endogenous genes within mouse embryonic stem (ES) cells. Merged with the concurrent breakthroughs of Mario Capecchi, this innovation proved that the mammalian genome was no longer locked, but could be edited at will. This foundational paper laid the direct groundwork for the 2007 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine, awarded to Oliver Smithies, Mario Capecchi, and Martin Evans. The Nobel committee praised them for establishing the very principles required to introduce specific gene modifications in mice. This exact technology birthed the "knockout mouse," fundamentally revolutionizing genetics by granting scientists the power to selectively delete and observe the function of almost any gene in a living mammal, profoundly elevating the study of human disease.

### Spatially Resolved, Highly Multiplexed RNA Profiling in Single Cells - 2015 - Science

In 2015, Kok Hao Chen, Xiaowei Zhuang, and colleagues unveiled Multiplexed Error-Robust Fluorescence In Situ Hybridization (MERFISH). This revolutionary imaging technology permitted scientists to simultaneously identify and precisely map the spatial location of thousands of distinct RNA species within individual, fully intact cells by employing combinatorial labeling and error-correcting barcodes. It was a colossal leap forward for transcriptomics and cell biology. Prior to MERFISH, single-cell RNA sequencing strictly required the harsh dissociation of tissue, completely destroying the vital spatial context of cell location. MERFISH spearheaded the explosive field of spatial transcriptomics, seamlessly merging genomics with high-resolution imaging, and allowing researchers to accurately map the complex architectural organization and intricate cell-to-cell signaling networks within intact tissues, such as the mammalian brain.

### Structural and Functional Features of Central Nervous System Lymphatic Vessels - 2015 - Nature

In 2015, Antoine Louveau, Jonathan Kipnis, and colleagues made a staggering discovery that forced the immediate rewriting of medical textbooks: they successfully identified fully functional lymphatic vessels lining the dural sinuses of the mammalian central nervous system. For decades, the absolute anatomical dogma dictated that the brain and spinal cord were entirely devoid of a lymphatic system and existed in a state of complete "immune privilege." This discovery completely rewrote the rules of neuroimmunology. It supplied the long-missing physical plumbing connecting the brain to the peripheral immune system, suddenly illuminating a clear mechanistic pathway explaining how immune cells and waste products physically transit in and out of the central nervous system. This revelation unlocked massive new avenues for deciphering and treating neurodegenerative diseases, multiple sclerosis, and brain tumors.

### Suppression of RNA Recognition by Toll-like Receptors - The Impact of Nucleoside Modification and the Evolutionary Origin of RNA - 2005 - Immunity

In 2005, Katalin Karikó, Drew Weissman, and colleagues published this foundational paper overcoming a massive, seemingly insurmountable roadblock in mRNA therapy: the reality that synthetic mRNA injected into the body provoked a severe, highly inflammatory immune response. They discovered that by actively incorporating naturally occurring modified nucleosides (such as pseudouridine) into the synthetic mRNA sequence, they could effectively blind the body's Toll-like receptors to its presence, rendering the mRNA completely stealthy and non-inflammatory. This vital breakthrough served as the cornerstone achievement that secured their 2023 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. The Nobel committee explicitly cited this 2005 discovery, emphasizing that eliminating this inflammatory hurdle was the exact key that enabled the unprecedented, rapid engineering of the highly successful mRNA vaccines during the COVID-19 pandemic.

### The C elegans Heterochronic Gene lin-4 Encodes Small RNAs with Antisense Complementarity to lin-14 - 1993 - Cell

In 1993, Rosalind Lee, Rhonda Feinbaum, and Victor Ambros published the astonishing revelation that the *lin-4* gene in *C. elegans* completely lacks the code to produce a protein. Instead, it generates a tiny, 22-nucleotide non-coding RNA that heavily regulates the translation of the *lin-14* mRNA through direct antisense complementarity. This marked the very first discovery of a microRNA (miRNA). The finding was a seismic shift for molecular biology. Dismissed for years as a quirky biological oddity specific to worms, miRNAs were eventually proven to be universally conserved and absolutely vital for fine-tuning gene expression across all animal species, including humans. This discovery threw open the doors to an entirely new, massive dimension of post-transcriptional gene regulation, reshaping the scientific understanding of embryonic development, cancer biology, and complex cellular networks.

### The Dependence of Cell-Free Protein Synthesis in E coli upon Naturally Occurring or Synthetic Polyribonucleotides - 1961 - PNAS

In 1961, Marshall Nirenberg and J. Heinrich Matthaei published the groundbreaking results of their legendary "Poly-U experiment." Utilizing a cell-free *E. coli* extract, they proved that introducing synthetic poly-uracil (Poly-U) RNA successfully directed the synthesis of a polypeptide chain constructed entirely of the amino acid phenylalanine. This was the definitive breakthrough that cracked the genetic code, conclusively proving that the RNA sequence UUU explicitly codes for phenylalanine. This monumental paper led directly to Marshall Nirenberg being awarded the 1968 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine (shared with Har Gobind Khorana and Robert Holley). The Nobel committee honored them for their brilliant interpretation of the genetic code and its exact function in protein synthesis, a monumental discovery that decoded the molecular dictionary of life and serves as the absolute bedrock of modern biology.

### Transposition of Native Chromatin for Fast and Sensitive Epigenomic Profiling of Open Chromatin, DNA-Binding Proteins, and Nucleosome Position - 2013 - Nature Methods

In 2013, Jason Buenrostro, Paul Giresi, Howard Chang, William Greenleaf, and colleagues unveiled ATAC-seq (Assay for Transposase-Accessible Chromatin using sequencing). This highly elegant technique hijacked a hyperactive Tn5 transposase to simultaneously cut and tag open, physically accessible regions of chromatin with necessary sequencing adaptors. ATAC-seq successfully democratized epigenomics. Prior mapping methods like DNase-seq were notoriously difficult, mandated massive quantities of cells, and were highly sensitive to slight protocol deviations. ATAC-seq, by contrast, was incredibly fast, exquisitely sensitive, and required exceptionally few cells, making the routine profiling of the open chromatin landscape—the physical regulatory "switches" of the genome—accessible to practically any laboratory. It is now an essential, ubiquitous tool for mapping gene regulation in human health, development, and disease, particularly thriving at the single-cell level.

### Viable Offspring Derived from Fetal and Adult Mammalian Cells - 1997 - Nature

In 1997, Ian Wilmut, Keith Campbell, and their team at the Roslin Institute published the staggering announcement that they had successfully cloned a mammal directly from an adult somatic cell, resulting in the birth of Dolly the sheep. By elegantly fusing an enucleated sheep egg with an adult mammary gland cell and utilizing somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT), they conclusively proved that the nucleus of a fully differentiated adult cell still harbored the complete, reversible genetic capacity to generate an entire, living organism. This paper forever changed biology and public consciousness. It decisively dismantled long-standing biological dogma regarding the irreversible nature of cellular differentiation and sparked intense global debates, sweeping ethical regulations, and the explosive, rapid growth of the modern stem cell and regenerative medicine fields.
