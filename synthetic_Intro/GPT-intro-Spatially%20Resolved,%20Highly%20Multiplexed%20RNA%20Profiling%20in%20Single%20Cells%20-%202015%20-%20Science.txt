# Introduction [464 words]

The expression level and spatial organization of RNAs in individual cells provide essential information about cellular state, function, and regulation. Differences in gene expression between cells underlie diverse biological behaviors, even within genetically identical populations, and the subcellular localization of RNAs can influence where proteins are synthesized and how cellular structures are organized. Thus, a complete description of the transcriptome should ideally report both the copy number and the spatial position of many RNA species in single cells.

Several approaches have enabled transcriptome analysis at the single-cell level, but each has important limitations. RNA sequencing can measure thousands of genes in individual cells and has revealed extensive heterogeneity among apparently similar cells. However, sequencing-based measurements require RNA extraction, amplification, and molecular conversion steps that can introduce bias and destroy the native spatial context of the molecules being measured. In contrast, single-molecule fluorescence in situ hybridization (smFISH) directly detects individual RNA molecules in fixed cells, providing absolute copy-number measurements together with subcellular localization. This imaging-based approach has been used to quantify transcriptional bursting, cell-to-cell variation, and RNA localization, but conventional implementations are limited to a small number of genes because only a few spectrally distinct fluorophores can be measured simultaneously.

Increasing the throughput of smFISH to the systems scale would enable a different class of single-cell measurements. If many RNA species could be imaged together, the natural variation in gene expression across individual cells could be used to infer co-regulated gene modules, constrain regulatory networks, and predict functions for poorly characterized genes. At the same time, the spatial distributions of many RNAs could be compared within the same cells, revealing groups of transcripts that share common localization programs. Achieving this goal requires a method that combines the molecular specificity and quantitative accuracy of smFISH with the multiplexing capacity normally associated with sequencing.

Combinatorial labeling provides a route to such highly multiplexed imaging. In this strategy, each RNA species is assigned a binary code word and identified by its pattern of signals across multiple rounds of hybridization and imaging. Because the number of possible code words grows exponentially with the number of hybridization rounds, this approach can, in principle, address thousands of RNA species. However, single-molecule measurements are intrinsically imperfect: hybridization events can be missed, fluorescent spots can be falsely detected, and errors accumulated over many imaging rounds can cause one RNA species to be mistaken for another. Without a mechanism for detecting or correcting these errors, the accuracy of combinatorial RNA imaging would deteriorate rapidly as the number of measured genes increased.

Here we report multiplexed error-robust fluorescence in situ hybridization (MERFISH), a single-molecule imaging method for highly multiplexed RNA profiling in individual cells. By combining combinatorial labeling, sequential hybridization and imaging, and error-robust encoding schemes, MERFISH measures the abundance and position of hundreds to thousands of RNA species in single cells. We demonstrate this approach in human fibroblasts by imaging 140 genes with an error-correcting code and 1001 genes with an error-detecting code, and show that the resulting data can be used to analyze cell-to-cell variation, infer co-varying gene groups, predict gene function, and identify spatially organized classes of RNAs.
