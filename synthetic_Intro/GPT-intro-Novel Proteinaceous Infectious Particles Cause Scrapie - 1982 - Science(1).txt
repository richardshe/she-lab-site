# Introduction

Scrapie is a transmissible degenerative disease of the central nervous system that occurs naturally in sheep and goats. Related disorders of humans, including kuru and Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD), share several pathological and biological properties with scrapie. All of these diseases are characterized by a prolonged incubation period, a progressive neurological syndrome, and a fatal outcome. The infectious agents responsible for these disorders have been difficult to define because they do not behave like conventional viruses, bacteria, or fungi. They are unusually resistant to heat and to many procedures that ordinarily inactivate microorganisms, yet they retain the capacity to reproduce within susceptible animals.

For many years the most reasonable assumption was that the scrapie agent, like all other known infectious agents, contained a nucleic acid genome. Attempts to identify such a genome, however, have been unsuccessful. The agent resists ultraviolet irradiation at doses that readily inactivate many small viruses. It is not measurably inactivated by a variety of nucleases, including enzymes that degrade DNA or RNA under conditions where control nucleic acids are destroyed. It also remains infectious after treatments expected to hydrolyze or chemically modify nucleic acids. These observations do not prove that the agent lacks nucleic acid, since a small genome might be shielded by protein or lipid. Nevertheless, they have made it increasingly difficult to assign the scrapie agent to any established class of infectious particle.

The biological analysis of the scrapie agent has been hindered by the absence of a tissue culture assay and by the failure of the native agent to elicit a useful immune response. Infectivity must therefore be measured in animals. Earlier studies in sheep and goats required long incubation periods and large numbers of animals. The adaptation of scrapie to mice and, subsequently, to hamsters provided more tractable systems, but quantitative assays still depended on serial dilution, inoculation, and prolonged observation for neurological disease. The development of a hamster bioassay based on the incubation time interval has made possible more systematic purification and inactivation studies.

Biochemical characterization of the scrapie agent has also been complicated by its distribution among many subcellular fractions and by its apparent heterogeneity in size, density, and charge. Several laboratories have reported partial purification procedures, including fractionation with microsomes, density gradients, ammonium sulfate precipitation, and detergent extraction. These studies established that the agent could be enriched but did not resolve its molecular composition. In particular, they did not determine whether infectivity was associated primarily with nucleic acid, protein, lipid, carbohydrate, or some complex of these macromolecules.

We have used the hamster model to develop a purification procedure for the scrapie agent from brain, where infectious titers are high. Detergent extracts were subjected to nuclease and protease digestion, followed by additional detergent extraction, ammonium sulfate precipitation, and agarose gel electrophoresis in Sarkosyl at low temperature. The resulting preparations permitted direct tests of the chemical requirements for infectivity. In the studies described here, we examine the sensitivity of the purified and partially purified agent to proteases, protein-modifying reagents, protein denaturants, nucleases, ultraviolet irradiation, psoralens, hydroxylamine, and other treatments directed against nucleic acids. The results indicate that a protein is required for infectivity, whereas no comparable evidence implicates a nucleic acid. These findings suggest that the scrapie agent represents a novel class of infectious particle and require reconsideration of the molecular basis of replication in these slow degenerative diseases.
