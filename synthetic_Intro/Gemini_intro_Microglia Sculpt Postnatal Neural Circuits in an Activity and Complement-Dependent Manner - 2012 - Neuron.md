# Introduction
Microglia are the primary resident immune cells of the mammalian central nervous system (CNS), widely recognized for their roles as active surveyors of the extracellular environment and rapid responders to injury and infection. Traditionally, studies of microglial function have predominantly focused on their behavior in the context of disease, where they act as classic macrophages to clear cellular debris, mediate inflammatory responses, and contribute to the pathology of various neurodegenerative disorders. However, recent advances in in vivo imaging and molecular profiling have revealed that microglia are highly dynamic even in the healthy, developing, and mature brain. They continuously extend and retract their processes, making transient but direct contacts with pre- and postsynaptic elements. Despite these intriguing observations suggesting an active engagement with the neural circuitry, the precise physiological functions of microglia at the synapse, as well as the molecular mechanisms that govern these interactions during normal CNS development, have remained largely elusive.

During postnatal development, neural circuits undergo a period of intense refinement. Initially, neurons form exuberant synaptic connections that must be precisely sculpted to establish mature, functional networks. This robust period of developmental synaptic pruning relies on the activity-dependent elimination of weak or inappropriate synapses while stronger, more active connections are maintained and strengthened. The retinogeniculate system has long served as a classic model for investigating this activity-dependent synaptic remodeling. In the visual system, retinal ganglion cells (RGCs) project to the dorsal lateral geniculate nucleus (dLGN) of the thalamus, where their inputs initially overlap extensively. Over the first postnatal week in the mouse, spontaneous neural activity, in the form of retinal waves, drives the segregation of these inputs into discrete, eye-specific territories. While the requirement for neural activity in this process is well established, the specific cellular executioners responsible for the physical removal of these intact, transient synapses have not been fully identified.

In the present study, we investigate the hypothesis that microglia are the active mediators of developmental synaptic pruning in the healthy CNS. Using the mouse retinogeniculate system as our model, we combine high-resolution confocal microscopy, in vivo phagocytosis assays, and three-dimensional electron microscopy to examine the dynamic interactions between microglia and RGC presynaptic terminals during the peak period of synaptic refinement in the dLGN. We provide compelling structural and functional evidence that microglia phagocytose intact presynaptic inputs, and that this engulfment is temporally restricted to the period of active remodeling. Furthermore, we demonstrate that this microglial pruning is dependent upon neural activity, as altering the balance of spontaneous activity between the two eyes directly influences the preferential engulfment of specific RGC inputs by microglia.

To identify the molecular pathways facilitating this targeted elimination, we focused on the classical complement cascade, which we previously showed is required for retinogeniculate pruning. We reveal that microglia in the developing dLGN uniquely express complement receptor 3 (CR3) during this critical window. We show that signaling through CR3 and its endogenous ligand, C3, is necessary for the microglial engulfment of synapses. Finally, using genetic mouse models deficient in CR3 or C3, we demonstrate that disrupting this microglia-specific phagocytic pathway results in sustained, long-term deficits in synaptic connectivity and precise circuit wiring. Together, our results define a fundamental role for microglia in sculpting postnatal neural circuits and identify the complement-dependent molecular mechanisms by which they engulf and remodel developing synapses.
