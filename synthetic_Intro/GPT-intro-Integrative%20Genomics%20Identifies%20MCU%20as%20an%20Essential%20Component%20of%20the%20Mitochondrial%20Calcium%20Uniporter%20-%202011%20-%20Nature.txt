# Introduction [346 words]

Mitochondria are central integrators of cellular metabolism, death signalling and intracellular Ca²⁺ homeostasis. In many mammalian cells, rapid Ca²⁺ entry into the mitochondrial matrix couples cytosolic Ca²⁺ signals to ATP production by activating dehydrogenases of the tricarboxylic acid cycle, and can also promote cell death when matrix Ca²⁺ loading becomes excessive. This uptake is mediated by the mitochondrial calcium uniporter, a high-capacity, low-affinity transport activity driven by the large membrane potential across the mitochondrial inner membrane. The uniporter is inhibited by ruthenium red and its more potent derivative Ru360, and has been characterized electrophysiologically and biochemically in isolated mitochondria for several decades.

Despite this extensive physiological literature, the molecular identity of the uniporter has remained unknown. This has represented a central gap in mitochondrial biology, because genetic manipulation of the uniporter would allow its contribution to metabolism, signalling and cell death to be tested directly in intact cells and animals. Several properties have constrained the search for its components. The uniporter activity is widely conserved in vertebrate tissues and many eukaryotes, appears to reside in the mitochondrial inner membrane, and is likely to involve low-abundance membrane proteins that are not readily identified by conventional purification.

We recently used comparative organellar proteomics and evolutionary conservation to identify MICU1, an EF-hand-containing mitochondrial protein required for normal mitochondrial Ca²⁺ uptake. MICU1, however, lacks predicted transmembrane helices, suggesting that it is unlikely to itself constitute the ion-conducting pore. We reasoned that additional uniporter components should share a similar evolutionary distribution and expression pattern with MICU1, and that independent genomic strategies might therefore converge on candidates in the same functional pathway.

Here we combine whole-genome phylogenetic profiling, genome-wide messenger RNA co-expression analysis and mitochondrial protein coexpression analysis to nominate a previously uncharacterized inner membrane protein, CCDC109A. We show that this protein, which we rename mitochondrial calcium uniporter (MCU), is essential for mitochondrial Ca²⁺ uptake in cultured cells and in vivo in mouse liver, forms oligomeric complexes in the inner membrane, interacts with MICU1, and contains conserved residues required for transport and Ru360 sensitivity. These findings identify MCU as an essential component of the mitochondrial calcium uniporter.
