Cellular differentiation in mammals is traditionally viewed as a unidirectional process, wherein pluripotent cells progressively lose potential and commit to specific tissue lineages. The discovery of induced pluripotent stem cells demonstrated that developmental restriction can be reversed by forced expression of defined transcription factors. Studies also show somatic nuclei can be reprogrammed by transfer into oocytes or fusion with embryonic stem cells. These approaches rely on exogenous reprogramming factors or complex, embryo-derived environments.

In plants, environmental stress can rapidly induce cellular reprogramming, allowing differentiated cells to transition into stem-cell-like states capable of generating new organisms. Whether mammalian cells possess a similar latent plasticity to undergo fate conversion in response to environmental cues remains unknown. In this study, we investigated the possibility that strong external stimuli could trigger the acquisition of pluripotency in fully committed mammalian somatic cells. We hypothesized that transient exposure to severe physical or chemical stressors might destabilize the somatic epigenetic state, releasing cells from their differentiated commitments. Here, we demonstrate that a simple, transient low-pH treatment is sufficient to reprogram mature, lineage-committed blood cells into pluripotent stem cells without the need for genetic manipulation or nuclear transfer.
