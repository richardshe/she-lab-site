Cancer immunoediting describes the process by which the immune system can protect the host against tumour development while also shaping the immunogenicity of cancers that ultimately arise. In experimental models, tumour rejection antigens have long been inferred from transplantation, vaccination and T-cell transfer studies, yet the molecular identity of the antigens recognized during effective tumour immunity has often remained uncertain. This question has become more urgent with the clinical development of antibodies that block inhibitory receptors on T cells. Monoclonal antibodies targeting cytotoxic T-lymphocyte associated antigen-4 (CTLA-4) or programmed death-1 (PD-1) can induce durable tumour regressions in patients with cancer, but the antigens targeted by the T cells unleashed by these therapies are incompletely defined.

One attractive class of antigens is generated by tumour-specific somatic mutations. Such mutant antigens are not expressed by normal tissues and therefore should escape central tolerance while conferring a high degree of tumour specificity. Recent advances in massively parallel sequencing and computational prediction of peptide binding to major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules now make it possible to survey the mutational landscape of an individual tumour and nominate candidate mutant epitopes. However, whether tumour-specific mutant antigens represent the dominant targets of checkpoint blockade therapy, whether mutant-antigen-specific T cells pre-exist in progressively growing tumours, and whether these antigens can be exploited therapeutically have not been established.

Here we combine exome sequencing, RNA sequencing, epitope prediction, peptide-MHC tetramer analysis and functional T-cell assays to identify mutant antigens targeted during checkpoint blockade-induced rejection of a methylcholanthrene-induced mouse sarcoma. We further test whether synthetic long-peptide vaccines incorporating these epitopes can reproduce the therapeutic effects of checkpoint blockade and use antigen-specific T cells to define the molecular programs induced by anti-PD-1 and/or anti-CTLA-4 therapy.
