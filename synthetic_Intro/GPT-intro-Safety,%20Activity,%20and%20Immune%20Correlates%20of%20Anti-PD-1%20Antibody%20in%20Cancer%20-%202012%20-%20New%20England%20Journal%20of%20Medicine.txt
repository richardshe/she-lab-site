# Introduction [242 words]

Therapies that augment antitumor immunity have produced durable tumor regressions in some patients with advanced cancer, but their clinical benefit has been limited by incomplete response rates and mechanism-specific toxic effects. One approach to improving this therapeutic index is to block inhibitory pathways that restrain activated T cells within the tumor microenvironment. Programmed death 1 (PD-1) is an inhibitory receptor expressed on activated T cells, B cells, and myeloid cells. Engagement of PD-1 by its ligands, PD-L1 and PD-L2, attenuates T-cell receptor signaling, cytokine production, and cytolytic function, thereby limiting immune-mediated tissue injury but also providing a mechanism by which tumors may evade immune destruction.

Expression of PD-L1 has been observed in multiple human cancers and may be induced by inflammatory signals in the tumor microenvironment. This pattern suggests that the PD-1–PD-L1 pathway can mediate adaptive immune resistance: an endogenous antitumor immune response may induce a counterregulatory signal that protects tumor cells from elimination. Preclinical studies have shown that blockade of this pathway can enhance antitumor T-cell responses, and early clinical observations have suggested that inhibition of PD-1 may have activity in advanced malignancies.

BMS-936558 is a fully human IgG4 monoclonal antibody that blocks PD-1. We conducted a phase 1 study to evaluate the safety, pharmacokinetics, pharmacodynamics, and antitumor activity of BMS-936558 in patients with selected advanced solid tumors and to explore whether pretreatment tumor-cell expression of PD-L1 was associated with objective response.
