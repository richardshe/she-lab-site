# Introduction
The experimental introduction of RNA into living cells has emerged as a valuable technique in certain biological systems to intentionally interfere with the function of a specific endogenous gene. Traditionally, such specific gene-silencing effects have been presumed to result from a straightforward antisense mechanism, which relies on the stoichiometric hybridization between the exogenously injected single-stranded antisense RNA and the complementary endogenous messenger RNA (mRNA) transcripts, thereby preventing translation. This phenomenon of RNA interference has been widely utilized in the nematode Caenorhabditis elegans to experimentally manipulate gene expression and deduce gene function. In this study, we systematically investigate the structural and delivery requirements of the interfering RNA molecules in C. elegans. To our surprise, we discovered that purified double-stranded RNA (dsRNA) was substantially more effective at producing targeted genetic interference than either the sense or antisense strand individually. Moreover, we found that only a few molecules of injected dsRNA were required per affected cell to achieve robust silencing. This remarkable potency argues strongly against a simple stoichiometric antisense mechanism and instead suggests the existence of a catalytic or amplification component inherent to the dsRNA-mediated interference process.
