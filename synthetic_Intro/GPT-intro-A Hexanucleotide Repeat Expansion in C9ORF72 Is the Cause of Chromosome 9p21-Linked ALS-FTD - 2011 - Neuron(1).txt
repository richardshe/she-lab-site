# Introduction

Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) is a fatal adult-onset neurodegenerative disease characterized by the selective loss of upper and lower motor neurons, leading to progressive paralysis and death, typically within several years of symptom onset. Frontotemporal dementia (FTD) is the second most common cause of early-onset dementia and is characterized clinically by progressive changes in behavior, personality, language, and executive function. Although ALS and FTD have historically been considered distinct clinical entities, there is increasing evidence that they represent overlapping manifestations of a common neurodegenerative disease spectrum. Cognitive and behavioral impairment are frequently observed in ALS patients, motor neuron dysfunction may occur in FTD patients, and some families contain individuals with ALS, FTD, or combined ALS-FTD.

This clinical overlap is mirrored at the pathological and genetic levels. TDP-43-positive neuronal inclusions are present in the majority of ALS cases and in a substantial fraction of FTD cases, and mutations in TARDBP, FUS, VCP, and other genes have been identified in patients with ALS, FTD, or both phenotypes. These discoveries have strengthened the view that disturbed RNA processing and protein homeostasis are central mechanisms in neurodegeneration. However, despite these advances, a large proportion of familial ALS and FTD remains genetically unexplained.

One of the most important unresolved loci for ALS and FTD lies on chromosome 9p21. Linkage studies in multiple large kindreds with autosomal-dominant ALS, FTD, or ALS-FTD identified a shared interval on chromosome 9, and subsequent genome-wide association studies confirmed a strong association between this region and ALS in population-based cohorts. This locus is of particular importance in Finland, where ALS has an unusually high incidence and where a founder haplotype at chromosome 9p21 accounts for a large fraction of familial disease. We previously refined this association signal to a linkage disequilibrium block containing three annotated genes, MOBKL2B, IFNK, and C9ORF72. However, conventional sequencing of coding exons and splice junctions within the region failed to identify a pathogenic mutation, suggesting that the causal lesion might reside in a poorly annotated, noncoding, or structurally complex portion of the locus.

The identification of the chromosome 9p21 mutation has been a major obstacle to understanding the relationship between ALS and FTD. Because the linked families and associated population cohorts strongly implicate this interval, we reasoned that deep sequencing of affected haplotypes, coupled with targeted screening of large case-control series, could reveal the pathogenic variant. Here we report the identification of a massive GGGGCC hexanucleotide repeat expansion within C9ORF72 as the cause of chromosome 9p21-linked ALS-FTD. We further show that this expansion is the most common genetic cause of familial ALS and FTD identified to date, accounting for a remarkably high proportion of disease in the Finnish population and a substantial fraction of familial ALS in outbred European-ancestry cohorts.
