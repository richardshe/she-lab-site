# Introduction [405 words]

Breast and ovarian cancers occur commonly in the general population, but their occurrence in multiple members of some families, often at unusually early ages, has long suggested the existence of inherited susceptibility alleles of large effect. The observation that breast and ovarian cancer can segregate together in the same kindreds further suggested that at least one such allele might influence the development of both tissues. Genetic linkage studies provided support for this view by mapping a major breast and ovarian cancer susceptibility locus, BRCA1, to the long arm of chromosome 17.

The isolation of BRCA1 has presented a formidable problem in positional cloning. The genetic interval defined by recombinant chromosomes was still large enough to contain many genes, and no obvious biochemical function could be assigned to the susceptibility locus from the cancer phenotype alone. Thus, the search for BRCA1 required the construction of a detailed physical map across the candidate region, the systematic identification of expressed sequences contained within that region, and the comparison of candidate transcripts with DNA from individuals carrying inherited susceptibility haplotypes.

A gene within the BRCA1 interval can be considered a strong candidate only if it satisfies several independent criteria. It should lie within the smallest region defined by meiotic recombination in linked kindreds. It should be expressed in tissues relevant to the disease phenotype, including breast or ovary. Most critically, it should contain rare sequence alterations in affected kindreds that segregate with the predisposing chromosome and are absent from appropriate control populations. Because cancer-predisposing alleles may include protein-truncating mutations, missense substitutions, splice alterations, or mutations affecting transcript production or stability, analysis of both genomic DNA and complementary DNA is required.

We have applied these criteria to expressed sequences from the 17q21 interval containing BRCA1. A transcript assembled from complementary DNA clones, hybrid-selected sequences, and polymerase chain reaction products encodes a predicted protein of 1863 amino acids and contains an amino-terminal zinc finger motif. This gene is expressed in several tissues, including breast and ovary, and extends across approximately 100 kb of genomic DNA. We now describe mutations in this gene in five kindreds presumed to segregate BRCA1 susceptibility alleles. These mutations include frameshift, nonsense, missense, and inferred regulatory alterations. Their location within the genetic interval, their predicted effects on gene function, and their cosegregation with disease-associated haplotypes strongly indicate that this gene is BRCA1.
