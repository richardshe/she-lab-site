# Introduction

Multicellular tissues contain many kinds of cells, each defined by a combination of developmental history, physiological function, and current state. The identities and activities of these cells are reflected in their mRNA expression programs. Measurements of gene expression in bulk tissue, however, average together the transcripts of many different cells, obscuring rare cell types, transient states, and the gene-expression relationships that distinguish one cell population from another. Single-cell RNA sequencing offers a direct way to measure this diversity, but existing approaches have been limited by the effort and expense required to prepare large numbers of individual libraries.

An ideal method for single-cell transcriptomics would be simple enough for routine use, inexpensive enough for extensive replication, and scalable enough to characterize complex tissues containing many related and rare cell populations. It would preserve the cell of origin for each transcript while allowing thousands of cells to be processed together. It would also count transcripts digitally, so that measurements from individual cells could be compared across experiments and used to infer both cell types and cell states.

Here we describe Drop-seq, a method for highly parallel single-cell RNA-seq. Drop-seq uses microfluidic droplets to isolate individual cells with barcoded microparticles. After cell lysis, mRNAs are captured on the co-encapsulated microparticle, such that all transcripts from a cell acquire the same cellular barcode and each molecule also receives a unique molecular identifier. Thousands of these single-cell transcriptomes attached to microparticles can then be reverse-transcribed, amplified, and sequenced together.

We validate Drop-seq using species-mixing experiments, synthetic RNA controls, and comparisons to bulk and single-cell expression measurements. We then apply the method to the mouse retina, a complex neural tissue with well-studied major cell classes and many incompletely characterized subtypes. Analysis of 44,808 retinal cells identifies 39 transcriptionally distinct populations, including known retinal cell classes and previously unrecognized molecular distinctions among amacrine cells. These results illustrate how routine, large-scale single-cell profiling can create molecular atlases of complex tissues and reveal biological structure that is difficult to observe by lower-throughput approaches.
