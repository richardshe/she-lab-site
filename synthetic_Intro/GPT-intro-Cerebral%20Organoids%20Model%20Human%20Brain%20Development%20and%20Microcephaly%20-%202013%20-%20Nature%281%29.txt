# Introduction

The human brain displays a degree of anatomical complexity and cellular diversity that has been difficult to model experimentally. Although animal models have provided essential insight into conserved mechanisms of neural induction, patterning and neurogenesis, many features of human cortical development are either absent or greatly reduced in commonly used model organisms. These include the pronounced expansion of neural progenitor populations, the organization of germinal zones, and the abundance of outer radial glia, all of which are thought to contribute to the evolutionary enlargement of the human cerebral cortex. Consequently, disorders that affect human brain growth can be difficult to reproduce in animals, limiting the ability to study their developmental origins.

Human pluripotent stem cells provide a complementary system for examining human development in vitro. Directed differentiation protocols have made it possible to generate many neural cell types, including cortical neurons, from embryonic stem cells and induced pluripotent stem cells. However, most approaches rely on exogenous patterning signals and produce relatively homogeneous populations or simple neural rosettes. Such systems have been valuable for defining cell-intrinsic differentiation programs, but they do not fully capture the three-dimensional architecture, regional heterogeneity, or long-range tissue interactions that characterize early brain development. Recent work in other organ systems has shown that pluripotent stem cells can self-organize into complex structures when provided with appropriate culture conditions, suggesting that intrinsic developmental programs may be sufficient to generate higher-order tissue organization.

Here we describe a three-dimensional culture system that allows human pluripotent stem cells to generate complex neural tissues, which we term cerebral organoids. Rather than imposing specific regional identities with patterning factors, this method promotes neuroectoderm formation, embeds developing tissues in an extracellular matrix scaffold, and maintains them in spinning culture to improve nutrient exchange. Under these conditions, organoids develop multiple discrete but interdependent brain regions, including dorsal cortical tissues with organized progenitor zones and neurons. These tissues display features of human cortical development, including outer radial glia and an organization resembling the outer subventricular zone.

We further apply this system to model primary microcephaly, a disorder of reduced brain size whose severity has been difficult to recapitulate in mice. Using patient-derived induced pluripotent stem cells carrying loss-of-function mutations in CDK5RAP2, together with RNA interference and rescue experiments, we find that mutant tissues undergo premature neuronal differentiation at the expense of progenitor expansion. These findings indicate that cerebral organoids can recapitulate aspects of human brain development and provide a tractable system for investigating human-specific neurodevelopmental disease mechanisms.
