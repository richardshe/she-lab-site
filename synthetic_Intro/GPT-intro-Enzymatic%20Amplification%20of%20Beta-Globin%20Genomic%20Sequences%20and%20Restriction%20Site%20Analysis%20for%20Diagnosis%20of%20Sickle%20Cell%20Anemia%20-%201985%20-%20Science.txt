# Introduction [359 words]

Prenatal diagnosis of sickle cell anemia has depended on the analysis of fetal DNA by restriction endonuclease digestion, Southern transfer, and hybridization with cloned globin gene probes. These methods are reliable, but require microgram quantities of relatively intact DNA and several days to complete after DNA has been prepared. In many prenatal samples, particularly those obtained early in gestation, the amount and quality of DNA may be limiting. A more rapid and sensitive procedure for detecting the beta-globin genotype would therefore be useful both for clinical diagnosis and for the analysis of defined human gene sequences.

Sickle cell anemia results from a single base substitution in the sixth codon of the beta-globin gene. This mutation changes the beta^A sequence to beta^S and eliminates a Dde I restriction site present in the normal allele. The same nucleotide change can be detected indirectly by restriction fragment length polymorphism analysis, but such assays generally require Southern blotting and large amounts of genomic DNA. We reasoned that if the small segment of the beta-globin gene containing the diagnostic base substitution could be selectively increased in copy number, allele detection could be performed by a simple oligonucleotide-based assay rather than by conventional genomic hybridization.

We have combined two procedures to achieve this goal. The first is primer-mediated enzymatic amplification of a defined genomic sequence. Two synthetic oligonucleotides complementary to opposite strands of the beta-globin gene are used to prime DNA synthesis across the region containing the sickle cell mutation. Repeated cycles of denaturation, primer annealing, and polymerase extension result in the exponential accumulation of the intervening sequence. The second procedure is oligomer restriction analysis, in which an end-labeled oligonucleotide probe is hybridized in solution to the amplified DNA and then digested with restriction enzymes that distinguish the beta^A and beta^S sequences.

Here we show that a 110-base pair segment of the beta-globin gene can be amplified approximately 220,000-fold from human genomic DNA. The amplified products can be analyzed by oligomer restriction digestion to determine whether a sample is homozygous normal, heterozygous, or homozygous for the sickle cell allele. The combined procedure permits beta-globin genotyping in less than one day and with substantially less starting DNA than is required for standard Southern blot analysis.
