# Introduction [222 words]
In addition to the cardiovascular system, lymphatic vessels constitute a distinct vascular network that maintains tissue-fluid homeostasis, supports immune-cell trafficking, and conveys soluble antigens from peripheral tissues to draining lymph nodes. The central nervous system has long been considered an exception to this general organization, as no classical lymphatic vasculature has been described within the brain or its surrounding meninges. This anatomical view has shaped the concept of brain immune privilege, despite accumulating evidence that the central nervous system is under constant immune surveillance and that cerebrospinal fluid-derived antigens and immune cells can reach the cervical lymph nodes.

How immune cells enter and leave the meningeal spaces, and how cerebrospinal fluid communicates with peripheral lymphoid organs, remains incompletely understood. Several routes for cerebrospinal fluid outflow have been proposed, including absorption through arachnoid granulations and drainage along olfactory pathways through the cribriform plate into nasal lymphatics. However, these mechanisms do not fully explain the organization of meningeal immune surveillance or the rapid appearance of central nervous system-derived material in deep cervical lymph nodes.

Here we identify a network of functional lymphatic vessels in the dura mater, running alongside the dural sinuses. These vessels express classical lymphatic endothelial markers, display the structural features of initial lymphatics, drain cerebrospinal fluid and immune cells, and connect the meningeal compartment with the deep cervical lymph nodes.
