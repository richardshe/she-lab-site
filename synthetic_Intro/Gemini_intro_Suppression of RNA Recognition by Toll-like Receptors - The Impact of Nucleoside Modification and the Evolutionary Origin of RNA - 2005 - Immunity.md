The mammalian innate immune system serves as the first line of defense against invading pathogens, relying on a repertoire of pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) to rapidly detect and respond to conserved microbial signatures. Prominent among these PRRs are the Toll-like receptors (TLRs), a family of transmembrane proteins that recognize diverse pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs), including lipids, lipoproteins, proteins, and nucleic acids. The recognition of nucleic acids by TLRs represents a critical mechanism for sensing viral and bacterial infections. For instance, TLR9 detects unmethylated CpG-rich DNA motifs that are prevalent in microbial genomes but rare and predominantly methylated in mammalian DNA. This distinction allows the immune system to exploit DNA methylation as a fundamental discriminant between self and non-self, ensuring robust antimicrobial responses while maintaining tolerance to host DNA.

In addition to DNA, RNA has emerged as a potent immunostimulatory molecule. It is well established that double-stranded RNA (dsRNA), a common intermediate in viral replication, is recognized by TLR3, leading to the activation of dendritic cells (DCs) and the robust production of type I interferons and inflammatory cytokines. More recently, single-stranded RNA (ssRNA) of viral origin, such as from influenza virus or human immunodeficiency virus, was identified as a natural ligand for mammalian TLR7 and human TLR8. Furthermore, host-derived RNA released from necrotic cells or transcribed in vitro has been shown to activate DCs, suggesting that the immune system can sense endogenous RNA under certain conditions, such as tissue damage.

Despite the well-characterized role of RNA in immune activation, a fundamental paradox remains: mammalian cells are packed with abundant self-RNA (including ribosomal, transfer, and messenger RNAs), yet this ubiquitous endogenous RNA does not typically provoke an autoimmune response under normal physiological conditions. Why does the immune system react robustly to viral and bacterial RNA, or even in vitro-transcribed RNA, but remain largely unresponsive to the vast quantities of host cellular RNA? We hypothesized that post-transcriptional modifications, which are naturally present in cellular RNAs, might serve as a molecular signature to suppress immune recognition, analogous to the role of CpG methylation in avoiding TLR9 activation by host DNA.

RNA undergoes extensive and diverse post-transcriptional modifications, with nearly a hundred different modified nucleosides identified to date. These modifications are most abundant in transfer RNA (tRNA) and ribosomal RNA (rRNA), but are also present in messenger RNA (mRNA). Crucially, the frequency and diversity of these modifications correlate with the evolutionary complexity of the organism; mammalian RNA is highly modified, whereas bacterial RNA contains significantly fewer modifications.

In this study, we systematically investigated the impact of naturally occurring nucleoside modifications on the immunostimulatory potential of RNA. By generating in vitro-transcribed RNAs incorporating specific modified nucleosides (such as 5-methylcytidine, N6-methyladenosine, 5-methyluridine, 2-thiouridine, and pseudouridine) and testing natural RNAs from diverse evolutionary sources, we demonstrate that nucleoside modifications potently suppress the ability of RNA to activate human DCs. We further show that unmodified RNA is a shared ligand for human TLR3, TLR7, and TLR8, and that the incorporation of modified nucleosides specifically abrogates signaling through these receptors. Our findings reveal that the innate immune system utilizes nucleoside modifications as a crucial mechanism to discriminate between self and non-self RNA, providing a novel evolutionary perspective on the origins of RNA immunity and the physiological significance of RNA modifications.
