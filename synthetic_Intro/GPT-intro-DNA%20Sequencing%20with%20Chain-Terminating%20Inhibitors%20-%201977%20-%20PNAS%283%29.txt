# Introduction

The determination of nucleotide sequences in DNA is an essential step in relating the chemical structure of genes to their biological function. Several methods have been described for obtaining such sequences, and the most useful of these at present is the “plus and minus” method. This procedure has been applied successfully to the DNA of bacteriophage phiX174 and to other small DNA molecules. However, the method involves a number of manipulations, including a preliminary synthesis step and subsequent separations, and the interpretation of the resulting patterns is sometimes made difficult by artefact bands and by uncertainty in the length of homopolymeric runs.

We have therefore sought a simpler procedure that would retain the advantages of enzymic copying from a defined primer, but would give more direct termination of the newly synthesized chains. The method described here is based on the use of nucleotide analogues that can be incorporated by DNA polymerase but that then prevent further extension of the chain. The most useful compounds for this purpose are the 2',3'-dideoxynucleoside triphosphates. Because these compounds lack the 3'-hydroxyl group required for formation of the next phosphodiester bond, their incorporation should terminate synthesis specifically at the corresponding nucleotide. Arabinonucleoside triphosphates may also act as terminators under some conditions.

In principle, four separate incubations are carried out with a primer-template complex, DNA polymerase, radioactive deoxynucleoside triphosphate, and one of the chain-terminating inhibitors. Each reaction gives a series of labelled DNA fragments ending at a particular base. The products are then separated by electrophoresis on denaturing acrylamide gels, and the sequence can be read from the pattern of bands. The procedure is closely related to the plus and minus method in that it uses a restriction fragment as primer and depends on gel fractionation of labelled extension products, but it differs in that termination is obtained directly during synthesis rather than by omission or limiting addition of particular nucleotides.

We have tested this inhibitor method mainly with the DNA of bacteriophage phiX174, for which a nearly complete provisional sequence is available. This DNA provides a useful substrate because many restriction fragments can be used as primers on either the viral or the complementary strand, and because discrepancies revealed by the new procedure can be compared with previous determinations. In some cases the primer fragment can be left attached to the newly synthesized DNA. In others it is necessary to remove it by restriction enzyme cleavage, or by introducing a single ribonucleotide that can subsequently be cleaved with ribonuclease.

The results show that sequences can usually be read for considerable distances from a priming site, and that the method gives patterns that are in many cases clearer than those obtained by the plus and minus procedure. The method also reveals errors in provisional regions of the phiX174 sequence. Although some difficulties remain, particularly where secondary structure causes compression of bands during electrophoresis, the use of chain-terminating inhibitors provides a rapid and accurate approach to DNA sequencing.
