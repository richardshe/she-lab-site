The determination of the exact sequence of nucleotides within a DNA molecule is of fundamental importance to virtually every branch of molecular biology. A precise understanding of genetic information is essential for elucidating the structure of genes, the mechanisms of gene expression and regulation, and the molecular basis of genetic diseases. While early efforts to sequence nucleic acids were slow and exceedingly complex, relying largely on the partial degradation of small RNA molecules, recent years have witnessed the development of more rapid and systematic techniques for determining the nucleotide sequence of DNA. 

Our laboratory recently described a relatively rapid and simple approach to DNA sequencing, known as the "plus and minus" method. This technique relies on the use of DNA polymerase to extend a primer hybridized to a single-stranded DNA template. In the initial extension phase, the primer is elongated in the presence of all four deoxynucleoside triphosphates, one of which is radioactively labeled, under conditions that result in a heterogeneous mixture of newly synthesized chains of varying lengths. These chains are then subjected to two distinct types of second-stage polymerase reactions. In the "minus" system, the chains are extended further in the presence of only three of the four triphosphates, causing them to terminate specifically at the position just before the missing nucleotide. In the "plus" system, an exonuclease degrades the chains until it reaches the specific nucleotide corresponding to the single triphosphate provided in the reaction mixture. By separating the products of these eight distinct reactions side-by-side on high-resolution polyacrylamide gels, the sequence of the newly synthesized DNA can be deduced from the pattern of radioactive bands.

While the "plus and minus" method has been used successfully to determine the complete sequence of the bacteriophage phiX174 genome, it suffers from several notable limitations. The procedure is technically demanding, requiring two separate incubation steps and an intermediate purification on a Sephadex column. Furthermore, the results can sometimes be ambiguous, particularly when trying to determine the exact number of identical consecutive nucleotides in "runs," or when dealing with artifactual bands arising from non-specific termination or uneven extension.

In this report, we describe a new, alternative method for determining nucleotide sequences in DNA that represents a significant improvement over the "plus and minus" technique in terms of speed, simplicity, and accuracy. This new method is based on the use of specific chain-terminating inhibitors of DNA polymerase. Specifically, we employ 2',3'-dideoxynucleoside triphosphates (ddNTPs) and arabinonucleoside triphosphates as analogues of the normal deoxynucleoside triphosphates (dNTPs). Because these analogues lack a 3'-hydroxyl group, their incorporation into a growing DNA chain by DNA polymerase completely prevents the addition of any subsequent nucleotides, thereby leading to specific chain termination.

By carrying out four separate DNA polymerase reactions—each containing the template, primer, all four normal dNTPs (one of which is radioactive), and a small, carefully optimized amount of one of the four specific chain-terminating analogues—we can generate four distinct sets of DNA fragments. Within each reaction, the fragments will terminate exclusively at positions corresponding to the incorporated analogue. When these four sets of fragments are resolved by high-resolution gel electrophoresis and visualized by autoradiography, the nucleotide sequence of the DNA can be read directly and unambiguously from the resulting pattern of bands. This method avoids the intermediate purification steps of the plus and minus method and provides much clearer resolution of consecutive identical nucleotides. Here, we demonstrate the utility of this chain-terminating inhibitor method by applying it to the DNA of bacteriophage phiX174.
