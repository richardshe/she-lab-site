# Introduction
Current genome-editing technologies, such as zinc finger nucleases, TALENs, and the widely used CRISPR/Cas9 system, rely fundamentally on the introduction of targeted double-stranded (ds) DNA breaks at a specified genomic locus. This initial cleavage event is intended to stimulate the cell's endogenous DNA repair pathways. However, the cellular response to these dsDNA breaks is heavily biased towards the error-prone non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) pathway, which typically induces an abundance of random insertions and deletions (indels) at the target site. While this is useful for gene disruption, it poses a significant challenge for the precise correction of specific genetic mutations. Although homology-directed repair (HDR) can be used to incorporate desired sequence changes from an exogenously supplied donor template, HDR is generally inefficient, highly variable across different cell types and cell states, and is almost always outcompeted by indel formation. Given that the vast majority of known human genetic diseases arise from point mutations rather than large structural variations, there is a critical need for an approach that can efficiently and cleanly correct point mutations without the deleterious consequences associated with dsDNA cleavage.

Here, we report the development of "base editing," a novel and fundamentally different approach to genome editing. This technology enables the direct, irreversible conversion of one specific target DNA base into another in a highly programmable manner, entirely bypassing the requirement for dsDNA backbone cleavage or the introduction of a homologous donor template. To achieve this, we engineered synthetic fusions comprising a catalytically impaired CRISPR/Cas9 and a naturally occurring cytidine deaminase enzyme. These chimeric proteins retain the remarkable ability of Cas9 to be directed to a specific genomic locus using a programmable guide RNA, yet they do not induce dsDNA breaks. Instead, upon binding to the target site, the tethered deaminase mediates the direct chemical conversion of cytidine to uridine on the exposed single-stranded DNA bubble, thereby directly effecting a C → T (or G → A) substitution. 

The resulting first-generation base editors successfully convert cytidines within a tightly defined window of approximately five nucleotides, demonstrating the capability to efficiently correct a variety of point mutations relevant to human disease in vitro. However, to translate this biochemical activity into permanent genomic alterations in living mammalian cells, we had to manipulate the cellular DNA repair response, which naturally attempts to revert the engineered U:G mismatch back to a C:G base pair. In four transformed human and murine cell lines, we show that second- and third-generation base editors—which incorporate a uracil glycosylase inhibitor (UGI) and utilize a Cas9 nickase to specifically target the non-edited strand—successfully bias the cellular repair machinery to favor the desired base-editing outcomes. This strategy results in the permanent correction of ~15-75% of total cellular DNA with minimal (typically ≤ 1%) indel formation. Base editing thus dramatically expands the scope, precision, and efficiency of genome editing for the targeted correction of point mutations.
