# Introduction

The ability to introduce defined mutations into the mammalian genome would provide a direct means of relating gene structure to gene function. At present, mutations in cultured mammalian cells are obtained either by selecting rare spontaneous variants or by introducing foreign DNA that integrates at apparently random positions in the genome. These approaches have been useful for isolating selectable mutants and for studying expression of transfected genes, but they do not provide a general method for altering a predetermined chromosomal locus. A procedure that would allow cloned DNA sequences to direct a mutation to their cognate site in the genome would make it possible to construct specific alleles in cultured cells and, in principle, in the mouse.

Homologous recombination offers such a route. In lower eukaryotes, cloned DNA introduced into the cell can recombine efficiently with the corresponding chromosomal sequences, thereby replacing or disrupting the endogenous locus. Similar events have been observed in mammalian cells, but generally at low frequency and against a large background of nonhomologous integration. Thus, a central question is whether homologous recombination can be used practically to mutate a specific gene in mammalian cells, and whether the products of such events can be recovered with sufficient efficiency to permit their routine analysis.

Embryo-derived stem (ES) cells provide a particularly attractive recipient for such experiments. These cells can be propagated in culture while retaining the capacity to contribute to many tissues after introduction into early embryos. If a defined mutation could be introduced into an ES cell chromosome, it should be possible to recover mice carrying that mutation. This would extend somatic cell genetics from cultured cell lines to the intact animal and would provide a general method for analyzing genes whose functions are revealed only during development or physiology.

We have chosen the mouse hypoxanthine phosphoribosyl transferase (Hprt) gene as a target for developing such a procedure. Hprt is useful because loss of gene function can be selected by resistance to the base analog 6-thioguanine (6-TG), whereas acquisition of an introduced neomycin resistance (neo) gene can be selected by resistance to G418. Thus, a targeting vector that both disrupts Hprt and carries neo should, after homologous recombination, generate cells that are resistant to both drugs. The Hprt locus is nevertheless not an especially favorable target. It is expressed at a low level in ES cells, spans more than 33 kb, and contains repetitive sequences that complicate molecular analysis. Success at this locus would therefore suggest that the method is not restricted to unusually favorable genes.

Our strategy was to insert a modified neo gene into exon 8 of a cloned fragment of Hprt and to introduce these recombinant molecules into ES cells. Homologous recombination between the exogenous Hprt sequences and the endogenous locus should transfer the neo insertion into the chromosome, inactivating Hprt and marking the altered allele. Because expression of the neo gene is required to recover targeted cells, we redesigned the neo cassette to function efficiently in ES cells while keeping the amount of nonhomologous DNA small. The cassette contains the Tn5 neo coding sequence, regulatory elements derived from the herpes simplex virus thymidine kinase promoter and a polyoma enhancer, and an improved translation initiation signal.

We also wished to determine how vector design affects the recovery of targeted mutations. Guided by recombination pathways characterized in yeast, we constructed two classes of neo-Hprt recombinant vectors. In one class, the introduced DNA is expected to replace the corresponding endogenous sequence. In the other, the introduced DNA is expected to insert into the locus, creating a duplication of Hprt sequences. These two products would have different genetic uses. Replacement vectors provide a simple means of disrupting a gene. Insertion vectors could permit the introduction of more subtle mutations, such as point mutations or small deletions, linked to a selectable marker elsewhere in the same cloned segment.

In this paper we describe the construction and use of these vectors to mutate the endogenous Hprt gene in mouse ES cells. We compare replacement and insertion vectors, examine the dependence of targeting frequency on the extent of homology, and characterize the resulting mutant cell lines by Southern transfer analysis and enzymatic assay. The results show that homologous recombination can be used to introduce a defined mutation into a specific mouse chromosomal locus in ES cells, and suggest a general route for producing mice carrying predetermined genetic alterations.
