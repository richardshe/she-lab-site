# Introduction
The acquired immune deficiency syndrome (AIDS) has recently emerged as a devastating and rapidly spreading epidemiological crisis. Initially recognized in homosexual men and subsequently identified in intravenous drug abusers, hemophiliacs, and Haitian immigrants, AIDS is characterized by a profound, irreversible depletion of the cellular immune system. This severe immunodeficiency leaves patients highly susceptible to opportunistic infections, such as Pneumocystis carinii pneumonia, and unusual malignancies, notably Kaposi's sarcoma. The epidemiological pattern of AIDS, including its transmission via blood products and intimate sexual contact, strongly suggests that the etiologic agent is a transmissible, infectious microorganism.

The clinical manifestations of AIDS, particularly the selective depletion of T-helper lymphocytes, point toward an infectious agent with a specific tropism for human T cells. Retroviruses are prime candidates for such an etiology. In animal models, various retroviruses are known to cause severe immunosuppression and lymphoid depletion, such as the feline leukemia virus in cats. In humans, the only previously identified horizontally transmitted retroviruses belong to the human T-cell leukemia virus (HTLV) family. HTLV isolates, primarily HTLV-I, are associated with aggressive adult T-cell malignancies and display a striking tropism for T-helper lymphocytes (OKT4+), which is the exact same cell population selectively depleted in AIDS. Although HTLV-I is typically oncogenic, producing leukemias and lymphomas by immortalizing infected T cells, we hypothesized that a related, yet distinct, T-lymphotropic retrovirus might be responsible for the cytopathic effects and profound immunodeficiency observed in AIDS patients.

Investigating this hypothesis, we focused on patients exhibiting the early signs of the syndrome, often referred to as the AIDS prodrome or lymphadenopathy syndrome, characterized by persistent, generalized lymph node enlargement and asthenia. Such patients might represent a more active phase of viral replication, before the terminal depletion of the target T cells occurs and opportunist infections mask the underlying cause. In this report, we describe the successful isolation and characterization of a novel, human T-lymphotropic retrovirus from cultured lymph node cells of a homosexual patient presenting with multiple lymphadenopathies. We demonstrate that this virus, while sharing certain morphological and biochemical characteristics with the known HTLV family, is antigenically and biologically distinct, representing a new class of human retrovirus that may play a central role in the pathogenesis of AIDS.
