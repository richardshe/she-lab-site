# Introduction
The early mammalian embryo provides a unique system for studying the progressive restriction of cellular potential during development. In the mouse, the cells of the inner cell mass of the blastocyst are pluripotential, capable of contributing to all somatic tissues and the germ line of an adult animal, as demonstrated by the formation of chimaeric mice following their injection into host blastocysts. Additionally, when transplanted to extra-uterine sites, early embryos can form teratocarcinomas, which are complex tumours comprising an array of differentiated tissues alongside a population of undifferentiated, pluripotential stem cells. These stem cells, known as embryonal carcinoma (EC) cells, can be established as permanent lines in tissue culture and serve as a widely used model for early embryonic development. EC cells share many biochemical and immunological properties with the undifferentiated cells of the early embryo and, remarkably, can also participate in normal embryogenesis when reintroduced into a developing blastocyst.

Despite the functional equivalence between EC cells and the normal embryonic cells from which they are derived, the transition from a normal embryo to a teratocarcinoma is accompanied by poorly understood changes. EC cell lines often exhibit karyotypic abnormalities and may lose their ability to differentiate fully or colonize the germ line. The isolation of pluripotential cells directly from the early embryo, bypassing the intermediate stage of in vivo tumour formation, would circumvent these issues and provide a more direct model of normal embryogenesis. Previous attempts to culture mouse embryos in vitro have generally resulted in the differentiation of the inner cell mass into a limited range of cell types, primarily extra-embryonic endoderm, with the eventual loss of pluripotential cells. We now report that under appropriate culture conditions, it is possible to isolate and maintain continuously proliferating lines of pluripotential cells directly from in vitro cultures of mouse blastocysts. These embryo-derived cell lines, which we term EK cells, retain a normal diploid karyotype and exhibit the full developmental potential characteristic of EC cells, including the ability to differentiate in vitro and form teratocarcinomas in vivo.
