The central nervous system (CNS) is an immune-privileged site, classically characterized by the presence of the blood-brain barrier and the apparent absence of a conventional lymphatic drainage system. In peripheral tissues, the lymphatic vasculature plays a critical role in immune surveillance and homeostasis by draining interstitial fluid and immune cells to regional lymph nodes, where immune responses are initiated. The long-standing dogma that the CNS lacks a similar lymphatic network has posed a persistent anatomical puzzle: how do immune cells and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF)-derived antigens exit the CNS to reach the cervical lymph nodes to facilitate immune surveillance?

Although it is increasingly recognized that the CNS is not entirely isolated from the immune system and undergoes constant immune surveillance within the meningeal compartments, the precise anatomical routes governing the trafficking of immune cells and the clearance of macromolecules from the CNS remain obscure. Previous studies suggested that CSF drains along olfactory nerve routes through the cribriform plate into nasal mucosa lymphatics, but this pathway does not fully account for the robust movement of immune cells to the deep cervical lymph nodes.

In our pursuit to identify the primary gateways for T-cell trafficking into and out of the meninges, we systematically examined the meningeal compartments of the mouse brain. Here, we report the discovery of a functional lymphatic vascular network lining the dural sinuses. These previously unrecognized meningeal lymphatic vessels express classical lymphatic endothelial cell markers, carry fluid and immune cells from the CSF, and connect directly to the deep cervical lymph nodes.
