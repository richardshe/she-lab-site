Cancer arises through the somatic evolution of normal cells. Mutations acquired during life generate genetic diversity within tissues, and those that confer a proliferative or survival advantage can expand clonally. The established cancers that are usually sequenced represent the end products of this process, after many rounds of mutation, selection, and tissue remodeling. Much less is known about the earliest phases of tumor evolution: how many mutations are present in histologically normal cells, whether cancer genes are already under selection before malignant transformation, and how large the resulting clones become while the tissue continues to function normally.

Normal human tissues are not genetically homogeneous. Clonal expansions have been described in blood, esophagus, liver, and skin, and age-associated mosaicism is increasingly recognized as a general feature of somatic biology. However, measuring the full burden and selective consequences of mutations in normal tissues remains technically difficult. Most mutant clones are small, and their variants are diluted among neighboring wild-type cells. Standard sequencing approaches either lack the sensitivity to detect such low-frequency mutations or require prior knowledge of specific lesions. As a result, the relationship between the mutational landscape of normal tissue and that of its corresponding cancers has remained uncertain.

Skin provides a particularly tractable system in which to study these questions. The epidermis is continually renewed, is directly exposed to ultraviolet light, and gives rise to common cancers whose genomes bear strong signatures of sunlight-induced DNA damage. Previous studies using immunohistochemistry and targeted assays identified patches of normal-appearing skin carrying mutations in genes such as TP53, suggesting that cancer-associated clones may be widespread long before tumors appear. Yet these approaches could not determine, in an unbiased manner, the density of driver mutations, the spectrum of genes under selection, or the extent to which normal clonal competition resembles that seen in cutaneous malignancy.

Here we used ultradeep sequencing of cancer-associated genes in small biopsies of physiologically normal, sun-exposed human eyelid epidermis to quantify somatic mutation and selection at high spatial resolution. By detecting low-frequency variants across hundreds of biopsies, we could estimate mutation burden per cell, identify genes with an excess of protein-altering mutations, infer the density of positively selected clones, and compare the driver landscape of normal skin with that of skin cancers. Our results show that aged sun-exposed epidermis is a dense field of evolving mutant clones, many carrying canonical cancer-driver mutations, and that the boundary between normal tissue and cancer is less genetic than it appears from the tumor alone.
