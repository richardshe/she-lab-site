# Introduction

Precise assembly of neural circuits requires the formation of exuberant connections followed by their selective elimination. This process, termed synaptic pruning, is particularly prominent during early postnatal development, when initially diffuse patterns of innervation are refined into mature circuits. In the visual system, retinal ganglion cell (RGC) axons project to the dorsal lateral geniculate nucleus (dLGN), where inputs from the two eyes initially overlap and subsequently segregate into eye-specific territories (Godement et al., 1984; Guido, 2008; Huberman et al., 2008; Sretavan and Shatz, 1986). This refinement is driven in large part by spontaneous retinal activity, including retinal waves, and depends on competition among neighboring axons for postsynaptic territory (Chen and Regehr, 2000; Feller, 1999; Hooks and Chen, 2006; Katz and Shatz, 1996; Penn et al., 1998; Shatz, 1990). Although the retinogeniculate system has long provided a powerful model for studying activity-dependent circuit refinement, the cellular mechanisms by which selected synaptic inputs are removed remain incompletely understood.

Microglia are the resident immune cells of the central nervous system and are best known for their roles in injury, infection, and neurodegeneration. However, in vivo imaging studies have revealed that microglia are highly motile in the healthy brain, continuously extending and retracting processes to survey the local environment (Davalos et al., 2005; Nimmerjahn et al., 2005). These dynamic processes contact synaptic elements, and microglia-synapse interactions are regulated by neuronal activity and sensory experience (Tremblay et al., 2010a; Wake et al., 2009). In addition, recent studies have implicated microglia in synaptic plasticity and developing hippocampal circuitry (Paolicelli et al., 2011; Pascual et al., 2012; Roumier et al., 2008). These observations raise the possibility that microglia may contribute directly to the remodeling of synaptic circuits during normal development. Yet the function of microglia at developing synapses, and the molecular signals that govern their interactions with synaptic elements, remain poorly defined.

One candidate pathway for mediating synapse elimination is the classical complement cascade. Complement proteins are well established as immune molecules that tag cellular material for phagocytic clearance in the periphery. In the developing central nervous system, complement component C1q and downstream component C3 localize to synaptic regions and are required for normal refinement of retinogeniculate projections (Stevens et al., 2007). Mice deficient in complement signaling exhibit persistent defects in eye-specific segregation, suggesting that complement proteins may mark synaptic inputs for elimination. However, the effector cell type that reads out these signals in the developing brain has not been established. Because microglia express complement receptors and possess phagocytic machinery, they are well positioned to mediate complement-dependent pruning of developing synapses.

Here we test the hypothesis that microglia actively remodel developing neural circuits by engulfing synaptic inputs in an activity- and complement-dependent manner. Using the postnatal mouse retinogeniculate system, we show that microglia engulf RGC presynaptic inputs during the peak period of synaptic pruning. We further demonstrate that this engulfment is developmentally regulated and biased toward inputs with relatively reduced activity. Finally, we identify complement receptor 3 (CR3) and its ligand C3 as a microglia-specific phagocytic signaling pathway required for normal engulfment and lasting refinement of retinogeniculate connectivity. Together, these findings define a cellular mechanism for complement-dependent synaptic pruning and reveal a physiological role for microglia in sculpting developing neural circuits.
