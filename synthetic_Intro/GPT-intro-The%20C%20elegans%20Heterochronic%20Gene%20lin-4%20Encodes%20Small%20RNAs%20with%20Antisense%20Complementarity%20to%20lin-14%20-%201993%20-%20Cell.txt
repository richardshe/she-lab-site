# Introduction [552 words]

The orderly progression of development requires that particular cell fates be expressed at the proper time as well as in the proper place. In C. elegans, the timing of many postembryonic cell lineage events is controlled by heterochronic genes. Mutations in these genes cause stage-specific patterns of cell division and differentiation to be reiterated or skipped, so that cells execute developmental programs inappropriate for their chronological stage. These phenotypes indicate that the products of heterochronic genes constitute a regulatory system that specifies the temporal identity of cells during larval development.

Among the best characterized of these genes is lin-14. High lin-14 activity is required during the first larval stage (L1) for the expression of L1-specific fates, whereas reduction of lin-14 activity permits the subsequent expression of later larval fates. The level of LIN-14 protein is normally high in the early L1 and then decreases during the L1-to-L2 transition. This decrease is developmentally important, since gain-of-function lin-14 mutations that prevent the normal reduction of LIN-14 protein cause reiteration of early larval fates, whereas loss-of-function lin-14 mutations cause precocious expression of later fates. Previous genetic and molecular analysis has shown that the temporal regulation of lin-14 depends on sequences in the 3' untranslated region (UTR) of the lin-14 mRNA.

The heterochronic gene lin-4 acts as a negative regulator of lin-14. lin-4 loss-of-function mutations cause retarded development similar to that caused by lin-14 gain-of-function mutations, and lin-14 loss-of-function mutations are epistatic to lin-4. These genetic relationships indicate that a principal function of lin-4 is to decrease lin-14 activity during the L1 stage. Consistent with this model, LIN-14 protein fails to undergo its normal temporal decrease in lin-4 mutant animals, although lin-14 mRNA levels are not substantially altered. Thus lin-4 appears to regulate lin-14 posttranscriptionally, most likely at the level of translation or protein accumulation.

The molecular nature of lin-4 has remained unknown. Since lin-4 acts in trans to regulate lin-14, it has been expected to encode a diffusible gene product. One possibility is that lin-4 encodes a conventional protein that recognizes the lin-14 3' UTR directly or indirectly. Another possibility, suggested by the unusual genetic specificity of lin-4 action and by the requirement for lin-14 3' UTR sequences, is that lin-4 could encode an RNA product that interacts with lin-14 mRNA. Natural antisense RNAs are known to regulate gene expression in several systems, although previously characterized examples that affect translation generally act through the 5' region of the target mRNA.

We have cloned lin-4 by chromosomal walking and transformation rescue and have defined a 693 bp genomic fragment sufficient for lin-4 activity. Comparison of functionally rescuing lin-4 genomic sequences from four Caenorhabditis species, together with mutational analysis of candidate open reading frames, indicates that lin-4 is unlikely to encode a protein. Instead, the lin-4 locus produces two small RNAs of approximately 61 and 22 nucleotides. These RNAs include sequences complementary to repeated elements in the lin-14 3' UTR, including sequences deleted in lin-14 gain-of-function alleles. These findings suggest that lin-4 regulates developmental timing by an antisense RNA-RNA interaction with lin-14 mRNA and raise the possibility that small regulatory RNAs may have specific roles in developmental gene control.
