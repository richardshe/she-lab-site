# Introduction
The diagnosis of genetic disorders at the DNA level has been significantly advanced by the use of restriction fragment length polymorphisms (RFLPs) and cloned gene probes. For sickle cell anemia, a severe autosomal recessive disease caused by a single nucleotide substitution (A to T) in the sixth codon of the beta-globin gene, prenatal diagnosis has traditionally relied on the analysis of fetal DNA obtained through amniocentesis. The mutation underlying the sickle cell allele (beta^S) serendipitously abolishes a recognition site for the restriction endonucleases Dde I and Mst II, which is present in the normal (beta^A) allele. This sequence variation allows for the direct detection of the beta^S mutation by restriction enzyme digestion and Southern blotting, bypassing the need for family linkage studies. Despite the elegance of this approach, the standard Southern blotting technique is labor-intensive, time-consuming, and requires relatively large quantities of high-molecular-weight genomic DNA, which can be difficult to obtain in certain clinical situations.

Recent advances in the chemical synthesis of oligonucleotides have provided a new class of highly specific hybridization probes. Under appropriately stringent conditions, short synthetic oligonucleotides can discriminate between sequences that differ by as little as a single base pair. We have previously demonstrated that such probes can be used to detect the sickle cell mutation directly in genomic DNA without relying on a fortuitous restriction site alteration. However, the sensitivity of oligonucleotide hybridization is limited by the complexity of the human genome and the small fraction of total DNA represented by a single-copy gene.

To overcome these limitations and increase the speed and sensitivity of genetic analysis, we have developed a novel method for the targeted amplification of specific genomic DNA sequences in vitro. This technique, termed the polymerase chain reaction (PCR), uses two flanking oligonucleotide primers and repeated cycles of DNA polymerase-mediated extension to achieve a massive, exponential increase in the number of copies of the target sequence. Here, we demonstrate the application of PCR to amplify a segment of the beta-globin gene, combined with a rapid solution hybridization and restriction digestion assay, to provide a highly sensitive and efficient method for the prenatal diagnosis of sickle cell anemia.
