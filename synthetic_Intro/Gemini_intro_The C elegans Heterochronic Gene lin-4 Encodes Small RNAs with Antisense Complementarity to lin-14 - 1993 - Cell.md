The temporal regulation of gene expression is a fundamental aspect of animal development, ensuring that specific cellular events occur in the correct sequence and at the appropriate time. In the nematode Caenorhabditis elegans, the timing of diverse postembryonic developmental milestones—such as cell divisions, migrations, and terminal differentiation—is controlled by a network of heterochronic genes. Mutations in these genes cause temporal transformations in cell fates, leading to the skipping of stage-specific events (a precocious phenotype) or the reiteration of earlier developmental programs at later stages (a retarded phenotype). The interaction between two of these heterochronic genes, lin-14 and lin-4, provides a paradigm for understanding how temporal developmental switches are executed.

The lin-14 gene plays a central role in specifying the timing of early larval events. A high level of LIN-14 protein is present in the nuclei of diverse cell types during the first larval stage (L1), programming cells to execute L1-specific developmental fates. During the transition from the L1 to the L2 stage, the level of LIN-14 protein decreases precipitously, a down-regulation that is essential for the progression to L2-specific cell fates. Gain-of-function (gf) mutations in lin-14 prevent this normal temporal decline, resulting in the persistent expression of LIN-14 protein and the inappropriate reiteration of L1 cell lineages in subsequent larval stages. Conversely, loss-of-function (lf) mutations in lin-14 cause the premature execution of late-stage cell lineages.

The down-regulation of LIN-14 protein is primarily controlled by the lin-4 gene. Loss-of-function mutations in lin-4 lead to a retarded developmental phenotype that closely resembles that of lin-14(gf) mutants. Genetic epistasis experiments demonstrate that lin-4 acts as a negative regulator of lin-14: in lin-4 mutant animals, the level of LIN-14 protein fails to decline at the end of the L1 stage, remaining inappropriately high throughout postembryonic development. The finding that lin-14 is epistatic to lin-4 indicates that the primary, if not sole, function of lin-4 is to repress lin-14 activity. Interestingly, this regulation is post-transcriptional. In lin-4 mutants, the steady-state levels of lin-14 mRNA are not significantly altered, yet the level of LIN-14 protein remains high, suggesting that lin-4 negatively regulates the translation of lin-14 mRNA.

The critical target for this lin-4-mediated repression lies within the 3' untranslated region (UTR) of the lin-14 transcript. All known lin-14(gf) mutations involve deletions or rearrangements that disrupt the lin-14 3' UTR, rendering the lin-14 mRNA immune to lin-4-dependent down-regulation. These data strongly suggest that the lin-4 gene product acts in trans to repress LIN-14 protein synthesis through an interaction that depends on the integrity of the lin-14 3' UTR.

To elucidate the molecular mechanism by which lin-4 regulates lin-14, we undertook the molecular cloning and characterization of the C. elegans lin-4 locus. Here, we report the identification of the lin-4 gene by chromosomal walking, physical mapping of a lin-4 null mutation, and transformation rescue. Remarkably, sequence analysis, mutagenesis, and evolutionary comparisons among four Caenorhabditis species indicate that the lin-4 gene does not encode a protein. Instead, it encodes two small transcripts, approximately 22 and 61 nucleotides in length. We show that these small RNAs contain sequences that are complementary to a repeated element within the lin-14 3' UTR. These findings provide compelling evidence for a novel mechanism of translational regulation, wherein a small non-coding RNA controls the temporal progression of development through an antisense RNA-RNA interaction.
