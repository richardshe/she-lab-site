# Introduction

Viruses that infect bacteria are under intense selective pressure to circumvent host defences, and bacteriophages encode inhibitors of restriction-modification systems, abortive infection pathways and other anti-phage mechanisms. Clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) and CRISPR-associated (cas) genes comprise a highly prevalent adaptive immune system that protects bacteria and archaea from phage and plasmid DNA. In these systems, short sequences acquired from foreign genetic elements are incorporated into CRISPR loci and transcribed into small CRISPR RNAs that guide Cas proteins to complementary invader sequences. Although phages can escape CRISPR/Cas targeting through mutation of the protospacer or protospacer adjacent motif, this route is intrinsically target-specific and should be vulnerable to acquisition of additional spacers. The apparent absence of phage-encoded inhibitors of CRISPR/Cas systems has therefore been notable. Here we use a naturally active type I-F CRISPR/Cas system in Pseudomonas aeruginosa to identify bacteriophage genes that inhibit CRISPR/Cas immunity. We show that diverse genes from related Mu-like phages act as anti-CRISPR proteins, function against multiple CRISPR targets, and are required for targeted phages to replicate in CRISPR/Cas-positive bacteria.
