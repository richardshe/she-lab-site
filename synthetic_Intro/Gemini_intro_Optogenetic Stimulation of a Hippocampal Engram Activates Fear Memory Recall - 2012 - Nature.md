# Introduction
A fundamental tenet of neuroscience is that specific memories are represented in the brain by the physical and chemical modifications of a sparse network of neurons. This specific neural ensemble, activated during learning and reactivated during retrieval, is known as a memory engram. Recent advances in immediate early gene-based tagging systems have allowed researchers to identify and capture the specific populations of neurons that are active during the encoding of a fear memory. Studies utilizing these techniques have demonstrated that the targeted ablation or pharmacological inhibition of these specific engram-bearing cells results in a significant reduction in memory expression, suggesting that these neural ensembles are necessary for mnemonic processes. 

However, a critical question remains unanswered: is the activation of this specific neural ensemble sufficient to drive the behavioural expression of a memory? Can we artificially induce the recall of a specific memory by directly stimulating the very neurons that were active during its formation? To address this, we utilized a transgenic approach to selectively express the light-activated cation channel channelrhodopsin-2 (ChR2) in a specific population of hippocampal dentate gyrus (DG) neurons that were activated during contextual fear conditioning. By subsequently illuminating these tagged neurons with blue light in a novel context, we were able to directly test whether optogenetic reactivation of a defined hippocampal memory engram is sufficient to elicit the behavioral output of that memory, namely, freezing.
