# Introduction [183 words]

In multicellular organisms, somatic-cell identities are generally maintained by stable epigenetic mechanisms that restrict cells to lineage-specific programmes. Experimental reversal of this restriction has classically required nuclear transfer into oocytes or the enforced expression of defined transcription factors, as in induced pluripotent stem cells. These approaches show that differentiated nuclei retain developmental potential, but they also imply that reprogramming requires specialized cellular environments or artificial genetic manipulation. Whether committed mammalian somatic cells possess a latent capacity for reprogramming that can be elicited by simple external stimuli remains unclear.

Cells in living tissues are exposed to diverse environmental perturbations, including changes in pH, osmotic pressure, mechanical stress and damage. In classical embryology, transient sublethal stress has been reported to alter developmental states, suggesting that strong environmental cues can release cells from otherwise stable fate constraints. Here we asked whether mammalian somatic cells, particularly lineage-committed haematopoietic cells, can acquire pluripotency after transient exposure to such stimuli. We focused on low-pH treatment and examined whether this perturbation induces activation of pluripotency markers, epigenetic remodeling and functional developmental potency.
