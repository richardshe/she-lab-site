# Introduction
Cancer is a disease driven by the accumulation of somatic mutations and genomic rearrangements. The prevailing model of cancer evolution posits that these genetic alterations are acquired sequentially over many cell cycles. According to this progressive rearrangement model, periods of genomic instability gradually sculpt the genomic landscape of a tumor, leading to increasingly complex karyotypic abnormalities and the successive disruption of tumor suppressor genes or activation of oncogenes. While this step-wise accumulation of genomic lesions is a hallmark of many human malignancies, advances in high-throughput DNA sequencing now provide the unprecedented resolution required to comprehensively characterize the architecture of cancer genomes and interrogate the temporal dynamics of their evolution. In particular, massively parallel paired-end sequencing has emerged as a powerful tool for discovering structural variations across the entire genome by mapping the exact locations and orientations of chromosomal breakpoints.

In the course of cataloging genomic rearrangements in chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) and other cancers, we have observed genomic profiles that challenge the conventional view of progressive, sequential mutational accumulation. Instead of a gradual acquisition of chromosomal structural changes, these profiles suggest a markedly different evolutionary trajectory in which massive genomic remodeling occurs during a single, catastrophic cellular event. We introduce the term "chromothripsis" to describe this phenomenon, wherein one or several chromosomes in a cell undergo massive shattering into tens or hundreds of fragments. These fragments are subsequently stitched together by the cell's DNA repair machinery in a haphazard fashion, producing a complex, localized medley of rearrangements. This single cataclysmic event results in a signature pattern of copy number oscillations alternating rapidly between two states, alongside a dense clustering of structural variants that cannot be easily explained by progressive independent breaks over time.

In this study, we leverage next-generation sequencing and high-resolution single nucleotide polymorphism (SNP) arrays to systematically characterize the genomic hallmarks of chromothripsis across various cancer types. We demonstrate that this phenomenon is not an isolated anomaly but is detectable in at least 2% to 3% of all human cancers, with a strikingly high prevalence in bone tumors such as osteosarcoma and chordoma. Furthermore, our findings indicate that cells surviving this genomic crisis can simultaneously acquire multiple cancer-driving lesions, such as the amplification of oncogenes or the concurrent disruption of several tumor suppressor genes. This catastrophic model of genomic rearrangement has profound implications for our understanding of the etiology of chromosomal instability and the rapid temporal emergence of cancer.
