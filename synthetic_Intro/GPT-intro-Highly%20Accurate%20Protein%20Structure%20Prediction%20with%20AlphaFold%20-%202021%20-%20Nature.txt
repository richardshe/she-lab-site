# Introduction [339 words]

Proteins carry out most of the molecular functions of the cell, and these functions are determined in large part by three-dimensional structure. Experimental methods such as X-ray crystallography, nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy and cryogenic electron microscopy have provided a detailed structural view of biology, but they remain difficult to apply at the scale of modern sequence databases. The number of known protein sequences now exceeds the number of experimentally determined structures by orders of magnitude, leaving most protein families without direct structural characterization.

The computational prediction of protein structure from amino-acid sequence has therefore long been a central goal of structural biology. Progress has been driven by two complementary sources of information. First, the accumulation of homologous sequences has made it possible to infer spatial contacts from evolutionary covariation. Second, physical and geometric constraints restrict the conformations that a folded protein can adopt. Existing methods have used these signals to produce increasingly useful models, particularly when related structures are available as templates. However, many predictions remain insufficiently accurate for detailed biological interpretation, especially for proteins without close homologues of known structure, and side-chain placement and domain organization have continued to present major challenges.

Here we describe AlphaFold, a substantially redesigned neural network system for protein structure prediction. AlphaFold uses the primary sequence, multiple sequence alignments and, where available, template information to reason directly over residue relationships and three-dimensional geometry. The model introduces an architecture that exchanges information between evolutionary and pairwise representations, followed by a structure module that iteratively constructs atomic coordinates while preserving the symmetries of three-dimensional space. Rather than treating structure prediction as a sequence of separately optimized steps, the network is trained end to end to produce full-atom models together with calibrated estimates of confidence.

We evaluate AlphaFold in the 14th Critical Assessment of protein Structure Prediction and on recently released Protein Data Bank structures not available during training. These results show that a learned model, when constrained by the appropriate biological and geometric inductive biases, can routinely approach experimental accuracy for single-chain protein structure prediction.
