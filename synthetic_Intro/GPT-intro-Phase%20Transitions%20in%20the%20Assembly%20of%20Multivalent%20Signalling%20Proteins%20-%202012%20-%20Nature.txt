# Introduction [226 words]

Many cellular processes require information encoded in weak molecular interactions to be converted into structures and activities on far larger length scales. This problem is particularly acute in signalling, where modular domains and short linear motifs often occur in multiple copies and bind one another with modest affinities. Such architectures are well suited to combinatorial regulation, but they also create the possibility that individual binding events can be coupled to collective physical transitions. How these transitions might organize intracellular space and alter biochemical activity remains poorly understood.

Polymer chemistry predicts that multivalent interactions can drive sharp changes in molecular organization once the number and occupancy of binding sites exceed a critical threshold. In biological systems, related principles have been invoked to explain the assembly of non-membrane-bound cellular bodies, puncta and signalling clusters, but the molecular basis and functional consequences of such behaviour have been difficult to establish. In particular, it is unclear whether phase separation can arise from ordinary folded protein interaction domains, whether the transition is governed by valency in a predictable manner, and whether entry into a condensed phase can directly regulate enzymatic or cytoskeletal activity.

Here we use synthetic multivalent proteins, RNA-binding systems and the physiological nephrin-NCK-N-WASP actin-regulatory pathway to show that multivalent interactions can generate liquid-like phase-separated droplets that contain dynamic supramolecular polymers. We further show that phosphorylation-controlled changes in valency tune the phase boundary and convert molecular assembly into switch-like activation of Arp2/3-mediated actin polymerization.
