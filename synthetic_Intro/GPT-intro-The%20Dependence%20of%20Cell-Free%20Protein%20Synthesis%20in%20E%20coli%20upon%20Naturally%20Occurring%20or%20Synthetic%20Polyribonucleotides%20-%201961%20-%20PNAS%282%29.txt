# Introduction

Previous studies have shown that cell-free preparations obtained from bacteria are capable of incorporating amino acids into protein under appropriate conditions. The nature of the component which determines the specificity and extent of this incorporation, however, has remained uncertain. Since DNA is removed or rendered ineffective in many such extracts, attention has been directed toward ribonucleic acid fractions which may carry genetic information from DNA to the protein-synthesizing system. It was therefore of interest to determine whether purified RNA preparations could restore amino acid incorporation in an E. coli extract depleted of endogenous activity, and whether the effect was due to soluble RNA, ribosomal RNA, or another RNA species. In addition, synthetic polyribonucleotides provide a means of testing whether a defined nucleotide sequence can direct the incorporation of a particular amino acid. The experiments described below examine these questions in a stable cell-free system.
