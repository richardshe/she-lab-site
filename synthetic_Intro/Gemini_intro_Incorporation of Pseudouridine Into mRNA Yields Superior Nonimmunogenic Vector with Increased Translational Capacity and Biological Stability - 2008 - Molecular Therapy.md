# Introduction
Gene therapy holds immense promise for treating a wide array of genetic and acquired diseases by introducing functional genetic material into patient cells. Historically, efforts have predominantly relied on viral vectors or plasmid DNA to deliver therapeutic genes. However, these traditional DNA-based approaches are fraught with significant challenges, including the risk of insertional mutagenesis, potential oncogene activation, and robust immune responses against viral capsids. Furthermore, for DNA vectors to be effective, they must breach the nuclear envelope and be transcribed, a process that is often inefficient, particularly in non-dividing primary cells.

In vitro-transcribed messenger RNA (mRNA) presents an attractive alternative to DNA-based gene delivery. Because mRNA exerts its function in the cytoplasm, it circumvents the need for nuclear entry, leading to rapid and efficient protein expression even in quiescent cells. Additionally, mRNA does not integrate into the host genome, thereby eliminating the risk of insertional mutagenesis. Despite these theoretical advantages, the clinical translation of mRNA therapeutics has been severely hampered by two major practical limitations: its inherent instability and its potent immunogenicity. Exogenous unmodified RNA is rapidly degraded by ubiquitous ribonucleases and is recognized by the innate immune system as a pathogen-associated molecular pattern, triggering the secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines such as interferon-alpha (IFN-α) and tumor necrosis factor-alpha (TNF-α). This innate immune activation not only causes toxicity but also profoundly suppresses the translation of the delivered mRNA.

In nature, cellular RNAs are extensively decorated with post-transcriptional nucleoside modifications, such as pseudouridine (Ψ), which are thought to distinguish self from non-self RNA and prevent auto-immune reactions. Building upon our previous findings that mammalian RNA and nucleoside-modified in vitro transcripts evade detection by Toll-like receptors, we sought to systematically investigate the impact of these natural modifications on the biological properties of therapeutic mRNA. Here, we demonstrate that the incorporation of pseudouridine into in vitro-transcribed mRNA dramatically enhances its translational capacity and biological stability while simultaneously abolishing its immunogenicity in vivo, establishing Ψ-modified mRNA as a vastly superior vector for gene replacement and therapeutic applications.
