# Introduction
The structural and functional organization of genetic material at the molecular level represents one of the central problems in modern biology. While classical genetic analysis successfully established that genes are arranged in a linear sequence along the chromosome, the limit of resolution achieved in higher organisms was insufficient to probe the internal structure of the gene itself. To determine whether the gene is indivisible or composed of a sequence of smaller, mutable elements, one requires a genetic system capable of resolving rare recombination events between extremely closely linked mutations. The rII region of the bacteriophage T4 provides an ideal system for such ultra-fine-scale mapping. Mutations within the rII region prevent the phage from producing progeny in Escherichia coli strain K12(lambda), although they grow normally on E. coli strain B. Because even a single recombinant wild-type particle can be easily detected among millions of mutant progeny by plating on strain K, the resolution of this system is high enough to theoretically separate mutations located at adjacent nucleotide pairs in the DNA molecule.

In a previous study, we demonstrated that the genetic map of the rII region is completely linear and that the functional unit, or cistron, can be genetically dissected into numerous distinct, mutable sites. We introduced a rapid method for topological mapping using a set of well-characterized, overlapping deletion mutations. This method allows any newly isolated point mutation to be unambiguously assigned to a specific segment of the map simply by conducting qualitative spot tests to observe the presence or absence of recombination with the reference deletions, thus circumventing the need for exhaustive, quantitative recombination frequency measurements. 

In the present investigation, we have expanded this set of overlapping deletions to divide the rII region into 47 distinct segments. We utilize this high-resolution map to determine the exact topographical distribution of hundreds of spontaneous and induced mutations. Our analysis identifies 308 distinct mutable sites within the two cistrons of the rII region. We find that the distribution of mutations across these sites is highly non-random, with certain specific locations ("hotspots") mutating at frequencies orders of magnitude higher than others. Furthermore, by comparing the spectra of spontaneous mutations to those induced by various chemical mutagens, both in vivo and in vitro, we reveal that each mutagen produces a unique and characteristic topographical fingerprint. These data provide direct evidence that the mutable sites within a gene are chemically heterogeneous and suggest that the sequence of nucleotide base pairs profoundly influences local mutability, offering a critical step toward ultimately translating the genetic map into a specific chemical sequence.
