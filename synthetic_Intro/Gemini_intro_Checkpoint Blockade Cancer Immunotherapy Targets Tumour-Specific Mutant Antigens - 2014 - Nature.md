The immune system plays a dual and seemingly paradoxical role in cancer development, acting both to suppress tumor outgrowth and to sculpt tumor immunogenicity. While the immune system can successfully eliminate many nascent neoplastic cells, clinically apparent cancers ultimately emerge in immunocompetent individuals. This escape is largely facilitated by the ability of developing tumors to subvert normal immune regulation, creating a profoundly immunosuppressive microenvironment. A central mechanism of this tumor-induced immunosuppression involves the co-option of immune checkpoint pathways, which are critical for maintaining self-tolerance and preventing autoimmunity under physiological conditions. 

Among the best-characterized immune checkpoints are cytotoxic T-lymphocyte associated antigen-4 (CTLA-4) and programmed death-1 (PD-1), both of which act as key inhibitory receptors on T cells. Monoclonal antibodies designed to block these inhibitory receptors—a therapeutic approach known as checkpoint blockade immunotherapy—have revolutionized clinical oncology. By releasing the brakes on the immune system, antibodies targeting CTLA-4 and/or PD-1 have generated durable, life-prolonging clinical responses in patients across a diverse array of advanced malignancies. 

However, despite these remarkable clinical successes, a fundamental question remains largely unanswered: what are the specific targets of the T cells that mediate tumor rejection following checkpoint blockade? While some studies have implicated shared, overexpressed tumor-associated antigens (TAAs), it has long been hypothesized that tumor-specific mutant antigens (neoantigens), generated by somatic mutations during tumorigenesis, might serve as the primary targets of the reactivated immune response. In this study, we utilize advanced genomics, bioinformatics, and mass spectrometry to systematically identify the exact mutated antigens targeted by T cells following checkpoint blockade in a murine sarcoma model. We demonstrate that tumor-specific mutant antigens are indeed major targets of both anti-PD-1 and anti-CTLA-4 therapies and show that these antigens can be successfully formulated into highly effective, personalized therapeutic vaccines.
