# Introduction
Mitochondria are dynamic organelles that serve not only as the primary site of cellular adenosine triphosphate (ATP) production but also as crucial hubs for metabolic integration and intracellular signaling. A fundamental aspect of mitochondrial biology is their capacity to rapidly accumulate massive amounts of calcium ions (Ca2+) from the cytosol. This process of mitochondrial Ca2+ uptake is essential for shaping cytosolic Ca2+ transients, buffering local Ca2+ microdomains, and regulating a wide array of cellular functions ranging from muscle contraction and neurotransmitter release to apoptosis. Crucially, elevations in matrix Ca2+ allosterically activate key rate-limiting dehydrogenases of the tricarboxylic acid (TCA) cycle, thereby matching mitochondrial energy production to increased cellular demand.

The primary conduit for this rapid Ca2+ influx across the highly impermeable inner mitochondrial membrane is a highly selective ion channel known as the mitochondrial calcium uniporter. First characterized functionally over fifty years ago, the uniporter is driven by the steep electrochemical gradient generated by the respiratory chain. Its activity is dependent on the membrane potential and can be specifically blocked by hexavalent ruthenium compounds such as ruthenium red and Ru360. Despite decades of intense biochemical and biophysical characterization—revealing complex properties such as high Ca2+ selectivity, sigmoidal activation kinetics, and regulation by cytosolic Ca2+ levels—the molecular identity of the uniporter channel has remained one of the most significant unsolved mysteries in cellular physiology.

The recent discovery of MICU1, a calcium-binding EF-hand protein essential for regulating uniporter activity, provided the first molecular handle on this critical transport system. However, MICU1 does not possess the structural features of an ion channel, indicating it likely acts as a regulatory subunit rather than the pore-forming component itself. To uncover the elusive pore of the uniporter, we employed a multifaceted integrative genomics strategy, combining genome-wide phylogenetic profiling, RNA co-expression, and organelle-specific protein co-expression analyses to identify genes functionally linked to MICU1. Here, we report the identification and characterization of a novel transmembrane protein, CCDC109A (which we name the mitochondrial calcium uniporter, or MCU), and demonstrate that it is an essential, pore-forming component of the mitochondrial calcium uniporter complex.
