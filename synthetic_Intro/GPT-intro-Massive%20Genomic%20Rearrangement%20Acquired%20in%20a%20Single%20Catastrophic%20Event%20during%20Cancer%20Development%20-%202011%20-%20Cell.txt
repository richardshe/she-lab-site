# Introduction

The evolution of cancer is driven by the somatic acquisition of genetic alterations that confer a selective advantage upon individual clones. These alterations include single nucleotide substitutions, small insertions and deletions, copy number changes, and structural rearrangements of chromosomes. In most conceptual models of tumorigenesis, such changes accumulate progressively over many cell generations, with successive rounds of mutation, selection and clonal expansion leading to increasing genomic complexity. This model is strongly supported by the stepwise acquisition of driver mutations in many tumor types and by the widespread observation of ongoing subclonal diversification within cancers.

Genomic rearrangements are among the most consequential of these alterations. Balanced translocations can create oncogenic fusion genes or place proto-oncogenes under the control of ectopic regulatory elements. Deletions can remove tumor suppressor genes, while amplifications can generate tens or hundreds of copies of oncogenes. In many cancers, rearrangements are scattered across the genome, reflecting generalized chromosomal instability, whereas in others they form complex local architectures associated with focal amplification, breakage-fusion-bridge cycles, or repeated cycles of DNA breakage and repair. Although these processes can generate highly abnormal chromosomes, they are generally assumed to arise through a succession of individually acquired events.

The advent of massively parallel sequencing now allows genomic rearrangements to be surveyed at high resolution across cancer genomes. Paired-end sequencing can identify rearranged genomic fragments by mapping read pairs whose ends align at unexpected positions, orientations, or distances from one another. When combined with genome-wide copy number and allelic ratio measurements, these data provide a direct view of the architecture produced by somatic DNA breakage and repair. They also make it possible to ask whether complex rearrangement patterns reflect gradual accumulation over time, or whether some cancers may acquire many rearrangements in a much more abrupt manner.

Here we describe a distinctive pattern of genomic remodeling in which tens to hundreds of rearrangements are confined to one or a few chromosomal regions. The rearrangements crisscross the affected regions in a mixture of orientations, while copy number oscillates rapidly between a small number of discrete states. These features are difficult to reconcile with progressive, independent acquisition of rearrangements, but are naturally explained by a single catastrophic event in which a chromosome or chromosomal region is shattered and then imperfectly reassembled. We term this phenomenon chromothripsis, from the Greek for chromosome shattering.

We identify the hallmarks of chromothripsis in chronic lymphocytic leukemia, in multiple cancer cell lines, and in primary bone tumors, where the phenomenon appears particularly frequent. We further show that such a genomic crisis can generate lesions likely to contribute directly to cancer development, including oncogene amplification and disruption or deletion of tumor suppressor genes. These findings suggest that, in a subset of cancers, major steps in genome evolution can occur not gradually, but in a single cellular catastrophe.
