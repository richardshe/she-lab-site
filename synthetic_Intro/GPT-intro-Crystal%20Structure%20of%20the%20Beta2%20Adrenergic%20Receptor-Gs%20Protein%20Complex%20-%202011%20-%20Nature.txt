# Introduction
G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) constitute the largest family of signalling receptors in the human genome and mediate cellular responses to hormones, neurotransmitters, sensory stimuli and chemokines. The central event in GPCR signalling is the conversion of extracellular ligand binding into intracellular activation of a heterotrimeric guanine nucleotide-binding protein. In the classical model, an agonist-bound receptor stabilizes a nucleotide-free state of the G protein, thereby promoting GDP release and allowing GTP binding to initiate downstream signalling. Despite extensive biochemical, pharmacological and biophysical studies, the structural basis by which an activated receptor catalyses nucleotide exchange has remained poorly understood.

The β2 adrenergic receptor (β2AR) and Gs, the stimulatory G protein for adenylyl cyclase, have provided one of the most intensively studied systems for understanding GPCR activation. Agonist binding to β2AR produces a high-affinity ternary complex with Gs that is destabilized by guanine nucleotides, a property that has long served as a functional signature of receptor activation. Previous crystal structures of GPCRs, including inactive-state structures of β2AR and other family A receptors, have revealed the architecture of the seven-transmembrane helical bundle and the ligand-binding pocket. More recently, active-state receptor conformations have been stabilized either by covalent modification, by retinal photoactivation in rhodopsin, or by nanobodies that mimic aspects of G protein binding. These structures have provided important insight into receptor activation, particularly movements of the cytoplasmic ends of transmembrane helices 5 and 6. However, they do not reveal how an intact heterotrimeric G protein engages an activated receptor or how this interaction is coupled to rearrangement of the nucleotide-binding pocket.

Structural studies of the GPCR-G protein complex have been limited by the dynamic and transient nature of this signalling intermediate. The receptor and G protein must be maintained in an agonist-bound, nucleotide-free state, while preserving a membrane protein complex with a large soluble component in detergent or lipidic phases suitable for crystallography. Additional challenges arise from the intrinsic conformational flexibility of the Gα subunit, particularly the relative orientation of its Ras-like GTPase and α-helical domains, which together form the guanine nucleotide-binding site.

Here we describe the crystallographic determination of the agonist-bound β2AR-Gs complex. The structure was enabled by stabilization of the complex with a high-affinity agonist, removal of nucleotide, detergent optimization, fusion of T4 lysozyme to the receptor amino terminus, and use of a camelid nanobody that binds the G protein heterotrimer. The resulting structure provides a high-resolution view of a GPCR in complex with its cognate heterotrimeric G protein and reveals the conformational changes in both receptor and G protein that underlie transmembrane signal transduction.
