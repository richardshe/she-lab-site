# Introduction
Proteins are the workhorses of biology, mediating almost every fundamental process in living organisms. The function of a protein is intimately linked to its three-dimensional (3D) structure, which is in turn determined by its linear sequence of amino acids. For over half a century, the "protein folding problem"—the challenge of computationally predicting a protein's 3D structure solely from its primary amino acid sequence—has stood as a grand challenge in structural biology and biophysics. Solving this problem would have profound implications, bridging the massive and ever-growing gap between the billions of known genomic sequences and the ~100,000 protein structures painstakingly determined by experimental methods such as X-ray crystallography, nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy, and cryogenic electron microscopy (cryo-EM).

While experimental structure determination is time-consuming, resource-intensive, and often technically challenging, computational prediction offers a potentially rapid and scalable alternative. Historically, computational methods have fallen broadly into two categories: template-based modeling, which relies on the presence of a known homologous structure, and free-modeling (or ab initio) approaches, which attempt to predict structure from physical principles or statistical potentials when no template is available. Despite steady, incremental progress over the decades, traditional methods have consistently struggled to achieve atomic-level accuracy, particularly in the absence of a close structural homolog.

Recent advances in deep learning, coupled with the availability of large sequence and structure databases, have catalyzed a revolution in the field. Initial neural network-based approaches focused on predicting distances or contacts between pairs of residues, using co-evolutionary signals derived from multiple sequence alignments (MSAs), which were then used to guide structure generation. Here, we present a novel and entirely redesigned deep learning system, AlphaFold, which directly predicts the 3D coordinates of all heavy atoms. By incorporating physical, geometric, and evolutionary constraints into a novel neural network architecture, AlphaFold achieves unprecedented, near-experimental accuracy in protein structure prediction.
