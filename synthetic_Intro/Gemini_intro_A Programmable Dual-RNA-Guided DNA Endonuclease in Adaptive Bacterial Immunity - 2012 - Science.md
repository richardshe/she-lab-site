Bacteria and archaea face a constant onslaught from bacteriophages and invasive plasmids. To defend against these pervasive genetic elements, prokaryotes have evolved an array of sophisticated defense mechanisms. Among these, the clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) and CRISPR-associated (Cas) proteins constitute a unique adaptive immune system. CRISPR-Cas systems capture short fragments of foreign DNA, termed spacers, and integrate them into the host genome within CRISPR loci. These loci are transcribed and processed into small guide RNAs (crRNAs), which then direct Cas nucleases to recognize and destroy invading nucleic acids bearing complementary sequences.

CRISPR-Cas systems are incredibly diverse and have been classified into three major types (I, II, and III) based on their distinct suite of Cas proteins and mechanisms of action. In Type I and Type III systems, target recognition and cleavage are typically mediated by large, multi-protein interference complexes, such as Cascade or the Csm/Cmr complexes. In striking contrast, the Type II CRISPR-Cas systems are remarkably simple, relying on a single large, multidomain protein, Cas9, to execute both crRNA biogenesis and target destruction. 

Recent studies of the Type II system from Streptococcus pyogenes have revealed that crRNA maturation requires a second, trans-encoded small RNA called tracrRNA. The tracrRNA contains a sequence highly complementary to the repeat region of the pre-crRNA transcript. Base pairing between the tracrRNA and pre-crRNA creates a double-stranded RNA structure that is recognized and cleaved by the host enzyme RNase III in the presence of Cas9, generating mature crRNAs. While the role of Cas9 and tracrRNA in crRNA processing is well established, the exact molecular mechanism by which Cas9 utilizes these RNAs to mediate DNA interference has remained unclear. It has been hypothesized that Cas9 acts as the sole effector nuclease, but its direct DNA cleavage activity has not been biochemically characterized in vitro.

In this study, we set out to elucidate the mechanism of DNA targeting and cleavage by the S. pyogenes Cas9 protein. We biochemically demonstrate that Cas9 is a DNA endonuclease that introduces double-stranded breaks at target sites complementary to the crRNA. Unexpectedly, we discovered that Cas9 relies on a dual-RNA guide consisting of both the mature crRNA and the tracrRNA to achieve site-specific DNA recognition and cleavage. Furthermore, we define the distinct roles of Cas9's two conserved nuclease domains, HNH and RuvC, in DNA strand cleavage. Finally, we show that the dual-tracrRNA:crRNA structure can be engineered into a single chimeric guide RNA, creating a highly programmable, RNA-guided DNA endonuclease system with profound potential for broad genome editing applications.
