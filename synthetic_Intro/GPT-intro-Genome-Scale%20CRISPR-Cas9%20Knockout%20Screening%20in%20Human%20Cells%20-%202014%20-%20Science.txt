# Introduction [170 words]
Systematic perturbation of gene function is a powerful strategy for linking genotype to phenotype in mammalian cells. Pooled RNA interference libraries have enabled genome-scale loss-of-function screens, but their utility is limited by incomplete transcript depletion, variable reagent efficacy, and off-target effects, which can reduce sensitivity and complicate interpretation. The CRISPR-associated nuclease Cas9, directed by a single guide RNA (sgRNA), introduces targeted DNA double-strand breaks that are repaired by non-homologous end joining, frequently generating insertions or deletions that disrupt gene function. Because Cas9 targeting is specified by short guide sequences, this system provides a simple framework for building large-scale libraries of genetic perturbations.

Here we develop a lentiviral CRISPR-Cas9 platform for genome-scale knockout screening in human cells. We designed a pooled GeCKO library targeting 18,080 genes and tested its performance in both negative and positive selection screens. By identifying genes required for cell viability and genes whose loss confers resistance to the BRAF inhibitor vemurafenib, we show that pooled Cas9-mediated mutagenesis enables robust, scalable, and highly specific interrogation of gene function.
