# Introduction
A fundamental goal in systems neuroscience is to achieve temporally precise, noninvasive control over the electrical activity of specific, well-defined populations of neurons within intact neural circuits. Understanding the complex mechanisms by which neural networks process information, encode memories, and drive behavior requires the ability to perturb specific elements of the circuit in a targeted manner and observe the resulting physiological and behavioral outcomes. Traditional techniques for manipulating neuronal activity, such as the use of stimulating electrodes, are highly invasive, inherently lack cellular specificity, and often result in the simultaneous activation of heterogeneous cell types and passing axonal fibers. Pharmacological interventions and the local application of excitatory or inhibitory compounds provide pharmacological specificity but operate on timescales far slower than the millisecond precision of natural neural coding, preventing the induction of physiologically relevant spike trains. While optical stimulation methods, such as the photo-uncaging of caged neurotransmitters (e.g., caged glutamate), have improved the spatial precision of activation, they still lack the ability to be genetically targeted to defined cell types and typically require the continuous application of exogenous chemical substrates.

To overcome these significant limitations, we sought to develop a fully genetically encoded, light-sensitive system that would allow for the rapid and reliable photostimulation of mammalian neurons. In this study, we adopted and engineered the naturally occurring algal protein Channelrhodopsin-2 (ChR2), a light-gated cation channel originally discovered in the green alga Chlamydomonas reinhardtii. Unlike complex vertebrate visual opsins that require multi-component G-protein-coupled signaling cascades to alter membrane potential, ChR2 serves simultaneously as both the photoreceptor and the ion channel. Upon illumination with blue light, ChR2 undergoes a rapid conformational change, directly opening a pore that allows the influx of positively charged ions, thereby depolarizing the cell membrane on a millisecond timescale.

Here, we harness this extraordinary naturally occurring protein for the precise control of mammalian neuronal spiking. We utilize lentiviral gene delivery to introduce a ChR2-fluorescent protein fusion into cultured rat hippocampal neurons, enabling the robust and stable expression of the channel at the plasma membrane. We demonstrate that high-speed optical switching can be used to elicit large-amplitude, rapidly activating photocurrents in these genetically modified neurons. Critically, we show that this system permits the reliable, millisecond-timescale control of single action potentials, the generation of sustained naturalistic spike trains, and the precise control of both excitatory and inhibitory synaptic transmission. This technology offers an unprecedented level of temporal and genetic control over neural activity.
