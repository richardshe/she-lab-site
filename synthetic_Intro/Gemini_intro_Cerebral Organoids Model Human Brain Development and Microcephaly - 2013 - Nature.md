The human brain is arguably the most complex and highly organized biological structure in existence. Its remarkable cognitive abilities arise from a dramatic evolutionary expansion in size, accompanied by the intricate regionalization and lamination of the cerebral cortex. Understanding the precise molecular and cellular mechanisms that govern human brain development is not only a fundamental goal of developmental biology but is also essential for uncovering the underlying causes of a wide spectrum of devastating neurodevelopmental, psychiatric, and neurodegenerative disorders. 

Historically, our knowledge of brain development has relied heavily on studies in model organisms, predominantly the mouse. While these animal models have provided invaluable insights into the basic principles of neurogenesis, neuronal migration, and circuit assembly, they possess significant limitations when attempting to model the unique features of the human brain. The human cerebral cortex, for example, is highly folded (gyrencephalic) and contains a massively expanded outer subventricular zone (OSVZ) populated by specialized outer radial glia (oRG) cells, features that are largely absent in the smooth (lissencephalic) rodent brain. Consequently, many human neurodevelopmental disorders, such as primary microcephaly, exhibit severe phenotypic discrepancies when modeled in mice, often failing to recapitulate the profound brain size reduction seen in patients. This underscores the critical need for an in vitro model system that can faithfully recapitulate the unique complexities of early human brain development.

Recent advancements in stem cell biology have demonstrated the remarkable capacity of mammalian pluripotent stem cells to self-organize into complex, three-dimensional (3D) tissue structures in vitro. By carefully modulating culture conditions to mimic the embryonic environment, researchers have successfully generated functional 3D organoids resembling optic cup, pituitary gland, and intestinal epithelia. Building upon these conceptual breakthroughs, we sought to develop a robust and reproducible 3D culture system capable of generating highly organized, human brain-like tissues from human embryonic stem cells (hESCs) and induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs).

In this study, we describe the establishment of a novel 3D organoid culture system that leverages the intrinsic self-organizing properties of human pluripotent stem cells to generate cerebral organoids. We show that these organoids can grow to remarkable sizes and develop discrete, interdependent brain regions, including structures resembling the dorsal cortex, ventral forebrain, choroid plexus, and retina. Crucially, the cortical regions within these organoids recapitulate key organizational features of early human cortical development, including a distinct progenitor zone architecture and the presence of abundant outer radial glia. Finally, we demonstrate the utility of this powerful new system for disease modeling by generating cerebral organoids from patient-specific iPSCs to model primary microcephaly. By investigating these patient-derived organoids, we uncover a disease-specific defect in neural progenitor proliferation and premature neuronal differentiation, providing novel mechanistic insights into the pathogenesis of this severe neurodevelopmental disorder.
