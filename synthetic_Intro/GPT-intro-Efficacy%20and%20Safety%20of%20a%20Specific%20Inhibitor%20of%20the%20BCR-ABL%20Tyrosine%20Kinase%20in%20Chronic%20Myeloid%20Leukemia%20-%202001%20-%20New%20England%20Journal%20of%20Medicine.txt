# Introduction
Chronic myeloid leukemia is a clonal myeloproliferative disorder characterized by expansion of myeloid cells and by the presence, in nearly all affected patients, of the Philadelphia chromosome. This abnormal chromosome results from a reciprocal translocation between chromosomes 9 and 22 and gives rise to the BCR-ABL fusion gene. The BCR-ABL protein has constitutively increased tyrosine kinase activity and transforms hematopoietic cells in experimental systems. Inhibition of this kinase therefore represents a rational strategy for the treatment of chronic myeloid leukemia.

Current therapy for chronic-phase chronic myeloid leukemia includes hydroxyurea, busulfan, interferon alfa, cytarabine, and allogeneic stem-cell transplantation. Interferon alfa can induce hematologic and cytogenetic responses and has been associated with prolongation of survival, but many patients do not have a cytogenetic response, lose their response, or cannot tolerate treatment. Allogeneic transplantation may be curative, but its use is limited by age, donor availability, and treatment-related morbidity and mortality. Thus, many patients require additional therapy after standard treatment has failed.

STI571, formerly CGP 57148B, is a 2-phenylaminopyrimidine derivative that selectively inhibits the ABL tyrosine kinase as well as the related platelet-derived growth factor receptor and c-kit kinases. In preclinical studies, STI571 inhibited the tyrosine kinase activity of BCR-ABL, blocked proliferation of BCR-ABL-positive cell lines, and suppressed growth of BCR-ABL-positive cells obtained from patients with chronic myeloid leukemia. The compound did not substantially inhibit the growth of normal hematopoietic progenitors at concentrations active against BCR-ABL-positive cells, suggesting that selective inhibition of the molecular lesion in chronic myeloid leukemia might be therapeutically feasible.

We conducted a phase 1, dose-escalation study of orally administered STI571 in patients with chronic-phase chronic myeloid leukemia in whom treatment with interferon alfa had failed or could not be tolerated. The primary objective was to determine the safety and tolerability of STI571; secondary objectives included assessment of hematologic and cytogenetic responses, pharmacokinetics, and inhibition of BCR-ABL kinase activity in vivo.
