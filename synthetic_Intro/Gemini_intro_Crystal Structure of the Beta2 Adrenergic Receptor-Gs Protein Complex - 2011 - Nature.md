G protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) represent the largest and most diverse family of membrane proteins in the human genome, comprising over 800 distinct members. These receptors act as the primary gatekeepers of cellular communication, mediating physiological responses to an extraordinary array of extracellular signals, including hormones, neurotransmitters, chemokines, local mediators, and even sensory stimuli such as light, odors, and taste. Due to their central role in regulating virtually every major physiological process—ranging from cardiovascular and pulmonary function to immune responses and neurotransmission—GPCRs are the targets for nearly one-third of all currently approved pharmaceutical drugs.

The fundamental paradigm of GPCR signal transduction involves the recognition of an extracellular ligand (agonist) by the receptor, which induces a conformational change that is transmitted across the plasma membrane to the intracellular surface. This active receptor conformation then binds and activates a heterotrimeric GTP-binding protein (G protein), composed of α, β, and γ subunits. Upon receptor binding, the G protein undergoes a critical nucleotide exchange process, releasing bound guanosine diphosphate (GDP) and binding guanosine triphosphate (GTP). This exchange triggers the dissociation of the G protein into active Gα-GTP and Gβγ subunits, which subsequently interact with various downstream effector enzymes and ion channels to propagate the cellular response. 

The β2 adrenergic receptor (β2AR), which mediates physiological responses to adrenaline and noradrenaline, has long served as the quintessential model system for understanding GPCR biology and G protein activation. The β2AR specifically couples to the stimulatory G protein, Gs, leading to the activation of adenylyl cyclase and the production of the second messenger cyclic AMP (cAMP). Over the past several decades, extensive biochemical, biophysical, and mutagenesis studies on the β2AR-Gs system have provided a wealth of indirect information regarding the receptor regions critical for G protein coupling and the biochemical steps involved in nucleotide exchange. However, a complete, mechanistic understanding of transmembrane signaling requires a detailed, high-resolution three-dimensional structure of the activated receptor in complex with its cognate G protein. 

Obtaining such a structure has been a monumental challenge in structural biology due to the inherent instability of the transient receptor-G protein complex, particularly when solubilized in detergents required for crystallization. The complex is highly dynamic, and the binding of nucleotides rapidly induces its dissociation. In this study, we have overcome these formidable technical barriers through a combination of innovative protein engineering, the use of a stabilizing camelid nanobody, and advanced lipidic cubic phase (LCP) crystallization techniques. Here, we present the high-resolution crystal structure of the active-state ternary complex composed of the agonist-bound human β2AR and the nucleotide-free Gs heterotrimer. This structure provides the first unprecedented, high-resolution view of how an agonist-bound GPCR engages and fundamentally alters the conformation of a G protein, revealing the structural basis for transmembrane signaling and G protein activation.
