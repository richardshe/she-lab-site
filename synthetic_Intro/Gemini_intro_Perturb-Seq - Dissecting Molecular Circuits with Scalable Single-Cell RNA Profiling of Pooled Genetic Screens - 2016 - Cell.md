# Introduction
The systematic perturbation of gene function is a cornerstone of biological research, enabling the elucidation of complex regulatory networks and cellular pathways. Genetic screens, particularly those utilizing the CRISPR-Cas9 system, have revolutionized our ability to identify genes required for cellular survival, proliferation, and resistance to various drugs or toxins. In a typical pooled genetic screen, large libraries of single-guide RNAs (sgRNAs) are introduced into a population of cells, and the functional consequences are measured by determining the enrichment or depletion of specific guides via bulk DNA sequencing. While incredibly powerful for identifying essential genes, these traditional pooled screens are largely limited to simple, one-dimensional phenotypes, such as cell death or the expression of a single fluorescent reporter. 

To fully dissect the architecture of molecular circuits, we require the ability to measure high-dimensional, complex phenotypes—such as genome-wide transcriptional profiles—across thousands of distinct genetic perturbations simultaneously. Until recently, mapping the transcriptomic consequences of multiple gene knockouts necessitated arrayed experiments in multi-well plates, an approach that is labor-intensive, expensive, and severely limited in scale.

In this study, we introduce Perturb-seq, a highly scalable platform that seamlessly merges pooled CRISPR-Cas9 genetic screening with single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq). By engineering a lentiviral vector that not only delivers an sgRNA to a cell but also uniquely identifies that perturbation via an expressed guide barcode (GBC) transcript, we can simultaneously read out both the specific genetic manipulation and the resulting whole-transcriptome profile in thousands of individual cells in a single pooled format. We demonstrate the power of Perturb-seq by analyzing over 200,000 single cells across immune cells and human cell lines. Focusing on the complex regulatory response of dendritic cells to lipopolysaccharide, we show that Perturb-seq can accurately identify the individual gene targets, modular gene signatures, and dynamic cell states controlled by various transcription factors. By decomposing these high-content measurements, Perturb-seq dramatically expands the scope and resolution of pooled genomic assays, allowing us to map the functional wiring of the genome at unprecedented scale.
