# Introduction
Embryonic stem (ES) cells, first established from the inner cell mass of mouse blastocysts nearly two decades ago, are pluripotent cell lines that can be cultured indefinitely in an undifferentiated state while retaining the capacity to differentiate into all embryonic germ layers. Mouse ES cells have provided an invaluable model for studying the mechanisms of early mammalian development and have revolutionized genetics through the creation of transgenic and knockout models. Despite these advances, the derivation of analogous cell lines from human embryos has proven elusive. Previous efforts have successfully isolated pluripotent cells from human teratocarcinomas (embryonal carcinoma or EC cells) and from human primordial germ cells (embryonic germ or EG cells). While these human lines share some properties with mouse ES cells, they are often karyotypically abnormal or exhibit constrained developmental potential. The availability of true human ES cell lines, possessing both a normal diploid karyotype and the full developmental potential to form all somatic lineages, would offer an unprecedented window into human developmental biology. Furthermore, such cell lines could provide a limitless source of human cells and tissues for drug discovery, pharmacological testing, and transplantation medicine. Here, we report the successful derivation and continuous in vitro culture of embryonic stem cell lines from human blastocysts.
