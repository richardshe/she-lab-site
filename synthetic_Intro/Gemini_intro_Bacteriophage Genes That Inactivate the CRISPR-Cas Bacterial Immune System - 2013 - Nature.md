Bacteriophages and their bacterial hosts are locked in a continuous evolutionary arms race. To survive the constant threat of viral predation, bacteria have evolved a diverse arsenal of defense mechanisms, including restriction-modification systems, abortive infection pathways, and surface receptor alterations. The most recently discovered, and perhaps most sophisticated, of these defenses is the clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) and CRISPR-associated (Cas) proteins. CRISPR/Cas systems function as an adaptive immune system, utilizing small guide RNAs derived from previously encountered invaders to direct the sequence-specific nucleolytic destruction of foreign nucleic acids. 

Given the intense selective pressure exerted by bacterial defenses, phages have rapidly evolved countermeasures. For instance, diverse phage-encoded proteins are known to specifically inhibit restriction-modification systems. However, despite the widespread distribution of CRISPR/Cas systems across the bacterial domain, specific viral genes dedicated to inactivating this RNA-guided immunity have not yet been identified. It has been widely assumed that phages escape CRISPR/Cas targeting primarily through the accumulation of point mutations within the targeted protospacer regions. In this study, we screened bacteriophages infecting the opportunistic pathogen Pseudomonas aeruginosa to identify specific, genetically encoded inhibitors of the type I-F CRISPR/Cas system.
