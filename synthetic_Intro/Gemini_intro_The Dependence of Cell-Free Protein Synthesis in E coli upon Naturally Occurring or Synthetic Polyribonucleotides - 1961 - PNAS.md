The biochemical mechanism by which genetic information is translated into the specific amino acid sequences of proteins remains one of the central problems in biology. While it is generally accepted that deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) harbors the genetic code, intermediate templates consisting of ribonucleic acid (RNA) are thought to direct the actual assembly of amino acids at the ribosomal site of protein synthesis. Previous studies have indicated a requirement for RNA in cell-free amino acid incorporation, yet the direct programming of specific protein sequences by defined RNA templates has not been conclusively demonstrated. In this communication, we describe a highly stable, cell-free system derived from Escherichia coli that exhibits a rigorous dependence on the addition of template RNA for the incorporation of amino acids into protein. We report that the addition of a synthetic polyribonucleotide, polyuridylic acid, specifically directs the synthesis of polyphenylalanine.
