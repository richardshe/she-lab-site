Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS) is a devastating and uniformly fatal neurodegenerative disease characterized by the progressive loss of upper and lower motor neurons. Frontotemporal dementia (FTD) is the second most common form of early-onset dementia and is clinically characterized by changes in behavior, personality, and language. Although historically considered distinct clinical entities, it is now widely recognized that ALS and FTD share significant clinical, pathological, and genetic overlap, leading to the concept of an ALS-FTD disease spectrum. Up to 50% of ALS patients develop some degree of cognitive or behavioral impairment during the course of their disease, while up to 15% of FTD patients also develop motor neuron dysfunction. Pathologically, the intracellular accumulation of the TAR DNA-binding protein 43 (TDP-43) is the primary hallmark of both sporadic ALS and the most common pathological subtype of FTD, frontotemporal lobar degeneration with TDP-43 inclusions (FTLD-TDP). 

Genetically, a substantial proportion of both ALS and FTD is familial, with autosomal dominant inheritance being the most common pattern. Several genes have been identified that can cause either ALS or FTD, including TARDBP, FUS, and VCP. However, the most robust genetic evidence linking the two disorders comes from linkage analysis. Over the past decade, several independent research groups have mapped a major locus for familial ALS, FTD, and mixed ALS-FTD to a 9-11 Mb region on the short arm of chromosome 9 (9p21.1-p21.2). Subsequent genome-wide association studies (GWAS) have also robustly identified this same chromosome 9p21 region as the major genetic susceptibility locus for both sporadic ALS and FTD. 

Despite intense sequencing efforts by multiple international consortia, the specific genetic defect underlying the chromosome 9p21 locus has remained elusive. We recently demonstrated that the association signal at chromosome 9p21 in both familial and sporadic ALS cases is driven by a single founder haplotype that is shared across multiple populations of European descent. This risk haplotype spans a 232 kb region encompassing three characterized genes: MOBKL2b, IFNK, and C9ORF72. However, extensive Sanger sequencing of the coding regions, splice sites, and highly conserved non-coding regions within this genomic interval failed to identify any pathogenic mutations. This led us to hypothesize that the causal variant might be a structural variation, a mutation located in a poorly characterized regulatory region, or a sequence change that is difficult to detect using standard sequencing technologies.

In the present study, we employed a combination of targeted enrichment and next-generation deep sequencing to exhaustively analyze the chromosome 9p21 linkage region in affected individuals from two well-characterized chromosome 9p21-linked pedigrees. This comprehensive approach allowed us to identify a massive hexanucleotide (GGGGCC) repeat expansion within the first intron of the C9ORF72 gene. We demonstrate that this expanded repeat completely segregates with disease in multiple families and is highly associated with both familial and sporadic ALS and FTD in the Finnish population and in wider European cohorts. Our findings resolve the long-standing mystery of the chromosome 9p21 locus and identify the most common genetic cause of these fatal neurodegenerative diseases discovered to date.
