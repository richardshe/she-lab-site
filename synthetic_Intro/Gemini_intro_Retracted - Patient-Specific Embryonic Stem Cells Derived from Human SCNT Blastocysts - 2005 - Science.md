Embryonic stem cells hold significant promise for the treatment of numerous degenerative diseases and injuries, owing to their unique capacity for self-renewal and pluripotency. Despite their immense potential, a major hurdle in the clinical application of human embryonic stem cells (hESCs) is the risk of immune rejection following transplantation into patients. Somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT) has been proposed as a method to circumvent this barrier. SCNT involves the transfer of a somatic cell nucleus from a patient into an enucleated oocyte, leading to the generation of a blastocyst from which patient-specific hESCs can be derived. Because these cells would be genetically identical to the donor patient, they are anticipated to be immunologically tolerated.

Although SCNT has been successfully demonstrated in various mammalian species, including sheep, mice, and cattle, its application to human cells has proven exceptionally challenging. Previous attempts at human therapeutic cloning have yielded limited success, characterized by low blastocyst formation rates and inefficient derivation of hESC lines. In 2004, our group reported the first successful derivation of a human SCNT-hESC line, demonstrating the proof of principle for human therapeutic cloning. However, this initial success was limited to autologous SCNT, utilizing somatic cells and oocytes from the same female donor, and the overall efficiency remained low.

To fully realize the clinical potential of SCNT, it is essential to establish reliable and highly efficient protocols for generating patient-specific hESCs from diverse patient populations, regardless of age or sex. This requires the successful application of heterologous SCNT, where donor somatic cells are transferred into oocytes from unrelated volunteers. In this study, we aim to significantly improve the efficiency of human SCNT and to demonstrate the robust derivation of patient-specific hESC lines from individuals suffering from a range of severe diseases and injuries. By optimizing key steps in the SCNT procedure, including oocyte enucleation, nuclear injection, and stem cell derivation on human feeder layers, we sought to establish a practical and ethically responsible framework for generating immune-matched pluripotent cells. Here, we report the highly efficient derivation of eleven new SCNT-hESC lines from patients with spinal cord injury, juvenile diabetes, and congenital hypogammaglobulinemia, providing a critical step forward in the development of personalized regenerative therapies.
