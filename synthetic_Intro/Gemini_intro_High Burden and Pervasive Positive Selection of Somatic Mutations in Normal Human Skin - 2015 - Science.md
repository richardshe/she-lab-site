# Introduction
Cancer is an evolutionary process driven by the progressive accumulation of somatic mutations in the genomes of cells. Some of these mutations, known as "drivers," confer a selective growth advantage, allowing the mutant clone to outcompete its neighbors and expand. Over time, the sequential acquisition of driver mutations in a single lineage can lead to malignant transformation, uncontrolled proliferation, and ultimately, tissue invasion and metastasis. Massive parallel sequencing of human cancers has provided an unprecedented catalog of the genes and pathways altered in established tumors, offering profound insights into the later stages of this evolutionary trajectory.

However, our understanding of the very earliest stages of cancer development remains fundamentally incomplete. Because cancer genomics has almost exclusively focused on fully transformed, clinically detectable tumors, the foundational landscape from which these tumors arise—the normal tissue—is largely unexplored. We know remarkably little about the rate at which somatic mutations accumulate in normal human cells over a lifetime, the mutational signatures left by environmental exposures in healthy tissue, or the extent to which normal cells undergo clonal expansion driven by positively selected mutations. Understanding the mutational burden and clonal dynamics of normal tissue is critical, as it defines the starting material for oncogenesis and helps distinguish mutations that are sufficient for transformation from those that merely confer a benign growth advantage.

Skin provides an ideal model system to investigate these early evolutionary dynamics. The epidermis is continuously replenished throughout life and is frequently exposed to mutagens, most notably ultraviolet (UV) radiation from sunlight. Epidemiological data show a clear link between cumulative sun exposure and the incidence of skin cancers, such as cutaneous squamous cell carcinoma (cSCC) and basal cell carcinoma (BCC). Yet, the genomic landscape of the physiologically normal skin cells that endure this chronic exposure is unknown.

To illuminate the hidden evolutionary life of normal cells, we utilized ultradeep targeted sequencing of 74 known cancer genes to characterize the somatic mutation landscape in 234 small biopsies of normal, sun-exposed eyelid epidermis from four individuals. By adapting algorithms to detect subclonal mutations at very low allele frequencies, we quantified the mutation burden, identified mutational signatures, and mapped the size and frequency of mutant clones. Remarkably, we reveal that physiologically normal skin is a complex patchwork of thousands of evolving clones, bearing a mutational burden similar to many cancers and exhibiting pervasive positive selection for driver mutations in key cancer genes.
