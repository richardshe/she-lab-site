# Introduction [386 words]

The hematopoietic system is maintained by rare stem cells that reside primarily in specialized bone marrow niches, yet these cells are not permanently sessile. In response to injury, infection, or cytokine-mediated mobilization, hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells enter the circulation and seed distant hematopoietic tissues. This migratory behavior exposes stem cells to a markedly different immune environment. Blood, spleen, liver, and marrow sinusoids contain abundant macrophages that continuously survey passing cells and remove aged, damaged, infected, or foreign elements by phagocytosis. Thus, circulating stem cells must preserve their regenerative capacity while avoiding inappropriate innate immune clearance.

One mechanism by which normal cells are protected from macrophage engulfment is expression of CD47, also known as integrin-associated protein. CD47 binds signal regulatory protein alpha (SIRP alpha) on macrophages and delivers an inhibitory signal that restrains phagocytosis. Loss of CD47 renders erythrocytes and lymphocytes susceptible to macrophage-mediated elimination, indicating that CD47 functions as a broadly used marker of self. However, it has not been clear whether regulated CD47 expression contributes to the physiological behavior of hematopoietic stem cells, particularly during inflammatory states in which both stem cell trafficking and macrophage activation are increased.

Malignant hematopoietic cells face a related but pathological problem. Leukemias arise within macrophage-rich tissues and disseminate through the blood, yet many evade destruction despite expressing abnormal or stress-associated features that could mark them for innate immune clearance. Cancer immunosurveillance has been studied largely in the context of lymphocytes and natural killer cells, whereas the possibility that macrophages impose a selective barrier on developing leukemias has been less well defined. If leukemic progenitors could acquire or constitutively maintain anti-phagocytic signals normally used by healthy stem cells, this would provide a simple mechanism by which malignant clones gain a survival advantage in the hematopoietic environment.

Here we show that CD47 is dynamically up-regulated on normal hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells during cytokine-induced mobilization and inflammatory stress, and that the amount of CD47 on these cells determines their susceptibility to macrophage clearance in vivo. We further show that mouse and human myeloid leukemias constitutively express elevated CD47, and that increasing CD47 expression on leukemia cells promotes tumor growth by protecting them from macrophage phagocytosis. These findings identify CD47 as a regulated determinant of hematopoietic stem cell fitness and as a mechanism co-opted by leukemia to evade innate immune surveillance.
