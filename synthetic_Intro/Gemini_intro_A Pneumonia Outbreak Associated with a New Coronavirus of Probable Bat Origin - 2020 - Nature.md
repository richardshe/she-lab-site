Coronaviruses (CoVs) are enveloped, positive-sense, single-stranded RNA viruses that belong to the subfamily Orthocoronavirinae within the family Coronaviridae. While most human coronaviruses cause only mild respiratory diseases, the emergence of the severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus (SARS-CoV) in 2002 and the Middle East respiratory syndrome coronavirus (MERS-CoV) in 2012 demonstrated that these viruses can cross species barriers to cause highly lethal pneumonia in humans. The natural reservoirs for both SARS-CoV and MERS-CoV are bats, with intermediate hosts facilitating their transmission to humans. Since the SARS outbreak, extensive surveillance of bat populations has revealed the vast genetic diversity of SARS-related coronaviruses (SARSr-CoVs) circulating in the wild. Notably, some of these bat SARSr-CoVs have been shown to use the human angiotensin-converting enzyme II (ACE2) receptor for cell entry, suggesting a continuous potential for spillover events. 

In late December 2019, an outbreak of a mysterious and severe respiratory disease was reported in Wuhan, Hubei Province, China. The initial cluster of patients was epidemiologically linked to a local seafood market, presenting with clinical symptoms strongly resembling viral pneumonia, including fever, cough, and dyspnea. The rapid spread of the disease and its clinical severity raised immediate public health alarms. In this study, we detail the urgent diagnostic investigation and pathogen discovery efforts undertaken during the early stages of this outbreak. We report the isolation, genomic characterization, and preliminary receptor usage analysis of a novel coronavirus, tentatively named 2019-nCoV, establishing its probable bat origin and its relationship to the SARSr-CoV lineage.
