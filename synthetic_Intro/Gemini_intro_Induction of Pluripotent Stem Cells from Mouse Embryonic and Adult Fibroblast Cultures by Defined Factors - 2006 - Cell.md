# Introduction
Embryonic stem (ES) cells, derived from the inner cell mass of mammalian blastocysts, possess the unique ability to grow indefinitely in culture while maintaining pluripotency—the capacity to differentiate into all somatic cell types. This remarkable dual property makes ES cells an invaluable resource for developmental biology, drug discovery, and regenerative medicine. However, the use of human ES cells is accompanied by significant ethical controversies regarding the destruction of human embryos, as well as biological challenges related to tissue rejection following transplantation.

A potential solution to these issues is the reprogramming of somatic cells back into a pluripotent state, enabling the generation of patient-specific, customized stem cells. It has been well established that the nuclei of differentiated cells can be reprogrammed to an embryonic-like state either by somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT) into unfertilized oocytes or by fusion with existing ES cells. These landmark experiments demonstrate that the unfertilized egg and the ES cell contain the necessary trans-acting factors to reset the epigenetic state of a somatic genome. Yet, the identity of these crucial reprogramming factors has remained elusive, and the technical difficulties and ethical concerns associated with SCNT limit its broad application.

We hypothesized that the factors that maintain pluripotency in ES cells might also be capable of inducing pluripotency in somatic cells. By identifying the core transcription factors responsible for establishing the ES cell identity, it might be possible to directly reprogram differentiated cells without the need for oocytes or cell fusion. In this study, we selected 24 candidate genes known to be highly expressed in ES cells and systematically tested their ability to induce pluripotency in mouse embryonic and adult fibroblasts.
