# Introduction [345 words]

The ability to deliver genetic information into mammalian cells safely and efficiently remains a central goal of gene therapy. Viral vectors have been developed for this purpose and can mediate robust expression of therapeutic proteins, but their use is limited by immunogenicity, restricted packaging capacity, difficulties in repeated administration, and the risk of insertional mutagenesis when integrating vectors are used. Plasmid DNA avoids some of these concerns, yet it must enter the nucleus and be transcribed before protein can be produced, a requirement that limits its efficacy in many primary and nondividing cells. Thus, alternative vectors that can direct transient and controllable protein expression without altering the genome are needed.

In vitro-transcribed mRNA represents such an alternative. Once delivered to the cytoplasm, mRNA can be translated immediately, does not require nuclear entry, and is incapable of genomic integration. In principle, these properties make mRNA suitable for vaccination, protein replacement, and other applications in which temporary expression is desirable. Despite these advantages, therapeutic use of mRNA has been hindered by two major limitations. First, RNA is considered biologically unstable and is rapidly degraded by ubiquitous ribonucleases. Second, in vitro-transcribed RNA is strongly immunostimulatory. Cellular receptors that detect foreign RNA, including endosomal Toll-like receptors and cytoplasmic RNA sensors, can induce type I interferons and inflammatory cytokines after exposure to unmodified transcripts. These innate immune responses can limit translation of the delivered RNA and raise safety concerns for systemic administration.

A distinguishing feature of cellular RNA is the presence of numerous naturally modified nucleosides. Transfer RNA, ribosomal RNA, small nuclear RNA, and other noncoding RNAs contain modified nucleotides that influence RNA structure, stability, and recognition by proteins. We and others have shown that incorporation of such modifications into RNA can reduce activation of innate immune receptors. Whether the same strategy can improve the functional properties of protein-encoding mRNA, however, has not been established.

Here, we examined whether replacement of canonical nucleotides with naturally occurring modified nucleotides could generate mRNAs with improved biological activity. We tested a panel of nucleoside-modified transcripts in cell-free systems, cultured mammalian cells, and mice. We found that complete substitution of uridine with pseudouridine markedly enhances translational capacity, increases biological stability, and prevents induction of interferon-alpha after systemic delivery. These findings identify pseudouridine-containing mRNA as a promising nonintegrating vector for therapeutic protein expression.
