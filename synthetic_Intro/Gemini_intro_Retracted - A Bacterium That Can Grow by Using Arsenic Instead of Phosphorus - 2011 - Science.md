# Introduction
The fundamental chemistry of life as we know it is remarkably conserved across all domains. All known terrestrial organisms are primarily composed of just six major elements: carbon, hydrogen, nitrogen, oxygen, sulfur, and phosphorus. These vital elements serve as the essential building blocks for the core macromolecules of life, making up the structural and functional bulk of nucleic acids (DNA and RNA), proteins, and lipid membranes. Phosphorus, in the form of inorganic phosphate, plays an especially critical structural role in the backbone of nucleic acids and is the central currency for cellular energy transfer via adenosine triphosphate (ATP). While these six elements are universally relied upon, it is theoretically possible that other elements from the periodic table, specifically those sharing similar chemical properties within the same group, could substitute and serve analogous biological functions under extreme environmental conditions. Arsenic, which sits directly below phosphorus on the periodic table, shares many chemical similarities with phosphorus but is generally highly toxic to biological systems because it can disrupt phosphate-dependent metabolic pathways. 

Here, we describe the discovery of an extraordinary bacterium, strain GFAJ-1, a member of the Halomonadaceae family, which was isolated from the harsh, hypersaline, and highly alkaline environment of Mono Lake, California—a unique ecosystem known for its unusually high concentrations of dissolved arsenic. Through rigorous cultivation in defined artificial media, we demonstrate that GFAJ-1 possesses the unprecedented ability to substitute arsenic for phosphorus to sustain its cellular growth and viability. Our extensive analytical data provide compelling evidence that this organism actively incorporates arsenate into its key intracellular macromolecules, replacing phosphate in structures where it is normally strictly required. Most notably, we detect the presence of arsenic integrated directly into the organism's nucleic acids and proteins. The ability of a living organism to functionally exchange one of the major, universally conserved bio-elements for a toxic metalloid challenges our fundamental understanding of biochemistry. This remarkable physiological adaptation not only expands the known parameters of biology but also carries profound evolutionary and geochemical implications for the boundaries of life on Earth and potentially elsewhere.
