# Introduction

The ability to alter genomic DNA at a chosen locus has transformed biological research and created new opportunities for the treatment of genetic disease. Programmable nucleases, including zinc-finger nucleases, transcription activator-like effector nucleases, and RNA-guided CRISPR/Cas9 systems, use sequence-specific DNA recognition to induce double-stranded DNA breaks (DSBs) at targeted genomic sites. These breaks are repaired by endogenous cellular pathways, most commonly by non-homologous end joining (NHEJ), which frequently generates insertions and deletions (indels), or less frequently by homology-directed repair (HDR) when an exogenous donor DNA template is supplied. Although these strategies have enabled efficient gene disruption and, in some cases, precise sequence replacement, they rely on the formation of DSBs and on cellular DNA repair outcomes that are often inefficient, stochastic, and difficult to control.

This limitation is especially consequential for the correction of point mutations. Single-nucleotide substitutions account for a large fraction of known pathogenic human genetic variation, yet current genome-editing approaches are poorly matched to the chemical simplicity of these lesions. To change one base pair into another using nuclease-mediated HDR, cells must first tolerate a DSB, copy information from an exogenous repair template, and avoid competing NHEJ events that can disrupt the target locus. In many therapeutically relevant cell types, HDR is inefficient or absent, while NHEJ is active and can introduce permanent genetic damage. As a result, attempts to correct point mutations using programmable nucleases often produce a mixture of rare desired alleles and more abundant indel-containing alleles. A strategy that directly converts one base into another without requiring DSBs or donor DNA would therefore offer a more natural solution to the problem of single-base genome editing.

Several features of CRISPR/Cas9 make it an attractive platform for such an approach. Cas9 can be programmed with a guide RNA to bind a chosen DNA sequence, and catalytically impaired Cas9 retains this targeting ability while no longer cleaving DNA. During target recognition, Cas9 locally unwinds the DNA duplex and exposes a short segment of single-stranded DNA within the R-loop. This transiently accessible DNA strand presents a potential substrate for enzymes that act on single-stranded nucleic acids. Cytidine deaminases, for example, catalyze the hydrolytic deamination of cytidine to uridine, a chemical change that, if installed in genomic DNA and resolved by replication or repair, can produce a C:G to T:A substitution.

Here we develop base editing, a programmable genome-editing strategy that directly converts targeted cytidines to thymidines without requiring DSB formation or a donor template. We engineer fusions between Cas9 variants and cytidine deaminases, identify architectures that mediate guide RNA-dependent deamination of DNA, and manipulate cellular DNA repair pathways to favor permanent installation of the desired base-pair change. We show that these base editors can efficiently edit endogenous genomic loci in mammalian cells, produce substantially fewer indels than Cas9-mediated HDR, and correct disease-relevant point mutations. These results establish a general framework for genome editing by direct chemical modification of bases rather than by nuclease-induced DNA breakage.
