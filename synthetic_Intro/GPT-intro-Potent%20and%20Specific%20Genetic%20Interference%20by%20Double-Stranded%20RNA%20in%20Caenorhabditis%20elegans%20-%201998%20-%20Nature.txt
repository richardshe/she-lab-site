# Introduction

Experimental inhibition of gene expression by introduced RNA has provided a useful, though incompletely understood, method for analysing gene function. In several systems, RNA molecules complementary to a cellular messenger RNA have been used to reduce the activity of the corresponding gene. Such antisense effects are generally presumed to reflect direct hybridization between the introduced RNA and the endogenous transcript, although the efficiency and specificity of these effects have varied considerably. In C. elegans, injection of RNA corresponding to defined genes has been reported to produce phenotypes resembling loss-of-function mutations, suggesting that RNA-mediated interference could be used as a genetic tool in this organism. Several observations, however, have complicated a simple antisense interpretation, including effects seen with sense RNA preparations and the ability of injected material to influence progeny. We therefore examined the structural requirements, potency and specificity of RNA-mediated interference in C. elegans. In particular, we compared purified sense, antisense and double-stranded RNA species corresponding to several genes with known mutant phenotypes.
