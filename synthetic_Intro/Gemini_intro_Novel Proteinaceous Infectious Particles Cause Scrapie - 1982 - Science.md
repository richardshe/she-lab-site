# Introduction
Scrapie, a naturally occurring, transmissible, and inevitably fatal neurodegenerative disease of sheep and goats, has puzzled investigators for decades due to the unprecedented physical and chemical properties of its infectious agent. Like kuru and Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (CJD) in humans, scrapie is characterized by a prolonged incubation period, sometimes spanning years, followed by progressive neurological dysfunction, ataxia, and characteristic spongiform vacuolation of the central nervous system. Unlike conventional viral or bacterial pathogens, the causative agent of scrapie evokes no recognizable inflammatory or robust immune response in the host, and it has consistently evaded detection by standard microscopic and molecular techniques.

Historically, efforts to isolate and characterize the scrapie agent have been severely hampered by the slow, tedious, and expensive nature of the required animal bioassays. Because no tissue culture system exists that supports the robust replication and titration of the agent, researchers have had to rely exclusively on animal inoculations. The original bioassays in sheep and goats took years to complete. The adaptation of the scrapie agent to mice in 1961 significantly advanced the field, though end-point titrations still required up to a year of observation. More recently, the transmission of the agent to the Syrian golden hamster has led to the development of a more rapid incubation-time assay, which allows for quantitative measurements in approximately 60 to 70 days, finally enabling more systematic biochemical fractionations.

Using these improved assays, substantial progress has been made toward purifying the infectious particles from hamster brain tissue. The scrapie agent has historically exhibited extreme heterogeneity in terms of its size, density, and charge, distributing widely across subcellular fractions. This heterogeneity, we demonstrate, largely arises from the profound hydrophobicity of the infectious particle, causing it to aggregate extensively with cellular membranes and proteins. By utilizing rigorous detergent extractions and sequential enzymatic digestions, we have achieved a 100- to 1000-fold purification of the agent relative to cellular protein.

In the course of these biochemical characterizations, a startling profile of the scrapie agent has emerged. Six independent lines of evidence indicate that a protein component is absolutely required for infectivity. The agent is readily inactivated by proteases like proteinase K and trypsin, as well as by protein-denaturing agents such as sodium dodecyl sulfate, chaotropic salts, phenol, and urea, and by chemical modification with diethyl pyrocarbonate. Conversely, the agent displays a remarkable, almost absolute resistance to procedures that specifically modify or destroy nucleic acids. It is impervious to digestion by a vast array of ribonucleases and deoxyribonucleases, it resists inactivation by psoralens and zinc-catalyzed hydrolysis, and it exhibits extreme resistance to ultraviolet irradiation. 

The extraordinary properties of the scrapie agent—namely, its absolute reliance on a protein for infectivity and its steadfast resistance to nucleic acid-inactivating treatments—distinctly separate it from all known viruses, plasmids, and viroids. Given that the dominant characteristics of the agent reflect those of a protein rather than a nucleic acid, we propose the term "prion" to denote a small, proteinaceous infectious particle that resists inactivation by most procedures that modify nucleic acids. The existence of prions challenges the central dogma of biology, posing fundamental questions about how an infectious pathogen can replicate without a nucleic acid genome. Understanding the molecular structure and replicative mechanism of the prion is not only critical for explaining the pathogenesis of scrapie, kuru, and CJD, but it may also provide novel insights into the etiologies of other chronic, degenerative diseases of the human central nervous system.
