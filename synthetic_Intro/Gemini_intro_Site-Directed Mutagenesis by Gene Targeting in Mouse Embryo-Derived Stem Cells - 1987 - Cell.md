The introduction of exogenous DNA sequences into the mammalian genome has become a powerful tool for analyzing gene expression, regulation, and cellular differentiation. Techniques such as microinjection, viral infection, and DNA transfection allow for the stable integration of cloned genes into cultured cells and the germline of mice. However, a major limitation of these approaches is that the integrated DNA sequences typically insert at random, non-homologous locations within the host genome. Because gene expression is profoundly influenced by the chromosomal environment, this random integration complicates the study of gene regulation in its normal chromosomal context. Furthermore, the inability to target integrations prevents the deliberate modification or disruption of specific endogenous genes, a capability that is essential for directly linking a gene to its physiological function and for generating precise animal models of human genetic diseases.

In contrast, homologous recombination between newly introduced DNA and cognate chromosomal sequences permits the targeted modification of the host genome. This approach, widely termed gene targeting, has been utilized with great success in yeast to generate targeted mutations, gene replacements, and precise chromosomal deletions. Extending this technology to mammalian systems would provide a robust method for directing specific mutations into any cloned gene within the mammalian genome. While homologous recombination has been demonstrated to occur between newly introduced plasmids and homologous chromosomal sequences in cultured mammalian cells, the frequency of such targeted events is generally several orders of magnitude lower than the frequency of random integration. Consequently, detecting the rare cells in which a targeted recombination event has occurred requires stringent selection or highly sensitive screening strategies.

Pluripotent mouse embryo-derived stem (ES) cells provide an ideal system for developing gene targeting techniques with the ultimate goal of generating mutant mice. ES cells are derived from the inner cell mass of preimplantation mouse blastocysts and can be maintained in an undifferentiated state in vitro. Crucially, when reintroduced into a developing blastocyst, these cells can colonize the embryo and contribute to all somatic tissues, as well as the germline, of the resulting chimeric mouse. Therefore, if a specific endogenous gene can be mutated by targeted recombination in cultured ES cells, it should be possible to introduce this defined mutation into the mouse germline. This would enable the derivation of mouse strains harboring precise genetic alterations, greatly facilitating the study of mammalian development and the generation of models for recessive genetic disorders.

In this study, we investigated the feasibility of using homologous recombination to direct a specific mutation into an endogenous gene in mouse ES cells. As our target locus, we chose the gene encoding the purine salvage enzyme hypoxanthine phosphoribosyl transferase (HPRT). The Hprt locus offers a unique advantage for these initial studies: it is X-linked, meaning that male-derived ES cells contain only a single functional copy of the gene. Inactivation of this single allele results in a complete loss of HPRT enzymatic activity. Furthermore, both positive and negative selections can be applied to the Hprt locus. Cells lacking HPRT activity can be readily selected by their resistance to the toxic base analog 6-thioguanine (6-TG). 

To mutate the endogenous Hprt gene, we constructed recombinant vectors carrying a cloned fragment of the mouse Hprt gene disrupted by the insertion of a neomycin resistance (neo) cassette. The neo gene serves two purposes: it disrupts the Hprt coding sequence to create a null allele, and it provides a selectable marker (resistance to the antibiotic G418) to identify cells that have stably integrated the vector DNA. By introducing these vectors into ES cells via electroporation and selecting for cells that are simultaneously resistant to both G418 and 6-TG, we successfully isolated clonal cell lines in which the endogenous Hprt gene had been selectively inactivated by homologous recombination. We describe the efficient targeting of the Hprt gene, examine the influence of vector design and the extent of sequence homology on targeting frequencies, and discuss the implications of these findings for the targeted modification of any gene in the mammalian genome.
