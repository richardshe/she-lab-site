During mammalian development, the fertilized egg undergoes progressive restrictions in developmental potential, ultimately giving rise to the specialized cell types comprising the adult organism. A fundamental question in biology is whether cellular differentiation involves irreversible genetic modifications. Nuclear transfer—inserting a donor nucleus into an enucleated unfertilized egg—provides a functional assay to test the developmental potential of a differentiated nucleus. While normal development has been achieved using nuclei from early embryonic blastomeres, the successful reprogramming of nuclei from adult tissues has proven exceptionally difficult.

Previously, we reported the birth of live sheep following nuclear transfer from a cultured embryonic cell line, demonstrating that cultured cells can remain totipotent if induced into a quiescent state. Building upon this strategy, we now investigate whether nuclei from advanced developmental stages can support full development. Here, we report the successful birth of live lambs derived by nuclear transfer from three distinct cell populations: an embryo-derived cell line, fetal fibroblasts, and a cell line derived from the mammary gland of an adult ewe.
