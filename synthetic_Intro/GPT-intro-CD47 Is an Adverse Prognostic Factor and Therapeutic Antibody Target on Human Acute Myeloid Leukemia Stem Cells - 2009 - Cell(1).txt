# Introduction

Acute myelogenous leukemia (AML) is an aggressive malignancy of the hematopoietic system characterized by the accumulation of immature myeloid blasts and failure of normal blood formation. Although many patients achieve morphologic remissions with induction chemotherapy, relapse remains common and is thought to result from persistence of rare leukemia stem cells (LSC) capable of self-renewal and re-initiation of disease. These cells are enriched within the Lin-CD34+CD38- fraction of many AML specimens, a compartment that also contains normal hematopoietic stem cells (HSC) and multipotent progenitors (MPP), creating a major therapeutic challenge: the identification of mechanisms that distinguish malignant from normal stem cells.

Several cell-surface molecules have been reported to be preferentially expressed on AML LSC, including CD44, CD96, CD123, and CLL-1. Such molecules are useful both as markers for disease assessment and as candidate therapeutic targets. However, most current therapies for AML do not specifically eliminate LSC, and few strategies directly exploit innate immune mechanisms that may normally recognize and remove malignant cells. Phagocytosis by macrophages is regulated by the balance of pro-phagocytic and anti-phagocytic signals. CD47 is a widely expressed cell-surface protein that binds signal regulatory protein alpha (SIRPα) on phagocytes and delivers an inhibitory “don’t eat me” signal. This interaction has been implicated in the protection of normal cells from macrophage-mediated clearance, suggesting that increased CD47 expression could provide malignant cells with a mechanism of immune evasion.

We hypothesized that human AML LSC upregulate CD47 to inhibit phagocytosis and thereby promote leukemia pathogenesis. This model predicts that CD47 expression should distinguish AML LSC from normal hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells, correlate with adverse clinical outcome, and represent a therapeutic target whose blockade enables macrophage-mediated elimination of LSC. Here we show that CD47 is more highly expressed on AML LSC than on normal HSC and MPP, that increased CD47 expression is an independent adverse prognostic factor in adult AML, and that blocking monoclonal antibodies directed against CD47 preferentially enable phagocytosis and in vivo targeting of human AML LSC.
