The packaging of eukaryotic genomes into chromatin provides a central layer of gene regulation by modulating the access of transcription factors, polymerases and chromatin regulators to DNA. Regulatory elements such as promoters, enhancers and insulators are typically marked by increased chromatin accessibility, characteristic nucleosome organization and occupancy by sequence-specific DNA-binding proteins. Genome-wide methods that measure these properties, including DNase-seq, FAIRE-seq, MNase-seq and ChIP-seq, have transformed the study of gene regulation. However, each method generally interrogates only one feature of chromatin architecture, and many require millions of cells, long multistep protocols or biochemical manipulations that can limit their application to scarce primary samples.

We sought to develop a rapid and sensitive assay that could extract multiple layers of epigenomic information directly from native chromatin. Hyperactive Tn5 transposase can fragment DNA and simultaneously insert sequencing adaptors in vitro, producing libraries suitable for high-throughput sequencing. We reasoned that, in intact nuclei, the probability of transposase insertion would be shaped by chromatin accessibility: open regulatory regions should be readily tagmented, whereas nucleosome-bound or compacted chromatin should be relatively protected. In addition, the positions of insertions and the sizes of resulting paired-end fragments might encode information about DNA-binding factor occupancy and nucleosome organization.

Here we describe an assay for transposase-accessible chromatin using sequencing (ATAC-seq), a simple two-step method for epigenomic profiling from as few as 500 cells. We show that ATAC-seq identifies open chromatin, resolves nucleosome positions, reveals transcription-factor footprints and enables personalized regulatory analysis on clinically relevant timescales.
