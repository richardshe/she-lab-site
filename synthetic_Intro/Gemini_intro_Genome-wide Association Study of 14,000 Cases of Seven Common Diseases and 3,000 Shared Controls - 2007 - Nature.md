# Introduction
Common, complex diseases such as coronary artery disease, diabetes, and autoimmune disorders place a massive burden on global public health. Unlike rare Mendelian disorders, where single highly penetrant mutations cause the disease, the genetic architecture of complex diseases is thought to involve numerous sequence variants, each contributing a relatively modest effect to the overall risk. For many years, the search for these susceptibility genes relied largely on linkage analysis in affected families or candidate gene association studies. While linkage analysis has successfully identified genetic determinants for many rare monogenic traits, it has generally lacked the power to resolve the complex, polygenic basis of common diseases. Conversely, candidate gene studies have been plagued by a lack of reproducibility and the fundamental limitation of relying on prior, often incomplete, biological hypotheses.

The completion of the Human Genome Project, coupled with the detailed mapping of human genetic variation and linkage disequilibrium patterns by the International HapMap Project, has paved the way for a more systematic and unbiased approach to gene discovery: the genome-wide association (GWA) study. By utilizing high-density microarrays to interrogate hundreds of thousands of single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) across the genome in large cohorts of cases and controls, GWA studies promise to identify novel susceptibility loci without requiring a priori knowledge of disease mechanisms.

Despite the theoretical promise of GWA studies, their practical implementation has been accompanied by significant methodological and logistical challenges. Key concerns have included the requirement for very large sample sizes to achieve adequate statistical power to detect variants with small effect sizes, the potential for confounding due to hidden population stratification, and the formidable costs and quality control issues associated with generating and analyzing millions of genotypes. Furthermore, the use of a shared control group across multiple disease phenotypes, a strategy that offers considerable efficiencies, has been debated due to the potential for misclassification bias.

To address these challenges and to comprehensively explore the genetic basis of common diseases, the Wellcome Trust Case Control Consortium (WTCCC) was established. Here, we present the results of a joint GWA study examining approximately 2,000 individuals for each of seven major diseases—bipolar disorder, coronary artery disease, Crohn's disease, hypertension, rheumatoid arthritis, type 1 diabetes, and type 2 diabetes—alongside a shared set of 3,000 controls drawn from the British population. This study represents one of the largest and most comprehensive GWA efforts to date. We demonstrate that with rigorous quality control, appropriate sample sizes, and careful consideration of population structure, GWA studies can successfully identify novel, robustly replicating susceptibility loci, offering profound new insights into the biology and genetic architecture of common human diseases.
