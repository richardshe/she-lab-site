# Introduction [324 words]

The early mouse embryo contains cells which retain a wide developmental potential. This has been shown most directly by the production of chimaeric animals after the introduction of cells from the inner cell mass or early post-implantation embryo into host blastocysts, and by the ability of such cells, when transplanted to an ectopic site, to give rise to teratocarcinomas. In the latter tumours, embryonal carcinoma (EC) cells provide a continuously growing population which can differentiate to form many somatic tissues, and which therefore offers an experimental system for studying the first steps of mammalian development in vitro.

The origin of EC cells has nevertheless placed an important limitation on their use. They are obtained after the formation of a tumour in vivo and after selection during serial passage, and may therefore have acquired genetic or epigenetic changes not present in normal embryonic cells. Many EC cell lines have abnormal karyotypes and differ in their capacity to differentiate or to contribute to normal development. It has consequently remained uncertain how far the properties of EC cells reflect those of the pluripotential cells of the embryo from which they are presumed to arise.

Attempts to grow such embryonic cells directly in culture have not previously yielded permanent, progressively growing cell lines. The inner cell mass of cultured blastocysts can proliferate for a short time and produce cell types resembling those of the early egg cylinder, but the pluripotential component has not been maintained as an undifferentiated culture. A direct method for isolating these cells would complete the experimental relationship between the embryo, EC cells and differentiated derivatives, and would provide cells of known embryonic origin for genetic and developmental studies.

We describe here the establishment of cell lines from mouse blastocysts cultured in vitro. The cells resemble feeder-dependent EC cells in morphology and growth, but are derived directly from normal embryos, have normal male or female karyotypes, and retain the ability to differentiate both in culture and as teratocarcinomas after injection into syngeneic mice.
