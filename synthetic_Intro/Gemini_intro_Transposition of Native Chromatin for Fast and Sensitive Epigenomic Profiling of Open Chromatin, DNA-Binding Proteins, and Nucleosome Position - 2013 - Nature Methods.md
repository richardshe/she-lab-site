The spatial organization and dynamic state of chromatin are fundamental determinants of gene regulation, dictating the accessibility of the genome to the transcriptional machinery. Open chromatin regions, largely depleted of nucleosomes, act as regulatory hubs that bind transcription factors (TFs) and other regulatory proteins to orchestrate cellular programs. Consequently, mapping the landscape of accessible chromatin is essential for understanding the epigenetic control of cellular identity, development, and disease. 

Existing methods for assaying chromatin accessibility, such as DNase I hypersensitive site sequencing (DNase-seq) and formaldehyde-assisted isolation of regulatory elements with sequencing (FAIRE-seq), have provided valuable genome-wide catalogs of regulatory elements. However, these techniques often require tens of millions of cells and involve complex, multi-step protocols with significant material loss, precluding their application to rare primary cell types or clinical samples. Furthermore, these methods generally assay single features of chromatin structure, requiring parallel techniques like MNase-seq or ChIP-seq to capture nucleosome positioning or specific TF binding.

To address these limitations, we have developed the Assay for Transposase-Accessible Chromatin using sequencing (ATAC-seq). This method relies on the direct in vitro transposition of sequencing adaptors into accessible chromatin regions using a hyperactive Tn5 transposase. ATAC-seq is a rapid, two-step protocol that can profile open chromatin, infer TF occupancy via footprinting, and map nucleosome positions simultaneously, requiring only 500 to 50,000 cells. Here, we validate ATAC-seq against established epigenomic methods and demonstrate its utility in defining patient-specific regulatory networks on a clinical timescale.
