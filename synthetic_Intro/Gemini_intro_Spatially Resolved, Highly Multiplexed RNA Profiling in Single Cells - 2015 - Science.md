The cellular transcriptome is a highly dynamic and spatially organized network of thousands of different RNA species, the precise regulation of which dictates cellular behavior, state, and identity. Characterizing the expression profile of individual cells is essential for understanding the vast heterogeneity of cell populations within tissues and the complex regulatory circuits that govern them. Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) has recently emerged as a powerful tool for profiling the transcriptomes of individual cells, providing unprecedented insights into cellular diversity, developmental trajectories, and gene regulatory networks. However, scRNA-seq inherently requires the dissociation of cells from their native environment and the extraction of RNA, which results in the complete loss of spatial information regarding RNA localization. 

The spatial organization of RNA within a cell is biologically significant. Subcellular localization of messenger RNAs (mRNAs) is a widespread mechanism used to target the translation of specific proteins to distinct subcellular compartments, thereby facilitating asymmetric cell division, cell migration, and synaptic plasticity. Similarly, the spatial distribution of non-coding RNAs is critical for their regulatory functions. Thus, a comprehensive understanding of the transcriptome requires not only knowing the abundance of each RNA species but also its precise spatial distribution within the native cellular context. 

Single-molecule fluorescence in situ hybridization (smFISH) provides an excellent method for determining both the copy number and the spatial localization of specific RNA species within individual intact cells. By using multiple fluorescently labeled oligonucleotide probes that bind to the same target RNA, smFISH enables the reliable detection of individual transcript molecules as bright, diffraction-limited spots. Despite its high sensitivity and quantitative nature, conventional smFISH is limited in its multiplexing capacity. Because it relies on spectral separation, typically only three to four distinct RNA species can be simultaneously imaged in a single cell. To capture a more comprehensive picture of the cellular transcriptome, it is necessary to drastically scale up the multiplexing throughput of smFISH to the hundreds or thousands of genes.

Several sequential hybridization and barcoding approaches have been developed to increase the multiplexing capabilities of FISH. However, scaling these approaches to simultaneously image thousands of distinct RNA species faces a critical challenge: as the number of sequential measurement rounds increases, the accumulation of single-molecule labeling and detection errors severely degrades the accuracy of RNA identification, leading to a high rate of misidentification.

Here, we present multiplexed error-robust fluorescence in situ hybridization (MERFISH), a highly multiplexed single-molecule imaging technology that overcomes these limitations. MERFISH employs combinatorial labeling and sequential imaging coupled with error-robust encoding schemes to accurately identify and spatially map thousands of different RNA species within individual fixed cells. By utilizing error-correcting codes, such as a modified Hamming distance 4 code, MERFISH detects and corrects single-molecule identification errors, ensuring high detection efficiency and a low misidentification rate. We demonstrate the capability of MERFISH to simultaneously image hundreds to over a thousand RNA species in hundreds of single human fibroblast cells. Using the vast amount of spatially resolved, single-cell expression data generated, we identify novel spatial distributions of RNAs, analyze intrinsic cell-to-cell variability in gene expression, and use expression co-variation to uncover regulatory networks and predict the functions of previously unannotated genes.
