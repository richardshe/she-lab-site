# Introduction

The ability to make precise changes at specified genomic sites is a long-standing goal of molecular biology and medicine. CRISPR-Cas nucleases have transformed genome editing by enabling efficient targeted DNA cleavage, but most precise sequence replacement still depends on cellular repair of double-strand breaks (DSBs). DSBs are efficiently resolved by non-homologous end joining, which is useful for gene disruption but often produces heterogeneous insertions and deletions. Installing defined sequence changes by homology-directed repair (HDR) requires an exogenous donor DNA template, competes poorly with end joining in many cell types, and is especially inefficient in non-dividing or terminally differentiated cells. Moreover, DSBs can generate unwanted byproducts including indels, large deletions, translocations, and other genome alterations that complicate biological interpretation and therapeutic development.

Several genome editing approaches have reduced the need for DSBs. Base editors, which combine catalytically impaired Cas proteins with deaminases, can install C•G-to-T•A or A•T-to-G•C transitions efficiently and with few indels. These methods have expanded the range of precise editing, but their chemistry restricts the kinds of mutations that can be made. Base editing cannot directly install transversions, small insertions, or small deletions, and editing outcomes can be limited by bystander bases within the activity window or by the absence of a protospacer adjacent motif (PAM) that appropriately positions the target nucleotide. Many pathogenic variants therefore remain difficult to correct without either making a DSB or relying on inefficient donor-dependent repair.

We sought to develop a genome editing strategy that could directly write new genetic information into a target locus without requiring DSBs or donor DNA templates. We reasoned that a Cas9 nickase could expose a 3′ hydroxyl group at a programmed genomic site, and that this DNA end might be used to prime reverse transcription from an RNA template covalently linked to the guide RNA. Such a guide RNA could both specify the genomic target through its spacer and encode the desired sequence change through an additional reverse transcription template. The resulting edited DNA strand would create a heteroduplex intermediate, whose resolution by endogenous DNA repair could permanently install the edit. We further hypothesized that nicking the non-edited strand could bias repair toward copying the edited strand, analogous to strategies used to improve base editing outcomes.

Here we describe prime editing, a versatile search-and-replace genome editing method that uses a Cas9 H840A nickase fused to an engineered reverse transcriptase and a prime editing guide RNA (pegRNA) that specifies both the target site and the desired edit. We develop prime editors through biochemical validation, yeast reporter assays, mammalian cell optimization, reverse transcriptase engineering, pegRNA design, and non-edited-strand nicking strategies. We show that prime editing can install all 12 possible base-to-base substitutions, targeted insertions, targeted deletions, and combinations of these changes in human cells. We apply prime editing to install or correct disease-relevant alleles including mutations in HBB, HEXA, and PRNP, and demonstrate activity in multiple human cell lines and primary post-mitotic mouse cortical neurons. Comparisons with base editing and Cas9-initiated HDR show that prime editing provides complementary capabilities, broader editing scope, and fewer unwanted byproducts in many settings. These results establish prime editing as a broadly programmable genome editing approach that substantially expands the range of precise sequence changes accessible in living cells.
