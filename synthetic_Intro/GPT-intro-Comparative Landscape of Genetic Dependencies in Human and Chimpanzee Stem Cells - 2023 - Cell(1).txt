# Introduction

Humans and chimpanzees differ in anatomy, cognition, life history, and disease susceptibility, yet their genomes are highly similar. This paradox has motivated decades of effort to identify the molecular changes that accompanied human evolution. Comparative genomics has nominated human-specific coding substitutions, accelerated noncoding elements, structural variants, and changes in gene expression or chromatin state. These studies have provided a rich catalogue of candidate loci. However, distinguishing the subset of changes that alter cellular function from the much larger background of neutral divergence remains a central challenge. Most comparative measurements are descriptive: they reveal that a gene or regulatory element differs between species, but do not establish whether that difference affects a cellular phenotype, acts within a pathway, or contributes to organismal traits.

Functional genetics offers a complementary strategy. Rather than beginning with molecular differences and asking which are important, loss-of-function perturbations begin with cellular behavior and ask which genes are required to maintain it. In model organisms, genetic screens have defined the pathways that control development, growth, and survival. In human cells, pooled CRISPR screens have similarly revealed that essentiality is not an intrinsic property of a gene, but a context-dependent feature of the surrounding cellular network. Genes that are dispensable in one genetic or environmental state can become essential in another, exposing latent dependencies that are not obvious from baseline expression or sequence alone. This principle has been exploited extensively in cancer biology, where comparisons across cell lines have uncovered vulnerabilities produced by oncogenic mutations, lineage state, and pathway rewiring.

Whether evolutionary divergence between closely related species has similarly reshaped genetic dependencies is largely unknown. Humans and chimpanzees diverged recently enough that most genes, pathways, and developmental programs are deeply conserved, but long enough ago for regulatory and cellular differences to accumulate. Comparative studies of primate pluripotent stem cells (PSCs), neural progenitor cells, and cerebral organoids have shown broad conservation of cell identity and differentiation trajectories, alongside species differences in gene regulation and developmental timing. These systems therefore provide an opportunity to ask a more direct question: have recent evolutionary changes altered the genes and pathways that human and chimpanzee cells require for proliferation, survival, or differentiation?

Several features make PSCs an attractive starting point for such an analysis. PSCs can be derived and propagated from multiple individuals of each species under controlled culture conditions, reducing the confounding effects of tissue availability and environmental history. They can be engineered with CRISPR interference (CRISPRi) machinery, enabling scalable and reversible repression of endogenous genes without inducing DNA double-strand breaks. They also retain the ability to differentiate into developmentally relevant cell types, allowing discoveries made in the pluripotent state to be tested in neural progenitors and organoids. At the same time, PSCs are rapidly dividing cells with specialized cell cycle properties, making them well suited for detecting perturbations that affect proliferation and self-renewal.

Cell cycle regulation is of particular interest in the context of human evolution. Expansion of the human cerebral cortex is thought to have depended in part on changes in the behavior of neural progenitor cells, including the balance between proliferative self-renewal and neurogenic differentiation. One influential model proposes that the length of G1 phase influences this balance: shorter G1 phases favor continued progenitor divisions, whereas longer G1 phases increase the probability of neuronal differentiation. Experimental manipulation of cyclins and cyclin-dependent kinases in animal models can alter progenitor proliferation and cortical size, supporting the plausibility of this mechanism. However, whether the molecular architecture of G1/S control changed specifically in the human lineage has remained unclear. Standard comparative transcriptomic or epigenomic approaches can identify species differences in the expression of cell cycle regulators, but they cannot determine how robustly human and chimpanzee cells tolerate perturbation of those regulators.

A systematic functional comparison must also contend with several sources of variation that can masquerade as species differences. Individual genetic background, culture adaptation, reprogramming history, karyotypic abnormalities, and differences in p53 pathway status can all influence the outcome of proliferation-based screens. In addition, CRISPR reagents designed for the human genome may not target orthologous sequences equally well in nonhuman primates. Finally, inferring whether a trait is human-derived or chimpanzee-derived requires comparison to an outgroup species, because a difference between two species alone does not reveal which lineage changed. These considerations argue for an experimental framework that uses multiple independently derived cell lines per species, perturbation reagents matched across genomes, validation of candidate effects, and phylogenetic comparison beyond the human-chimpanzee pair.

Here, we developed a comparative CRISPRi screening platform to map genetic dependencies in human and chimpanzee PSCs. We first performed genome-wide loss-of-function screens to identify genes whose repression differentially affected proliferation in the two species. We then constructed a targeted validation library with sgRNAs matched between human and chimpanzee genomes and screened an expanded panel of p53-responsive PSC lines from multiple individuals. This approach identified a stringent set of 75 genes with species-specific effects on cellular proliferation. These genes were organized into coherent biological processes and protein complexes, including lysosomal V-ATPase-associated signaling, UFMylation, chromatin regulation, and cell cycle control, indicating that recent evolutionary divergence altered functional pathways rather than isolated genes.

We further investigated two classes of species-specific dependencies. Human PSCs displayed increased sensitivity to depletion of ATP6AP1, linking species differences in growth to altered reliance on lysosome-associated mTORC1 signaling. Conversely, human PSCs were unexpectedly robust to depletion of the G1/S regulators CDK2, CDK4, and Cyclin E1, whereas chimpanzee PSCs required these factors for normal proliferation. Mechanistic experiments indicated that elevated CDK1 activity could compensate for loss of Cyclin E-CDK2 activity in chimpanzee cells, suggesting that altered network architecture can buffer human cells against perturbations to canonical G1/S regulators. Importantly, human-specific robustness to CDK2 and Cyclin E1 depletion persisted in neural progenitor cells and cerebral organoids, connecting species-specific genetic dependencies in stem cells to a developmental context relevant to cortical expansion.

Finally, by extending selected perturbations to orangutan PSCs, we inferred the phylogenetic origin of several dependencies. Human-specific robustness to perturbations of G1/S regulators was not shared by chimpanzee or orangutan cells, supporting the inference that this property evolved along the human lineage. Together, these results establish comparative loss-of-function screening as a strategy for discovering cellular and molecular changes that emerged during recent primate evolution. More broadly, they show that closely related species can differ not only in the expression or sequence of genes, but in the functional dependencies that reveal how conserved pathways have been rewired.
