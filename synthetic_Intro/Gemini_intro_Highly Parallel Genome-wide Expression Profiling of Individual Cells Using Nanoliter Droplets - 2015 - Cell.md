# Introduction
Tissues are complex ecosystems composed of numerous, diverse cell types that interact to perform physiological functions. Traditional methods for profiling gene expression, such as bulk RNA sequencing, average the transcriptomes of thousands to millions of cells, masking the profound cellular heterogeneity that exists within even the most seemingly uniform tissues. To truly understand tissue architecture, developmental trajectories, and the specific cellular responses to disease or perturbation, it is necessary to resolve gene expression at the fundamental unit of biology: the single cell. 

Single-cell RNA sequencing (scRNA-seq) has emerged as a transformative technology for dissecting cellular diversity, enabling the discovery of rare cell populations, the mapping of continuous developmental states, and the identification of novel gene regulatory networks. However, the broad application of early scRNA-seq methods has been significantly hindered by limitations in throughput, cost, and ease of use. Conventional approaches typically require the physical isolation of individual cells into separate wells of a microtiter plate—often using fluorescence-activated cell sorting (FACS) or manual picking—followed by independent, labor-intensive library preparation reactions for each cell. Even with the advent of integrated microfluidic circuits, processing more than a few hundred cells remains a formidable and expensive undertaking. 

To realize the full potential of single-cell genomics, a technology is needed that can rapidly, efficiently, and inexpensively profile thousands or tens of thousands of individual cells in a single experiment. Here, we introduce Drop-seq, a highly scalable, droplet-based microfluidic approach that addresses this bottleneck. Drop-seq isolates individual cells into nanoliter-sized aqueous droplets along with specially designed, barcoded microparticles. By capturing and tagging the mRNA from each cell with a unique barcode before pooling all transcripts for a single, massive parallel sequencing reaction, Drop-seq can profile thousands of cells simultaneously. We demonstrate the power and accuracy of Drop-seq by generating a comprehensive molecular atlas of the mouse retina, analyzing the transcriptomes of nearly 45,000 individual cells and identifying 39 distinct cell populations, thereby proving its capability to systematically deconstruct complex mammalian tissues.
