# Introduction
The physiological mechanisms that tightly balance food intake and energy expenditure are fundamental in determining whether an individual will be obese or lean. Disruption of this energy homeostasis leads to obesity, a condition associated with significant morbidity and mortality, including an increased risk for type II diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and hypertension. While the regulation of body weight is influenced by a complex interplay of genetic, environmental, and behavioral factors, evidence from human and animal studies strongly suggests a major genetic component. In the mouse, one of the key genetic determinants regulating energy balance is the obese (ob) gene. The recessive mutation of the ob locus results in a phenotype of profound, early-onset obesity, hyperphagia, decreased energy expenditure, and type II diabetes, closely resembling the severe, morbid obesity syndrome seen in humans. 

Classical physiological experiments, including parabiosis studies in rodents, have suggested that the regulation of energy balance involves a feedback loop between the central nervous system and peripheral tissues. According to the lipostasis theory, a circulating factor produced by adipose tissue acts on the hypothalamus to regulate food intake and modulate energy expenditure, effectively signaling the size of the body fat depot to the brain. The profound phenotypic defects observed in ob/ob mice led to the hypothesis that the ob gene encodes this elusive satiety factor, or a critical component of its signaling pathway. Despite extensive study of the physiological and metabolic abnormalities in ob/ob mice, the biochemical nature of the ob gene product and its exact molecular function have remained unknown. 

To elucidate the molecular basis of this obesity syndrome, we undertook a positional cloning strategy to isolate the ob gene. In this paper, we describe the high-resolution genetic and physical mapping of the ob locus on mouse chromosome 6. Through the construction of yeast artificial chromosome (YAC) and P1 bacteriophage contigs, followed by exon trapping, we successfully identified the ob gene. We demonstrate that the ob gene is expressed exclusively in white adipose tissue and encodes a highly conserved secreted protein. Furthermore, we identify the specific mutations responsible for the obese phenotype in two independent mutant strains, establishing that this secreted molecule is indeed the product of the ob gene.
