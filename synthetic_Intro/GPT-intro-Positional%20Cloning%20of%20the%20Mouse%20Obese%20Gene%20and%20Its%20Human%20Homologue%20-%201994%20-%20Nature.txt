# Introduction

The regulation of body weight in mammals requires that food intake and energy expenditure be matched over long periods of time. Although transient changes in feeding behaviour and metabolic rate can produce large fluctuations in energy balance, the size of the adipose depot is normally maintained within relatively narrow limits. This observation has led to the proposal that body fat is subject to physiological feedback control, but the molecular nature of the signals that communicate the state of peripheral energy stores to the central nervous system has remained unknown.

Several inherited forms of obesity in the mouse provide genetic models for identifying such signals. Among these, mutations at the obese (ob) locus produce a recessive syndrome of severe obesity, hyperphagia, decreased energy expenditure, insulin resistance and diabetes. The phenotype is evident early in life and is accompanied by numerous endocrine and metabolic abnormalities, including defects in thermoregulation and fertility. These pleiotropic features have suggested that ob acts at an early point in the pathways that regulate energy balance, rather than representing a secondary consequence of increased adiposity alone.

Physiological studies have further indicated that the product of ob may participate in a humoral signalling pathway. Parabiosis experiments, in which the circulation of obese and lean animals was joined, suggested that some forms of obesity result from the absence of a circulating satiety factor, whereas others reflect an inability to respond to such a factor. In particular, the relationship between the ob and diabetes (db) mutations raised the possibility that ob encodes a blood-borne signal and that db encodes a component required for its response. Direct biochemical identification of this putative factor, however, has not been possible.

A molecular approach to the ob locus therefore offers a means of testing the lipostatic model of body-weight regulation. The ob gene had previously been mapped to proximal mouse chromosome 6, close to the molecular markers Pax4 and D6Rck13. Here we describe the positional cloning of ob, the identification of mutations in two independent obese mouse strains, and the cloning of the homologous human gene. The predicted protein has the properties of a secreted polypeptide and is expressed predominantly in adipose tissue, suggesting that adipocytes themselves may produce a conserved signal that regulates the size of the fat depot.
