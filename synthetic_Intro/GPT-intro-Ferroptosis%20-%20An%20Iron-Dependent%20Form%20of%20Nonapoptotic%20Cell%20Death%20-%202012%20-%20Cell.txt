# Introduction [436 words]

The regulated elimination of cells is essential for development, tissue homeostasis, and the response to injury. Apoptosis has served as the principal model for genetically controlled cell death, and much is known about the caspase-dependent machinery that executes this process. Yet many cells die in ways that do not exhibit the canonical morphological or biochemical features of apoptosis. Such nonapoptotic death processes have been implicated in degenerative disease, ischemic injury, and tumor suppression, but in most cases the molecular events that initiate and execute these forms of death remain poorly understood.

The possibility that cancer cells might be selectively vulnerable to nonapoptotic death is of particular interest. Many tumors acquire mutations that disable apoptotic pathways, thereby limiting the efficacy of therapies that rely on the intact engagement of this machinery. We previously identified small molecules, termed RAS-selective lethal (RSL) compounds, that kill engineered tumor cells expressing oncogenic RAS while sparing matched isogenic cells lacking this oncogene. One such compound, erastin, induces a form of death that is not blocked by caspase inhibition and is associated with the accumulation of reactive oxygen species. These observations suggested that RSL compounds may reveal a regulated lethal mechanism distinct from apoptosis and exploitable in apoptosis-resistant cancer cells.

Oxidative stress has long been associated with cell death, but the specific chemical and genetic pathways that convert reactive oxygen species into lethal signals remain obscure. Iron is a particularly plausible mediator of oxidative damage because it can participate in redox reactions and is frequently dysregulated in cancer and in pathological states of the nervous system. Notably, several forms of glutamate-induced neurotoxicity are oxidative and iron dependent. In these settings, excess glutamate can inhibit cystine uptake through the cystine/glutamate antiporter system xc-, depleting cysteine-derived antioxidant capacity and sensitizing cells to oxidative damage. Whether such neurotoxic processes are mechanistically related to the death induced by RSL compounds has not been established.

Here, we define a form of regulated, iron-dependent, nonapoptotic cell death that we term ferroptosis. We show that erastin triggers the accumulation of lethal lipid reactive oxygen species, that this process is morphologically, biochemically, and genetically distinct from apoptosis, necrosis, and autophagy, and that it depends on a discrete set of genes linked to iron regulation, metabolism, and lipid biology. We identify ferrostatin-1 as a potent inhibitor of this process and show that it suppresses both erastin-induced death in cancer cells and glutamate-induced death in organotypic brain slices. Finally, we demonstrate that erastin inhibits system xc--mediated cystine uptake, connecting RSL-induced cancer cell death to an oxidative mechanism previously implicated in neurodegeneration.
