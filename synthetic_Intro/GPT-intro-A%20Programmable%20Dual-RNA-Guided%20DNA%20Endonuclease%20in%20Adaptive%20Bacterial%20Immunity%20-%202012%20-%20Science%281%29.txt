# Introduction

Bacteria and archaea have evolved diverse defense systems that limit infection by viruses and other mobile genetic elements. Among these, clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR) and CRISPR-associated (Cas) proteins constitute an adaptive immune pathway in which short fragments of foreign DNA are integrated into CRISPR loci and later used as sequence-specific guides to silence matching invaders. CRISPR loci are transcribed and processed into small CRISPR RNAs (crRNAs), each containing a guide sequence derived from a previously encountered element. Together with Cas proteins, these RNAs direct recognition and destruction of complementary nucleic acids, thereby providing heritable resistance to reinfection.

CRISPR/Cas systems are broadly distributed and mechanistically diverse. In Type I and Type III systems, crRNAs function within multisubunit protein complexes that recognize foreign nucleic acids and recruit or activate nuclease activities. By contrast, Type II systems are genetically more compact and are defined by the presence of Cas9, a large multidomain protein containing predicted HNH and RuvC-like nuclease domains. Genetic studies have implicated Cas9 in both crRNA biogenesis and DNA interference, and have shown that Type II immunity requires a short protospacer-adjacent motif (PAM) in the target DNA. However, the molecular basis for target recognition and cleavage in Type II systems has remained unclear.

A distinctive feature of some Type II CRISPR/Cas systems is the requirement for a trans-activating CRISPR RNA (tracrRNA), a small noncoding RNA that base pairs with repeat sequences in the pre-crRNA transcript and promotes RNase III-dependent crRNA maturation. Whether tracrRNA functions only during crRNA processing or also participates directly in target DNA interference has not been established. Similarly, although Cas9 is required for interference in cells, it has not been determined whether Cas9 itself is the crRNA-guided DNA endonuclease, how its two nuclease domains contribute to cleavage, or what RNA and DNA features are sufficient to program its activity.

Here we reconstitute the Streptococcus pyogenes Type II CRISPR/Cas system in vitro and show that Cas9 is a dual-RNA-guided DNA endonuclease. We find that mature crRNA and tracrRNA together direct Cas9 to cleave double-stranded DNA at sites specified by crRNA complementarity and an adjacent PAM. We define the strand-specific contributions of the HNH and RuvC-like domains, identify RNA and target-sequence requirements for cleavage, and show that the two natural RNAs can be engineered into a single chimeric guide RNA. These results reveal the mechanism of Type II CRISPR DNA interference and establish Cas9 as an RNA-programmable enzyme for site-specific DNA cleavage.
