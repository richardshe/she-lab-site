Breast cancer is one of the most common and devastating malignancies affecting women, with a lifetime risk approaching one in nine in developed countries. While the majority of breast cancer cases are thought to be sporadic, arising from a combination of environmental and somatic genetic factors, approximately 5 to 10 percent of cases are attributable to strongly penetrant, dominantly inherited susceptibility genes. Families carrying these genetic predispositions frequently exhibit a high incidence of early-onset breast cancer, and often, an associated increase in the risk of ovarian cancer. 

In 1990, genetic linkage analysis of numerous large kindreds segregating early-onset breast cancer provided the first compelling evidence for the existence of a major breast cancer susceptibility locus, designated BRCA1. This groundbreaking work mapped the BRCA1 gene to the long arm of chromosome 17 (17q21). Subsequent genetic studies refined this localization and established that mutations in the BRCA1 locus are responsible for disease in approximately 45 percent of families with multiple cases of breast cancer only, and in an overwhelming majority (greater than 80 percent) of families with a history of both breast and ovarian cancer. The identification of BRCA1 is therefore of immense clinical and biological importance, promising to elucidate the fundamental mechanisms of breast and ovarian tumorigenesis and to provide a means for the early detection and clinical management of individuals at high genetic risk.

Despite intense and highly competitive positional cloning efforts over the past four years, the isolation of the BRCA1 gene has proved exceptionally challenging. The defined genetic interval on chromosome 17q21 was remarkably gene-rich, and the lack of visible chromosomal rearrangements or clearly defined large deletions in patients complicated the search. To isolate the responsible gene, we initiated a systematic approach encompassing the construction of a high-resolution physical map, the exhaustive isolation of candidate transcribed sequences, and the rigorous screening of these candidates for disease-segregating mutations in affected families.

Here, we report the successful identification and characterization of a strong candidate for the BRCA1 gene. Through the integration of genetic and physical mapping techniques, we isolated a large transcription unit from the minimal BRCA1 interval that is expressed in breast and ovarian tissues. By screening DNA from members of eight independent kindreds linked to chromosome 17q, we identified five distinct, potentially disease-causing mutations within this gene. These sequence alterations—including frameshifts, a nonsense mutation, a missense mutation, and a presumptive regulatory mutation—co-segregate precisely with the cancer susceptibility trait. Furthermore, we characterize the genomic structure of the candidate gene and present its full-length complementary DNA sequence, revealing a novel protein containing a structurally conserved zinc finger domain. These findings strongly support the conclusion that we have isolated the long-sought BRCA1 gene.
