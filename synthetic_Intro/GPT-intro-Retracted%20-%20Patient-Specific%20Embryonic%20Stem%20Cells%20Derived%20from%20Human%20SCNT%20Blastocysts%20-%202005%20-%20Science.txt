# Introduction [345 words]
Human embryonic stem cells (hESCs), because of their capacities for prolonged self-renewal and differentiation into derivatives of all three embryonic germ layers, provide an experimental system for studies of human development and a possible cellular source for regenerative medicine. However, the clinical application of hESCs is constrained by immunological incompatibility between donor-derived cells and recipient patients. Even if reliable differentiation into therapeutically useful cell types is achieved, transplantation of genetically unrelated hESC derivatives would be expected to require histocompatibility matching or systemic immunosuppression, as in organ transplantation.

Somatic cell nuclear transfer (SCNT) offers one strategy for deriving patient-specific hESCs. In this approach, the nucleus of a somatic cell from a patient is transferred into an enucleated oocyte, and embryonic stem cells are derived from the resulting nuclear transfer (NT) blastocyst. Such NT-hESCs would contain the nuclear genome, and therefore the major histocompatibility complex, of the patient. In principle, these cells could provide immune-matched material for transplantation after directed differentiation. They could also provide genetically defined, disease-specific human cells for studies of pathogenesis, drug discovery, and developmental mechanisms in vitro.

Previous work in animals has shown that nuclear transfer can reprogram differentiated somatic nuclei and support embryonic development, and mouse studies have suggested that NT-derived embryonic stem cells can be used in disease models after genetic correction or lineage-specific differentiation. Human application, however, requires evidence that patient nuclei of different ages, sexes, and disease states can support NT blastocyst formation and hESC derivation. It also requires rigorous demonstration that resulting cell lines are pluripotent, chromosomally normal, genetically matched to the somatic donor, and compatible at the major histocompatibility complex loci relevant to transplantation.

Here we report the derivation of patient-specific NT-hESC lines from individuals with disease or injury, including spinal cord injury, juvenile diabetes, and congenital hypogammaglobulinemia. We evaluate the efficiency of NT blastocyst formation and hESC establishment, characterize pluripotency and chromosomal normality, confirm genetic identity between each NT-hESC line and its corresponding patient donor, and assess human leukocyte antigen matching. These findings provide a basis for evaluating the genetic, epigenetic, and immunological properties of patient-specific NT-hESCs and define remaining requirements for preclinical development before any consideration of clinical use.
