# Introduction
The regulated elimination of cells is essential for the development and maintenance of multicellular organisms, and its dysregulation is a hallmark of numerous human diseases. Apoptosis, the most extensively characterized form of programmed cell death, is driven by a cascade of caspase proteases and is critical for normal tissue turnover and the removal of damaged cells. The execution of apoptosis requires a specific genetic program, including the activation of proapoptotic Bcl-2 family members such as Bax and Bak. In cancer, the evasion of apoptosis is a fundamental mechanism of tumor progression and a major cause of resistance to conventional chemotherapeutics. Consequently, there is intense interest in identifying alternative, nonapoptotic pathways of cell death that could be exploited to selectively eradicate apoptosis-resistant cancer cells.

In addition to apoptosis, several other forms of regulated cell death have been described, including various forms of necrosis (such as necroptosis) and autophagic cell death. These processes are morphologically and biochemically distinct from apoptosis and are mediated by different molecular machineries. Recent high-throughput screening efforts have identified small molecules capable of inducing nonapoptotic death in specific genetic contexts. We previously discovered a structurally diverse class of lethal compounds, collectively termed RAS-selective lethal (RSL) compounds, which include erastin and RSL3. These molecules were initially identified for their ability to selectively kill human tumor cells harboring oncogenic mutations in the HRAS, KRAS, or NRAS genes. Subsequent studies revealed that RSL-induced cell death does not exhibit the classic features of apoptosis, such as caspase activation or chromatin condensation, nor does it require Bax or Bak.

Instead, RSL-induced cell death is characterized by its dependence on intracellular iron and can be completely suppressed by iron chelators. Furthermore, this form of death is associated with the accumulation of reactive oxygen species (ROS), suggesting an oxidative mechanism. However, the precise nature of this death pathway, its relationship to other known forms of cell death, and its broader physiological and pathological relevance remain poorly defined. In this study, we demonstrate that erastin triggers a unique, iron-dependent form of nonapoptotic cell death that we have termed "ferroptosis." We show that ferroptosis is morphologically, biochemically, and genetically distinct from apoptosis, necrosis, and autophagy. Through systematic modulatory profiling and genetic screening, we uncover the regulatory network governing ferroptosis and identify a potent small-molecule inhibitor of this process, ferrostatin-1. Our findings indicate that ferroptosis is caused by the lethal accumulation of lipid-based ROS resulting from the inhibition of cystine uptake and that this pathway may also underlie the excitotoxic cell death observed in certain neurodegenerative conditions.
