The immune system is capable of recognizing and eliminating tumor cells; however, tumors often exploit immune checkpoint pathways to evade immune surveillance and destruction. One such pathway is mediated by the programmed death 1 (PD-1) receptor, an inhibitory molecule expressed on activated T cells, B cells, and natural killer cells. When PD-1 engages its ligands, PD-L1 (B7-H1) or PD-L2 (B7-DC), which are frequently upregulated on the surface of various solid tumors, it transduces inhibitory signals that suppress T-cell proliferation, cytokine production, and cytolytic activity. This mechanism of adaptive immune resistance effectively blunts the antitumor immune response, allowing cancer cells to proliferate unchecked.

Preclinical models have demonstrated that blockade of the PD-1–PD-L1 interaction can reinvigorate exhausted T cells and restore their antitumor activity, leading to durable tumor regression. Based on these compelling biological rationales, monoclonal antibodies targeting PD-1 or its ligands have entered clinical development. BMS-936558 is a fully human IgG4 monoclonal antibody that specifically binds to PD-1, thereby preventing its interaction with both PD-L1 and PD-L2. 

We conducted a phase 1 dose-escalation and expansion study to evaluate the safety, pharmacokinetics, and clinical activity of BMS-936558 in patients with selected advanced solid tumors. In addition to assessing the tolerability and objective response rates across different tumor types and dose levels, we investigated the potential role of PD-L1 expression on tumor cells as a predictive biomarker for clinical response to PD-1 blockade therapy.
