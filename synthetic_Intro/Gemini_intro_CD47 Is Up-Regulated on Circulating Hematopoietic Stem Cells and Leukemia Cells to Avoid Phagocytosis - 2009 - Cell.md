The clearance of senescent, damaged, or apoptotic cells is primarily orchestrated by the innate immune system, specifically through the action of professional phagocytes such as macrophages. To navigate this highly vigilant environment and prevent the destruction of healthy tissues, cells must display markers that explicitly instruct macrophages to refrain from engulfment. These markers function as "don't eat me" signals, counterbalancing the "eat me" signals frequently exposed on dying cells.

CD47, also known as integrin-associated protein (IAP), is a transmembrane glycoprotein that serves as a critical "don't eat me" signal. It binds to its cognate receptor, signal regulatory protein alpha (SIRPα), which is heavily expressed on macrophages and dendritic cells. The engagement of SIRPα by CD47 initiates a signaling cascade that actively suppresses the cytoskeletal rearrangements required for phagocytosis, protecting the CD47-bearing cell from being engulfed. The physiological importance of this interaction is illustrated by the rapid clearance of CD47-deficient red blood cells and platelets by splenic macrophages when transfused into wild-type hosts.

While the role of CD47 in protecting circulating erythrocytes is well established, its function in regulating the survival of other migrating cell populations remains largely unexplored. Hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells (HSPCs) primarily reside within the protective niches of the bone marrow. However, under conditions of physiological stress, infection, or the administration of mobilizing cytokines, HSPCs exit the marrow and migrate into the peripheral blood, eventually seeding extramedullary sites such as the spleen and liver. During this migratory phase, HSPCs must pass through macrophage-rich sinusoidal networks, making them highly vulnerable to phagocytic clearance.

In this study, we investigated the mechanisms by which migrating HSPCs evade innate immune surveillance. We demonstrate that CD47 expression is transiently and robustly upregulated on both murine and human HSPCs during cytokine- or inflammation-induced mobilization, acting as an essential survival mechanism against macrophage-mediated phagocytosis. Strikingly, we discovered that this physiological response is hijacked by neoplastic cells. CD47 is constitutively overexpressed on a wide range of murine and human myeloid leukemias, conferring a critical survival advantage to leukemic progenitors by allowing them to evade immune clearance. Furthermore, overexpression of CD47 in a human leukemia cell line dramatically increases its pathogenicity in vivo, revealing CD47 upregulation as a fundamental mechanism of immune evasion in cancer.
