# Introduction
The ability to perturb gene expression on a genome-wide scale is a foundational tool for uncovering gene function and identifying therapeutic targets in human cells. For the past decade, RNA interference (RNAi), primarily through short hairpin RNA (shRNA) libraries, has been the standard method for such large-scale functional screens. However, RNAi suffers from inherent limitations, including incomplete silencing of target genes and substantial off-target effects, which can confound the interpretation of screening results and obscure the identification of essential genes or novel drug resistance mechanisms.

The clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats (CRISPR)-associated endonuclease Cas9 has recently emerged as a powerful technology for targeted genome editing. By simply altering the sequence of a short guide RNA (sgRNA), Cas9 can be programmed to induce double-strand breaks at specific genomic loci, generating loss-of-function mutations through error-prone non-homologous end joining. We hypothesized that the high efficiency and specificity of Cas9-mediated editing could be harnessed to conduct genome-wide knockout screens in human cells. Here, we present the development and application of a genome-scale CRISPR-Cas9 knockout (GeCKO) library, demonstrating its utility for identifying both essential genes and mechanisms of drug resistance.
