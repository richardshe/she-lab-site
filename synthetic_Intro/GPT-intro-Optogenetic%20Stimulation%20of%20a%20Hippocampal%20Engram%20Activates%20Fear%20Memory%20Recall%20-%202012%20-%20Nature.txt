# Introduction [238 words]

A central question in neuroscience is how a specific experience is represented in the brain. Since the early proposal of a memory engram, it has been hypothesized that learning induces stable physical changes in a restricted population of neurons, and that subsequent reactivation of this population gives rise to memory recall. Recent studies have begun to identify neuronal ensembles that are recruited during learning. In fear conditioning, sparse populations of neurons in the amygdala or hippocampus can be marked by activity-dependent gene expression, and manipulation of these populations has shown that they are important for memory expression. Ablation or inhibition of neurons activated during training impairs later recall, supporting the idea that such cells are necessary components of a memory trace.

However, necessity does not establish sufficiency. It remains unknown whether direct reactivation of neurons that were naturally active during learning can elicit the behavioural output of the corresponding memory in the absence of the original sensory cues. This question is particularly relevant for contextual fear memory, in which the hippocampus is thought to encode a representation of the environment that becomes associated with an aversive event. Here we combine activity-dependent genetic labelling with optogenetic stimulation to test whether dentate gyrus neurons activated during fear conditioning are sufficient to drive memory recall. By selectively reactivating these cells in a neutral context, we examine whether a defined hippocampal ensemble can function as a cellular substrate of a specific memory.
