The evolutionary trajectory that led to the emergence of modern humans involved profound anatomical, cognitive, and behavioral changes, distinguishing our species from our closest living relatives, the great apes. Understanding the biological basis of these human-specific traits is one of the most compelling challenges in evolutionary biology and genetics. While comparative genomics has successfully identified millions of genetic differences—including single nucleotide variants, structural variants, and gene duplications—that distinguish humans from chimpanzees and other primates, translating this vast catalog of genetic variation into functional understanding remains incredibly difficult. The vast majority of these genetic changes reside in non-coding regions and are presumed to be largely neutral. Even among those changes that alter protein sequence or gene expression, determining which specific molecular alterations drove evolutionary adaptations, rather than merely reflecting genetic drift, poses a significant hurdle.

Historically, efforts to bridge the gap between genotype and phenotype in human evolution have relied heavily on comparative transcriptomics and epigenomics. By profiling gene expression and chromatin accessibility across various tissues and developmental stages in humans, chimpanzees, and macaques, researchers have identified thousands of genes with species-specific expression patterns. These studies have provided valuable insights into the regulatory networks that have been rewired during primate evolution, particularly in tissues like the brain. However, gene expression profiling inherently captures both direct regulatory changes and widespread indirect downstream effects, making it difficult to pinpoint the core driver genes within complex networks. More importantly, differential expression does not necessarily equate to differential functional importance. A gene might be expressed at identical levels in human and chimpanzee cells, yet the cells might rely on that gene to entirely different degrees due to rewiring in interacting pathways or compensatory mechanisms. Thus, purely observational approaches cannot directly evaluate the functional consequence of losing a gene's activity in one species versus another.

In model organisms such as yeast, flies, and mice, loss-of-function genetic screens have long served as the gold standard for defining the networks of genes essential for cellular survival, proliferation, and differentiation. The recent advent of CRISPR-Cas9 technology, and specifically CRISPR interference (CRISPRi), has revolutionized our ability to perform highly specific, scalable, genome-wide loss-of-function screens in mammalian cells. By precisely repressing transcription at targeted loci, CRISPRi allows for the systematic cataloging of genetic dependencies—the specific genes that a cell type absolutely requires for its growth and survival. Projects like the Cancer Dependency Map (DepMap) have utilized these tools to uncover massive landscapes of genetic dependencies across hundreds of human cancer cell lines, revealing how specific mutations or cellular states alter a cell's reliance on particular genes and pathways.

Despite these technological advances, the concept of a "genetic dependency" has primarily been explored within the context of disease or developmental stage within a single species. Whether the fundamental essentiality of a gene can diverge between closely related species—and whether comparative functional genomics can uncover such species-specific dependencies—has remained largely unexplored. Given that humans and chimpanzees diverged from a common ancestor only six to eight million years ago, their genomes are remarkably similar, sharing approximately 99% sequence identity in alignable regions. At the cellular level, pluripotent stem cells (PSCs) from humans and chimpanzees display nearly identical morphologies, share core pluripotency transcriptional networks, and exhibit similar capacities for directed differentiation into diverse somatic lineages. Because of this profound similarity, it has been widely assumed that the core requirements for basic cellular processes, such as proliferation and survival, would be strictly conserved between the two species.

We hypothesized, however, that the evolutionary changes which shaped human-specific physiological traits might have subtly but significantly rewired fundamental cellular pathways, altering the dependencies of human cells on specific genes. If true, a comparative loss-of-function approach could uncover "species-specific genetic dependencies," providing a functional window into the molecular pathways that diverged during human evolution. To test this, we established a robust platform for comparative functional genomics by engineering CRISPRi machinery into multiple human and chimpanzee PSC lines. PSCs provide an ideal, highly scalable in vitro model for this comparison, as they capture the earliest stages of embryonic development, a period during which many critical species-specific developmental trajectories are established.

In this study, we perform paired, genome-wide CRISPRi screens in human and chimpanzee PSCs to systematically evaluate the essentiality of nearly every protein-coding gene in the primate genome. We demonstrate that, while the vast majority of essential genes are perfectly conserved, a stringent subset of genes exhibits profound, reproducible, species-specific effects on cellular proliferation. We discover that these divergent dependencies do not simply occur randomly but are highly enriched for genes operating within coherent functional modules, including specific metabolic signaling complexes and core regulators of the cell cycle. 

Through rigorous secondary validation screens across an expanded panel of human and chimpanzee individuals, we pinpoint exactly 75 genes whose essentiality diverged during recent evolutionary history. Remarkably, we find that human PSCs have evolved a unique robustness to the depletion of several critical regulators of the G1/S cell cycle transition, including cyclin-dependent kinase 2 (CDK2) and Cyclin E1 (CCNE1). In contrast, chimpanzee PSCs retain a strict reliance on these factors for cell cycle progression. Through functional and pharmacological assays, we link this differential dependency to a broader rewiring of cell cycle checkpoint enforcement and progression in human cells.

Furthermore, we explore the developmental implications of these findings by extending our CRISPRi platform to differentiating neural progenitor cells (NPCs) and three-dimensional cerebral organoids. We show that the human-specific robustness to cell cycle perturbations persists in the neural lineage. The regulation of the G1-phase length has long been proposed as a key developmental mechanism controlling the balance between progenitor expansion and neuronal differentiation during cortical development. Our functional genomics data reveal that the molecular inputs governing this critical cell cycle transition have significantly diverged between humans and chimpanzees. Finally, by integrating orangutan stem cells into our functional assays, we infer the phylogenetic origin of these divergent dependencies, demonstrating that the human-specific robustness to CDK2 and CCNE1 depletion represents a derived trait that emerged specifically along the hominin lineage. Collectively, our findings establish comparative functional genomics as a powerful new paradigm for dissecting the molecular basis of human evolution, revealing that the fundamental landscape of essential genes is not static but can be dynamically reshaped during recent evolutionary history.
