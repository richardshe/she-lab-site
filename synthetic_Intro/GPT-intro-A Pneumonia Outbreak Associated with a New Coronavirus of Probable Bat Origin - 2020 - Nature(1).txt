# Introduction [222 words]

Coronaviruses (CoVs) are enveloped, positive-sense RNA viruses that infect a broad range of mammalian and avian hosts and have repeatedly crossed species barriers to cause human disease. The outbreak of severe acute respiratory syndrome (SARS) in 2002–2003, which was traced to animal markets in southern China, revealed the pandemic potential of bat-borne SARS-related coronaviruses (SARSr-CoVs). Since then, extensive surveillance has identified a large and genetically diverse reservoir of SARSr-CoVs in horseshoe bats, including viruses that are closely related to SARS-CoV and some that can use human angiotensin converting enzyme II (ACE2) for cell entry.

In December 2019, a cluster of patients with pneumonia of unknown cause was reported in Wuhan, Hubei province, China. Many of the earliest patients had epidemiological links to the Huanan seafood market, where live animals were also sold, raising concern that a zoonotic pathogen had entered the human population. The clinical presentation and winter occurrence of the outbreak were reminiscent of SARS, and suggested that a coronavirus should be considered among the possible causative agents.

Here we report the identification, genome sequencing and initial characterization of a novel coronavirus from patients with severe pneumonia in Wuhan. We show that this virus, provisionally named 2019-nCoV, belongs to the SARSr-CoV species, is closely related to a bat coronavirus, can be isolated in cell culture, elicits antibody responses in infected patients and uses ACE2 as a cellular entry receptor.
