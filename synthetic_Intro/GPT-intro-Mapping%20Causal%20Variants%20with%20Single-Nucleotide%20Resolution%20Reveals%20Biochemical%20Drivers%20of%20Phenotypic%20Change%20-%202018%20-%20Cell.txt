# Introduction [226 words]

A central goal of genetics is to identify the exact DNA sequence changes that transform genotype into phenotype. The rapid accumulation of whole-genome sequences has made it increasingly straightforward to discover statistical associations between genetic markers and biological traits. Yet association is not causation. In natural populations, causal variants are embedded within haplotype blocks that contain many linked passenger mutations. As a result, most genome-wide association studies localize phenotypic effects to broad genomic intervals, but cannot determine which nucleotide, gene, or biochemical perturbation actually drives the trait.

Model organisms provide a potential solution because crosses can restrict the number of possible alleles and use meiotic recombination to separate nearby variants. However, even the most powerful inbred crosses have generally stopped short of causal nucleotides. Quantitative trait loci typically contain many polymorphisms, and resolving them requires laborious allele replacement or backcrossing experiments that cannot be scaled across the genome. Thus, a long-standing practical gap has persisted between mapping a locus and understanding the molecular basis of phenotypic variation.

Here we show that this gap can be closed by pushing inbred crossing to its practical limit in Saccharomyces cerevisiae. By combining reduced parental divergence, six generations of recombination, dense genotyping, and quantitative phenotyping, we created a genetic panel in which adjacent variants could be distinguished statistically. This strategy enabled genome-wide mapping of natural causal variants at single-nucleotide resolution, revealing the coding, synonymous, and regulatory changes that collectively shape quantitative traits.
