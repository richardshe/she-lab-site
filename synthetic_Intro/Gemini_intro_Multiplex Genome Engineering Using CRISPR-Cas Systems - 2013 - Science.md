# Introduction
The precise modification of genomic sequences is essential for investigating the functional elements of the genome and developing novel therapeutic strategies. While highly targeted genome editing has been achieved using engineered nucleases such as zinc finger nucleases and transcription activator-like effector nucleases, these technologies require the complex design of custom DNA-binding proteins for each new target. The type II CRISPR (clustered regularly interspaced short palindromic repeats)/Cas adaptive immune system of Streptococcus pyogenes has emerged as a promising alternative, utilizing short RNA molecules to guide the Cas9 endonuclease to specific DNA sequences for site-directed cleavage. Here, we engineered the CRISPR/Cas9 system for mammalian cells. We demonstrate that this RNA-guided nuclease can efficiently induce precise double-strand breaks at endogenous loci in human and mouse cells, facilitate homology-directed repair, and allow for simultaneous multiplexed editing, establishing it as a highly programmable genome engineering tool.
