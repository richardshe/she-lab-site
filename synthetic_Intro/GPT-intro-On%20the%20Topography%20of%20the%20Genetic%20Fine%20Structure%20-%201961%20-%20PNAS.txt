# Introduction

The genetic map, as ordinarily drawn, is a line upon which mutations are placed in an order and at distances proportional to recombination frequencies. Such a representation has been highly useful, but it leaves open two questions about the fine structure of the genetic material. First, is the order really linear when examined at the smallest intervals now accessible? Second, when many mutations have been placed upon such a line, are the mutable points distributed as if they were equivalent, or does the genetic material have a characteristic local topography?

The rII region of phage T4 offers a favorable object for this inquiry. Mutants of this region grow readily on one bacterial strain, E. coli B, but fail to grow on another, E. coli K. Thus the occurrence of a standard type recombinant in a cross between two rII mutants can be detected with great sensitivity by a simple plating test. Since very large numbers of mutants can be isolated and compared, the region can be examined more closely than has been possible for most genetic systems.

In a previous study the rII region was analyzed with the aid of mutations that behave genetically as deletions. These deletions, by overlapping one another, make it possible to divide the region into consecutive intervals and to assign point mutations to them by qualitative recombination tests. A deletion which covers the point mutation gives no standard type recombinants; one which does not cover it gives a positive result. In this way much of the labor of making quantitative crosses among all possible pairs of point mutants is avoided. The same material also provides a test of the topology of the region, since certain patterns of overlap would be expected if branches or other departures from a line existed.

The present work extends that analysis in two directions. First, by using additional deletions, the rII region has been divided into a larger number of small segments, permitting a closer test of linearity and a more precise placement of point mutations. Second, many independently arising mutations, both spontaneous and induced, have been mapped within these segments and compared with one another. The induced mutations include those produced by several agents acting in the host cell and by others acting directly upon extracellular phage particles.

The resulting map is therefore not merely a topology, in the sense of an order of points, but a topography, in the sense of a landscape of mutable sites. Some sites recur with extraordinary frequency, while others are found only once or not at all in the present collection. Different mutagens reveal different patterns of favored sites. These differences provide a genetic approach to the chemical individuality of positions within a cistron.
