# Introduction
The development of a complex, multicellular organism from a single fertilized egg is a highly coordinated process that relies on the precise spatial and temporal regulation of gene expression. In the fruit fly Drosophila melanogaster, early embryogenesis provides a uniquely tractable model system for understanding the genetic mechanisms that govern the initial partitioning of the body plan. The morphological blueprint of the Drosophila larva is characterized by a repeating pattern of homologous units along its antero-posterior axis, specifically three thoracic and eight abdominal segments. While each segment ultimately acquires a unique morphological identity—evident in the specific structures and polarity of the denticle bands on the ventral cuticle—the foundational process of segment formation itself must first subdivide the embryonic field into discrete repeating domains. 

Although numerous homeotic mutations have been described that dramatically alter the specific identity of individual segments, these mutations generally do not disrupt the total number or the basic spatial delineation of the segments themselves. This suggests that the genetic processes responsible for defining the initial segmental framework of the embryo are distinct from those that subsequently specify the unique character of each segmental unit. To uncover the genes that construct this fundamental body plan, we have undertaken systematic genetic screens for embryonic lethal mutations that cause specific, reproducible alterations in the basic segmentation pattern of the larva. 

In this report, we describe fifteen distinct genetic loci which, when mutated, lead to profound defects in the number, spacing, and polarity of the larval segments. Through a detailed phenotypic analysis of homozygous mutant embryos, we have been able to categorize these loci into three distinct phenotypic classes based on their specific effects on the body plan. These include the gap mutants, which lack large, contiguous stretches of multiple segments; the pair-rule mutants, which exhibit deletions in homologous structures in alternating segments; and the segment-polarity mutants, which possess the normal number of segments but display characteristic pattern deletions and mirror-image duplications within each individual segment. The distinct nature of these mutant phenotypes provides striking genetic evidence that the morphological process of segmentation in Drosophila involves at least three hierarchical levels of spatial organization during early embryonic development. These loci likely represent the majority of the key genes required to establish the segmental body plan of the organism.
