# Introduction

A central goal of neuroscience is to understand how defined neurons and neural circuits transform patterns of electrical activity into information processing, behavior and plasticity. Achieving this goal requires methods for perturbing activity with the same temporal precision with which neurons normally communicate. Electrical stimulation has long provided millisecond-timescale control, but it is poorly suited for selectively activating genetically or anatomically specified cell populations, and it can recruit passing axons or mixed populations of nearby cells. Pharmacological and genetic methods can be targeted more precisely, but generally lack the speed needed to impose or test the timing of individual spikes and synaptic events. Thus, a method that combines genetic specificity, noninvasiveness and millisecond-scale control of membrane potential has been a longstanding need.

Light provides an attractive physical stimulus for such control because it can be delivered rapidly, patterned in space and time, and applied without mechanical contact with the cell. Several optical approaches have been used to modulate neural activity, including photolysis of caged neurotransmitters, glutamate uncaging and heterologous expression of light-sensitive signaling proteins. These approaches have provided important tools for probing circuit function, but each has substantial limitations. Uncaging methods can be fast but are not intrinsically genetically targeted and often require high light powers or exogenous chemical substrates. Earlier genetically encoded photostimulation methods have relied on multicomponent signaling pathways, synthetic cofactors or slower biochemical cascades, limiting their utility for controlling spike timing on the natural timescale of neural computation.

Microbial rhodopsins offer a potential route around these limitations. Channelrhodopsin-2 (ChR2), from the green alga Chlamydomonas reinhardtii, is a light-gated cation channel that opens rapidly in response to blue light. Because ion flux through ChR2 directly depolarizes the membrane, it could in principle allow optical control of excitability without requiring a metabotropic signaling cascade. However, for ChR2 to serve as a practical tool for neuroscience, several requirements must be met. It must be expressed stably in mammalian neurons, traffic to the plasma membrane, generate photocurrents large and fast enough to reach spike threshold, recover sufficiently between light pulses to support repeated stimulation, and do so without compromising basal electrical properties or cell viability.

Here we show that lentiviral expression of a ChR2-yellow fluorescent protein fusion protein in cultured mammalian hippocampal neurons enables reliable optical control of neural activity. Using high-speed optical switching, we find that brief pulses of blue light can evoke single spikes, precisely timed spike trains, naturalistic Poisson-distributed firing patterns, subthreshold depolarizations and synaptically transmitted excitation or inhibition. These results establish ChR2 as a single-component, genetically targetable actuator for temporally precise optical control of neural activity.
