Acute myeloid leukemia (AML) is a devastating hematologic malignancy characterized by the accumulation of immature myeloid blasts in the bone marrow and blood. Despite intensive chemotherapy, the overall prognosis for adult AML patients remains poor, largely due to high rates of relapse. It is now widely recognized that AML is hierarchically organized and driven by a rare subpopulation of cells termed leukemia stem cells (LSCs). AML LSCs possess self-renewal capacity and are inherently resistant to conventional chemotherapies, allowing them to persist as minimal residual disease and initiate relapse. Consequently, developing novel therapies that specifically eradicate the AML LSC population is essential for achieving durable cures.

The innate immune system, particularly macrophages, plays a vital role in clearing abnormal or damaged cells. To evade macrophage-mediated destruction, normal cells display "don't eat me" signals. CD47 is a widely expressed transmembrane protein that interacts with signal regulatory protein alpha (SIRPα), an inhibitory receptor on macrophages. The binding of CD47 to SIRPα initiates a signaling cascade that potently inhibits phagocytosis.

We previously demonstrated that CD47 is upregulated on murine hematopoietic stem cells during mobilization to protect them from macrophage clearance, and is also highly expressed on various murine and human leukemias. We hypothesized that increased CD47 expression on human AML LSCs contributes to disease pathogenesis by allowing these cells to evade innate immune surveillance. Here, we investigate the clinical and therapeutic significance of CD47 in human AML. We find that CD47 is significantly upregulated on human AML LSCs compared to normal hematopoietic stem cells. Furthermore, we demonstrate that high CD47 expression is an independent, adverse prognostic factor for overall and event-free survival in three independent cohorts of adult AML patients. Crucially, we show that disruption of the CD47-SIRPα interaction using blocking monoclonal antibodies directed against CD47 enables the preferential phagocytosis of AML LSCs by macrophages in vitro and inhibits leukemic engraftment in vivo. These findings establish CD47 as an important prognostic marker and a novel, highly promising therapeutic target for the elimination of AML LSCs.
