# Introduction

The establishment of a segmental pattern is one of the most conspicuous features of insect embryogenesis. In Drosophila the metameric subdivision of the body is first recognized during gastrulation and is later expressed in the regular arrangement of cuticular structures in the larva. Each segment has a characteristic morphology, but all thoracic and abdominal segments share a common organization and polarity. Thus, the larval cuticle provides a simple and reproducible pattern in which alterations of segment number, position and polarity can be recognized with considerable precision.

Many mutations are known which transform the identity of one segment into that of another. These homeotic mutations have shown that segmental identity can be altered without necessarily abolishing segmentation itself. Much less is known about the genes required for the generation of the segmental subdivision. Mutations of this kind would be expected to change the number or arrangement of segments rather than only their regional character. Since the differentiated larval pattern preserves the results of earlier embryonic events, such mutations should allow an analysis of the steps by which the initially continuous egg becomes divided into a series of homologous units.

We have therefore searched systematically for embryonic lethal mutations which alter the cuticular pattern of the Drosophila larva. The mutations described here were isolated without regard to any particular chromosome region or previously known locus. The mutant embryos were examined for reproducible deviations from the normal segmental pattern. Although many embryonic lethal mutations have no obvious effect on the larval cuticle, a small number cause specific and characteristic changes in segmentation.

The phenotypes of these mutants fall into three main groups. In one group, a defined part of each segment is deleted and the remaining part is duplicated with reversed polarity. In a second group, homologous parts of alternating segments are missing, suggesting a repeat unit larger than the individual segment. In a third group, one continuous region of the embryo fails to form the normal sequence of segments. These classes do not correspond to transformations of segment identity, but rather to different alterations in the spatial organization of the embryo.

The mutant phenotypes therefore provide genetic evidence that segmentation is not established in a single step. Instead, the embryo appears to pass through successive levels of organization, involving the whole egg, a double-segmental repeat, and the individual segment. The loci defined by these mutations identify zygotic functions required for this process.
