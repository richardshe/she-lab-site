# Introduction

In mammals, differentiation is normally accompanied by progressive restrictions in developmental potential, but the extent to which these restrictions reflect irreversible changes in genetic information has remained uncertain. Nuclear transfer provides a direct test of this question, because the nucleus of a donor cell must be reprogrammed by the cytoplasm of an enucleated oocyte to direct development of an entire animal. Previous studies have shown development after transfer of nuclei from early embryonic blastomeres, and live offspring have recently been obtained from an embryo-derived cell line induced to become quiescent before transfer. These results suggested that compatibility between donor and recipient cell-cycle stage may be critical for reprogramming. It was not known, however, whether nuclei from fetal or adult somatic cells could support normal development to term. We have therefore derived cell populations from a day-9 embryo, a day-26 fetus and adult mammary gland, induced them to quiescence, and used them as nuclear donors to enucleated sheep oocytes.
