Chronic myeloid leukemia (CML) is a clonal myeloproliferative disorder of the hematopoietic stem cell that is characterized in its initial chronic phase by the massive overproduction of mature and maturing granulocytes. If left untreated, the disease inevitably progresses from the relatively indolent chronic phase to an accelerated phase and ultimately to a rapidly fatal acute leukemia known as the blast crisis. The cytogenetic hallmark of CML is the Philadelphia (Ph) chromosome, which is present in the leukemic cells of more than 90 percent of patients. The Ph chromosome is formed by a reciprocal translocation between the long arms of chromosomes 9 and 22, resulting in the fusion of the breakpoint cluster region (BCR) gene on chromosome 22 with the Abelson murine leukemia (ABL) gene on chromosome 9. 

The resulting chimeric BCR-ABL gene encodes a constitutively activated, cytoplasmic tyrosine kinase. Extensive experimental evidence from both in vitro and in vivo models has firmly established that the continuous, deregulated tyrosine kinase activity of the BCR-ABL fusion protein is not merely a marker of CML, but is the essential, driving pathogenetic event that causes the transformation of normal hematopoietic stem cells into leukemic cells. Because BCR-ABL is uniquely expressed in leukemic cells and its kinase activity is strictly required for the maintenance of the malignant phenotype, it represents an ideal and highly specific molecular target for therapeutic intervention. 

STI571 (formerly CGP 57148B) is a rationally designed, orally bioavailable 2-phenylaminopyrimidine derivative that functions as a potent and highly specific competitive inhibitor of the ATP-binding site of the BCR-ABL tyrosine kinase. In preclinical studies, STI571 was shown to effectively suppress the proliferation and induce the apoptosis of BCR-ABL-positive CML cell lines, as well as fresh leukemic cells isolated from patients with CML, while largely sparing normal hematopoietic progenitor cells.

Based on these compelling preclinical findings, we conducted a phase 1, dose-escalating clinical trial of STI571 in patients with chronic-phase CML who had previously failed to respond to, or were unable to tolerate, standard therapy with interferon alfa. The primary objective of this study was to determine the safety, tolerability, and maximum tolerated dose of continuous oral daily administration of STI571. The secondary objectives included the evaluation of the drug's pharmacokinetic profile and its potential antileukemic activity, as assessed by hematologic and cytogenetic responses.
