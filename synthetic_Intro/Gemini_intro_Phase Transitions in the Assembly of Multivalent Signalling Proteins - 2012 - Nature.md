# Introduction
Biological systems are highly organized across multiple length scales, from the Angstrom-scale interactions of individual amino acids to the micrometre-scale compartmentalization of the entire cell. A major challenge in modern cell biology is understanding the precise physical mechanisms by which molecular-level binding events are translated into the assembly of large, macroscopic cellular structures. While it is well known that many signalling proteins contain multiple interaction domains—such as tandem arrays of SH3 domains and proline-rich motifs—the functional consequences of this multivalency have primarily been studied in the context of small, discrete protein complexes. 

However, polymer theory suggests that when multivalent molecules interact, they can undergo a sharp transition to form large, cross-linked, dynamic networks. In this study, we demonstrate that interactions between diverse engineered and naturally occurring multivalent macromolecules, including multi-domain proteins and RNA, can drive macroscopic liquid-liquid demixing phase separations in aqueous solution. This process produces micrometre-sized, liquid-like droplets that are highly enriched in the interacting components. We show that this macroscopic phase transition is thermodynamically coupled to a molecular sol-gel transition, driven by the valency of the interacting species.

We apply this principle to the natural tripartite signalling system comprising the transmembrane protein nephrin, the adaptor protein NCK, and the actin-nucleating factor N-WASP. We find that the phase separation of these components is strictly regulated by the degree of nephrin phosphorylation and corresponds directly to a sharp, switch-like activation of the Arp2/3 complex. Our findings suggest that phase transitions driven by multivalent interactions provide a general physical mechanism by which cells can spatially organize and biochemically regulate complex signalling pathways.
