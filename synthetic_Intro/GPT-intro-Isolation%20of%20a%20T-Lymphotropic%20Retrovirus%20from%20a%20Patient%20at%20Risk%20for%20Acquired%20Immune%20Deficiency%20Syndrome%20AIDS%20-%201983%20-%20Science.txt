# Introduction

Acquired immune deficiency syndrome (AIDS) has recently been described in young adults with severe opportunistic infections, Kaposi's sarcoma, or both, and with marked impairment of cellular immunity. The disease has been observed with particular frequency in homosexual men, but also in hemophiliacs, drug users, and other patients without a known congenital immune defect. In many cases the onset of the syndrome is preceded by persistent lymphadenopathy, asthenia, fever, loss of weight, and serological evidence of previous or continuing infection with herpesviruses. These observations have suggested that AIDS may be caused, or at least favored, by an infectious agent transmitted between humans.

Several features of the disease point especially to an agent acting on T lymphocytes. Patients with AIDS often show a selective decrease in the T-helper lymphocyte population and a profound defect in cell-mediated immune reactions. A persistent infection of lymphoid cells could therefore account both for the early lymphadenopathy and for the later failure of immune surveillance. However, no virus has yet been reproducibly isolated from patients at risk for AIDS, and the relationship of the many viruses found in such patients to the immune defect remains uncertain.

Human T-cell leukemia virus (HTLV) has recently been isolated from cultured T lymphocytes of patients with certain T-cell leukemias and lymphomas. These viruses are type-C retroviruses, contain a reverse transcriptase, and can be propagated in activated human T lymphocytes. Serological studies have indicated that HTLV-related viruses are horizontally transmitted in man and that related antigens may occur in patients with different lymphoproliferative disorders. Since these viruses have a marked tropism for T cells, it was of interest to determine whether a related retrovirus could be detected in patients with early clinical signs of AIDS.

We have therefore examined lymph node and blood lymphocytes from a patient with generalized lymphadenopathy and epidemiological risk for AIDS. The cultures were stimulated with phytohemagglutinin and T-cell growth factor and were also treated with antiserum to human alpha interferon, since endogenous interferon may inhibit the release of retroviruses from chronically infected cells. We report here the isolation and serial transmission to normal lymphocytes of a new T-lymphotropic retrovirus. Although related to HTLV by some structural and antigenic properties, this virus is immunologically distinct from known HTLV-I isolates and may represent a member of a broader family of human T-lymphotropic retroviruses associated with pathological syndromes including AIDS.
