The identification of inherited factors that influence susceptibility to common disease has long been a central objective of human genetics. Family and twin studies have established substantial heritable components for many common disorders, yet, for most such diseases, progress in identifying the underlying variants has been slow. Linkage approaches have been highly successful for Mendelian disease, but have generally had limited power to detect the modest effects expected for common susceptibility alleles. Candidate-gene studies, although biologically motivated, have often been constrained by incomplete knowledge of disease mechanisms and by inadequate sample sizes, and many reported associations have failed to replicate.

Recent advances in genomic resources and genotyping technology have transformed the prospects for association analysis. Dense maps of single nucleotide polymorphisms, together with information about linkage disequilibrium from the International HapMap Project, now make it possible to survey a substantial fraction of common variation across the genome. At the same time, high-throughput array-based genotyping has enabled studies of a scale not previously feasible. These developments provide an empirical test of whether genome-wide association (GWA) studies can identify reproducible genetic effects for common diseases, and whether those effects will be sufficiently informative to illuminate pathophysiological pathways.

Several features of common disease genetics make large, carefully designed studies essential. The variants most likely to be detected by current platforms are common alleles of modest effect, requiring thousands of cases and controls for adequate power. Conversely, the examination of hundreds of thousands of markers creates a substantial burden of multiple testing and makes stringent quality control indispensable. Subtle differences in DNA handling, genotype calling, population ancestry or control ascertainment can generate apparent associations that are unrelated to disease biology. For these reasons, the success of GWA studies depends not only on marker density and sample size, but also on robust experimental design, uniform analysis procedures and independent evidence for replication.

The use of shared controls offers an efficient strategy for studying multiple diseases, but its reliability has not been extensively evaluated in genome-wide data. Such an approach could increase power and reduce cost, particularly for national collections in which large numbers of well-characterized disease samples are available. However, shared controls also raise several concerns, including the possibility of disease misclassification within the reference group, differences between control collections, and confounding by population structure. These issues are particularly relevant in populations, such as that of Great Britain, that are often treated as relatively homogeneous but nevertheless contain detectable geographical variation in allele frequencies.

Here we report a large collaborative GWA study of seven common familial diseases: bipolar disorder, coronary artery disease, Crohn's disease, hypertension, rheumatoid arthritis, type 1 diabetes and type 2 diabetes. For each disease we genotyped approximately 2,000 cases and compared them with a shared set of approximately 3,000 controls drawn from two British control collections. We used the Affymetrix GeneChip 500K Mapping Array Set, developed a genotype-calling algorithm for the project, and applied extensive quality-control and analytical procedures. The study was designed both to identify susceptibility loci for these diseases and to assess general methodological questions concerning power, population structure, shared controls and the allelic architecture of common human disease.
