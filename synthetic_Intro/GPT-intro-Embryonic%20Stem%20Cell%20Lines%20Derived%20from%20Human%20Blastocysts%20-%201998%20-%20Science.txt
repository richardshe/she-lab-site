# Introduction [196 words]

Embryonic stem (ES) cells are pluripotent cell lines derived from the inner cell mass of the preimplantation embryo. Mouse ES cells can be maintained indefinitely in an undifferentiated state and, when returned to the embryo, can contribute to all tissues of the adult, including the germ line. These properties have made mouse ES cells central to experimental mammalian genetics and to the analysis of early developmental decisions. However, early development differs substantially among mammals, and the extrapolation from mouse to human development is necessarily incomplete. Primate ES cell lines, recently derived from rhesus monkey and common marmoset blastocysts, resemble human embryonal carcinoma cells in morphology and marker expression, but retain a normal karyotype and a broader developmental potential. The derivation of analogous cell lines from human blastocysts would provide a reproducible in vitro system for studying early human development and for identifying conditions that direct differentiation to clinically important cell types. Such cells could also provide genetically normal, expandable starting material for drug discovery, toxicology, and transplantation medicine. We report here the derivation and characterization of pluripotent human ES cell lines from donated blastocysts produced by in vitro fertilization.
