# Introduction [548 words]

The mammalian innate immune system detects infection through receptors that recognize conserved molecular patterns present in microorganisms but absent, inaccessible, or chemically altered in host cells. Among the best characterized of these receptors are Toll-like receptors (TLRs), which initiate inflammatory and antiviral responses after encountering microbial products. Several TLRs recognize nucleic acids. TLR9 is activated by DNA containing unmethylated CpG motifs, a sequence feature enriched in bacteria and viruses but largely suppressed or methylated in vertebrate genomes. TLR3 recognizes double-stranded RNA, a molecular signature commonly associated with viral replication. More recently, TLR7 and TLR8 have been implicated in the recognition of RNA and small nucleoside analogs. These findings demonstrate that nucleic acids can function as potent immune stimuli and that the innate immune system has evolved mechanisms to distinguish potentially dangerous nucleic acid molecules from the abundant nucleic acids present in host cells.

This distinction is not trivial. Host cells contain large amounts of RNA in many forms, including ribosomal RNA, transfer RNA, messenger RNA, small nuclear RNA, and mitochondrial RNA. Under normal conditions these molecules are sequestered from endosomal RNA sensors, but tissue injury, necrosis, or defective clearance of cellular debris can expose self RNA to immune cells. Indeed, RNA released from necrotic mammalian cells can activate dendritic cells, whereas apoptotic cells are generally less inflammatory. Such observations raise a central question: if RNA itself can activate innate immune receptors, how does the immune system avoid responding continuously to the RNA of the host?

One possible mechanism is that immune recognition depends not only on the presence and localization of RNA, but also on its chemical composition. Naturally occurring RNA is not composed simply of the four canonical nucleosides adenosine, cytidine, guanosine, and uridine. During RNA maturation, many RNAs acquire posttranscriptional nucleoside modifications, including methylated bases, altered uridines, and pseudouridine. Nearly one hundred modified nucleosides have been described, and their abundance varies widely among RNA species and organisms. Transfer RNA is extensively modified, ribosomal RNA contains numerous conserved modifications, and eukaryotic RNA generally contains more modified nucleosides than bacterial RNA. The biological roles of many of these modifications remain incompletely understood, although their conservation suggests functional importance.

Chemical modification is already known to provide a basis for self-nonself discrimination in nucleic acid biology. Bacteria methylate selected residues in their own DNA, thereby protecting the genome from restriction enzymes that cleave unmodified foreign DNA. In vertebrates, methylation of CpG motifs reduces the stimulatory potential of self DNA, whereas unmethylated CpG-rich microbial DNA activates TLR9. By analogy, modified nucleosides in host RNA might reduce its capacity to stimulate RNA-sensitive immune receptors. Conversely, RNA species that contain few modifications, such as bacterial RNA or mitochondrial RNA, might retain stronger immunostimulatory activity.

Despite decades of evidence that RNA can induce immune responses, the effect of naturally occurring nucleoside modifications on RNA-mediated immune activation has not been systematically examined. In this study, we tested the hypothesis that nucleoside modification suppresses the capacity of RNA to activate dendritic cells and human RNA-sensing TLRs. We compared RNAs isolated from mammalian, bacterial, and mitochondrial sources with in vitro-transcribed RNAs containing defined modified nucleosides, including 5-methylcytidine, N6-methyladenosine, 5-methyluridine, 2-thiouridine, pseudouridine, and 2'-O-methyluridine. By examining cytokine secretion, dendritic cell maturation, and signaling through human TLR3, TLR7, and TLR8, we sought to determine whether nucleoside modification represents a molecular feature by which the innate immune system discriminates among different classes of RNA.
