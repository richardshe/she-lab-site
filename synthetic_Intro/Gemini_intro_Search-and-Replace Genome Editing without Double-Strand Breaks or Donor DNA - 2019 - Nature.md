The development of CRISPR-Cas9 programmable nucleases has revolutionized the life sciences by enabling targeted disruption of genes and, in the presence of a donor DNA template, the introduction of specific genetic changes via homology-directed repair (HDR). However, the reliance of these traditional genome editing methods on the generation of DNA double-strand breaks (DSBs) presents significant limitations. The repair of DSBs by the error-prone non-homologous end joining (NHEJ) pathway often yields unpredictable insertions and deletions (indels) at the target locus, compromising editing precision. Furthermore, HDR is generally inefficient, particularly in post-mitotic or non-dividing cells, and can be accompanied by high frequencies of indel byproducts, making precise and efficient gene correction challenging for many therapeutic applications.

To address the limitations of DSB-mediated editing, base editors were developed. Cytidine and adenine base editors, which consist of catalytically impaired Cas9 fused to a nucleobase deaminase enzyme, can efficiently install targeted transition mutations (C·G to T·A, and A·T to G·C) without generating DSBs or requiring a donor DNA template. Base editors represent a major advance in precision genome editing, demonstrating high efficiency and minimal indel formation across various cell types. However, base editing currently cannot install transversions, insertions, or deletions. Moreover, base editors require a strictly positioned protospacer adjacent motif (PAM) to place the target nucleotide within a narrow activity window. If multiple cytidine or adenine bases are present within this window, bystander mutations can occur alongside the desired edit.

Because transversion point mutations, insertions, and deletions collectively account for the majority of known pathogenic human genetic variants, there is a critical need for a precision genome editing technology that can directly and efficiently install any combination of these changes without the detrimental consequences of DSBs. Such a method would ideally write new genetic information into a specified genomic locus directly from an engineered component, bypassing the need for exogenous donor DNA templates and the unpredictable nature of DSB repair. 

Here, we describe prime editing, a novel and versatile 'search-and-replace' genome editing approach that directly writes new genetic information into a specified DNA site without generating DSBs or requiring donor DNA. Prime editing is mediated by a prime editor (PE) protein, consisting of a catalytically impaired Cas9 endonuclease fused to an engineered reverse transcriptase. The PE is programmed by a prime editing guide RNA (pegRNA), which acts as both the guide sequence to specify the target locus and the template that encodes the desired edit. Upon binding to the target site, the Cas9 nickase domain nicks the non-edited DNA strand, allowing the reverse transcriptase domain to extend the 3' end of the nicked strand using the pegRNA as a template. This process introduces the new genetic information directly into the genome. We systematically engineered and optimized the PE components and pegRNA design to maximize editing efficiency and purity. We demonstrate that prime editing can efficiently install targeted insertions, deletions, and all twelve possible types of point mutations in human cells with remarkable precision and substantially fewer indel byproducts than Cas9-initiated HDR. We apply prime editing to precisely correct the primary genetic causes of sickle cell disease and Tay-Sachs disease, highlighting its potential to dramatically expand the capabilities of precision genetic medicine.
