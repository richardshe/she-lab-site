# Introduction [140 words]

Programmable genome editing technologies enable targeted modification of genetic information and provide powerful tools for interrogating biological function. Existing approaches based on zinc finger nucleases and transcription activator-like effector nucleases require protein engineering for each genomic target, limiting their scalability for systematic perturbation of multiple loci. Type II CRISPR/Cas systems provide an alternative strategy in which a Cas9 nuclease is guided to complementary DNA sequences by short RNAs and cleaves targets adjacent to a protospacer-adjacent motif. This RNA-guided mechanism has been reconstituted in vitro and in bacterial cells, suggesting that CRISPR/Cas components might be adapted for programmable genome engineering in eukaryotic cells. Here, we engineer type II CRISPR/Cas systems for activity in mammalian cells and test whether heterologous expression of Cas9 and guide RNAs can direct efficient, specific, and multiplexed modification of endogenous genomic loci.
