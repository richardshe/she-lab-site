# Introduction

Since its emergence in late 2019, severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) has spread globally, causing substantial morbidity, mortality, and societal disruption. Coronavirus disease 2019 (Covid-19), the clinical illness caused by SARS-CoV-2, ranges from asymptomatic infection to respiratory failure and death, with older adults and persons with coexisting conditions at highest risk for severe outcomes. Although nonpharmaceutical interventions can reduce transmission, durable control of the pandemic requires vaccines that can be developed rapidly, manufactured at scale, and shown in large clinical trials to be safe and effective.

The trimeric spike glycoprotein of SARS-CoV-2 mediates viral entry and is the principal target of neutralizing antibodies. Vaccine candidates that induce immune responses against spike therefore represent a rational approach to prevention of Covid-19. RNA-based vaccines offer a platform in which the antigen is encoded directly by a synthetic RNA molecule, allowing rapid design after release of a viral genetic sequence. BNT162b2 is a lipid nanoparticle-formulated, nucleoside-modified RNA vaccine candidate encoding the full-length SARS-CoV-2 spike protein, modified by two proline substitutions to stabilize the protein in the prefusion conformation and preserve neutralization-sensitive epitopes.

In phase 1 testing, BNT162b2 induced SARS-CoV-2 neutralizing antibody responses and cellular immune responses while producing mainly transient local and systemic reactions. On the basis of these data, a 30-μg dose of BNT162b2 administered twice, 21 days apart, was selected for further evaluation. Establishing clinical efficacy and safety required a large, randomized trial with sufficient representation of persons at increased risk for Covid-19 complications and with prospective surveillance for symptomatic, laboratory-confirmed disease. We conducted an ongoing multinational, placebo-controlled, observer-blinded, phase 2/3 trial to evaluate the safety and efficacy of two doses of BNT162b2 in persons 16 years of age or older. Here, we report the primary efficacy analysis and safety data available through a median follow-up of approximately 2 months after the second dose.
